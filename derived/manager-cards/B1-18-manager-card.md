# SKIDS Blood Clinic — Manager Card

## What this clinic does

The Blood Clinic investigates blood-related concerns that go beyond simple iron-deficiency anaemia (which is managed by the Nutrition Clinic). When iron medicine does not bring the blood count up, this clinic runs detailed blood tests to find the exact type of anaemia and the right treatment. It also identifies thalassemia trait (a carrier state — not a disease, but important for future family planning), screens for bleeding disorders in children who bruise easily or bleed excessively, monitors children with low platelets (ITP), and evaluates persistent lumps or swollen glands. For serious blood conditions or suspected cancer, the clinic refers urgently to a blood specialist or cancer specialist.

## Top parent objections (use these scripts)

| Objection | Your response |
|---|---|
| "Iron didn't work — just give stronger iron." | "The doctor has checked why the iron didn't work. There are different types of anaemia, and some don't respond to iron at all. The blood test tells us the exact type so we can give the right treatment." |
| "Thalassemia trait — does this mean my child is sick?" | "No — carrier status is not a disease. Your child is healthy. We identify it so that future family planning is informed. This is very common in India." |
| "She's bruising — is it leukaemia?" | "Bruising in children is very common and usually not serious. The blood test checks the platelet count and the blood cells. In most cases, it is a temporary immune response. The doctor will explain the results clearly." |
| "Why weekly blood tests for ITP? Can't you just treat it?" | "Most children with ITP recover on their own without medicine. We monitor the platelet count to make sure it is trending upward. Treating when it's not needed exposes your child to medicine side effects unnecessarily." |
| "G6PD — can he ever eat normally?" | "Yes — most foods are safe. There is a short list of specific foods and medicines to avoid. We give you a card to carry and share with teachers and doctors. Day-to-day eating is not affected." |

## Follow-up schedule (for scheduling)

| Condition | Next visit | Re-engage if missed by |
|---|---|---|
| ITP (observation — weekly platelet count) | Weekly for 4 weeks | 3 days |
| ITP (on treatment) | 48-72 hours after treatment, then weekly | 3 days |
| Thalassemia trait identified | 3 months (counselling reinforcement) | 4 months |
| G6PD deficiency confirmed | 3 months (avoidance-list reinforcement) | 4 months |
| Anaemia workup (treatment started) | 4 weeks (recheck blood count) | 6 weeks |
| Bleeding-disorder screening | Per blood specialist management plan | Per specialist guidance |
| Swollen gland under observation | 4 weeks | 6 weeks |

**High-priority loss-to-follow-up:** A child with ITP on weekly platelet monitoring. Missing the weekly blood test means we cannot track whether the platelet count is recovering or dropping dangerously. Manager contacts family within 3 days of a missed visit. Also high-priority: thalassemia-trait families who need genetic counselling follow-through.

---

## What we do NOT promise

- We do not manage thalassemia major (the severe form requiring regular blood transfusions) — that goes to a blood specialist centre.
- We do not manage sickle-cell disease with complications — that goes to a blood specialist.
- We do not diagnose or treat leukaemia or lymphoma — those go urgently to a cancer specialist.
- We do not perform bone-marrow tests — those are done by a specialist.
- We do not give blood transfusions — those are done at a hospital.
- We do not manage haemophilia beyond initial screening — that goes to a blood specialist.

## Escalation

If the doctor flags a specialist referral, the Manager Console will show:
- Child's name and the specialist type needed (blood specialist, cancer specialist, emergency room)
- Contact details for the partner specialist
- Urgency level (same-day emergency for very severe anaemia or active severe bleeding; within 7 days for suspected cancer, very low platelets, or moderately severe anaemia; within 4 weeks for confirmed thalassemia major, sickle-cell disease, haemophilia, or chronic low platelets)
- Your job: coordinate the appointment and follow up within the stated timeframe
- For ITP families: weekly check-in calls during the observation phase to ensure blood tests are being done
- For thalassemia-trait families: reminder about partner-testing advice at adolescent milestones
