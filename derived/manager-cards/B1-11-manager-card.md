# Speech & Language Clinic — Manager Card

## What this clinic does

The Speech & Language Clinic assesses children who are slow to talk, hard to understand, or who stutter. A speech-language pathologist (SLP) works with the child and the family to build language skills through structured therapy and parent coaching. Every child's hearing is checked first — hearing problems are the most common fixable cause of language delay.

## What parents say when they arrive

- "She's not talking yet."
- "He only says a few words."
- "We can't understand what she says."
- "He doesn't seem to understand instructions."
- "She stutters."
- "He was talking and stopped."
- "She won't talk at school but talks at home."
- "He has a lisp."
- "She drools and can't chew properly."

## Top parent objections (use these scripts)

| Objection | Your response |
|---|---|
| "He'll talk when he's ready." | "Most late talkers do catch up. But the assessment tells us which ones need help. If he needs help, starting now gives him the best chance." |
| "Does he need a doctor for talking?" | "The speech therapist is more like a teacher than a doctor. She teaches your child — and your family — techniques to build language, just like a tutor helps with maths." |
| "Is speaking two languages causing the delay?" | "No. Children who speak two languages reach the same milestones. We assess in both languages. Dropping a language is not recommended." |
| "It's because of too much screen time." | "Screen time can slow language because it replaces conversation. We help you build more talking time into the day — without shaming." |

## What we do NOT promise

- We do not perform hearing tests at this clinic — those happen at the Hearing & Ear Clinic first.
- We do not treat voice problems (hoarseness) — those go to an ENT specialist.
- We do not treat swallowing or feeding difficulties — those go to a specialist team.
- We do not diagnose autism — if autism is suspected, the child is assessed at the Behavioural and Developmental clinics.
- We do not treat cleft-palate-related speech — those cases go to a cleft team.
