# SKIDS Infections Clinic — Manager Card

## What this clinic does

The Infections Clinic evaluates children who seem to get sick very frequently, manages common infections like dengue, malaria, and typhoid, coordinates TB screening, and investigates prolonged fevers that have not been diagnosed. The clinic places a strong emphasis on responsible antibiotic use — not prescribing antibiotics for viral illnesses like common colds, and choosing the right antibiotic when one is genuinely needed. For children with frequent serious infections, the clinic runs tests to check whether the immune system is working properly. Severe infections, confirmed TB, and immune-system conditions are referred to specialist centres.

## Top parent objections (use these scripts)

| Objection | Your response |
|---|---|
| "Every doctor gives antibiotics for fever — why doesn't yours?" | "Our doctors follow national and international guidelines for when antibiotics actually help. Most fevers in children are viral, and antibiotics don't work on viruses. If the fever turns out to be bacterial, we start antibiotics immediately. This approach protects your child from antibiotic side effects and resistance." |
| "Why do we need daily visits for dengue?" | "The critical phase of dengue happens when the fever breaks. Daily blood tests let us catch warning signs before they become dangerous. This monitoring is what keeps your child safe at home instead of in the hospital." |
| "The immune-system tests are expensive — is this really necessary?" | "If your child meets the criteria for frequent serious infections, these tests are the only way to rule out an immune-system problem. Most children test normal, and that's reassuring. If a problem is found early, treatment is much more effective." |

## Follow-up schedule (for scheduling)

| Condition | Next visit | Re-engage if missed by |
|---|---|---|
| Viral illness (no antibiotics — counselling only) | No scheduled follow-up | Via routine screening |
| Dengue without warning signs | Daily during fever phase | Same day (phone immediately) |
| Dengue post-hospital discharge | 48 hours after discharge | Same day |
| Malaria (on treatment) | Day 3 (blood test), day 7, day 28 | 1 day (for day-3 test) |
| Recurrent-infection evaluation | 2 weeks (results review) | 3 weeks |
| Prolonged fever under investigation | Weekly until resolved | 3 days |
| Typhoid (on antibiotics) | Day 7 (response check) | 3 days |
| TB screening (referred to TB centre) | 4 weeks (confirm enrolment) | 2 weeks |

**High-priority loss-to-follow-up:** A child on daily dengue monitoring who misses a visit. Manager contacts family by phone the same day — no exceptions. Also high-priority: children on active malaria or typhoid treatment who miss follow-up.

---

## What we do NOT promise

- We do not manage severe infections requiring hospital admission (blood poisoning, meningitis, severe pneumonia) — those go to the emergency room.
- We do not manage HIV — that goes to a specialist centre.
- We do not treat TB — we screen for it and refer to the government TB treatment centre (DOTS programme). The screening is at SKIDS; the treatment is at the TB centre.
- We do not manage confirmed immune-system disorders beyond initial testing — those go to an immune specialist.
- We do not manage severe dengue with shock or cerebral malaria — those go to the emergency room immediately.

## Escalation

If the doctor flags a specialist referral, the Manager Console will show:
- Child's name and the specialist type needed (emergency room, hospital admission, immune specialist, TB centre)
- Contact details for the partner facility
- Urgency level (same-day emergency for blood poisoning, severe dengue, or cerebral malaria; within 7 days for dengue with warning signs, complicated malaria, or prolonged fever; within 4 weeks for confirmed immune-system condition, TB referral, or persistent swollen glands)
- Your job: coordinate the referral and follow up within the stated timeframe
- For dengue daily monitoring: ensure daily visit slots are available and contact the family each morning during the fever phase
- For TB referral: follow up at 2 weeks to confirm the family has enrolled at the TB treatment centre
