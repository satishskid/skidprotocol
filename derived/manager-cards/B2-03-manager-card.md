# SKIDS Rheumatology Clinic — Manager Card

## What this clinic does

The Rheumatology Clinic manages autoimmune and inflammatory conditions in children: juvenile arthritis (joint inflammation caused by the immune system), lupus (an immune condition that can affect skin, joints, kidneys, and blood), dermatomyositis (immune-related muscle and skin disease), vasculitis (inflamed blood vessels, including Kawasaki disease), and periodic fever syndromes (recurring fevers from immune over-activity). The doctor prescribes anti-inflammatory and immune-calming medicines, coordinates eye checks (juvenile arthritis carries a risk of silent eye inflammation), and monitors with regular blood tests. This is specialist care available only at SKIDS clinics where a credentialed children's rheumatologist is on staff. At clinics without this specialist, children are referred to our named rheumatology partner.

## Top parent objections (use these scripts)

| Objection | Your response |
|---|---|
| "Joint swelling comes and goes — we will come when it flares." | "I understand. The challenge with juvenile arthritis is that inflammation can be present even when the swelling looks better, and that invisible inflammation damages the joint over time. The regular visits include blood tests that detect this hidden inflammation. Treating early and consistently prevents joint damage that cannot be reversed later." |
| "The blood tests are too frequent — my child is afraid of needles." | "We understand, and our phlebotomist is very experienced with children. The monthly tests are only for the first three months on methotrexate — after that, we move to every three months. The tests protect your child's liver and blood, which is why we cannot skip them. We can use numbing cream before the blood draw." |
| "The medicine is too expensive." | "We agree that rheumatology medicines, especially biologics, are expensive. The Premium subscription includes the routine medicines and blood tests, which reduces the overall cost. For biologics specifically, there may be patient-assistance programmes — the doctor can discuss options. The alternative to treating is joint damage, which is more expensive and more painful to manage later." |
| "We want to try homeopathy first." | "We respect your choice. The doctor's request is that your child continues the prescribed medicines alongside homeopathy, because juvenile arthritis can damage joints if not treated, and that damage is permanent. As long as both continue, we monitor safely." |
| "The eye checks seem unnecessary — my child's eyes are fine." | "That is exactly the point — the type of eye inflammation that happens with juvenile arthritis is silent. The child feels nothing, and by the time there are symptoms, vision may already be affected. The eye checks catch it early, when it is easy to treat. Skipping them is the biggest avoidable risk in juvenile arthritis care." |

## Follow-up schedule (for scheduling)

| Condition | Next visit | Re-engage if missed by |
|---|---|---|
| Juvenile arthritis — active, on medicine adjustment | Every 4 weeks | 14 days |
| Juvenile arthritis — inactive on stable medicine | Every 3 months | 14 days |
| Juvenile arthritis — on biologic injection | Every 3 months (injection schedule runs separately) | 7 days for injection; 14 days for visit |
| Juvenile arthritis — eye check (high risk) | Every 3 months (ophthalmologist) | 30 days |
| Lupus — active or flare | Every 2-4 weeks | 7 days |
| Lupus — stable | Every 1-3 months | 14 days |
| Dermatomyositis — active | Every 4 weeks | 14 days |
| Kawasaki disease — after treatment | 2 weeks, 6 weeks, 6 months, 12 months (heart scan at each) | 7 days |
| Periodic fever (PFAPA) | Every 3 months | 14 days |
| Purple-spot rash (IgA vasculitis) | 2 weeks, 4 weeks, 3 months, 6 months, 12 months (urine check at each) | 14 days |

**High-priority loss-to-follow-up:** Any child on immune-suppressing medicine (methotrexate, biologics, steroids for lupus). Missing follow-ups means blood-test monitoring is missed, which protects the liver and blood. Manager phone call at 14 days; rheumatologist notified. Also: biologic injection overdue by 7 days gets a phone call.

## What we do NOT promise

- We do not offer this service without a credentialed children's rheumatologist on staff. If the specialist is not available at your clinic, the child is referred to our rheumatology partner.
- We do not manage life-threatening immune crises (macrophage activation syndrome) in clinic — those go to the emergency room and a tertiary rheumatology centre. We recognise the warning signs and transfer immediately.
- We do not perform kidney biopsies — those are done by a kidney specialist. We manage the immune treatment around the biopsy.
- We do not perform joint procedures at most clinics — if joint aspiration or injection is needed, it may be done at a partner facility.

## Escalation

If the doctor flags a specialist referral or emergency, the Manager Console will show:
- Child's name and the type of emergency or specialist needed (emergency room, tertiary rheumatology, kidney specialist, ophthalmologist, surgeon)
- Contact details for the partner specialist or emergency room
- Urgency level (same-day emergency, within 7 days, or within 4 weeks)
- Your job: coordinate the appointment and follow up within the stated timeframe
- **For same-day emergencies (suspected macrophage activation syndrome in a child with juvenile arthritis — high fever, looking very unwell; lupus brain involvement; kidney failure in lupus):** coordinate immediate transfer to the emergency room. Confirm by phone that the receiving team is expecting the child. This is life-threatening.
- **For eye-check overdue in juvenile arthritis:** treat as high priority. Silent eye inflammation can cause permanent vision loss if missed. Reschedule the ophthalmology appointment the same day you notice the overdue flag.
