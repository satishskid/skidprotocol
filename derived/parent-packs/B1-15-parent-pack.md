# Understanding Your Child's Tummy Problems — For Parents

## What is it?

Recurring tummy pain, reflux, chronic diarrhoea, and difficulty digesting certain foods are among the most common reasons children visit the doctor. In most cases, the gut is sensitive but not damaged — this is called functional abdominal pain. The nerves in the gut are more reactive than usual, which causes real pain even though there is no disease or damage. About 10–20% of school-age children experience this.

In some cases, there is a specific cause that needs treatment: reflux (stomach acid irritating the food pipe), coeliac disease (the body reacting to gluten in wheat, barley, and rye), a stomach bug called H. pylori, or — rarely — inflammatory bowel disease. The doctor's job is to tell the difference and treat the right cause.

## What does SKIDS do about it?

Here is how we help your child:

- **Take a careful history.** We ask detailed questions about the pain pattern, what makes it better or worse, diet, bowel habits, and any warning signs that suggest a specific cause.
- **Check for warning signs.** We look for specific features (like blood in the stool, weight loss, or pain that wakes your child at night) that tell us whether further investigation is needed. If none are present, we can often make the diagnosis from the history alone.
- **Run targeted tests when needed.** If the pattern suggests a specific cause, we test for it — a blood test for coeliac disease, a stool test for H. pylori, or blood markers for inflammation. We only test what is clinically needed.
- **Treat reflux.** If your child has reflux, we try dietary and positioning changes first. If those are not enough, a short course of acid-reducing medicine (usually 4–8 weeks) lets the lining heal. We aim to stop the medicine once symptoms settle.
- **Treat H. pylori.** If the stool test confirms this stomach bug, a two-week course of antibiotics clears it. We retest after treatment to confirm it is gone.
- **Screen for coeliac disease.** A simple blood test checks for the antibody. If positive, we arrange confirmation testing and then our dietitian helps your family transition to a gluten-free diet — with practical Indian recipes and alternatives.
- **Manage functional tummy pain.** We address diet, sleep, activity, and stress together. Most children improve significantly within 3–6 months.

## What you can do at home

- **Keep a symptom diary.** Note when the pain happens, what your child ate, bowel habits, and what seemed to help. Bring this to every visit — it helps the doctor spot patterns.
- **Maintain regular meals.** Skipping meals or grazing all day can worsen tummy pain. Three meals and two small snacks, with adequate fibre and water, is a good routine.
- **Reduce gas-producing foods if bloating is a problem.** Beans, cabbage, cauliflower, and carbonated drinks can increase bloating. Adjust gradually.
- **Home remedies can help.** Ajwain (carom seeds), jeera (cumin) water, and hing (asafoetida) are safe for mild bloating and many families find them helpful. Use them alongside — not instead of — the doctor's plan.
- **Manage stress.** Tummy pain in children often gets worse with school stress, exam pressure, or family tension. Regular physical activity, enough sleep, and relaxation time all help.
- **For reflux:** Avoid lying down right after eating. Smaller, more frequent meals are better than large ones. Avoid late-night snacking. For infants, keep upright for 20–30 minutes after feeds.
- **For coeliac disease:** Follow the gluten-free diet strictly. Rice, millets (ragi, jowar, bajra), dal, vegetables, fruits, eggs, meat, and dairy are all naturally gluten-free. Our dietitian will give you specific recipes and a shopping guide.

## When to call us

**Call SKIDS immediately if:**
- There is blood in your child's vomit or stool — especially if your child looks pale, dizzy, or has a fast heartbeat.
- Your child has severe abdominal pain with a rigid, tender tummy that hurts to touch.
- An infant is vomiting green (bile-stained) fluid.
- Your child has severe crampy pain with bloody, jelly-like stool (especially a toddler).

**Call SKIDS soon (within a few days) if:**
- Your child is losing weight or not growing well.
- Diarrhoea has lasted more than two weeks.
- Tummy pain is waking your child from sleep at night.
- Persistent vomiting that is not improving.
- You notice a lump in your child's tummy.

## Common questions

**Q: It's just gas — does she really need tests?**
A: Many families describe tummy pain as "gas" or "acidity." The doctor's assessment looks at the specific pattern of symptoms and checks for warning signs. In most cases, the answer is reassuring — there is no serious disease. But checking is important because a few children have treatable causes like coeliac disease or H. pylori.

**Q: Does my child need a camera test (endoscopy)?**
A: Most children with tummy pain do not need endoscopy. The doctor uses specific criteria to decide when it is truly needed — usually when there are warning signs, when treatment has not worked, or when a specific diagnosis needs confirmation. When it is not needed, doing it unnecessarily exposes your child to sedation without benefit.

**Q: Gluten-free is impossible in an Indian kitchen.**
A: It feels overwhelming at first, but many Indian staples are naturally gluten-free: rice, all millets (ragi, jowar, bajra), all dals, vegetables, fruits, eggs, meat, and dairy. Our dietitian provides Indian-specific recipes and a practical transition plan. Families manage it successfully — the gut heals completely once gluten is removed, and your child will feel much better.

**Q: He's had tummy pain for years — nothing works.**
A: Chronic functional tummy pain is frustrating. The key is that this is not "one medicine" problem — it responds to a combination of diet, activity, sleep, and stress management, addressed together over several months. Most children improve significantly within 3–6 months on a structured programme.

**Q: Can he just take the medicine and not change the diet?**
A: Medicine helps in the short term — it reduces acid or treats the infection. But dietary and lifestyle changes are what keep the symptoms away long-term. Think of it as medicine for the acute problem and lifestyle for the lasting fix. The two work together.
