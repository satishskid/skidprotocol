# Understanding Your Child's Asthma — For Parents

## What is it?

Asthma means your child's airways are more sensitive than usual. When they are irritated by things like colds, dust, pollen, exercise, or cold air, the airways narrow, swell, and produce extra mucus. This causes coughing, wheezing (a whistling sound when breathing), chest tightness, and breathlessness.

Asthma is very common — about 1 in 10 children has it. It runs in families, and children with eczema or allergies are more likely to develop it. The good news is that asthma is very manageable. With the right treatment, most children with asthma live completely normal lives, play sports, and outgrow the worst of it as they grow up.

The key to good asthma care is using two types of medicine: a **preventer** (taken daily to keep the airways calm) and a **reliever** (taken when symptoms flare up). The preventer is the important one — it prevents attacks rather than just treating them.

## What does SKIDS do about it?

Here is how we help your child:

- **Confirm the diagnosis.** We use breathing tests (spirometry for children 6 and older, or clinical assessment for younger children) to confirm whether your child has asthma and how it responds to medicine.
- **Start the right level of treatment.** Based on how often and how severely your child has symptoms, the doctor prescribes the appropriate preventer and reliever.
- **Teach inhaler technique.** The inhaler works much better with a spacer device. We demonstrate the technique and check it at every visit — because technique matters more than dose.
- **Give you a written action plan.** Every child with asthma gets a traffic-light action plan:
  - **Green** (doing well): continue daily preventer.
  - **Yellow** (getting worse): use reliever + increase preventer + contact us.
  - **Red** (emergency): use reliever immediately + go to the emergency room.
- **Identify triggers.** Working with the Allergy Clinic, we identify what triggers your child's asthma (dust mites, pollen, pets, mould) and help you reduce exposure.
- **Monitor with peak flow.** We provide a peak-flow meter so you can track your child's breathing at home and spot early warning signs.
- **Review regularly.** We check control every 3 months. When asthma is well controlled for several months, we gradually step down treatment.

## What you can do at home

- **Give the preventer inhaler every day as prescribed.** Even when your child feels well. The preventer works by keeping the airways calm over time — skipping it allows inflammation to build up.
- **Use the spacer every time.** The spacer gets more medicine into the lungs and less into the mouth. Always use it — for both the preventer and the reliever.
- **Rinse the mouth after the preventer.** A quick rinse (swish and spit) after the preventer inhaler prevents mouth irritation.
- **Keep the reliever accessible.** At home, at school, and during sports. If your child uses the reliever more than twice a week, let us know — it means the asthma is not well controlled.
- **Reduce triggers at home.** Encase pillows and mattresses in dust-proof covers. Avoid smoking anywhere near your child. Keep pets out of the bedroom. Fix damp and mould. Avoid strong incense or cooking fumes in enclosed spaces.
- **Use the peak-flow meter.** Record readings twice daily (morning and evening) for the first two weeks, then as the doctor advises. Bring the diary to every visit.
- **Pre-medicate before exercise if advised.** If your child's asthma flares with exercise, two puffs of the reliever 15 minutes before PE or sports prevents symptoms. Exercise is good for your child — asthma should not stop them.

## When to call us

**Call emergency services or go to the ER immediately if:**
- Your child is struggling to breathe — speaking in single words, using neck or rib muscles to breathe, or unable to complete a sentence.
- Lips or fingernails look blue or grey.
- The reliever inhaler is not helping after 3 doses (given 20 minutes apart).
- Your child is drowsy, confused, or unusually quiet during a breathing episode.
- Peak flow is in the red zone (below 50% of personal best).

**Call SKIDS soon (within a day or two) if:**
- Your child is waking at night because of coughing or wheezing.
- Your child is using the reliever more than twice a week.
- Your child is missing school or avoiding PE because of breathing problems.
- Peak flow is in the yellow zone (50–80% of personal best) and not improving.

## Common questions

**Q: Are inhaler steroids dangerous?**
A: The preventer inhaler contains a very small dose of medicine — measured in micrograms (thousandths of a milligram). At this dose, the medicine stays in the airways and does not affect your child's growth, bones, or immune system. It is completely different from steroid tablets or injections. The risk of not using the preventer — more attacks, more ER visits, more missed school — is much greater.

**Q: Will my child become dependent on the inhaler?**
A: No. Inhalers are not addictive. Many children step down or stop inhalers as they grow and their asthma improves. The preventer is used to keep the airways calm — like brushing teeth prevents cavities. The doctor reviews every 3 months whether your child still needs the current level of treatment.

**Q: Why can't we just use the reliever when needed?**
A: Using only the reliever means you are treating attacks after they happen, instead of preventing them. Children who use only a reliever have more attacks, more ER visits, and more missed school. The preventer keeps the airways calm so attacks don't happen in the first place.

**Q: Is a nebuliser better than an inhaler with spacer?**
A: Actually, an inhaler with spacer delivers medicine more efficiently than a nebuliser, with fewer side effects. A nebuliser is not "stronger" — it just looks more medical. For most children, the inhaler with spacer is the better choice. If your child is very young and cannot use a spacer at all, the doctor may prescribe a nebuliser temporarily.

**Q: Can we try ayurveda / homoeopathy instead?**
A: The doctor respects your choices. We ask that you continue the preventer alongside any complementary approach so your child is protected. The preventer is the safety net — it prevents attacks while you explore other options.

## What happens next

- **New diagnosis:** Your next visit is in 4–6 weeks. At that visit, we will check whether symptoms have improved, re-check spacer technique, and decide whether to adjust treatment.
- **After an asthma attack:** We will call or see you within 48–72 hours to make sure recovery is on track, then again at 2 weeks.
- **Stable asthma:** Follow-ups are every 3 months. Once asthma is well controlled for two consecutive visits, follow-ups move to every 6 months.
- **Stepping down treatment:** When asthma is well controlled for 3 months or more, we gradually reduce the preventer dose. If symptoms return, we step back up immediately.
