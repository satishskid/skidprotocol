# Understanding Your Child's Behaviour — For Parents

## What are behavioural and emotional concerns in children?

Many children go through phases of difficult behaviour, worry, or low mood. For some children, these difficulties are more persistent and begin to affect school performance, friendships, family life, or the child's own happiness. Attention difficulty (sometimes called ADHD) means the child's brain has a harder time sustaining focus, sitting still, or controlling impulses — not because of laziness or poor parenting, but because of how the brain's attention system works. Anxiety means worry that goes beyond normal nervousness and starts to interfere with daily life — avoiding school, avoiding friends, or physical symptoms like stomach aches. Low mood (depression) in teenagers can look like withdrawal, irritability, loss of interest, or fatigue rather than obvious sadness. All of these are well understood, have effective treatments, and respond best when caught early.

## What does SKIDS do about it?

Here is how we help your child:

- **Careful assessment.** We use validated questionnaires completed by parents, teachers, and (for older children) the child themselves. These are not opinion-based — they give us specific, comparable scores.
- **Structured therapy.** Through our LISSUN partner, we offer evidence-based programmes: cognitive-behavioural therapy (CBT) for anxiety and low mood, parent-training programmes for younger children's behaviour, and social-skills support. Sessions can be in-clinic or by video.
- **Parent-training programmes.** For behavioural concerns, much of the work is with the parent — learning specific techniques for routines, instructions, and consequences that reduce conflict and improve the child's self-regulation.
- **School-liaison support.** We write to your child's school with specific, practical recommendations for classroom accommodations — seating position, task-breaking, exam support — based on the assessment findings.
- **Medication when needed.** For moderate-to-severe attention difficulty, we may recommend medication (methylphenidate). This is only started after a thorough assessment including a heart check (ECG). For moderate adolescent low mood, an antidepressant (fluoxetine) may be added alongside therapy. Medication decisions are always discussed with you.
- **Adolescent privacy.** For teenagers, part of the visit is a private conversation. What your teenager shares is confidential unless there is a safety concern (risk of harm to self, risk of harm to others, or abuse). This is explained to both you and your teenager at every visit.

## What you can do at home

- **Keep routines consistent.** Predictable morning, homework, and bedtime routines help children with attention difficulty and anxiety. Visual schedules (pictures or checklists) work well for younger children.
- **Use clear, brief instructions.** Get your child's attention first, give one instruction at a time, and follow through consistently. Praise specific good behaviour rather than general praise.
- **Reduce screen time before bed.** Sleep affects behaviour, mood, and attention. No screens for one hour before bedtime.
- **Engage the whole family.** Share techniques learned in the parent-training programme with grandparents and other caregivers. Consistency across adults is what makes the techniques work.
- **Monitor medication if prescribed.** Give the medicine at the same time each day. Watch for appetite changes, sleep difficulty, or mood changes, and note them in the side-effect log on the parent app. Keep all follow-up appointments.
- **Stay connected with school.** Share the school-liaison letter. Follow up with the teacher to check whether the recommended accommodations are in place.

## When to call us

**Call SKIDS immediately or go to the emergency room if:**
- Your child talks about wanting to die, not wanting to be alive, or harming themselves
- Your child hears or sees things that are not there
- Your child has a sudden, extreme change in behaviour — not sleeping, very agitated, or behaving very unusually
- Your child has harmed themselves (cutting, burning, or similar)
- Your child is in danger or is putting others in danger

**Call the clinic within a few days if:**
- Medication side effects are bothering your child (appetite loss, sleep difficulty, irritability)
- Your child's mood, behaviour, or school performance is getting worse
- Your teenager seems more withdrawn, is skipping school, or has stopped activities they used to enjoy
- You are struggling to implement the techniques from the parent-training programme
- You have concerns about substance use (alcohol, smoking, other substances)

## Common questions

**Q: Does going to this clinic mean my child has a "mental health problem"?**
A: The clinic helps children whose behaviour, attention, mood, or anxiety is affecting their daily life. We describe findings in practical terms — "attention difficulty affecting school," "worry that is stopping her from enjoying activities." Formal diagnostic terms are used later, with your consent, and only when they help access school support or specialist care.

**Q: Will medication change my child's personality?**
A: The medication helps the brain's focus system work more efficiently — like glasses help eyes see. Your child is still the same person. The most common side effects (reduced appetite, sometimes difficulty sleeping) are manageable and monitored closely. We review whether medication is still needed every year.

**Q: Is ADHD caused by bad parenting?**
A: No. Attention difficulty is a brain-based difference in how the attention and impulse-control systems work. Parenting strategies can help manage it, but they do not cause it. The parent-training programme teaches techniques that work specifically with this type of brain.

**Q: What if therapy doesn't work?**
A: If the first approach (therapy alone or parent-training alone) does not improve things within 6-12 weeks, we step up — adding medication if it was not already started, or adjusting the therapy approach. If the concern exceeds what our clinic manages, we refer to a specialist psychiatrist.

**Q: My teenager won't talk to me about what's going on. Can you help?**
A: Yes. Part of what we offer is a private conversation with your teenager. Teenagers are more likely to open up when they know the conversation is confidential (with safety exceptions). This is not about excluding you — it is about creating a space where your teenager feels safe enough to be honest, which is what leads to effective help.

**Q: We are also seeing a traditional healer / doing prayers. Is that a problem?**
A: We respect your family's choices. Prayers and rituals are often an important source of comfort. We ask only that you share with us any specific remedies or preparations your child is taking, as some can interact with medication.

## What happens next

Your next visit depends on your child's plan:
- **Behavioural-first approach (no medication):** We review in 12 weeks with the same questionnaires to see if scores have improved.
- **Medication started:** Weekly check-ins for the first 4 weeks (phone or app), then an in-clinic visit at 4 weeks to review side effects, dose, and response.
- **LISSUN therapy:** Sessions are typically weekly or fortnightly. The therapist sends progress notes to the clinic. You will see them in the parent app.
- **Annual reviews:** For ongoing management, annual visits include a heart tracing (ECG) for children on stimulant medication, and a full re-assessment of whether the current plan is still needed.

Medication trackers, mood logs, LISSUN session calendars, and parent-training tools are available in your SKIDS parent app.
