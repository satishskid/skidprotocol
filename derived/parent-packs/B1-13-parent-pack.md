# The Adolescent Health Visit — For Parents

## What is it?

Adolescence — roughly ages 10 to 18 — is a period of rapid physical, emotional, and social change. The adolescent health visit is a structured check-up designed specifically for teenagers. It covers physical development (puberty, growth, menstrual health), mental well-being (mood, stress, school, friendships), safety (online behaviour, road safety, substance awareness), and age-appropriate health education.

A key feature of the adolescent visit is that part of it is conducted privately with your teenager. This is standard practice in adolescent medicine worldwide — not because we expect problems, but because teenagers are more likely to share health concerns honestly when they have a private space to do so. Everything your teenager shares stays private unless they say they want to harm themselves, harm someone else, or are being hurt by someone. In those cases, you will always be informed.

## What does SKIDS do about it?

Here is how we support your teenager:

- **Full health check.** We measure growth, check blood pressure, assess physical development, and screen for scoliosis and other age-specific concerns.
- **Confidential conversation.** In a private part of the visit, the doctor asks your teenager about school, friends, mood, sleep, diet, safety, and any topics they want to discuss. This is the same conversation we have with every teenager.
- **Menstrual health.** For girls, we assess period patterns and manage painful, heavy, or irregular periods. We help with practical solutions.
- **Vaccinations.** We check that your teenager is up to date, including the HPV vaccine (which protects against certain cancers) and boosters.
- **Sports clearance.** If your teenager needs a fitness certificate for school or sports, we perform the required evaluation.
- **Mental-health screening.** We use validated questionnaires to check for mood and anxiety concerns. If needed, we connect your teenager with a trained counsellor or therapist.
- **Substance awareness.** We screen for alcohol, tobacco, and other substance use using a standard tool. If concerns arise, we provide brief counselling and, if needed, specialist support.

## What you can do at home

- **Keep the lines of communication open.** Teenagers need privacy and independence, but they also need to know you are available. Let them know you are willing to listen without immediately reacting.
- **Support healthy routines.** Regular sleep (8–10 hours), daily physical activity, balanced meals, and limited screen time form the foundation of adolescent health.
- **Talk about safety.** Online safety, road safety, and helmet use are practical conversations that save lives. Keep them matter-of-fact and regular.
- **Respect growing independence.** Your teenager is developing their own identity. Supporting their autonomy — while maintaining appropriate boundaries — is one of the most important things you can do.
- **Watch for warning signs.** Withdrawal from friends, dropping grades, changes in sleep or appetite, persistent sadness, or loss of interest in activities they used to enjoy — these are signs to talk to the doctor.

## When to call us

Call SKIDS immediately if:
- Your teenager says they want to hurt themselves or end their life — this is an emergency. Stay with them and call us right away.
- Your teenager has been assaulted or is in danger.
- Your teenager has consumed a large amount of alcohol or drugs and is unresponsive, vomiting, or confused.

Call SKIDS soon (within a few days) if:
- Your teenager's mood has been persistently low for more than two weeks.
- Your teenager is refusing to attend school.
- Periods are causing severe pain, very heavy bleeding, or have not started by age 15.
- You are concerned about an eating pattern (restricting food, binge eating, excessive exercise about weight).

## Common questions

**Q: Why does part of the visit happen without me?**
A: This is standard practice for every teenager at SKIDS — not specific to your child. Teenagers are more likely to talk about health concerns when they have privacy. The doctor shares all general medical information with you. Confidential information is protected unless there is a safety concern — then you are always informed.

**Q: My child doesn't need a mental-health check.**
A: The psychosocial conversation is part of every adolescent visit, just like measuring height or checking blood pressure. It covers school, friendships, sleep, diet, and safety. It is not a sign that we suspect a problem — it is a routine part of caring for a teenager.

**Q: Is the HPV vaccine safe and necessary?**
A: Yes. The HPV vaccine prevents certain cancers and is recommended for boys and girls, ideally before age 15. It has been given to millions of adolescents worldwide with an excellent safety record. We recommend it as part of the standard adolescent vaccine schedule.

**Q: What if my teenager tells the doctor something I should know?**
A: If your teenager shares something that indicates a safety risk — self-harm, being harmed by someone, or a medical emergency — the doctor will involve you. Otherwise, the confidential information stays between the doctor and your teenager. This trust is what makes teenagers willing to seek help when they need it.

**Q: My teenager is moody and withdrawn — is that just normal?**
A: Some mood variability is normal in adolescence. But persistent sadness, loss of interest, sleep changes, or withdrawal from friends for more than two weeks is worth checking. The screening tools we use help us tell the difference between normal teenage ups and downs and something that needs attention.

## What happens next

- **Routine annual visit:** Your next visit is in 1 year. At that visit, we will repeat the full health check and psychosocial conversation.
- **Period concerns on treatment:** We review in 3 months to check if the treatment is helping.
- **Mood or anxiety support:** Follow-up is usually within 2–4 weeks, coordinated with the Behavioural Clinic if needed.
- **Sports clearance:** If all findings are clear, the certificate is issued the same day.
