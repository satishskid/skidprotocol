# Understanding Your Child's Sleep — For Parents

## What is a sleep problem?

Sleep problems in children are very common and usually treatable. In babies and toddlers, the most frequent issue is that the child has learned to fall asleep only with a specific help — feeding, rocking, or a parent lying next to them — and then needs that same help every time they briefly wake during the night (which all children do). In older children, difficulty falling asleep may be related to screen use, worry, or an irregular schedule. In teenagers, the body clock often shifts later, making it hard to fall asleep before midnight and hard to wake for school. Some children snore loudly, which can be a sign that their airway is partially blocked during sleep — this can affect their growth, learning, and daytime energy. Most sleep problems improve significantly with the right changes.

## What does SKIDS do about it?

Here is how we help your child:

- **Assess the sleep pattern.** We ask detailed questions about bedtime, wake time, night-waking, snoring, daytime sleepiness, and screen habits. We may ask you to keep a sleep diary for two weeks.
- **Teach sleep foundations.** Consistent bedtime, calming bedtime routine, dark and quiet room, no screens before bed, and age-appropriate sleep duration targets.
- **Deliver a structured behavioural programme.** For babies and toddlers with frequent night-waking, we offer a 4-6 week programme. You choose from three evidence-based approaches — including options where you stay in the room. Our counsellor guides you through it weekly.
- **Screen for breathing problems during sleep.** If your child snores, we use a questionnaire and a simple overnight finger-clip oxygen sensor (loaned to you for two nights at home) to check whether oxygen levels are dropping. If they are, we refer to an ENT specialist.
- **Help teenagers reset their body clock.** Morning sunlight, evening screen reduction, and sometimes a short course of low-dose melatonin to shift the sleep-onset time earlier.
- **Check iron levels for restless legs.** If your child has uncomfortable leg sensations at bedtime, we check iron stores and supplement if low.

## What you can do at home

- **Keep a consistent bedtime and wake time.** Aim for the same times on weekdays and weekends (within one hour). This anchors the body clock.
- **Create a bedtime routine.** A predictable sequence of 20-45 minutes — bath, pyjamas, teeth, story, prayer or song, lights out. The routine signals the brain that sleep is coming.
- **Remove screens from the bedroom.** No phone, tablet, or TV in the bedroom. No screens for at least one hour before bedtime. Use warm-tone screen settings in the evening.
- **Keep the room dark, quiet, and cool.** Use blackout curtains if needed. A gentle night-light is fine for children who are afraid of the dark.
- **Limit caffeine.** No tea, coffee, cola, or energy drinks after midday for school-age children. No caffeine at all for young children.
- **Encourage daytime activity.** At least one hour of active play or outdoor time daily. This builds healthy sleep pressure.
- **Expose your child to morning daylight.** Thirty minutes of natural light within the first hour of waking helps set the body clock.

## When to call us

**Call SKIDS immediately if:**
- Your child stops breathing during sleep and turns blue or grey
- Your child has an episode during sleep that looks like a seizure (stiffening, jerking, unresponsive)
- Your child cannot be woken after a sleep episode

**Call the clinic within a few days if:**
- Snoring is getting louder or more frequent, especially with gasping or pausing
- Your child is excessively sleepy during the day despite sleeping enough hours
- Sleep problems are causing significant school difficulties, mood changes, or family stress
- The behavioural programme is not showing improvement after 2 weeks of consistent effort
- Your teenager's sleep pattern is worsening despite the recommended changes

## Common questions

**Q: Is sleep training harmful to my baby?**
A: Research consistently shows that evidence-based sleep programmes do not harm children's emotional development or attachment. All methods involve some short-term adjustment. The programme we use is guided by a trained counsellor, and you choose the approach that feels right for your family.

**Q: We co-sleep with our child. Is that a problem?**
A: Co-sleeping is a family choice and is very common. We work within whatever sleeping arrangement you prefer. If your baby is under 12 months, we will discuss safe-sleep practices within a shared bed. The programme can improve sleep quality without changing where your child sleeps.

**Q: My teenager says they "can't" fall asleep early. Are they just being difficult?**
A: In teenagers, the body clock genuinely shifts later — it is biological, not laziness. The brain produces melatonin (the sleep signal) later in the evening. Our programme uses morning light, evening routine changes, and sometimes a short course of melatonin to gradually shift the clock back to a manageable time.

**Q: Is melatonin a sleeping pill? Will my child become dependent?**
A: Melatonin is not a sleeping pill — it is a signal that tells the body clock "it is time to sleep." It does not force sleep. We use it at very low doses, for a limited time (usually 4-12 weeks), and only after behavioural approaches have been tried. It is not addictive. We use only pharmaceutical-grade preparations.

**Q: Should I be worried about the snoring?**
A: Occasional light snoring is common and harmless. Regular loud snoring — especially with pauses, gasping, mouth-breathing, or daytime sleepiness — may mean the airway is partially blocked (usually by enlarged tonsils or adenoids). The overnight oxygen recording tells us if this is affecting your child. If it is, an ENT specialist will assess whether the tonsils/adenoids need to be removed.

## What happens next

Your next visit depends on your child's situation:
- **Behavioural programme:** Weekly sessions for 4-6 weeks. Most families see real change within 2 weeks. Daily check-ins via the parent app help you stay on track.
- **Snoring investigation:** After the overnight oxygen recording (two nights at home), bring back the device and we review the results. If oxygen levels are normal, we continue to monitor. If they are low, we refer to the ENT specialist.
- **Teenager sleep-phase programme:** We review at 6 weeks. If the schedule has shifted, we continue; if not, we adjust the plan.
- **Melatonin trial:** We review at 4 weeks. If it is working, we continue for another 4-8 weeks and then taper. If not, we stop and reassess.

Sleep diary tools, bedtime routine guides, and programme check-ins are available in your SKIDS parent app.
