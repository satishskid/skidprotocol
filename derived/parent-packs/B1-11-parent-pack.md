# Understanding Speech & Language Delay — For Parents

## What is it?

Some children take longer than expected to start talking, to be understood clearly, or to understand what others say. This is called a speech or language delay. It is one of the most common concerns in early childhood — and one of the most treatable.

Language develops in a predictable pattern: babbling around 6 months, first words around 12 months, two-word phrases around 2 years, and sentences around 3 years. When a child falls behind this pattern, an assessment tells us whether to wait or act. The most important first step is checking hearing, because hearing is the foundation for speech. Once we know the child hears well, a speech therapist can begin targeted work.

## What does SKIDS do about it?

Here is how we help your child:

- **Check hearing first.** Before we start any speech work, we make sure your child hears clearly. If hearing is the issue, that is treated first.
- **Assess where your child is.** We use a structured assessment to measure how much your child understands (receptive language) and how much they express (expressive language), and we check their speech sounds and clarity.
- **Start therapy if needed.** A speech-language pathologist (SLP) works with your child one or two times a week, using play-based activities to build vocabulary, sentence skills, clarity, or fluency.
- **Coach you.** What you do at home every day matters more than what happens in the therapy room. We teach you specific techniques — narrating, expanding, reading, and reducing screen time — so your child practises language all day, not just during sessions.

## What you can do at home

- **Talk to your child constantly.** Narrate what you are doing: "I'm washing the red cup. Now I'm putting it on the shelf." This gives your child a stream of language to absorb.
- **Follow your child's lead.** If your child points at a dog, say "Dog! Big brown dog!" rather than changing the topic. Build on what interests them.
- **Read together every day.** Even five minutes of shared reading builds vocabulary faster than any other single activity.
- **Reduce screen time.** Screens do not teach language — they replace the back-and-forth conversation that builds it. Aim for less than one hour a day for children over 2, and none for children under 18 months.
- **Expand what your child says.** If your child says "car," you say "Yes! A fast red car." If they say "want milk," you say "You want more milk? Here it is."
- **Don't correct — model.** If your child says "goggy" for "doggy," don't say "No, say doggy." Instead, say "Yes, that's a doggy!" They learn from hearing the right version, not from being corrected.

## When to call us

Call SKIDS immediately if:
- Your child was talking and has stopped — loss of words at any age needs urgent assessment.
- Your child does not point, gesture, or make eye contact — this may indicate a broader developmental concern.

Call SKIDS soon (within a week) if:
- Your child is 18 months old and not saying any words.
- Your child is 24 months old and not putting two words together.
- You cannot understand most of what your 4-year-old says.
- Your child's stuttering is getting worse, lasting more than 6 months, or causing visible frustration (eye blinking, head movements, avoiding speaking).

## Common questions

**Q: Boys talk late — is this normal?**
A: It is true that mild delays are slightly more common in boys. But "common" does not mean "ignore it." The assessment tells us whether your son is a late bloomer who will catch up, or whether he needs help now. If he needs help, the earlier we start, the better the outcome.

**Q: We speak two languages at home. Is that confusing him?**
A: No. Children who grow up with two languages reach the same milestones as children with one language. They may have a brief quiet period as they sort out the two languages, but this is normal. We assess in both languages. Never drop a home language — it is part of your child's identity and thinking.

**Q: Does my child need a doctor for talking?**
A: The speech therapist is more like a specialised teacher than a doctor. She uses play, stories, and activities to teach your child how to use language — and she teaches you how to support that at home. Think of it as tutoring for language.

**Q: Is screen time causing the delay?**
A: Excessive screen time is linked to slower language development in toddlers, because it replaces the back-and-forth conversation that builds language. Reducing screen time and increasing face-to-face talking time helps. We help you make this change without guilt.

## What happens next

- **If your child is a late talker (understanding is fine, just not talking much yet):** We see you again in 3 months to check progress. Most late talkers catch up by age 3.
- **If your child needs speech therapy:** Sessions happen weekly or twice weekly. You will also have monthly parent-coaching sessions so you can practise techniques at home. The doctor reviews progress every 3 months.
- **If your child stutters:** We start a structured programme and check in at 2 weeks, then monthly for 6 months.
