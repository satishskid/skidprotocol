# Understanding Your Child's Brain and Nerve Health — For Parents

## What are neurological conditions in children?

The brain and nerves control everything your child does — moving, thinking, speaking, feeling. When something affects how the brain or nerves work, it can show up as seizures (epilepsy), headaches (migraine), difficulty with movement (cerebral palsy), muscle weakness, or involuntary movements like tics. These conditions are well understood and, in most cases, very treatable. Specialist care available at select SKIDS clinics means your child is looked after by a children's neurologist with specific training in these conditions.

---

## What does SKIDS do about it?

Here is how we help your child:

- **Epilepsy.** Epilepsy means the brain has a tendency to produce seizures. It is one of the most common brain conditions in children — about 1 in 100 children has it. We start medicine at a low dose and increase it gradually. Most children with epilepsy become seizure-free on medicine and live completely normal lives. The medicine course is usually two years seizure-free before we consider stopping.
- **Migraine.** Migraine in children is common and is not "just a headache." It causes intense pain, often with nausea and sensitivity to light and sound. We treat the attacks when they happen and, if they happen often, prevent them with a daily medicine. We also help identify triggers (sleep, stress, certain foods).
- **Cerebral palsy.** This means the part of the brain that controls movement developed differently. It is not a disease that gets worse over time. We manage it with physiotherapy, specialist equipment (braces, standing frames), and careful monitoring of hips, spine, and nutrition. Many children with cerebral palsy lead full, active lives.
- **Neuromuscular conditions.** Conditions like muscular dystrophy affect the muscles. We monitor strength, breathing, and heart health, and coordinate with physiotherapy and other specialists.
- **Movement disorders and tics.** Tics (blinking, throat clearing, sudden movements) are common in children and often improve over time. We monitor, provide behavioural strategies, and use medicine only when tics significantly interfere with daily life.
- **Febrile seizures.** These are seizures triggered by fever — very common (about 1 in 25 children) and not harmful to the brain. They do not mean your child has epilepsy. We counsel you on what to do if it happens again and monitor to make sure no further issues develop.
- **Investigation of lost skills.** If your child has lost skills they previously had, we investigate urgently with brain scans, blood tests, and electrical brain tests (EEG) to find the cause and start treatment.

## What you can do at home

- **Keep a seizure diary.** Log every seizure in the SKIDS parent app: when it happened, what it looked like, how long it lasted, and what happened afterward. You can upload a video — this is extremely helpful for the doctor. If your child has migraines, keep a headache diary (timing, triggers, pain level).
- **Give medicine at the same time every day.** Do not skip doses. Do not stop the medicine suddenly — this is dangerous and can cause a prolonged seizure. If you are running low, contact the clinic before you run out.
- **Know seizure first aid.** Lay your child on their side. Do not put anything in their mouth. Time the seizure. If it lasts more than 5 minutes, call an ambulance.
- **Encourage normal activity.** Once seizures are controlled, your child can participate in all activities except unsupervised swimming and unprotected heights. Do not over-restrict — children with epilepsy can play sports, go on school trips, and enjoy their childhood.
- **Do the physiotherapy.** For children with cerebral palsy or muscle conditions, daily physiotherapy exercises at home are the single most important thing you can do. Short daily sessions are better than long occasional ones.
- **Manage migraine triggers.** Regular sleep, regular meals, adequate water, reduced screen time, and stress management all help reduce migraine frequency.

## When to call us

**Call an ambulance immediately if:**
- A seizure lasts more than 5 minutes
- Your child has a seizure and does not wake up or respond afterward
- Your child has a seizure for the first time
- Your child has a sudden, severe headache ("the worst headache ever") with vomiting, neck stiffness, or confusion
- Your child suddenly cannot move one side of their body, or their speech becomes slurred

**Call SKIDS immediately if:**
- Your child has a seizure type they have not had before
- Seizures are happening more often than usual
- Your child is vomiting and cannot keep their medicine down
- Your child has run out of anti-seizure medicine
- Your child on medicine for an overactive thyroid develops a high fever with sore throat (if on related medicines)

**Call the clinic within a few days if:**
- Medicine side effects are bothering your child (tiredness, dizziness, weight changes, mood changes)
- Your child's headaches are getting worse or more frequent
- Physiotherapy sessions are being missed
- You have questions about school participation, sports, or travel

## Common questions

**Q: Does epilepsy mean my child's brain is damaged?**
A: No. Epilepsy is a tendency of the brain to produce seizures. It does not mean brain damage, and it is not caused by anything the family did. It is a medical condition, treatable and common. Most children with epilepsy are intellectually normal and do well in school.

**Q: Will my child have epilepsy forever?**
A: Many children outgrow epilepsy. After two years seizure-free on medicine, we consider slowly reducing and stopping the medicine. About 70% of children remain seizure-free after stopping. Even if seizures return, restarting the medicine almost always works again.

**Q: Is epilepsy contagious?**
A: No. Epilepsy is not contagious. It is not caused by spirits, past-life events, or anything external. It is a brain condition, like asthma is a lung condition.

**Q: Can my child play sports?**
A: Yes, once seizures are controlled. The only restrictions are unsupervised swimming and unprotected heights. Your child can run, play cricket, ride a bicycle (with a helmet), and go on school trips. Over-restriction does more harm than good.

**Q: My child had a febrile seizure. Does that mean they have epilepsy?**
A: In most cases, no. Febrile seizures are very common and are triggered by fever, not by an underlying brain condition. About 1 in 3 children who have one febrile seizure will have another during their next fever, but fewer than 5% develop epilepsy. We do not treat febrile seizures with daily medicine.

**Q: We are also seeing a traditional healer. Is that a problem?**
A: We respect your choices. The one thing we ask is that your child continues the prescribed medicine. Stopping anti-seizure medicine suddenly is dangerous — it can cause a prolonged seizure. If you want to explore other therapies alongside, that is fine, as long as the medicine continues.

**Q: The EEG and MRI sound scary. Are they painful?**
A: No. The EEG involves sensors placed on the scalp — no needles, no pain. The child sits or lies down for about 30 minutes. The MRI is a scan that uses magnets, not radiation — it is noisy but painless. For younger children who need to stay still, we may use mild sedation, which is very safe.

## What happens next

Your next visit depends on your child's condition:
- **Epilepsy (new, dose adjustment):** Every 4 weeks until seizures are controlled and the dose is stable.
- **Epilepsy (stable):** Every 3 months for medicine review, seizure-diary check, and blood tests.
- **Migraine (on preventive medicine):** Every 6-8 weeks during dose adjustment, then every 3 months.
- **Cerebral palsy:** Every 6 months for progress review, physiotherapy plan update, and equipment check. Annual hip X-ray for children with more limited mobility.
- **Febrile seizure:** Follow-up at 3 months, 6 months, then annually until age 6.
- **Muscle conditions:** Every 3-4 months for strength testing, breathing checks, and heart monitoring.

Your SKIDS parent app will show seizure and headache diaries, medicine trackers, physiotherapy session logs, investigation timelines, and seizure first-aid guidance.
