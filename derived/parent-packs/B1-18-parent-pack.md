# Understanding Your Child's Blood Health — For Parents

## What are these conditions?

**Anaemia (when iron medicine didn't work):** If your child's blood count did not improve after iron medicine, it means the anaemia is not caused by iron deficiency alone. There are several types of anaemia, and each has a specific cause and treatment. We run detailed blood tests to find the exact type so we can treat it correctly.

**Thalassemia trait:** This is a carrier state — not a disease. Your child is healthy and will remain healthy. About half the population in some regions of India carries this trait. The reason we identify it is for future family planning: if both partners carry the trait, there is a 25% chance their children could have thalassemia major, which is a serious condition requiring regular blood transfusions. Knowing this now is empowering information, not a problem.

**ITP (low platelets):** Your child's body is temporarily making antibodies against its own platelets — the cells that help blood clot. This is very common after viral infections in children. It causes bruises and small red dots on the skin. The good news is that in most children, the platelet count recovers on its own within a few weeks to months. We monitor with weekly blood tests and only treat with medicine if the count drops very low or there is active bleeding.

**G6PD deficiency:** This is an inherited condition where a specific enzyme in the red blood cells is low. Most of the time, your child is completely well. Problems only arise if your child is exposed to certain foods, medicines, or chemicals that can trigger the red blood cells to break down. We give you a list of things to avoid, and with that simple precaution, your child leads a normal life.

## What does SKIDS do about it?

Here is how we help your child:

- **Find the exact type of anaemia.** We run a detailed blood test that looks at the shape, size, and type of blood cells, and checks for specific conditions like thalassemia trait or vitamin deficiencies.
- **Provide genetic counselling for thalassemia trait.** We explain what carrier status means, advise on partner testing for future family planning, and provide written information for family records.
- **Monitor low platelets (ITP).** We check the platelet count weekly to track recovery. Most children recover without any medicine. If the count drops very low or there is active bleeding, we start treatment in consultation with a blood specialist.
- **Screen for bleeding disorders.** For children who bruise easily or bleed a lot from minor injuries, we run tests to check the clotting system and identify conditions like von Willebrand disease.
- **Issue a G6PD avoidance card.** We provide a card listing foods and medicines to avoid, plus a letter for school so teachers are aware.
- **Evaluate swollen glands.** For lumps that persist for more than 4 weeks, we run blood tests to check the cells and, if needed, refer to a specialist for further evaluation.

## What you can do at home

- **For thalassemia trait:** No medicine or dietary change is needed. Do not take iron supplements unless the doctor has confirmed your child is also iron-deficient. Keep the counselling summary for future reference when your child is older.
- **For G6PD deficiency:** Avoid naphthalene mothballs (very common in Indian households for clothing storage — switch to neem leaves or camphor alternatives). Avoid fava beans. Always tell any doctor, dentist, or pharmacist about the G6PD status before any medicine is prescribed. Carry the G6PD card in your wallet.
- **For ITP:** Avoid contact sports (football, kabaddi, wrestling) until the platelet count is above 50,000. Watch for new bruises, bleeding from gums or nose, or blood in urine or stool, and report them to the clinic immediately.
- **For anaemia under treatment:** Give the prescribed medicine as directed. Come for the follow-up blood test so we can confirm the treatment is working.
- **Keep follow-up appointments.** For ITP especially, weekly blood tests are important to track whether the platelet count is recovering.

## When to call us

**Call SKIDS immediately or go to the emergency room if:**
- Your child becomes suddenly very pale, weak, or short of breath
- Your child develops yellow skin or eyes with dark-coloured urine (possible blood-cell breakdown — especially important for G6PD families)
- There is bleeding that does not stop — from the nose, gums, or a wound
- You see blood in the urine or stool
- Your child has a high fever with a rash that does not fade when pressed (petechiae)

**Call the clinic within a few days if:**
- New bruises or small red dots appear on the skin
- Nosebleeds are happening more often
- Your child seems more tired or pale than usual
- You notice swelling of glands (lumps in the neck, armpit, or groin) that are growing or not going away
- Your teenager with heavy periods is feeling dizzy or fatigued

## Common questions

**Q: Thalassemia trait — does this mean my child cannot marry normally?**
A: Your child can absolutely marry and have children. The trait simply means that when choosing a partner, it is helpful to test the partner too. If both carry the trait, genetic counselling helps the couple plan. Half the population in some Indian regions carries this trait — it is common and it is not a disease.

**Q: The red dots on her skin — is it cancer?**
A: In most children, small red dots (petechiae) and bruising are caused by ITP — a temporary immune response where the body's own antibodies reduce the platelet count. This is very common after viral infections and usually resolves on its own. The blood test tells us clearly whether the blood cells are normal. Cancer is rare and looks very different on the blood test.

**Q: G6PD — can my child eat everything?**
A: Yes, with a few specific exceptions. Fava beans should be avoided. Naphthalene mothballs should be removed from the home. Certain medicines (like primaquine for malaria) are not safe. We give you a complete list. Day-to-day Indian food is absolutely fine.

**Q: Why can't you just treat ITP with medicine instead of watching?**
A: In most children with ITP, the platelet count recovers on its own within weeks to months. Giving medicine when it is not needed exposes your child to side effects without any added benefit. We treat only when the count drops very low or there is active bleeding. Watching and monitoring is itself the treatment — and the most effective one for typical ITP.

**Q: My child's iron medicine did not work. Does that mean the anaemia is serious?**
A: Not necessarily. There are many types of anaemia. Some are caused by vitamin B12 or folate deficiency (easily treated), some by thalassemia trait (does not need treatment), and some by other causes. The detailed blood test tells us exactly which type it is. Once we know the cause, we can give the right treatment.

## What happens next

Your next visit depends on your child's condition:
- **ITP (observation):** Weekly blood tests for 4 weeks to track the platelet count. If recovering, monthly for 3 months, then we discharge your child from follow-up.
- **Thalassemia trait:** A follow-up visit at 3 months to reinforce the counselling, then annual blood tests and a reminder about partner testing when your child reaches adolescence.
- **G6PD deficiency:** A follow-up visit at 3 months to review the avoidance list, then annual check-ins with education updates at school entry and adolescence.
- **Anaemia (treatment started):** Recheck blood count at 4 weeks to confirm improvement.
- **Swollen gland under observation:** Recheck at 4 weeks. If resolved, no further follow-up needed.

Your child's blood test results, platelet trend graph, G6PD avoidance card, and thalassemia counselling summary are available in your SKIDS parent app.
