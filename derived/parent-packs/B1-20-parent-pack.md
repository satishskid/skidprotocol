# Understanding Bedwetting — For Parents

## What is bedwetting?

Bedwetting (nocturnal enuresis) is very common in children. About 1 in 10 children at age 7 still wets the bed at night, and 1-2 in 100 teenagers still do. It is not your child's fault, not laziness, and not a behaviour problem. Bedwetting happens because your child's bladder and brain wake-up signal are still maturing. The bladder fills at night, but the brain does not yet send a strong enough signal to wake the child up. There is also a strong family connection — if one parent had bedwetting as a child, there is about a 44% chance their child will too; if both parents did, it is 77%. Most children outgrow it on their own at a rate of about 15% per year, but treatment can speed that up significantly and prevent years of embarrassment and social difficulty.

## What does SKIDS do about it?

Here is how we help your child:

- **Understand the type of bedwetting.** We ask detailed questions to determine whether this is simple nighttime wetting or whether there are other factors like daytime accidents, constipation, or a medical cause. Constipation is a surprisingly common contributor — a full bowel presses on the bladder — and treating it often fixes the bedwetting.
- **Rule out medical causes.** A simple urine test checks for infection, sugar (diabetes), and other problems. In most cases, the test is completely normal.
- **Start the bedwetting alarm programme.** This is the most effective treatment. We loan your family a bedwetting alarm — a small device with a moisture sensor. When wetting starts, the alarm sounds, and over time, your child's brain learns to wake up when the bladder is full. The programme runs for 12-16 weeks. We train you on exactly how to use it and check in every month.
- **Provide medicine for specific situations.** For sleepovers, camps, or travel, a single dose of desmopressin (a medicine that reduces urine production overnight) can keep your child dry for one night. If the alarm does not work after a full trial, desmopressin can also be used as a daily medicine for a few months.
- **Address the emotional impact.** Bedwetting can affect your child's confidence and social life. We screen for this and, if needed, connect you with our Behavioural Clinic for support.

## What you can do at home

- **Never punish your child for wetting the bed.** Punishment makes bedwetting worse, not better. Your child cannot control it. Reassure them that it is common and that they will grow out of it.
- **Use a sticker chart for dry nights.** Reward dry nights positively. A simple chart on the wall where your child places a sticker for each dry night is motivating. Do not penalise wet nights — just leave that day blank.
- **Manage fluids sensibly.** Make sure your child drinks plenty during the day (this actually helps train the bladder). Avoid large amounts of fluid in the 2 hours before bed. Avoid caffeine (cola, tea, chocolate drinks) in the evening.
- **Use the alarm correctly.** When the alarm sounds, your child should wake up, stop the stream, go to the toilet, change clothes and bedding (with help as needed), and reset the alarm. In the first few weeks, you will likely need to wake your child — the alarm may not wake them at first. This is normal. Persistence is the key.
- **Protect the mattress.** Use a waterproof mattress cover. Keep spare sheets and pyjamas beside the bed for easy night-time changes.
- **Address constipation.** If your child has hard or infrequent stools, tell the doctor. Treating constipation often improves or resolves bedwetting.
- **If using desmopressin:** Your child must not drink large amounts of fluid from 1 hour before the medicine until 8 hours after. This is important to prevent a rare but serious side effect.

## When to call us

**Call SKIDS immediately if:**
- Your child is on desmopressin and develops a headache, confusion, or a seizure (very rare — related to fluid balance; stop the medicine and go to the emergency room)

**Call the clinic within a few days if:**
- Your child starts drinking and urinating much more than usual (not related to hot weather or exercise)
- You notice blood in the urine or pain when urinating
- Your child who was previously dry for 6 months or more starts wetting again, especially if something stressful has happened
- The alarm programme is not going well and you need help troubleshooting
- Your child is being bullied or is very upset about the bedwetting

## Common questions

**Q: He'll grow out of it — why treat?**
A: You are right that most children do outgrow bedwetting eventually. But at 15% per year, a 7-year-old who wets every night has years of wet beds ahead without treatment. The alarm programme can make your child dry in 3-4 months. That is years of confidence, sleepovers, and school trips your child does not have to miss.

**Q: Is it because he is a deep sleeper?**
A: Deep sleep is part of the picture. The bedwetting alarm works precisely on this — it trains the brain to respond to a full bladder during sleep. Over 12-16 weeks, most children start waking up before the alarm even goes off.

**Q: Should I wake him at night to go to the toilet?**
A: Waking your child at your bedtime can reduce wet nights, but it does not teach the child to wake on their own. Think of it as a bridge — it helps while you are starting the alarm programme, but the alarm is what does the real training.

**Q: My child shares a bed with siblings. How do I use the alarm?**
A: We have body-worn sensors that clip to the underwear instead of a pad on the bed. These are less likely to disturb siblings. We can also discuss sleeping arrangements to give the alarm programme the best chance of working.

**Q: Can I just use the medicine instead of the alarm?**
A: The medicine (desmopressin) works well while your child takes it, but bedwetting often returns when the medicine is stopped. The alarm has a higher long-term cure rate because it trains the brain. We recommend trying the alarm first. If it does not work, the medicine is the next step.

**Q: Is the bedwetting because of something emotional?**
A: Primary bedwetting (your child has never been consistently dry) is almost always a developmental and genetic matter, not emotional. However, if a child who was dry starts wetting again (secondary bedwetting), stress can sometimes be a trigger. We assess this and offer support if needed.

## What happens next

Your next visit depends on the plan:
- **Education and support (mild bedwetting):** We see you in 4 weeks to review the wet/dry diary.
- **Alarm programme:** Monthly visits for 4 months (12-16 weeks). We check the alarm technique, review the diary, troubleshoot, and celebrate progress. Success is 14 consecutive dry nights.
- **Desmopressin (medicine):** A check at 2 weeks to assess the dose, then monthly for 3 months, then a structured withdrawal.

Your child's wet/dry diary, alarm programme progress, and sticker chart are all available in the SKIDS parent app.
