# Understanding Your Child's Weight — For Parents

## What does "overweight" or "obese" mean for a child?

Doctors use a growth chart that compares your child's weight and height to thousands of other children of the same age and sex. If your child's weight is above the healthy range for their height — specifically above the 85th percentile — they are classified as "overweight." Above the 95th percentile is "obese." These are medical terms, not judgements. They tell us that the body is carrying more weight than is healthy for that height and age, and that early metabolic changes (in blood sugar, cholesterol, liver, or blood pressure) may already be starting. The good news is that in children, these changes are almost always reversible with lifestyle changes. For growing children, the goal is usually not weight loss — it is slowing weight gain so that height catches up, and the body composition improves naturally.

## What does SKIDS do about it?

Here is how we help your child:

- **Confirm and classify.** We plot your child's weight, height, and body mass index on the growth chart, measure waist circumference, and check for physical signs like dark skin patches on the neck (a sign the body is working harder to manage blood sugar).
- **Run metabolic screening.** A simple blood test checks blood sugar, cholesterol, and liver health. We also measure blood pressure at every visit.
- **Deliver the Healthy Weight Programme.** A 12-week structured programme for the whole family. Our dietitian builds a food plan that works for your kitchen and your culture — not a restrictive diet, but practical changes: reducing sugary drinks, cutting down on packaged snacks, increasing vegetables and lentils, and right-sizing portions. We also set physical activity goals and reduce screen time.
- **Use motivational support.** Change is hard. We use a structured approach to help you identify barriers, set realistic goals, and build confidence. The programme works with the family, not just the child.
- **Monitor metabolic markers.** If blood sugar is in the pre-diabetes range, we monitor it every 3 months with lifestyle intervention. If cholesterol or liver markers are elevated, we give lifestyle a 6-month trial before considering specialist referral.
- **Support body image.** We frame weight management in terms of health and energy, not appearance. We never use shame-based language. For teenagers, we offer private dietary counselling if they prefer.

## What you can do at home

- **Eliminate sugary drinks.** This is the single most impactful change. Replace cola, packaged juice, flavoured milk, and energy drinks with water, buttermilk, or plain lassi. One sugary drink per day adds significant empty calories over a year.
- **Reduce ultra-processed food.** Biscuits, chips, instant noodles, bakery items, and packaged snacks are calorie-dense and nutrient-poor. Aim for no more than one serving per day.
- **Increase vegetables and lentils.** At least 5 servings of fruit and vegetables per day. Dal, rajma, chana — these are protein-rich and filling.
- **Right-size portions.** Use smaller plates. Serve one helping; offer seconds only if the child is genuinely still hungry. The dietitian will show you age-appropriate portion sizes.
- **Make it a family change.** Everyone eats the same way. Do not single out the child with different food while others eat differently. Shared meals without screens help everyone eat more mindfully.
- **Get moving.** At least 60 minutes of active play, sport, or exercise daily. If outdoor activity is limited (traffic, pollution), try indoor alternatives: dancing, skipping, stair climbing, home exercises.
- **Reduce screen time.** No more than 2 hours of entertainment screen time per day. No screens during meals.
- **Prioritise sleep.** Children who sleep less tend to gain more weight. Follow age-appropriate sleep targets.

## When to call us

**Call SKIDS immediately or go to the emergency room if:**
- Your child is urinating very frequently, drinking excessively, and losing weight rapidly (signs of diabetes)
- Your child has severe headache with vision changes (possible high blood pressure emergency)
- Your child has sudden hip or knee pain with a limp (possible bone problem related to weight)
- Your child faints or feels very unwell

**Call the clinic within a few days if:**
- Your child is struggling with the dietary changes and you need support
- You are having difficulty getting your child to be physically active
- Your child is being bullied about their weight
- Your teenager seems distressed about body image, is skipping meals, or exercising excessively
- The blood test results you received seem concerning

## Common questions

**Q: My child is chubby but healthy. Isn't chubbiness normal in children?**
A: Many families associate childhood chubbiness with health. The growth chart gives us an objective measure. If your child's weight is above the healthy range, the blood tests tell us whether early metabolic changes are happening — even when the child looks and feels fine. Acting early prevents problems like diabetes and high blood pressure later.

**Q: Will the programme put my child on a strict diet?**
A: No. For growing children, we do not restrict calories — we adjust what is eaten so that calories match what the body needs for growth without excess. The dietitian works with your family's actual food habits and culture. The changes are practical: more dal and vegetables, less biscuits and sugary drinks, right-sized portions.

**Q: Is this genetic? Nothing we can do?**
A: Genetics does play a role — some families are more prone to weight gain. But genetics is not destiny. Think of it this way: genetics loads the gun, but the food and activity environment pulls the trigger. The programme modifies the environment, and that makes a real difference.

**Q: My child eats very little — how can they be overweight?**
A: Portion sizes can be larger than they appear, and some foods (fried items, sweets, packaged snacks) pack a lot of calories into a small volume. The dietitian uses food models and portion guides to reveal what is actually being consumed. Many families are surprised by the 24-hour food recall.

**Q: Can you just give a medicine to reduce the weight?**
A: For children, lifestyle changes are the first and most effective treatment. Weight-loss medicines exist but are used only for a small number of severe cases and only under specialist supervision. The Healthy Weight Programme is designed to achieve what medicines cannot — lasting family-level habit change.

**Q: My child is being teased at school. What can I do?**
A: Weight-related bullying is unfortunately common. We take it seriously. Our Behavioural Clinic can help if it is affecting your child's mood, self-esteem, or school attendance. Frame weight management at home in terms of health and energy, not appearance. Never use words like "fat" or compare your child to siblings or peers.

## What happens next

Your next visit depends on your child's plan:
- **Overweight with no metabolic risk:** We see your child in 3 months to replot the growth chart.
- **Healthy Weight Programme:** 12 weeks with dietitian sessions (weekly for the first 4 weeks, then fortnightly), doctor visits at weeks 1, 6, and 12, and weekly check-ins via the parent app.
- **Pre-diabetes monitoring:** Blood sugar recheck at 3 months.
- **Cholesterol or liver concerns:** Blood test recheck at 3 months and 6 months.
- **Post-programme maintenance:** Visits every 3 months for the first year to keep the momentum going, then every 6 months.

BMI charts, dietary targets, activity logs, and motivational content are available in your SKIDS parent app.
