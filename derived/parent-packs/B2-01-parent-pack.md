# Understanding Your Child's Hormonal Health — For Parents

## What are hormonal (endocrine) conditions?

Hormones are chemical messengers that control growth, energy, blood sugar, and puberty. When a child's body makes too much, too little, or none of a particular hormone, it affects how they grow, develop, and feel. The most common hormonal conditions in children are diabetes (the body cannot manage blood sugar properly), thyroid problems (the gland that controls energy and growth), growth-hormone deficiency (not growing as expected), and puberty that starts too early or too late. All of these are well understood and treatable. Specialist care available at select SKIDS clinics ensures your child is managed by a children's endocrinologist with specific training in these conditions.

---

## What does SKIDS do about it?

Here is how we help your child:

- **Type 1 diabetes.** Your child's body has stopped making insulin, so we provide it from outside — by injection or, in some cases, a pump. We teach your child and family how to check blood sugar, give insulin, and count carbohydrates. We monitor with a blood test called HbA1c every three months. Most children with type 1 diabetes live fully normal, active lives.
- **Type 2 diabetes.** When blood sugar is high because of weight and lifestyle factors, we start with medicine (usually metformin) and work closely with the nutrition team on diet and exercise. If needed, insulin is added.
- **Thyroid problems.** An underactive thyroid is treated with a small daily tablet that replaces the missing hormone. An overactive thyroid is treated with medicine that slows the gland down. Both need regular blood tests to keep the dose right.
- **Growth-hormone treatment.** If testing confirms your child's body is not making enough growth hormone, a daily injection at bedtime replaces it. Most children adjust to the injection quickly, and we expect to see significant growth improvement in the first year.
- **Early or late puberty.** If puberty is starting too early, we can slow it down with a medicine given by injection every few months, which protects your child's adult height and gives them more time to develop at a natural pace. If puberty is delayed, we investigate the cause and treat accordingly.
- **Regular monitoring.** Every condition needs ongoing check-ups — blood tests, growth measurements, and medicine adjustments as your child grows.

## What you can do at home

- **Diabetes: learn the routine.** Blood-sugar checking, insulin, and carbohydrate counting will become part of daily life. The first few weeks are the hardest; after that, it becomes routine. Use the SKIDS parent app to log blood sugars and track trends.
- **Know the signs of low blood sugar.** Shakiness, sweating, confusion, irritability — give a fast-acting sugar (juice, glucose tablet) immediately, then a snack. Keep the emergency card in your child's school bag.
- **Give thyroid medicine correctly.** Give levothyroxine on an empty stomach, at the same time each day (usually morning), 30 minutes before food. Do not skip doses.
- **Growth-hormone injection at bedtime.** Follow the injection technique you were taught. Rotate injection sites. Store the medicine in the fridge.
- **Keep all follow-up appointments.** Hormonal conditions need regular monitoring because your child is growing and doses change. Missing visits means doses may fall behind your child's needs.
- **Talk to your child's school.** For diabetes, the teacher must know how to recognise and respond to low blood sugar. We provide a school communication pack.

## When to call us

**Call SKIDS immediately or go to the emergency room if:**
- Your child with diabetes is vomiting repeatedly, breathing fast, or smelling fruity on the breath (this could be a dangerous blood-sugar crisis)
- Your child with diabetes has very low blood sugar that does not improve after giving sugar, or they lose consciousness
- Your child on thyroid medicine for an overactive thyroid develops a high fever with a very fast heartbeat
- Your child on adrenal medicine is vomiting and cannot keep the medicine down, especially during illness

**Call the clinic within a few days if:**
- Blood-sugar readings are consistently outside the target range despite following the plan
- Your child is experiencing side effects from any medicine (nausea, headache, mood changes)
- Growth seems to have slowed despite being on growth-hormone treatment
- You have missed insulin doses or run out of supplies
- You have questions about diet, exercise, school management, or travel with diabetes

## Common questions

**Q: Will my child have diabetes forever?**
A: Type 1 diabetes is a lifelong condition, but it is completely manageable. With the right care, your child can do everything — school, sports, travel, and eventually have their own family. Type 2 diabetes can sometimes be reversed with weight loss and lifestyle changes, but it needs ongoing monitoring.

**Q: Is insulin addictive?**
A: No. Insulin is a hormone the body needs to survive. In type 1 diabetes, the body has stopped making it, so we replace it — just as we replace thyroid hormone when the thyroid stops working. It is not a drug you become dependent on; it is something your child's body needs.

**Q: Will growth-hormone injections make my child taller than they would have been?**
A: Growth-hormone treatment replaces what the body is not making. It helps your child reach their natural growth potential — not beyond it. We monitor carefully to ensure growth is appropriate.

**Q: My child is developing early — will the treatment delay everything permanently?**
A: No. The treatment pauses early puberty to protect your child's adult height and give them more time to develop at a natural pace. When treatment stops (usually around the normal age of puberty), development resumes naturally.

**Q: Can we manage diabetes with diet alone and avoid insulin?**
A: In type 1 diabetes, insulin is essential — diet alone cannot replace it. Diet is an important part of management (carbohydrate counting helps match insulin doses to food), but it works alongside insulin, not instead of it. In type 2 diabetes, diet and exercise are the foundation, and some children do achieve good control without insulin.

**Q: Other people in the family have diabetes. Will my other children get it too?**
A: Type 1 diabetes has a small genetic component, but most siblings do not develop it. Type 2 diabetes runs more strongly in families, but lifestyle changes (healthy eating, regular exercise, maintaining a healthy weight) significantly reduce the risk. We can screen your other children if you are concerned.

## What happens next

Your next visit depends on your child's condition:
- **Newly diagnosed diabetes:** We see your child within 1 week of starting insulin, then every 2 weeks for the first 2 months, then every 3 months.
- **Stable diabetes:** Every 3 months for HbA1c and review. One comprehensive annual check (eyes, kidneys, heart, thyroid).
- **Thyroid:** Every 4-6 weeks until the dose is stable, then every 3-6 months.
- **Growth-hormone treatment:** Every 3-6 months for height measurement, blood tests, and dose adjustment.
- **Early puberty on treatment:** Every 3-6 months to track development and growth.

Your SKIDS parent app will show blood-sugar logs, medicine schedules, appointment reminders, supply reorder alerts, and educational content specific to your child's condition.
