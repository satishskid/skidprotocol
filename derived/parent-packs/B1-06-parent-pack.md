# Understanding Your Child's Allergy — For Parents

## What is an allergy?

An allergy happens when your child's immune system overreacts to something that is normally harmless — a food, pollen, dust mites, an insect sting, or a medicine. The immune system treats that substance as a threat and produces a reaction. This can show up as a runny or blocked nose, itchy or watery eyes, hives (raised itchy patches on the skin), swelling, stomach pain, vomiting, or in serious cases, difficulty breathing. Some children react to one thing; others react to several. The good news is that allergies can be identified precisely with testing, managed with a clear plan, and in many cases children outgrow certain food allergies over time. What matters most is knowing exactly what your child reacts to and having a plan ready.

## What does SKIDS do about it?

Here is how we help your child:

- **Find out what causes the reaction.** We do skin-prick tests or blood tests targeted to the specific allergens your child's history suggests. We do not do broad "allergy panels" — we test for what matters clinically.
- **Build an avoidance plan.** Our team shows you how to read food labels, prepare safe meals, manage school and restaurant situations, and reduce dust or pollen exposure at home.
- **Create an action plan.** Every family with a confirmed allergy gets a written action plan — step-by-step instructions for what to do if a mild reaction happens and what to do if a serious reaction happens.
- **Provide adrenaline auto-injectors.** If your child is at risk of a serious reaction, we dispense two auto-injectors (one for school, one for home) and our nurse trains you and your child to use them confidently.
- **Treat nasal allergy.** For children with persistent sneezing, congestion, and itchy nose, we use saline rinses, antihistamine tablets, and nasal sprays. If these are not enough, we may offer a desensitisation programme.
- **Test whether an allergy has been outgrown.** For allergies that commonly resolve (milk, egg, wheat, soy), we monitor blood tests over time and can do a supervised food challenge in the clinic when the results suggest tolerance may have developed.

## What you can do at home

- **Learn to read labels.** Check every packaged food for your child's allergen. Look for "may contain" warnings. Our team will show you what to look for in English, Hindi, and your regional language.
- **Reduce dust-mite exposure.** Use mattress and pillow encasings. Wash bedding weekly in hot water. Vacuum with a HEPA filter. Reduce soft toys and heavy curtains in the bedroom.
- **Keep auto-injectors accessible.** Always carry the auto-injector. Check the expiry date monthly. Keep one at school and one at home or on your person. Replace before expiry — we will remind you.
- **Practise the action plan.** Review the action plan with everyone who cares for your child — grandparents, school staff, babysitters. Know the difference between a mild reaction (skin only) and a serious reaction (breathing difficulty, throat tightness, faintness).
- **Prepare for family events and travel.** Carry safe food to gatherings. Brief the host or restaurant. Have the auto-injector on hand. Identify the nearest hospital at travel destinations.
- **Give nasal spray correctly.** Aim away from the centre wall of the nose (toward the ear). Do not sniff hard. Allow 4-6 weeks for full effect.

## When to call us

**Call SKIDS immediately or use the action plan if your child has any of these:**
- Difficulty breathing, wheezing, or noisy breathing after eating or being stung
- Throat tightness or swelling of the tongue or lips
- Feeling faint, dizzy, or collapsing
- Vomiting combined with a skin reaction (hives, swelling) after a known allergen
- Any reaction that feels like it is getting worse quickly

**Use the adrenaline auto-injector first, then call an ambulance. Do not wait to see if it improves.**

**Call the clinic within 24 hours if:**
- Your child has a mild reaction (skin-only hives, mild oral itch) after accidental exposure to a known allergen
- Nasal allergy symptoms are getting worse despite regular treatment
- Hives are lasting more than 6 weeks
- You are unsure whether a new food or medicine is safe for your child
- The auto-injector is approaching its expiry date and you have not received a replacement reminder

## Common questions

**Q: Will my child outgrow the allergy?**
A: It depends on the allergen. Milk, egg, wheat, and soy allergies are commonly outgrown during childhood. Peanut, tree nut, fish, and shellfish allergies tend to persist. We monitor your child's test results over time and can do a supervised food challenge when the numbers suggest tolerance may have developed.

**Q: The auto-injector needle looks frightening. Is it safe?**
A: The auto-injector is designed for non-medical people to use in an emergency. The needle is hidden — you press the device against the outer thigh, it clicks, and the medicine is delivered through the muscle. It is not intravenous. Our nurse will demonstrate with a trainer device and have you practise until you feel confident.

**Q: Can we manage by just being careful with food?**
A: Careful avoidance prevents most reactions, but accidental exposures happen — hidden ingredients in spice mixes, shared cooking surfaces, festival sweets with nuts. The action plan and auto-injector are for those moments. Avoidance is the first line; the injector is the safety net.

**Q: My child has nasal allergy. Will the nose spray cause side effects?**
A: The nasal spray (a corticosteroid) acts locally in the nose and is not the same as steroid tablets. At the doses we use, side effects are minimal. It takes 4-6 weeks to work fully. The most common issue is mild nosebleeds if the spray hits the centre wall of the nose — aiming toward the ear prevents this.

**Q: Someone did a broad allergy panel and said my child is allergic to many things. Should I eliminate all of them?**
A: Broad panels often produce false positives — the test may be positive for a food your child eats without any problem. We only act on test results that match your child's actual reaction history. Unnecessary elimination diets restrict nutrition without benefit.

**Q: We use traditional remedies (honey, mustard oil, ayurvedic preparations) for allergy symptoms. Is that okay?**
A: Some traditional remedies are harmless alongside our treatment. We are happy to discuss what you use specifically. The important thing is that traditional remedies do not replace the auto-injector for serious reactions — nothing replaces adrenaline in an emergency.

## What happens next

Your next visit is scheduled based on your child's condition:
- **Nasal allergy:** We will see your child in 6 weeks to 3 months to check how the treatment is working. We will re-assess symptoms and adjust medicines if needed.
- **Food allergy:** We will see your child in 3 months to reinforce the action plan, check auto-injector technique, and review school-letter status. After that, annual visits with repeat blood tests to track whether the allergy is resolving.
- **Desensitisation programme:** We will check in at 1 week after the first dose, then every 3 months for the duration of the 3-year course.
- **Auto-injector replacement:** We will send a reminder 30 days before expiry. Please respond promptly — an expired injector may not work when needed.

All visit summaries, your child's action plan, allergen-avoidance checklists, and auto-injector technique videos are available in your SKIDS parent app.
