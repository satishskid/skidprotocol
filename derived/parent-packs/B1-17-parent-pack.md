# Understanding Your Child's Kidney Health — For Parents

## What are these conditions?

**Urine infections (UTI):** Bacteria enter the urinary tract and cause an infection. Symptoms include fever, pain when passing urine, frequent urination, and sometimes smelly or cloudy urine. In babies, the only sign may be an unexplained fever. Urine infections are common in children and are treated with antibiotics. We also check with an ultrasound to make sure the kidneys and urinary tubes are normal.

**Nephrotic syndrome:** The kidneys start leaking protein into the urine, which causes puffiness — usually around the eyes in the morning and in the legs by evening. The good news is that most children respond very well to the medicine (prednisolone). You will see the puffiness go down, usually within 1-2 weeks. The full course is about 3 months, and it is very important to complete it even when your child looks better.

**High blood pressure:** In children, high blood pressure often has a specific cause — the kidneys are the first thing we check. Blood tests, urine tests, and an ultrasound tell us whether the kidneys are involved. In most cases, lifestyle changes (diet and exercise) are enough to bring the blood pressure down.

## What does SKIDS do about it?

Here is how we help your child:

- **Diagnose accurately.** We confirm urine infections with a proper urine test (not a bag specimen, which can give false results). We confirm blood pressure with multiple readings on different days. We check kidney function with blood and urine tests.
- **Treat urine infections.** We prescribe the right antibiotic based on the urine test results, and we re-test the urine after treatment to confirm the infection has cleared.
- **Manage nephrotic syndrome.** We start the standard medicine course, monitor your child's weight and urine daily during the first phase, and guide you through the gradual dose reduction over 3 months.
- **Investigate blood pressure.** We check whether the kidneys, hormones, or weight are the cause, and we manage with diet and exercise changes first.
- **Prevent recurrent infections.** For children who get repeated urine infections, we may prescribe a low-dose preventive medicine and work on toileting habits, hydration, and constipation (which is a common contributor).
- **Follow up on birth scans.** If your baby's scan before birth showed a kidney concern, we monitor with repeat ultrasounds at the right intervals.

## What you can do at home

- **Complete the full antibiotic course** for urine infections. Do not stop early even if your child seems better.
- **For nephrotic syndrome:** weigh your child every morning before breakfast and check the urine with the dipstick we provide. Log both in the parent app. These daily checks help us catch any return of the condition early.
- **Push fluids.** Make sure your child drinks enough water throughout the day. Good hydration helps prevent urine infections and supports kidney health.
- **Treat constipation.** Constipation is a common cause of urine infections and bladder problems. If your child has hard or infrequent stools, talk to the doctor about it.
- **For high blood pressure:** reduce salt in cooking, increase fruits, vegetables, and lentils, encourage active play for at least 60 minutes daily, and limit sugary drinks and packaged snacks.
- **Do not stop the nephrotic syndrome medicine early.** Even when your child looks completely well, the medicine needs to be tapered gradually over the full course. Stopping too soon is the most common reason the condition comes back.

## When to call us

**Call SKIDS immediately or go to the emergency room if:**
- Your child's face or body becomes suddenly very puffy (nephrotic syndrome worsening)
- Your child is producing very little or no urine
- Your child has a severe headache with vision changes (possible blood pressure emergency)
- Your child with nephrotic syndrome develops a high fever with stomach pain (possible serious infection)
- Your child is on the kidney medicine and seems very unwell

**Call the clinic within a few days if:**
- Fever or pain returns after a urine infection was treated
- You see blood in the urine
- The urine dipstick shows protein returning (nephrotic syndrome families)
- Your child is struggling with the medicine or having side effects
- You have questions about the blood pressure diet changes

## Common questions

**Q: The doctor said "steroids" for 3 months — are steroids dangerous?**
A: The medicine (prednisolone) used for nephrotic syndrome is a different type of steroid from bodybuilding steroids. It is a short course with a gradual taper. Side effects like increased appetite, weight gain, and mood changes are temporary and go away after the course is completed. The alternative — leaving the condition untreated — leads to serious complications including infections and blood clots.

**Q: Does kidney problem mean dialysis?**
A: No. Most childhood kidney conditions we manage — urine infections, nephrotic syndrome, mild high blood pressure — do not progress to kidney failure. Nephrotic syndrome in children has an excellent outcome: most children respond to treatment and outgrow the condition.

**Q: Why does my child need a urine test through a catheter? Can't you use a bag?**
A: Bag urine specimens are unreliable for testing. Skin bacteria contaminate the sample and can lead to incorrect diagnoses and unnecessary treatment. A clean-catch or catheter specimen is quick and gives us an accurate result.

**Q: My child keeps getting urine infections. Why?**
A: Recurrent urine infections can happen because of constipation (full bowels press on the bladder), poor toileting habits (holding urine too long), or sometimes because the tubes between the kidney and bladder allow urine to flow backward. We investigate the cause and put a prevention plan in place.

**Q: His blood pressure is high — will he need medicine forever?**
A: In children, we first find the cause. If it is weight-related, lifestyle changes often normalise the blood pressure. If it is kidney-related, treating the kidney issue helps. Medicine is only added if lifestyle changes alone do not work after 6 months, and even then, many children can eventually stop the medicine.

## What happens next

Your next visit depends on your child's condition:
- **Urine infection:** Recheck urine 4 weeks after treatment to confirm the infection is cleared. Annual urine screening at routine check-ups.
- **Nephrotic syndrome:** Weekly visits during the first 6 weeks (weight and urine check), then monthly during the tapering phase, then every 3 months for the first year after the medicine course finishes.
- **Blood pressure monitoring:** Monthly for the first 3 months, then every 3 months if stable.
- **Blood or protein in urine (monitoring):** Every 3 months with urine and blood pressure checks.

Your child's medicine schedule, daily weight log, urine dipstick results, and blood pressure readings are all tracked in your SKIDS parent app.
