# Understanding Constipation in Children — For Parents

## What is constipation?

Constipation means your child's stools are hard, infrequent, painful, or difficult to pass. Many children develop a cycle: a hard stool causes pain, so the child starts holding it in to avoid the pain, which makes the next stool even harder and larger. Over time, the bowel stretches and loses its ability to push properly. In some children, this leads to soiling (stool leaking into the underwear) — this is not the child's fault and is not a behaviour problem. The stretched bowel has lost sensation, and loose stool leaks around the hard blockage without the child even feeling it. Constipation is very common in children and is almost always "functional" — meaning there is no underlying disease causing it.

---

## What does SKIDS do about it?

Here is how we help your child:

- **Confirm the diagnosis.** The doctor checks your child's tummy, asks about stool patterns, and uses a standard scale (the Bristol Stool Scale) to classify the stool type. In most cases, no blood tests or scans are needed.
- **Clear the blockage first.** If stool has built up, we start with a higher dose of a gentle medicine (PEG) for 3-6 days. This softens and clears the blockage from above — no enemas or invasive procedures are needed.
- **Keep the stool soft for months.** After clearance, your child takes a daily dose of PEG for at least 3-6 months. This gives the stretched bowel time to shrink back to its normal size and regain its pushing ability. We adjust the dose so your child has one soft stool every day.
- **Build good toileting habits.** We teach a toileting routine: sitting on the toilet for 5-10 minutes after meals, 2-3 times a day, with a foot stool so the knees are above the hips (the natural squatting position). Positive reinforcement (sticker charts) — never punishment.
- **Improve the diet.** Our dietitian helps increase fibre (fruits, vegetables, whole grains, dal) and fluids (water, not juice), and reduce excessive milk if that is contributing.
- **Wean the medicine gradually.** After at least 3 months of daily soft stools, we reduce the medicine dose slowly over several weeks. If stools harden at any step, we go back to the previous dose. Stopping too soon is the most common reason constipation comes back.

## What you can do at home

- **Give the medicine every day as prescribed.** Mix PEG powder in water or juice. Give it at the same time each day. Do not skip days or stop early — the bowel needs months to recover.
- **Encourage regular toilet sits.** After breakfast and after dinner, have your child sit on the toilet for 5-10 minutes. Use a foot stool so the knees are higher than the hips. Make it relaxed — a book or a song helps. Never force or rush.
- **Increase fibre.** Aim for fruits, vegetables, and whole grains at every meal. Dal, rajma, chana, oats, roti (whole wheat), apples, pears, papaya, and guava are all good sources. Your child's daily fibre target is their age plus 5 grams (for example, an 8-year-old needs about 13 grams per day).
- **Increase water.** Encourage water throughout the day. Fibre without enough water can actually make constipation worse.
- **Limit excess milk.** Too much milk (more than 2 glasses a day for toddlers, less for older children) can be constipating. The dietitian will guide you on the right amount.
- **Use the sticker chart.** Reward your child for sitting on the toilet (not for producing a stool — the sitting is the habit we are building). Celebrate dry underwear days positively. Never punish soiling.
- **Do not stop the medicine without the doctor's guidance.** Even if your child seems completely better, the medicine needs to be reduced gradually. Stopping suddenly is the top reason constipation returns.

## When to call us

**Call SKIDS immediately or go to the emergency room if:**
- Your child has severe stomach pain with vomiting (especially green or yellow vomit)
- Your child's abdomen is very swollen and hard
- Your child has not passed any stool or gas for several days and is in pain

**Call the clinic within a few days if:**
- The medicine does not seem to be working after 1-2 weeks (stools still hard)
- Your child's soiling is getting worse despite treatment
- You see blood in or on the stool (most commonly from a small tear caused by hard stool — this heals when the constipation is treated, but let us know)
- Your child is refusing to sit on the toilet or is very distressed
- You are having trouble getting your child to take the medicine
- The PEG has run out and you need a refill

## Common questions

**Q: Will my child become dependent on the medicine?**
A: No. PEG (polyethylene glycol) is an osmotic agent — it simply holds water in the stool to keep it soft. It is not addictive and does not make the bowel lazy. The bowel works normally once PEG is stopped. The reason we use it for 3-6 months is to give the stretched bowel time to recover its size and strength. We wean it off gradually and only stop when the bowel is ready.

**Q: He's soiling his pants — is he doing it on purpose?**
A: No. When constipation causes a large build-up of hard stool, the bowel stretches and loses its normal sensation. Loose stool leaks around the blockage without the child even feeling it. This is involuntary. Punishing your child will make it worse, not better. Once we treat the constipation, the soiling stops.

**Q: Can't we just change the diet instead of using medicine?**
A: Diet is an essential part of the plan and helps prevent constipation from coming back. But once a blockage has developed, diet alone cannot clear it. Think of it this way: diet prevents the problem; medicine fixes it once it has built up. Both are needed, but the medicine does the heavy lifting in the first few months.

**Q: What about isabgol (psyllium husk)?**
A: Isabgol is a reasonable fibre supplement and can be used alongside the treatment. However, it is not a substitute for PEG when there is impaction. Make sure your child drinks plenty of water with isabgol — fibre without water can make things worse. Castor oil is not recommended for children.

**Q: How long will this take?**
A: Most children start having soft daily stools within 1-2 weeks of starting the medicine. The full programme takes 3-6 months. Weaning the medicine takes several more weeks. After stopping the medicine, we follow up for 3 months to make sure there is no relapse. It requires patience, but the results are lasting.

**Q: My child also wets the bed. Is that related?**
A: Yes, very often. A bowel full of hard stool presses on the bladder, which can cause or worsen bedwetting. We treat the constipation first, and in many children, the bedwetting improves or resolves. If it does not, our Bedwetting Clinic can help with the next step.

## What happens next

Your next visit depends on the plan:
- **After blockage clearance:** We see you in 1-2 weeks to confirm the blockage has cleared and the daily medicine is working.
- **Maintenance phase:** Monthly visits for 3-6 months to adjust the medicine dose and track progress.
- **Weaning phase:** Visits every 4 weeks as we gradually reduce the medicine. If stools harden, we go back to the previous dose.
- **After stopping the medicine:** A follow-up at 3 months to confirm no relapse.

Your child's stool diary, Bristol Stool Scale tracker, toileting routine timer, and PEG dose are all available in the SKIDS parent app.
