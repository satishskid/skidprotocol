# Understanding Your Child's Nutrition and Growth — For Parents

## What are growth and nutrition concerns?

Every child grows at their own pace, but doctors use growth charts to check that a child's weight and height are tracking within a healthy range for their age. When a child's growth slows down or their weight drops on the chart, it is a signal to look more closely. The most common reasons are simple and fixable: not enough calories or nutrients in the diet, iron deficiency (which causes tiredness and paleness), or vitamin D deficiency (which affects bones). Some children are "picky eaters" who eat a narrow range of foods, which can lead to gaps in their nutrition. Breastfeeding mothers sometimes need support with latch and milk supply. In most cases, a structured plan with dietary changes and targeted supplements brings growth back on track within weeks to months.

## What does SKIDS do about it?

Here is how we help your child:

- **Plot growth carefully.** We measure weight, height (and arm circumference for younger children), calculate the growth score, and track the trend over time on a growth chart. One measurement is a snapshot; the trend tells the story.
- **Run targeted blood tests.** If we suspect a deficiency, we check haemoglobin (for anaemia), iron stores, vitamin D, B12, and other nutrients as needed.
- **Build a dietary plan with our dietitian.** The dietitian sits with you (and ideally the main person who cooks and feeds) to review what your child actually eats, find the gaps, and suggest specific changes that fit your kitchen, your budget, and your child's preferences.
- **Prescribe supplements when needed.** If blood tests show a deficiency, we prescribe the right supplement at the right dose for your child's weight, for a defined period, and recheck to confirm it has worked.
- **Support breastfeeding.** For mothers having difficulty, we offer a 4-session programme covering latch, positioning, milk supply, and common problems.
- **Guide starting solids.** We run a structured complementary-feeding programme from 6 months onward, including which foods to start, when to introduce allergens, and how to build variety.
- **Help picky eaters.** A structured programme with the dietitian over 8-12 weeks, focused on expanding food variety without mealtime battles.
- **Manage cow's-milk-protein allergy.** If your child reacts to milk protein, we guide a structured elimination and reintroduction plan, working with the Allergy Clinic.

## What you can do at home

- **Give iron medicine correctly.** On an empty stomach (morning is best), with a glass of orange juice or lemon water (vitamin C helps absorption). Avoid giving it with milk, tea, or calcium supplements. The stool will turn dark — this is normal.
- **Offer energy-dense foods.** Add ghee, butter, or oil to dal and rice. Use nut pastes (if no allergy). Full-fat dairy. Egg. These small additions increase calories without increasing volume.
- **Feed at regular times.** Three meals and two snacks at predictable times. Avoid constant grazing, which reduces appetite at mealtimes.
- **Avoid force-feeding.** It feels natural to insist, but research shows that force-feeding usually makes the problem worse over time. Offer a variety of foods; let your child decide how much to eat. The dietitian can show you specific alternatives.
- **Give vitamin D as prescribed.** Most Indian children, especially those in cities, do not get enough sun exposure. The supplement is safe at the prescribed dose.
- **Involve the whole family.** If grandparents are involved in feeding, bring them to the dietitian session. The plan works best when everyone at the table follows the same approach.

## When to call us

**Call SKIDS immediately if:**
- Your child is suddenly very pale, extremely tired, or breathless
- Your child has persistent vomiting and cannot keep food or fluids down
- Your child has lost weight rapidly over a few weeks
- Your newborn is not feeding well and has lost more than 10% of birth weight
- You notice swelling in your child's feet, hands, or face along with poor appetite

**Call the clinic within a few days if:**
- Your child is not taking the supplement as prescribed (nausea, stomach pain, refusing it)
- Eating seems to be getting worse rather than better
- You are worried about your child's energy level, paleness, or fatigue
- You are breastfeeding and having difficulty that was not resolved at the last session
- Your adolescent is skipping meals, exercising excessively, or talking about dieting in a concerning way

## Common questions

**Q: My child is small but healthy and active. Should I be worried?**
A: Some children are naturally smaller — they follow their own growth curve consistently, and that is fine. The concern is when the curve drops or the child crosses downward on the chart. If your child's growth is steady along their own line, they are likely constitutionally smaller, and we simply monitor.

**Q: Why does my child need supplements if we eat well at home?**
A: Each supplement we prescribe is based on a specific deficiency found on a blood test. Indian diets — even healthy ones — can be low in certain nutrients (especially iron, vitamin D, and B12 in vegetarian families). The supplement fills a specific gap that diet alone may not fill quickly enough.

**Q: Will iron medicine make my child constipated?**
A: It can. If constipation occurs, increase fluids and fibre-rich foods (fruits, vegetables, whole grains). We can also switch to a different form of iron that is gentler on the stomach. The dark stools are a normal effect of the medicine, not bleeding.

**Q: My child is a very picky eater. Will they outgrow it?**
A: Many children expand their food choices as they get older. However, waiting without intervention risks nutritional gaps during critical growth years. The picky-eater programme is designed to gently expand food variety over 8-12 weeks — working with your child's preferences, not against them. Most families see measurable improvement.

**Q: We are vegetarian. Can our child get everything they need?**
A: Yes, with attention to specific nutrients. Vegetarian diets in Indian children can meet all requirements, but need particular focus on iron (from dal, green leafy vegetables, and iron-fortified foods), B12 (from dairy, or supplements if vegan), zinc, and protein. The dietitian will review your family's specific diet and suggest practical additions.

**Q: My mother-in-law says the child needs to eat more and pushes food. What should I do?**
A: We hear this often. Grandparents want the best for the child, and in many families, feeding is an expression of love. We would love to have your mother-in-law at the next dietitian session — when the whole feeding team understands the approach, it works better. The goal is the same love, through the right foods, without the mealtime battles.

## What happens next

Your next visit depends on your child's plan:
- **Mild growth concern:** We see your child in 3 months to replot the growth chart and check the trend.
- **Growth rehabilitation programme:** Visits every 2-4 weeks for 12 weeks, with dietitian sessions included. Most children show catch-up growth within this period.
- **Iron deficiency treatment:** Blood recheck at 4 weeks to confirm the medicine is working. Total treatment is about 3 months, then a maintenance dose.
- **Vitamin D treatment:** Recheck at 12 weeks.
- **Picky-eater programme:** Weekly sessions for 4 weeks, then biweekly.
- **Breastfeeding support:** 4 sessions over 4 weeks, or as needed until breastfeeding is established.

Growth charts, supplement reminders, diet diary tools, and recipe guides are available in your SKIDS parent app.
