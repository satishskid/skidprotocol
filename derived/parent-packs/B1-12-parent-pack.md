# Understanding Learning Difficulties — For Parents

## What is it?

Some children find reading, writing, or maths much harder than their classmates — not because they are less intelligent, but because their brain processes certain kinds of information differently. This is called a specific learning disability (SLD). About 5–10% of all children have one.

The most common types are difficulty with reading (sometimes called dyslexia), difficulty with writing (dysgraphia), and difficulty with maths (dyscalculia). These often become noticeable around 2nd or 3rd class, when the schoolwork gets harder. A child with a learning disability is typically smart — they understand things when you explain them, but struggle when they have to read, write, or calculate on their own.

The good news: with the right kind of support — specific teaching methods, extra time, and sometimes technology — most children with learning disabilities do very well.

## What does SKIDS do about it?

Here is how we help your child:

- **Check hearing and vision first.** We make sure your child can see and hear clearly, because these basic senses affect all learning.
- **Screen for attention problems.** About a third of children with learning disabilities also have attention difficulties. We check for both, because the treatment is different.
- **Assess reading, writing, and maths.** We test your child's skills in the clinic and collect information from the teacher using a structured questionnaire.
- **Arrange formal testing if needed.** If the screening shows a likely learning disability, we refer to a psychologist for detailed testing that confirms the diagnosis.
- **Write a school letter.** We provide your child's school with specific recommendations: preferential seating, extra time, simpler instructions, and other adjustments.
- **Connect you with remedial education.** A remedial educator uses specialised teaching methods (not just more of the same tuition) designed for the way your child's brain works.
- **Help with exam accommodations.** If your child has a confirmed learning disability, they may be eligible for extra time, a scribe, or other support in board exams under Indian law.

## What you can do at home

- **Create a structured homework routine.** Same time, same place, short breaks every 20–30 minutes. Reduce distractions.
- **Praise effort, not just results.** "I can see you worked really hard on that" matters more than "Why did you only get 60%?"
- **Never punish for poor grades.** Your child is not being careless or lazy. Punishment makes the frustration worse and damages motivation.
- **Do not take away play or physical activity.** Exercise and play are essential for your child's well-being and actually help learning.
- **Read aloud together.** Even if your child struggles to read alone, reading together builds vocabulary, comprehension, and a love of stories.
- **Talk to the teacher.** Share what you learn from the assessment. Teachers who understand the difficulty can make small adjustments that help enormously.

## When to call us

Call SKIDS soon if:
- Your child is increasingly anxious, tearful, or refusing to go to school because of academic stress.
- Your child's teacher reports that accommodations are not helping and difficulties are getting worse.
- Your child is showing signs of low self-esteem or depression related to school.

## Common questions

**Q: He's just lazy — can't he try harder?**
A: Children with learning disabilities often work much harder than their classmates for poorer results. The difficulty is not about effort — it is about how the brain processes written words, numbers, or handwriting. The assessment shows us exactly where the difference is, so we can teach to it.

**Q: Will more tuition help?**
A: Regular tuition gives more of the same kind of teaching. If the child's brain processes information differently, more of the same does not help. Remedial education uses specific techniques — like multisensory reading methods — designed for the way your child learns. That is the difference.

**Q: She reverses letters — is that dyslexia?**
A: Reversing letters (like b and d) is completely normal up to about age 7. After age 7, if it persists alongside other reading difficulties, it may be one sign of dyslexia — but we look at the whole picture, not just one sign.

**Q: Will he ever be "normal"?**
A: Many children with learning disabilities develop strong strategies for working around their difficulty. With early support, they succeed in school and in careers. Identification and the right kind of teaching are what make the difference.

**Q: What is the disability certificate, and should we get one?**
A: Under Indian law, a specific learning disability is a recognised condition. The certificate gives your child legal rights to exam accommodations — extra time, a scribe, and sometimes exemption from certain subjects. These make a concrete difference in board exams. The word "disability" can feel heavy, but the practical benefits are significant.

## What happens next

- **If your child is at the early stage (at risk):** We set up school accommodations and monitor for one school term (3–4 months). Many children improve with these adjustments alone.
- **If formal testing is needed:** We arrange the assessment with a psychologist. Results usually take 2–4 weeks. We then meet to review the results and build a plan.
- **If a learning disability is confirmed:** We connect you with a remedial educator, set up exam accommodations, and follow up every 3 months for the first year, then every 6 months.
