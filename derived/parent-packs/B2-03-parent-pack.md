# Understanding Your Child's Joint and Immune Health — For Parents

## What are rheumatological conditions in children?

Sometimes a child's immune system — which normally fights infections — becomes overactive and begins attacking the body's own tissues. This can cause swelling in the joints (juvenile arthritis), problems with skin, kidneys, and blood (lupus), muscle weakness with skin rash (dermatomyositis), or inflamed blood vessels (vasculitis, including Kawasaki disease). Some children get recurring fevers every few weeks from immune over-activity (periodic fever syndromes). These conditions can sound frightening, but they are well understood and very treatable. Most children with juvenile arthritis achieve remission — meaning no active disease — within a few years of starting treatment. Specialist care available at select SKIDS clinics ensures your child is managed by a children's rheumatologist with specific training in these conditions.

## What does SKIDS do about it?

Here is how we help your child:

- **Juvenile arthritis.** We treat the joint inflammation step by step: first with anti-inflammatory medicine, then with a medicine called methotrexate that calms the immune system, and if needed, with biologic injections that target the specific part of the immune system causing the problem. Most children respond well and can move, play, and do sports normally with treatment. We also arrange regular eye checks, because juvenile arthritis can cause silent eye inflammation.
- **Lupus.** Lupus needs lifelong monitoring and treatment. All children with lupus take hydroxychloroquine (a medicine that protects against flares). During flares, we add other immune-calming medicines. We monitor the kidneys carefully, because they are the organ most at risk.
- **Kawasaki disease.** This condition inflames blood vessels, especially around the heart. We treat it promptly with a medicine given through a drip (immunoglobulin) and aspirin. Most children respond very well within 24-48 hours. We do heart scans at diagnosis, 2 weeks, 6 weeks, and beyond to check the blood vessels.
- **Periodic fevers (PFAPA).** If your child gets fevers every few weeks with mouth ulcers and sore throat, this is likely PFAPA — the immune system overreacting periodically. Episodes can be stopped with a single dose of prednisolone. Most children grow out of it by age 10. If episodes are very frequent, tonsil removal cures it in most children.
- **Dermatomyositis.** We treat muscle weakness and skin rash with steroids and methotrexate, monitoring muscle strength and blood tests regularly.
- **Regular monitoring.** All these conditions need ongoing blood tests, check-ups, and medicine adjustments. We track disease activity with specific scores so we can see whether your child is improving.

## What you can do at home

- **Give medicines exactly as prescribed.** Methotrexate is usually given once a week — mark the day on your calendar. Do not skip doses. If your child feels nauseous after methotrexate, give it at bedtime and mention it at the next visit. Always give folic acid as prescribed alongside methotrexate.
- **Watch for morning stiffness.** If your child limps or is stiff when waking up, note how long it lasts. A warm bath in the morning helps. Log this in the SKIDS parent app — the doctor uses this information to adjust treatment.
- **Keep all eye-check appointments.** For children with juvenile arthritis, the eye checks are essential. The type of eye inflammation that can occur is completely silent — your child will not complain of eye pain or blurred vision until it is advanced. Catching it early is simple to treat; catching it late can affect vision permanently.
- **Report infections promptly.** If your child is on immune-calming medicine and develops a fever, cough, or any infection, call the clinic. These medicines lower one part of the immune system, so infections need to be assessed quickly.
- **Encourage activity and physiotherapy.** Joint movement is important. Swimming is excellent for children with arthritis. Follow the physiotherapy exercises prescribed — they maintain joint mobility and muscle strength.
- **Sun protection (lupus).** If your child has lupus, sun exposure can trigger flares. Use sunscreen (SPF 50+), a hat, and protective clothing when outdoors.

## When to call us

**Call SKIDS immediately or go to the emergency room if:**
- Your child with juvenile arthritis has a high fever and looks very unwell — especially if they seem much sicker than a normal fever would explain (this could be a serious immune reaction that needs hospital treatment)
- Your child with lupus has blood in their urine, swelling around the eyes or legs, or suddenly becomes confused or behaves unusually
- Your child on immune-calming medicine has a high fever that does not respond to paracetamol
- Your child on Kawasaki treatment develops a new fever after initial improvement

**Call the clinic within a few days if:**
- New joint swelling or your child cannot walk or use a hand
- Medicine side effects are bothering your child (nausea from methotrexate, weight gain from steroids, injection-site reactions from biologics)
- You have missed a biologic injection or run out of medicine
- Your child's morning stiffness is getting longer
- Your child is missing school because of pain, fatigue, or stiffness
- You notice a new rash (especially butterfly-shaped on the face, or purple spots on the legs)

## Common questions

**Q: Arthritis is for old people — can children really get it?**
A: Yes. Juvenile arthritis is one of the most common chronic conditions in children. It is caused by the immune system attacking the joint lining, not by wear and tear like adult arthritis. Most children do very well with treatment, and many achieve complete remission.

**Q: Will methotrexate harm my child? It sounds like a strong medicine.**
A: Methotrexate is also used in cancer treatment, but at much higher doses. The dose we use for arthritis is very low and is one of the best-studied medicines in children's rheumatology. It works by calming the immune system. We monitor your child's liver and blood with regular tests to make sure everything stays healthy. Most children tolerate it well.

**Q: Will my child need medicine for life?**
A: It depends on the condition. For juvenile arthritis, many children achieve remission and can stop medicine after a period of being disease-free. For lupus, lifelong treatment is usually needed, but it keeps the condition well controlled. The doctor will discuss the expected course for your child's specific condition.

**Q: The eye checks feel unnecessary — my child sees perfectly well.**
A: That is exactly why we check. The eye inflammation that can happen with juvenile arthritis is completely painless and invisible to the child. By the time your child notices a problem, the damage may already be done. The eye check is a 5-minute examination that catches it when treatment is simple and effective.

**Q: Can my child play sports?**
A: Yes. In fact, we encourage it. Movement keeps joints healthy. Swimming is particularly good for children with arthritis. The only restriction is during an active flare in a specific joint — rest that joint until the inflammation settles. Otherwise, your child should be as active as they want to be.

**Q: We want to try homeopathy or Ayurvedic medicine.**
A: We respect your family's choices. The one thing we ask is that the prescribed medicines continue alongside any other therapy. Juvenile arthritis can permanently damage joints if not treated, and lupus can damage kidneys. As long as both approaches continue, we can monitor safely.

**Q: Will lupus affect my daughter's ability to have children when she is older?**
A: Most women with well-controlled lupus have healthy pregnancies. It does require careful planning with the doctor — certain medicines need to be adjusted before and during pregnancy. With good disease control and planning, the outlook is very positive.

## What happens next

Your next visit depends on your child's condition:
- **Juvenile arthritis (active, starting treatment):** Every 4 weeks until the inflammation settles and the medicine dose is stable.
- **Juvenile arthritis (stable):** Every 3 months for blood tests and joint checks. Eye checks every 3-6 months depending on risk.
- **Lupus (active or flare):** Every 2-4 weeks until stable. Urine checks at every visit.
- **Lupus (stable):** Every 1-3 months with blood and urine tests.
- **Kawasaki disease:** Heart scans at 2 weeks, 6 weeks, 6 months, and 12 months after treatment. If heart vessels are normal at 12 months, no further scans are needed.
- **Periodic fevers (PFAPA):** Every 3 months to track episode frequency. Most children grow out of it by age 10.

Your SKIDS parent app will show joint-symptom diaries, medicine trackers, blood-test schedules, eye-check appointment reminders, and educational content specific to your child's condition.
