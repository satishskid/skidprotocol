# Understanding Infections in Children — For Parents

## What are these conditions?

**Recurrent infections:** Many children who seem to be "always sick" actually have a normal immune system. Children in daycare or school settings catch 6 to 8 colds a year, and that is normal. When infections are unusually frequent, severe, or caused by unusual germs, we test to make sure the immune system is working properly. In most cases, it is.

**Dengue:** A viral illness spread by mosquitoes. Most children recover fully in 7 to 10 days. The critical period is when the fever breaks — that is when we watch most carefully for warning signs. Daily visits during the fever phase let us catch any changes early and keep your child safe at home rather than in the hospital.

**Malaria:** An infection spread by mosquitoes, diagnosed with a blood test. It is treated with specific anti-malarial medicines. Completing the full course is essential to prevent the infection from coming back.

**Typhoid:** A bacterial infection that causes prolonged fever, stomach pain, and fatigue. It is confirmed with a blood test and treated with a course of antibiotics.

---

## What does SKIDS do about it?

Here is how we help your child:

- **Find out why your child keeps getting sick.** We take a detailed infection history — how many infections, what type, how often. If the pattern is concerning, we run blood tests to check the immune system. Most children test normal, and that is reassuring.
- **Monitor dengue safely at home.** We check your child's platelet count and blood concentration every day during the fever phase. We watch for warning signs and, if any appear, we arrange hospital admission immediately.
- **Treat malaria and typhoid correctly.** We diagnose with proper tests (not guesswork), prescribe the right medicine, and follow up to confirm the infection is cleared.
- **Screen for TB.** If your child has been in contact with someone who has TB, we do a skin test or blood test to check for infection. If TB is confirmed, we refer to the government TB treatment centre for the full treatment course.
- **Counsel on antibiotic use.** We explain when antibiotics help and when they do not. Most childhood fevers and colds are caused by viruses, and antibiotics do not work on viruses. Using them unnecessarily creates resistant bacteria that are harder to treat later.
- **Investigate prolonged fever.** If your child has had fever for more than a week without a clear cause, we run a structured set of tests to find the diagnosis.

## What you can do at home

- **For dengue:** Give only paracetamol for fever — never ibuprofen or aspirin. Push fluids (water, ORS, coconut water, buttermilk). Come in every day for the blood test. If your child develops severe stomach pain, persistent vomiting, bleeding from the gums or nose, or becomes very drowsy — come to the clinic immediately, do not wait for the next day.
- **For malaria:** Complete the full medicine course, even if your child feels better. If the doctor has prescribed primaquine, make sure the G6PD test has been done first. Use mosquito nets and repellents to prevent re-infection.
- **For common colds and viral fevers:** Keep your child hydrated and comfortable. Paracetamol for fever and pain. No antibiotics are needed. If the fever lasts more than 5 days or your child seems to be getting worse, come back for a review.
- **For typhoid:** Complete the full antibiotic course. Light, easily digestible food. Plenty of fluids. Handwashing for the whole family.
- **Prevent mosquito bites:** Use bed nets, apply mosquito repellent, and eliminate standing water around the home (flower pots, cooler trays, open containers).

## When to call us

**Call SKIDS immediately or go to the emergency room if:**
- Your child has a high fever with a rash that does not fade when you press on it
- Your child is drowsy, difficult to wake, or confused
- Your child has severe stomach pain with persistent vomiting (dengue warning sign)
- Your child is bleeding from the gums, nose, or in the stool or vomit
- Your child has difficulty breathing or is breathing very fast
- Your child has a stiff neck with fever

**Call the clinic within a few days if:**
- Fever has lasted more than 5 days without a clear cause
- Your child is not improving on antibiotics after 48-72 hours
- You are concerned about how often your child is getting sick
- You have been in contact with someone who has TB
- Your child has a swollen gland that has not gone down after 4 weeks

## Common questions

**Q: Why didn't the doctor give antibiotics? My child has a fever.**
A: Antibiotics work against bacteria, not viruses. Most childhood fevers and colds are caused by viruses. Using antibiotics when they are not needed does not help your child get better faster, and it creates resistant bacteria that are harder to treat in the future. If at any point the infection turns out to be bacterial, we will start antibiotics immediately.

**Q: My child is always catching something. Is the immune system weak?**
A: Probably not. Children in group settings (daycare, school) are exposed to many viruses and getting 6-8 colds a year is completely normal. We run specific blood tests only when the infection pattern is unusual — very frequent, very severe, or caused by uncommon germs. In most cases, the immune system is working perfectly well.

**Q: Dengue platelet count is dropping — should I panic?**
A: The platelet count alone does not determine how serious the dengue is. What matters more are the clinical warning signs — severe stomach pain, persistent vomiting, bleeding, drowsiness. A child with a platelet count of 80,000 and no warning signs is safer than one with 120,000 and persistent vomiting. That is why we check both the blood test and the warning signs every day.

**Q: Can malaria come back?**
A: Yes, especially with the P. vivax type, which can hide in the liver and cause relapses. That is why the full medicine course — including the 14-day medicine (primaquine) if prescribed — is essential. If your child gets fever again after malaria treatment, come in for a blood test.

**Q: My child was in contact with TB. Does that mean he has TB?**
A: Not necessarily. The skin test or blood test checks whether your child has been exposed to the TB bacteria. A positive test means exposure, not necessarily active disease. If the test is positive, we do a chest X-ray and refer to the TB treatment centre for a full evaluation. TB is completely curable when treated properly.

## What happens next

Your next visit depends on your child's condition:
- **Dengue:** Daily visits during the fever phase. After the fever breaks, a recheck at day 7 and day 14 if platelet recovery is slow.
- **Malaria:** Day 3 (blood test to confirm the infection is clearing), day 7, and day 28 (to check for relapse).
- **Typhoid:** Day 7 (to check the response to antibiotics) and day 14 (end of course).
- **Recurrent infections:** Results review at 2 weeks. If immune-system tests are normal, a 3-month follow-up and then discharge to routine screening.
- **TB screening:** If referred to the TB treatment centre, a follow-up at 4 weeks to confirm enrolment, then coordination every 3 months.

Your child's infection history, daily dengue monitoring log, and test results are available in your SKIDS parent app.
