# Understanding Throat and Airway Problems --- For Parents

## What are common throat problems in children?

Sore throats are one of the most frequent childhood complaints. Most are caused by viruses and get better on their own in a few days. Some are caused by a specific bacteria called Group A Streptococcus ("strep throat"), which needs antibiotic treatment -- importantly, treating strep throat protects your child's heart from rheumatic fever, which is still common in India. Some children get tonsillitis repeatedly, and their tonsils may become enlarged enough to affect breathing during sleep or cause difficulty swallowing. Croup is another common condition -- a viral infection that causes a distinctive barking cough and noisy breathing, usually worst at night in children under 6 years.

## What does SKIDS do about it?

We use a rapid test (result in 5-10 minutes) to determine whether your child's sore throat is caused by strep bacteria. If positive, we prescribe the right antibiotic. If negative, we provide pain relief and reassurance. For children with recurrent tonsillitis, we carefully log every episode so we know exactly when a referral to our ENT surgeon partner is warranted. For croup, we treat with a steroid medicine that reduces airway swelling, and observe your child in the clinic until we are confident they are safe to go home. For children who have had rheumatic fever, we administer the protective injection every three weeks at our clinic.

## What you can do at home

- **Sore throat comfort:** Give paracetamol or ibuprofen as prescribed for pain. Warm fluids and soft foods help. Warm salt-water gargles work well for children aged 6 and above.
- **Finish the antibiotic:** If your child has strep throat, complete all 10 days of the antibiotic even though they will feel better in 2-3 days. Stopping early increases the risk of the infection returning and, rarely, of rheumatic fever.
- **Croup at home:** Keep your child calm -- crying and agitation make the breathing noise worse. Cool night air can help. If the noisy breathing returns at rest (not just with crying), go to the hospital immediately.
- **After tonsil surgery:** Soft, cool foods for 2 weeks. Ice cream is fine and soothing. Paracetamol only for pain (no ibuprofen -- bleeding risk). Adequate fluids. No strenuous activity for 2 weeks.

## When to call us

Call SKIDS or go to the emergency department immediately if:
- Your child has noisy breathing at rest with drooling and high fever (do not try to look in the throat)
- Your child cannot swallow saliva or is drooling excessively
- Bleeding from the throat after tonsil surgery -- any amount
- Swelling on one side of the throat with inability to open the mouth
- Your child's croup is not improving despite the medicine we gave, or the noisy breathing is getting worse

## Common questions

**Q: Why won't the doctor give antibiotics for every sore throat?**
A: Most sore throats are caused by viruses, and antibiotics do not work against viruses. The rapid test tells us which sore throats are caused by bacteria and need antibiotics. Using antibiotics when they are not needed leads to resistance, making them less effective when truly needed. Think of it as using the right medicine for the right infection.

**Q: My child has had tonsillitis three times this year. Why not just remove them?**
A: Tonsil removal is a surgery with real risks (pain, bleeding, infection). The evidence shows it helps when infections reach a specific frequency -- and we track every episode carefully. If the pattern continues and meets the criteria, we will refer to the ENT surgeon. Removing tonsils too early means surgery that might not have been necessary.

**Q: Is croup dangerous?**
A: Mild-to-moderate croup sounds alarming but is usually manageable. The steroid medicine we give is very effective and works within hours. The barking cough may last 3-5 days, but the worst is usually the first night. Severe croup is rare but does require hospital care -- we will tell you exactly what to watch for.

**Q: Why is the rheumatic fever injection so important?**
A: Rheumatic fever can damage heart valves. The injection prevents new episodes of strep infection that could trigger another attack. Missing injections increases the risk of further heart damage. We make it as convenient as possible -- same clinic, same nurse, scheduled reminders.

**Q: My child's tonsils look huge but he has no symptoms. Is that a problem?**
A: Large tonsils alone are not always a problem. They become a concern when they cause snoring with breathing pauses during sleep, difficulty swallowing, or repeated infections. We assess the size and symptoms together to decide the right course of action.

## What happens next

Your next visit depends on your child's condition:
- **Strep throat:** No routine follow-up needed if improving. Return at 48-72 hours if not better.
- **Croup:** We will call you the next day to check. Return if symptoms worsen.
- **Recurrent tonsillitis:** We log each episode. When the criteria for surgical referral are met, we coordinate with the ENT surgeon.
- **Rheumatic fever injection:** Every 3 weeks at our clinic. We will send you reminders.
- **After tonsil surgery:** Day 7-10 and 4 weeks post-surgery.
