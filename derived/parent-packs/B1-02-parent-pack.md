# Understanding Your Child's Hearing and Ear Health --- For Parents

## What are common hearing and ear problems in children?

Ear infections are one of the most common childhood illnesses. Your child might have pain, fever, or pull at their ear. After an ear infection, fluid can collect behind the eardrum -- this is very common and usually clears on its own within three months, but it can temporarily muffle hearing. Some children are born with or develop hearing loss, which can range from mild (difficulty hearing soft sounds) to profound (unable to hear speech without help). Hearing is essential for speech and language development, especially in the first three years of life. That is why we screen hearing at regular intervals -- catching hearing problems early means we can act early, and early action makes a real difference.

## What does SKIDS do about it?

We screen your child's hearing using a quick, painless device that checks inner-ear function. If the screen shows a "refer" result, we repeat it and, if needed, do more detailed hearing tests. For ear infections, we treat with appropriate medicine or, when safe, monitor to see if it clears on its own. For fluid behind the eardrum, we monitor with regular hearing tests over three months. If hearing loss is confirmed, we coordinate with our audiology and ENT specialist partners for hearing aids or further evaluation. We also safely remove ear wax when it blocks the eardrum, and counsel adolescents on protecting their hearing from loud earphone use.

## What you can do at home

- **Ear infection care:** Give pain relief as prescribed. If your child was given antibiotics, complete the full course even if they feel better.
- **Ear wax:** Do not use cotton buds inside the ear -- they push wax deeper. Wax usually comes out on its own. If it is blocking hearing, we can remove it safely at the clinic.
- **Protect hearing:** For adolescents, follow the 60/60 rule -- no more than 60% volume on earphones, for no more than 60 minutes at a time.
- **Watch for signs:** If your child frequently says "what?", turns up the TV, seems inattentive, or is not developing speech as expected, bring them in for a hearing check.
- **After ear infections:** Keep the follow-up appointment to make sure the infection has fully cleared and no fluid remains.

## When to call us

Call SKIDS immediately if your child has:
- Swelling or redness behind the ear (not just inside)
- Ear pain with high fever, stiff neck, or altered behaviour
- Sudden hearing loss in one or both ears
- Facial drooping on one side along with ear symptoms
- Foul-smelling ear discharge that does not improve with treatment

These situations need same-day or next-day attention.

## Common questions

**Q: The hearing screen was a "refer" -- does my child have hearing loss?**
A: Not necessarily. Many "refer" results turn out to be normal on repeat testing. Wax, fluid, or a fussy baby can cause a refer. We repeat the test in two weeks. If it refers again, we do more detailed testing. Try not to worry until we have the full picture.

**Q: Will my child grow out of hearing loss?**
A: Hearing loss caused by fluid behind the eardrum often resolves as the fluid clears. However, permanent hearing loss (sensorineural) does not go away on its own. The important thing is to identify it early so we can provide the right support -- hearing aids, speech therapy, and school accommodations can make an enormous difference.

**Q: Are hearing aids really necessary? He seems to hear fine at home.**
A: Children with mild hearing loss often manage well in quiet environments like home. But classrooms are noisy, and even mild hearing loss can affect learning and language development. Modern hearing aids are small, discreet, and very effective. Think of them as enabling tools, like glasses for the ears.

**Q: Warm oil drops helped last time -- can I use them again?**
A: Warm oil drops are a common home remedy and are not harmful if the eardrum is intact. However, they do not replace antibiotic treatment when the doctor has diagnosed a bacterial ear infection. For fluid behind the eardrum, no home remedy speeds up resolution -- the body clears the fluid on its own over time.

**Q: My child keeps getting ear infections. Should the tonsils come out?**
A: Recurrent ear infections (three or more in six months, or four or more in a year) may benefit from a small tube placed in the eardrum by an ENT surgeon. This is a simple procedure that reduces infection frequency and prevents hearing problems from fluid buildup. The doctor will refer when the time is right.

## What happens next

Your next visit depends on your child's condition:
- **Ear infection (treated):** We check at 48-72 hours (by phone) and again at 4 weeks to confirm resolution.
- **Fluid behind the eardrum:** We recheck hearing at 3 months. If it has cleared, no further action. If not, we discuss next steps.
- **Hearing loss (confirmed):** We monitor hearing every 3-6 months and coordinate with our specialist partners for hearing aids or further assessment.
- **Newborn hearing refer:** Follow-up within 7 days is essential. We will guide you through every step.
