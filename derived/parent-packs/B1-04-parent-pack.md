# Understanding Your Child's Oral Health --- For Parents

## What are common dental problems in children?

Dental cavities are the most common chronic disease in children worldwide. They start as tiny white spots on the teeth where minerals are being lost -- at this stage, they are completely reversible with fluoride and dietary changes. If left untreated, they progress to actual holes (cavities) that need fillings from a dentist. The biggest cause is not poor brushing alone -- it is how often your child's teeth are exposed to sugar throughout the day. Every sugary snack or drink bathes the teeth in acid. Other common concerns include teething discomfort in babies, mouth sores, oral thrush (white patches in young babies), thumb-sucking habits that can affect tooth alignment, and dental injuries from falls or sports.

## What does SKIDS do about it?

At the Oral Health Clinic, we assess your child's cavity risk, looking at their teeth, diet, brushing habits, and family history. We apply fluoride varnish -- a quick, painless protective coating painted onto the teeth that strengthens enamel and prevents cavities. This takes 30 seconds and is safe from the time the first tooth appears. We train you and your child on proper brushing technique with the right amount of fluoride toothpaste. We work with our dietitian to help reduce sugar frequency in the diet. If we find actual cavities, we refer to our children's dentist partner for treatment while continuing preventive care at our clinic. We also screen for crooked teeth and can guide you on when an orthodontic evaluation makes sense.

## What you can do at home

- **Brush twice a day** with fluoride toothpaste. For children under 3: a rice-grain-sized smear. For children 3 and above: a pea-sized amount. Parents should supervise (and help) until age 6-7.
- **Limit sugar to mealtimes only.** The issue is not whether your child ever eats sweet things -- it is how often. Sweets at meals are fine; sweets as snacks between meals are what cause cavities.
- **No bedtime bottle with milk or juice after 12 months.** If your child needs a bottle at bedtime, use water only. Milk or juice in a bedtime bottle bathes the teeth in sugar overnight -- this is the single biggest risk factor for early childhood cavities.
- **Water is the default drink** between meals. Limit juice to one small cup per day.
- **Replace the toothbrush** every 3 months or when the bristles are frayed.
- **Floss** once your child's teeth are touching each other (usually by age 2-3).

## When to call us

Call SKIDS or go to the emergency department immediately if:
- Your child knocks out a permanent tooth -- pick it up by the crown (not the root), rinse gently, try to put it back in the socket or store it in cold milk, and get to the dentist within 30-60 minutes
- Swelling of the face or around the eye from a tooth infection
- Your child cannot eat or drink due to severe mouth sores
- A tooth is pushed up into the gum or displaced sideways after a fall

For a knocked-out baby tooth: do NOT try to put it back. Clean the area gently and call us for advice.

## Common questions

**Q: Baby teeth will fall out anyway -- why bother treating them?**
A: Baby teeth hold space for the permanent teeth growing underneath. If a baby tooth is lost early to decay, the neighbouring teeth shift and there may not be enough room for the adult tooth. Untreated cavities also cause pain, infection, difficulty eating, and can damage the developing permanent tooth. Prevention now saves pain and cost later.

**Q: Is fluoride varnish safe?**
A: Yes. The amount of fluoride in the varnish is tiny -- less than what is in a cup of tea. It is painted onto the teeth in a thin coat and is not swallowed. Fluoride varnish is the single most effective way to prevent cavities and is recommended by pediatric dental associations worldwide, starting from the first tooth.

**Q: My child eats mithai and jaggery -- should I stop all sweets?**
A: You do not need to ban sweets. The key is frequency, not total amount. Sweets at mealtimes (when saliva production is high and helps protect teeth) are much less harmful than sweets as snacks throughout the day. Aim for no more than 3 sugar exposures per day, ideally at meals.

**Q: My child is terrified of dental visits. What can I do?**
A: Dental anxiety is very common. At SKIDS, we start gently -- the first visit might just be looking at and counting the teeth. Fluoride varnish is quick and painless. Over a few visits, most children become comfortable. We use child-friendly language ("tooth paint" instead of "fluoride application") and distraction techniques. If anxiety is severe, we can work with our behavioural team to help.

**Q: When should my child first see a dentist?**
A: We begin oral health checks and fluoride varnish from the first tooth eruption (around 6 months). Your child should see a children's dentist (pedodontist) by age 1, or sooner if we identify cavities or concerns.

## What happens next

Your next visit depends on your child's cavity risk:
- **Low risk:** Fluoride varnish at every routine screening visit (twice a year). Keep up the good brushing and diet habits.
- **Moderate risk:** Every 4 months for fluoride varnish and reassessment. We will work on dietary changes and brushing technique.
- **High risk:** Every 3 months for intensive prevention. If cavities are present, we refer to our children's dentist for treatment and continue prevention at our clinic.
