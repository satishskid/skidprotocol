# Understanding Your Child's Skin --- For Parents

## What are common skin problems in children?

Eczema (atopic dermatitis) is one of the most common childhood skin conditions. Your child's skin barrier does not hold moisture as well as it should, making it dry, itchy, and prone to red, inflamed patches. Eczema tends to flare and settle in cycles and often runs in families alongside asthma and allergies. Acne is very common in teenagers and is caused by hormonal changes, not by poor hygiene. Other common childhood skin problems include scabies (an itchy mite infection that spreads through close contact), impetigo (a bacterial infection causing honey-coloured crusts), ringworm (a fungal infection despite its name), and various rashes from allergies or irritants. Most of these are manageable with the right treatment and consistent care.

## What does SKIDS do about it?

At the Skin Clinic, we diagnose your child's skin condition, assess how serious it is using standardised scoring, and create a step-by-step treatment plan. For eczema, this means a generous moisturiser routine (the foundation of treatment), medicated creams for flare-ups, and identifying what triggers the flares. For acne, we use a combination of creams and, when needed, a short course of oral medicine. For skin infections like scabies or ringworm, we prescribe the right medicine for the whole family when needed. We take photographs at each visit so we can track progress together. If the condition does not respond to our treatment after a fair trial, we refer to our children's skin specialist with all your child's history and photos ready.

## What you can do at home

- **Moisturise generously:** Apply a thick, fragrance-free moisturiser (like petroleum jelly or a ceramide cream) twice a day and within 3 minutes of every bath. Cover the whole body, even areas that look clear. For moderate eczema, your child needs about 250-500 grams per month.
- **Bathing:** Keep baths short (under 10 minutes), lukewarm (not hot), with a mild soap-free cleanser. Pat dry gently -- do not rub.
- **Clothing:** Cotton next to the skin. Avoid wool and rough synthetics.
- **Medicated cream technique:** Apply a thin layer only to the red/active patches. The doctor or nurse will show you exactly how much to use (the "fingertip unit" method). The medicated cream is for flares; the moisturiser is for every day.
- **Acne care:** Use a gentle cleanser twice daily. Do not pick or squeeze spots -- that is what causes lasting marks. Apply creams as directed, and use sunscreen daily (the medicine can make skin sun-sensitive).
- **Scabies:** Treat all family members at the same time, even if they have no symptoms. Wash bedding and clothes in hot water or seal in plastic bags for 72 hours.

## When to call us

Call SKIDS immediately if your child has:
- A rash with blisters or skin peeling, especially if the mouth or eyes are also affected
- A spreading area of red, warm, painful skin with fever
- Eczema with sudden eruption of small painful blisters (punched-out appearance) and fever
- Hives (raised, itchy welts) along with difficulty breathing, swelling of lips/tongue, or feeling faint
- Any rash with unexplained bruising or purple spots

These situations need same-day attention at the hospital.

## Common questions

**Q: Is steroid cream dangerous for my child?**
A: This is a very common concern. The steroid creams we prescribe are low-to-medium strength, used only on active patches, for 7-14 days per flare. At these doses and durations, they are safe and well-studied in children. The risk of undertreating eczema (leading to worse flares, skin infections, and lost sleep) is greater than the risk of using the cream as prescribed. We name the exact medicine, the exact area to apply it, and the exact duration -- so you know precisely what you are using.

**Q: Will eczema go away as my child grows up?**
A: Many children do outgrow eczema or find it becomes much milder with age. The first few years often need the most active management. By school age, most children are on a simple maintenance routine. The moisturiser habit, however, is lifelong for children with dry, eczema-prone skin.

**Q: Does diet cause acne?**
A: There is a possible weak link between very high-sugar diets and acne, and a possible link with skim milk, but the evidence is limited. We do not recommend strict dietary restrictions for acne. Your child's nutrition comes first. The creams and medicines we prescribe are far more effective than dietary changes for acne.

**Q: Can I use neem, turmeric, or coconut oil on the skin?**
A: Many families use these, and most are not harmful. However, they do not replace the moisturiser or medicated cream for eczema or acne. If you wish to continue a home remedy, you can use it alongside the prescribed treatment -- just make sure the medicated cream goes directly on the skin, not on top of an oil layer. Let us know what you are using so we can advise.

**Q: The itching from scabies is still there after treatment. Did it not work?**
A: Itching can persist for 2-4 weeks after successful scabies treatment. This is your body's immune response to the dead mites -- it does not mean the treatment failed. We only re-treat if new burrows appear. Be patient; the itch will settle.

## What happens next

Your next visit depends on your child's condition:
- **Mild eczema:** 4 weeks to check progress, then every 3 months until stable.
- **Moderate eczema:** 2-4 weeks, then every 6-8 weeks until controlled.
- **Severe eczema:** 2 weeks, then every 4 weeks. If not improving after 4 weeks of our best treatment, we refer to the skin specialist.
- **Acne:** 6-12 weeks to assess. Improvement takes 8 weeks -- do not stop the treatment before then.
- **Skin infections:** Day 7 check (often by phone or photo). Complete the full course of medicine.

At each visit, we re-score severity, review your photos, and adjust the treatment plan.
