# Referral Trigger Discipline

§7 of every protocol is sacred. This document explains why, and what
makes a §7 trigger acceptable versus unacceptable.

---

## Why §7 carries the weight it does

§5 (Severity grading) and §7 (Hard referral triggers) are the two
sections of every SKIDS protocol that carry medico-legal weight. §5
defines what severity looks like; §7 defines what is out of scope.

Together, these two sections answer the question every pediatrician
asks at every visit:

> "Can I manage this in this clinic, or does this child need someone
> with capabilities I do not have?"

The protocol's job is to make that question answerable without
judgement calls under pressure. The doctor reads the §5 severity
grade, looks at the §7 checklist, and gets a binary answer. There is
no middle ground, no "consider referring," no "use clinical judgement."

---

## The cost of a vague §7

A vague §7 fails in one of two directions, both bad.

**Failure mode 1 — Under-referral.** The trigger says "refer if not
improving." A junior pediatrician interprets this generously, keeps
trying topical regimens for 8 weeks, and the child develops complications
that timely referral would have prevented. Patient harm. Defensibility:
none.

**Failure mode 2 — Over-referral.** The trigger says "refer if uncertain."
A junior pediatrician interprets this defensively, refers everything,
and SKIDS becomes a triage centre that adds no value. Commercial harm.
The platform thesis collapses.

A specific, threshold-based §7 prevents both failure modes by removing
judgement from the moment of decision.

## What makes a §7 trigger acceptable

### 1. Specific, not abstract

| Acceptable | Unacceptable |
|---|---|
| "SCORAD ≥40 after 4 weeks of mid-potency steroid" | "Severe eczema not improving" |
| "GINA step ≥4 with persistent symptoms" | "Refractory asthma" |
| "PSQ score >0.33 OR overnight oximetry desat <90% for >5%" | "Suspected significant OSA" |
| "Hb <7 g/dL OR signs of haemodynamic compromise" | "Severe anaemia" |

If the trigger uses an adjective that requires interpretation, replace
the adjective with a number or named criterion.

### 2. Time-bound

Every trigger specifies urgency:

```
Emergency (same day)        Life-threatening or rapidly progressive
Urgent (within N days)      Serious but not immediately threatening
Routine (next available)    Important but stable
```

A trigger without time-bounding is incomplete. The doctor needs to know
not just whether to refer but how fast.

### 3. Destination-named

Every trigger names the receiving clinician or partner by role (and the
SKIDS clinic operations document maps role → specific named partner).
"Refer to a specialist" is not acceptable. "Refer to the named partner
pediatric ENT surgeon for consideration of adenoidectomy" is.

### 4. Handoff-specified

Every trigger specifies what the SKIDS pediatrician sends with the
referral. This is what makes the receiving clinician's life easy and
keeps SKIDS in the long-term care relationship. The handoff packet is
auto-generated by Companion when the trigger fires.

Standard handoff elements:
- The severity score with all underlying findings
- A complete history of treatments tried and their outcomes
- Current medications and doses
- Any imaging or labs done
- The reason for referral, in clinical terms
- Parent contact details and consent confirmation

### 5. Non-negotiable

A §7 trigger is not advisory. It is a hard rule. When the trigger fires,
the case is out of the SKIDS pediatrician's scope, and the referral is
made.

The Companion Doctor Module enforces this: when a trigger fires
mid-consult, the §6 therapeutic plan flow is paused, and the doctor
must acknowledge the referral has been initiated before resuming.

---

## When a junior doctor wants to override §7

There is a structured override path. It is deliberately friction-heavy
because most overrides are bad ideas.

To override a §7 trigger:
1. The junior doctor escalates to the SKIDS clinic's senior pediatrician
   (or the named protocol owner) by phone or Companion alert
2. The senior reviews the case and either confirms or overrides the
   junior's instinct
3. If override approved, the senior takes documented co-responsibility
   for the case
4. The override is logged in §12 quarterly review

A junior doctor cannot override a §7 trigger alone. This is not
bureaucratic — it is what makes the platform defensible.

## §7 vs. §5 vs. §6.X complex tier

These three sections form a layered escalation:

```
Mild severity         → §6.1 Light tier intervention      (in-clinic, allied-delivered)
Moderate severity     → §6.2 Standard tier intervention   (in-clinic, ped-delivered)
Severe severity       → §6.3 Intensive tier intervention  (in-clinic, intensive-delivered)
Beyond severe         → §7 Hard referral                  (out of clinic scope)
```

The boundary between §6.3 (Intensive in-clinic) and §7 (refer out) is
the most important boundary in the protocol. It is where the SKIDS
pediatrician's scope ends and the credentialed subspecialist's scope
begins.

The protocol author must define this boundary with the same precision
they would use to define the boundary between negligence and standard
of care — because that is exactly what it is.

---

## Format of §7 in the protocol

Use this structure exactly. Three urgency tiers + handoff specification.

```markdown
## §7 Hard referral triggers [SACRED SECTION]

The case escalates out of this protocol's scope when ANY of the
following is true:

### 7.1 Emergency referral (same day)

- [ ] [Specific finding] → [Named partner / hospital] within [time]
- [ ] [Specific finding] → [Named partner / hospital] within [time]

### 7.2 Urgent referral (within N days)

- [ ] [Specific finding] → [Named partner / subspecialist] within [N days]
- [ ] [Specific finding] → [Named partner / subspecialist] within [N days]

### 7.3 Routine referral (next available appointment)

- [ ] [Specific finding] → [Named partner / subspecialist]
- [ ] [Specific finding] → [Named partner / subspecialist]

### 7.4 Handoff communication

When referring, the SKIDS pediatrician must send the receiving
clinician:
1. [Specific clinical data point]
2. [Specific clinical data point]
3. [Specific clinical data point]

This packet is auto-generated by Companion when the §7 trigger fires.
```

## Editorial review of §7

A §7 passes editorial review only if:

- [ ] Every trigger is specific (numeric or named-criterion based)
- [ ] Every trigger is time-bound (emergency / urgent / routine)
- [ ] Every trigger names the receiving clinician by role
- [ ] The handoff packet is fully specified
- [ ] No trigger uses an undefined adjective ("severe," "refractory," "complex")
- [ ] The boundary between §6.3 Intensive and §7 referral is unambiguous
- [ ] The named protocol owner has personally signed off on §7

The last item is non-negotiable. §7 is the section that defines the
SKIDS pediatrician's scope, and it must be signed off by a credentialed
subspecialist who is willing to defend that scope in front of a medical
council.

---

## A worked counter-example

Below is a §7 that would fail editorial review, with reasons.

```
### 7.1 Emergency referral

- Refer if the child looks very unwell.
- Refer if you're worried about something serious.

### 7.2 Urgent referral

- Refer if not improving.
- Refer if the diagnosis is unclear.
- Refer if the parents are concerned.
```

Every line fails:

- "Looks very unwell" → not specific. Replace with named criteria
  (e.g., toxic appearance, signs of dehydration grade 3+, RR above
  age-percentile threshold).
- "Worried about something serious" → not actionable. Replace with the
  specific things to be worried about.
- "Not improving" → not time-bound or threshold-bound. Replace with
  specific failure-of-response criterion (e.g., SCORAD reduction <30%
  after 4 weeks of optimised therapy).
- "Diagnosis is unclear" → judgement call. Replace with specific
  unexplained findings (e.g., persistent rash with systemic symptoms,
  weight loss without identified cause).
- "Parents are concerned" → conflates parent anxiety with clinical
  necessity. Parent concern triggers a parent-communication response
  per §10, not necessarily a §7 referral.

The protocol author rewrites every line with a specific, time-bound,
destination-named, handoff-specified trigger. The editorial reviewer
signs off only when this is true for every line.

---

## The bar, restated

> Every §7 trigger should be specific enough that two different
> SKIDS pediatricians, given the same patient, would make the same
> referral decision. If they would not, the trigger is not yet ready.

This is the bar. Every protocol's §7 must clear it.
