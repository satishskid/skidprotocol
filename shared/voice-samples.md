# Voice Samples

Concrete examples of the SKIDS protocol voice — and of the voices it
deliberately is not. Use as reference when you can't tell whether a
draft sentence is in voice.

---

## The voice, restated

Senior pediatrician writing for a junior pediatrician. Confident,
specific, no padding, no marketing, no exclamation marks. Decisions
over discussions. Numbers over adjectives. Active voice.

---

## Voice in action — good examples

### From a §4 diagnostic workflow

> Examine the tympanic membrane with pneumatic otoscopy. A cloudy or
> opaque membrane with reduced mobility supports the diagnosis of acute
> otitis media. Bulging is more specific than redness alone — erythema
> can occur with crying or fever in a normal ear [AAP-2013-AOM].

This works because: imperative voice, named technique, specific
findings, distinguishes diagnostic confounders, cites at point of claim.

### From a §6 therapeutic options

> Apply mometasone 0.1% cream once daily to active eczema patches for
> 7–14 days. Step down to emollient-only maintenance once flares clear
> (SCORAD <15). Reassess at 4 weeks. If SCORAD remains ≥40 at 4 weeks
> with documented adherence, escalate to §7 referral [AAD-2014-AD].

This works because: specific drug, dose, frequency, duration; explicit
step-down trigger; explicit step-up trigger linked to §7; cited.

### From a §7 hard referral trigger

> Refer to pediatric ENT for adenoidectomy consideration if all of the
> following are present: parent-reported habitual snoring ≥3 nights/week,
> witnessed apnoeic pauses, OR PSQ score >0.33 with overnight oximetry
> showing desaturation <90% for >5% of recording time. Refer within 14
> days. Send the ENT surgeon: PSQ scoresheet, oximetry trace, ENT exam
> findings, current weight percentile [AAP-2012-OSA].

This works because: specific multi-criterion trigger, named
subspecialist destination, time-bound, handoff packet specified.

### From a §10 parent-facing communication

> Aanya has eczema. Her skin barrier doesn't hold moisture well, which
> makes it itchy and red. We're going to do two things: a small amount
> of medicated cream once a day for two weeks to calm the active
> patches, and a daily moisturiser to rebuild her skin barrier so flares
> get less frequent. We'll see her back in four weeks. Most children at
> this stage are noticeably better by then.

This works because: child named, plain English, no jargon, two clear
actions, expected timeline, sets reasonable expectation without
over-promising.

## Voice violations — examples to avoid

### Marketing register

**Don't write:**
> Our comprehensive, evidence-based approach to pediatric dermatology
> delivers world-class outcomes through customised therapeutic
> protocols tailored to each child's unique skin care journey.

**Why it fails:** Marketing language ("world-class," "comprehensive,"
"unique journey"), no specific clinical content, no citations.

**In voice:**
> The SKIDS Skin Clinic manages atopic dermatitis, acne, infectious
> dermatoses, and reactive dermatoses up to severe intensity tier per
> §6.3, with §7 escalation for steroid-resistant cases.

### Over-hedging

**Don't write:**
> Topical corticosteroids are generally considered to be one of the
> options that may be beneficial in many cases of pediatric atopic
> dermatitis, depending on severity and other clinical factors.

**Why it fails:** "Generally considered to be one of the options"
hedges what AAD already states unambiguously. The hedging serves no
clinical purpose.

**In voice:**
> Mid-potency topical corticosteroid (mometasone 0.1% cream) once daily
> is first-line for active flares of moderate atopic dermatitis
> [AAD-2014-AD].

### Over-explaining

**Don't write:**
> It is important to remember that the diagnosis of acute otitis media
> can sometimes be challenging in young children, and clinicians should
> always take care to assess the tympanic membrane carefully because the
> appearance of the membrane is critical to making the correct diagnosis.

**Why it fails:** "It is important to remember," "should always take
care," "the appearance of the membrane is critical" — all true, none
specific. Padding around a single useful instruction.

**In voice:**
> Examine the tympanic membrane with pneumatic otoscopy. Bulging is more
> specific than redness alone [AAP-2013-AOM].

### Soft register

**Don't write:**
> Hi parent! So your little one's skin is being a bit cranky right now
> 😊 Don't worry, this is super common and we'll get it sorted in no
> time! Just put a little of this cream on whenever it looks bad, okay?

**Why it fails:** Emojis, exclamation marks, infantilising tone, vague
instruction ("a little," "whenever it looks bad").

**In voice:**
> Aanya has eczema. We're going to use a small amount of medicated cream
> once a day for two weeks to calm the active patches, and a daily
> moisturiser to keep her skin barrier strong. Most children are
> noticeably better by the four-week visit.

### Vague referral trigger

**Don't write:**
> Refer to a specialist if you're worried about something serious or
> if the child isn't getting better with standard treatment.

**Why it fails:** "Worried," "something serious," "standard treatment"
— all undefined. Two different SKIDS pediatricians would interpret
this differently.

**In voice:**
> Refer to pediatric dermatology within 4 weeks if SCORAD remains ≥40
> after a documented trial of mid-potency topical steroid plus daily
> emollient plus identified-trigger avoidance, OR if there are signs
> of secondary bacterial infection (yellow crusts, warmth, lymphadenopathy)
> [AAD-2014-AD].

### Adjective-driven dosing

**Don't write:**
> Treat with appropriate antibiotics at standard pediatric doses for
> a sufficient duration.

**Why it fails:** Three undefined adjectives in one sentence
("appropriate," "standard," "sufficient"). This is not a clinical
instruction.

**In voice:**
> Amoxicillin 80–90 mg/kg/day PO in 2 divided doses × 7 days for
> children <2 years; × 5 days for children ≥2 years [AAP-2013-AOM].

## When you can't tell

If you're unsure whether a draft sentence is in voice, apply this test:

1. **The "junior pediatrician test":** Would a competent first-year
   SKIDS pediatrician find this sentence specific and actionable?
2. **The "medical council test":** If a case based on this sentence
   went wrong, could you defend the recommendation in front of a
   medical council using the citation provided?
3. **The "padding test":** Could you delete the first 5 words without
   losing clinical content? If yes, delete them.
4. **The "adjective test":** Are there clinical adjectives that should
   be replaced by numbers or named criteria?

If a draft sentence fails any of these, rewrite.

## When the senior pediatrician voice is wrong

Three specific places in every protocol where the voice shifts:

**§10 Parent-facing communication** — write at parent reading level,
not pediatrician reading level. Plain English, no jargon, child named,
warm but professional. Still specific (no "soon," no "many"; instead
"in four weeks," "most children").

**§11 Manager handoff (objection responses)** — write at SKIDS clinic
manager reading level. Conversational, confident, anchored in clinical
facts. Not chatty.

**§9 Companion software workflow** — write at engineering team reading
level. Data-types, field-precise, screen-precise. Not warm, not
narrative; technical specification.

These shifts are intentional. The protocol document serves four
audiences and the voice has to match each one in the right section.
