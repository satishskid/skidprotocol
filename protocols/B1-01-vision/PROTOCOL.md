# SKIDS Vision Clinic

> The SKIDS Vision Clinic confirms refractive errors flagged at A1
> screening, dispenses spectacles, runs the myopia-control programme,
> manages mild conjunctivitis and digital eye strain, and routes
> strabismus, amblyopia, leukocoria, and other structural / surgical
> ophthalmic findings to the named pediatric ophthalmology partner.

---

## §1 Service definition

**What this clinic does:**
- Confirms A1's Welch Allyn Spot Vision refer-result with on-site autorefraction and manifest refraction by the SKIDS optometrist; issues age-appropriate visual-acuity testing (LEA symbols / HOTV / Snellen / LogMAR).
- Dispenses spectacles for confirmed refractive errors per the SKIDS frame-and-lens catalogue.
- Operates the SKIDS pediatric myopia-control programme — counselling, low-dose atropine 0.01% / 0.05% eye drops, behavioural intervention (outdoor time targets, near-work hygiene), and progression surveillance [IMI-2021].
- Manages mild allergic conjunctivitis (intermittent and seasonal patterns) with topical antihistamine / mast-cell stabiliser drops [AAO-Conj-2018].
- Manages uncomplicated bacterial conjunctivitis with topical antibiotic where indicated [AAP-Conj-2013].
- Manages digital eye strain / convergence insufficiency with the 20-20-20 protocol, near-work counselling, and lubricating drops; routes refractory cases to pediatric ophthalmology / optometric vision therapy.
- Performs Ishihara colour-vision testing once between ages 8 and 12 and at adolescent transition for vocational counselling.
- Issues sports-clearance vision certificates for school and competitive sport programmes.
- Surveils benign external findings (chalazion, hordeolum, mild blepharitis, isolated subconjunctival haemorrhage in non-trauma context) within Tier 1 scope.

**What this clinic does not do:**
- Manage strabismus, amblyopia (beyond detection), congenital cataract, retinoblastoma, retinopathy of prematurity, or any retinal disease — these are §7.
- Perform any surgical or laser intervention.
- Initiate atropine concentrations above 0.05% (higher concentrations are pediatric ophthalmology decision).
- Manage uveitis (cross-references B2-03 Rheumatology; routes per §7).
- Manage acute traumatic eye injury (§7.1 emergency to pediatric ophthalmology / pediatric ER).
- Diagnose glaucoma, optic-nerve disease, or visual-field defects beyond initial screening.

**Age range covered:** 0 – 18 years. Specific age-band considerations:
- 0 – 3 years: red-reflex examination at every A1 well-visit; instrument-based screening (Welch Allyn Spot) from 12 months; B1-01 reviews any A1 refer-result with optometric input.
- 3 – 5 years: introduces age-appropriate vision charts (LEA / HOTV); critical period for amblyopia detection and treatment.
- 5 – 18 years: Snellen / LogMAR; refractive error prevalence rises sharply in school-age (myopia surge); myopia-control programme is the highest-volume B1-01 intervention.

**SKIDS subscription tier(s):**
- **Basic ₹2,999** — A1 routing here is supported. First B1-01 visit + spectacle dispensing on per-visit pricing. Lens replacement for prescription change at out-of-pocket rate.
- **Advanced ₹6,999** — up to 4 B1-01 visits per year included; one spectacle replacement per year for prescription change covered. Suitable for myopia-control programme participants and stable refractive-error users.
- **Premium ₹12,999** — unlimited B1-01 visits within the clinically indicated set; two spectacle replacements per year covered; priority booking for §7 ophthalmology partner referral. Suitable for progressive myopia (>0.50 D/year) and any child also under §7 partner ophthalmology surveillance.

**SKIDS clinical tier:** Tier 1, with a key allied-staff dependence — the SKIDS optometrist is integral to B1-01 operations. Without an in-clinic optometrist, B1-01 cannot operate at full capability and degrades to a routing-only role (A1 refers any vision Orange directly to the SKIDS-T3 pediatric optometrist or pediatric ophthalmologist). Protocol governance sits with the named pediatric ophthalmologist or developmental-behavioural pediatrician with vision-screening interest identified in §2 (assignment pending at editorial pass). Complex tier per §7 escalates to Tier 3 partner pediatric ophthalmology (strabismus surgery, cataract, retinal disease, glaucoma).

## §2 Evidence base

This protocol governs primary-care management of pediatric vision problems at SKIDS clinics. It follows the most current recommendations from:

- **[AAP-AAO-2016-Vision]** — Donahue SP, Baker CN; AAP Section on Ophthalmology; American Academy of Ophthalmology; American Association for Pediatric Ophthalmology and Strabismus; American Association of Certified Orthoptists. Procedures for the Evaluation of the Visual System by Pediatricians. *Pediatrics.* 2016;137(1):e20153597. The governing source for age-banded vision screening and the indications for ophthalmology referral.
- **[AAPOS-Vision-2024]** — American Association for Pediatric Ophthalmology and Strabismus. Vision Screening Recommendations, current version. Used for instrument-based screening cutoffs and age-appropriate chart selection.
- **[IMI-2021]** — International Myopia Institute. Clinical Management Guidelines (2021 white papers, updated annually). The governing source for myopia classification, progression criteria, and management options including low-dose atropine.
- **[AAO-Conj-2018]** — American Academy of Ophthalmology. Conjunctivitis Preferred Practice Pattern, 2018. Governing source for differential diagnosis and management of allergic, bacterial, and viral conjunctivitis.
- **[AAP-Conj-2013]** — American Academy of Pediatrics. Red book / clinical reports on neonatal conjunctivitis and pediatric conjunctivitis. Used alongside AAO-Conj for the pediatric-specific overlay.
- **[IAP-Vision-2022]** — Indian Academy of Pediatrics, Section on Ophthalmology. Position statement on pediatric vision screening in the Indian context, 2022. Used in preference to AAP-AAO where the two diverge on Indian-context epidemiology (high myopia prevalence rise, vitamin A deficiency screening). **Departures from cited guidelines:**

1. *Universal myopia-control offer to all confirmed myopes ≥ 6 years.* AAPOS endorses myopia control selectively (progressive cases). IMI-2021 supports earlier intervention. India's pediatric myopia prevalence has risen sharply over the last decade and its myopia onset is younger than US/Western data. B1-01 offers myopia-control counselling and behavioural intervention to all confirmed myopes ≥ 6 years; low-dose atropine is offered to children with documented progression > 0.50 D/year OR myopia onset before age 10. This is a more inclusive offer than AAPOS framing, defended on Indian-context epidemiology and the strong IMI-2021 evidence base [IMI-2021].

2. *Spectacles dispensed in-clinic.* Most pediatric ophthalmology clinics refer to optical-shop dispensing. SKIDS keeps a frame-and-lens catalogue in-clinic and dispenses on the same visit. This reduces the dropout between prescription and use (substantial in Indian out-of-pocket cost-sensitive practice) and is operational rather than clinical [NEEDS EXPANSION IN EDITORIAL PASS — confirm in-clinic dispensing scope and the SKU catalogue with operations].

3. *Bacterial conjunctivitis treatment threshold.* AAO recommends clinical judgement; many cases are viral and self-limited. B1-01 follows AAO with a slight conservatism: empirical topical antibiotic only for purulent discharge present > 48 hours, OR for any neonatal conjunctivitis (where infectious causes including N. gonorrhoeae and Chlamydia mandate treatment and §7 routing) [AAO-Conj-2018, AAP-Conj-2013].

4. *Atropine concentration ceiling at 0.05% in B1-01.* Higher concentrations (0.1%, 1%) are clinically reasonable for some myopia-control regimens but require pediatric ophthalmology supervision per IMI-2021. B1-01 caps at 0.05%; cases needing stronger concentrations route to §7.3.

No other material departures.

## §3 Presenting concerns

A child arrives at SKIDS B1-01 Vision via one of these paths:

| Parent language | Clinical translation | Routing |
|---|---|---|
| "She's squinting at the TV / can't see the board." | Suspected myopia in a school-age child. | §4 acuity + refraction pathway. |
| "He holds books very close to his face." | Suspected myopia or accommodative dysfunction. | §4 acuity + refraction pathway. |
| "Her eyes look crossed sometimes." | Suspected strabismus — esotropia or exotropia. | §4 strabismus pathway → §7.3 routine to pediatric ophthalmology. |
| "His eye drifts outward when he's tired." | Intermittent exotropia. | §4 strabismus pathway → §7.3. |
| "We failed the school vision screening." | NPCB / RBSK / school-screening referral. | §4 full acuity + refraction; reconcile with school-screening result. |
| "The Welch Allyn at our last A1 visit said refer." | A1 device refer-result. | §4 refraction confirmation pathway. |
| "She rubs her eyes constantly during pollen season." | Allergic conjunctivitis. | §4 anterior-segment pathway → §6 antihistamine drops. |
| "His eye has been red and sticky for two days." | Conjunctivitis (bacterial / viral / allergic differential). | §4 anterior-segment pathway. |
| "He says his eyes hurt after using the tablet." | Digital eye strain / convergence insufficiency. | §4 acuity + accommodation pathway → §6 behavioural + lubricants. |
| "Her glasses are getting stronger every year." | Progressive myopia. | §4 myopia surveillance + IMI-2021 progression assessment. |
| "He has a white spot in the photo flash of his eye." | Possible leukocoria — retinoblastoma, congenital cataract, Coats disease. | §7.1 emergency. |
| "She got hit in the eye and now can't see well." | Acute traumatic eye injury. | §7.1 emergency. |
| "His eyelid has a painful swelling." | Hordeolum / chalazion / preseptal vs. orbital cellulitis. | §4 anterior-segment + lid pathway → §7 if orbital signs. |
| "She squints in bright light or has unequal pupils." | Possible photophobia (uveitis), anisocoria (Horner / third-nerve palsy). | §7 routing per finding. |
| "Doctor said his eye doesn't track properly." | Suspected nystagmus / cranial nerve palsy. | §7.2 urgent. |
| "Vocational guidance says he needs a colour-vision test." | Routine Ishihara screening. | §4 colour-vision pathway. |

**Routing decisions:**
- A1 Orange vision flag → B1-01 within 4 weeks (per A1 §6.3 routing matrix).
- B1-01 confirms or refutes the A1 refer-result; if refractive only, manages within Tier 1; if structural / surgical / amblyopia / strabismus indicated, routes per §7.
- Acute traumatic eye injury and suspected leukocoria → §7.1 immediately at presentation; B1-01 does not commence non-emergency workup.
- Progressive myopia not previously assessed → full §4 + IMI-2021 progression workup at presentation; offer myopia-control programme per §6.

**Coming from SKIDS Screening (A1):**
- A1 §5.2 Vision domain Orange flag (any Welch Allyn refer-result, parent-reported visual concern, school-screening referral) routes here.
- A1 §5.5 Lesion-surveillance Orange that involves the eye (e.g., café-au-lait macule on the eyelid, infantile hemangioma in periocular zone — these may also be jointly cared with B1-05 Skin) routes here for the ophthalmic dimension.

## §4 Diagnostic workflow

### 4.1 Structured history

Ask, in this order:

1. *Onset and duration.* When did the parent / child first notice the problem? Is it constant or intermittent? Triggers (fatigue, illness, near-work, bright light)?
2. *Visual symptoms.* Blurred vision (distance vs. near), double vision, eye strain, headache (frontal, peri-orbital, after near-work), photophobia, watering, itching, foreign-body sensation, red eye.
3. *School performance.* Position in class, board-reading difficulty, copying-from-board difficulty, change in academic performance (often a marker of uncorrected refractive error).
4. *Near-work and screen time.* Hours per day of near work; reading distance; screen position; outdoor time per day. The IMI-2021 risk profile uses these explicitly.
5. *Prior optical correction.* Previous spectacles, age of first prescription, current prescription (bring prior records / photos of last prescription card), compliance with wear, age of current spectacles.
6. *Atropine therapy history.* Any prior pediatric atropine use for any indication; tolerance and side effects.
7. *Family history.* Parental and sibling refractive errors (myopia heritability is strong); strabismus / amblyopia history; congenital eye disease; retinoblastoma in family.
8. *Birth history.* Premature birth (ROP screen?); birth weight; oxygen therapy; congenital infections (CRS, CMV).
9. *General medical history.* Atopic disease (allergic conjunctivitis association); juvenile arthritis (uveitis screen — link with B2-03 Rheumatology); diabetes (rare in pediatric, but retinopathy implication).
10. *Trauma.* Any prior eye injury; current trauma (ask explicitly even if not the presenting complaint).

**Red flags to elicit at history (any presentation):**
- Sudden vision loss (any timeframe).
- White pupil reflex on photographs (leukocoria).
- Eye pain with vomiting (raised IOP — congenital glaucoma, acute angle closure rare).
- Diplopia (binocular — neurological; monocular — refractive / cataract).
- Headache pattern suggestive of intracranial pathology (early morning, vomiting, focal neurology).
- Prior radiation / chemotherapy (secondary cataract, radiation retinopathy).

### 4.2 Examination

Examine for, in this order:

- *External inspection.* Eyelid position (ptosis), eyelid margins (blepharitis), lashes (trichiasis), periorbital region (cellulitis, lesions), facial symmetry. Document head tilt or chin lift (compensatory posture for ocular misalignment or ptosis).
- *Pupil examination.* Size (anisocoria), shape, direct and consensual reflex, RAPD (relative afferent pupillary defect — swinging-flashlight test).
- *Red reflex.* Both eyes simultaneously through the direct ophthalmoscope at arm's length, in a darkened room. Asymmetry (Brückner test) is sensitive for amblyogenic conditions including refractive error, cataract, strabismus, retinoblastoma. ANY abnormal red reflex (white, dim, asymmetric) → §7.1 if leukocoria suspected, §7.2 if other [AAP-AAO-2016-Vision].
- *Ocular motility.* Cardinal positions of gaze (six positions); cover-uncover test; alternate cover test; near-point of convergence (NPC); accommodation (range and speed where age permits).
- *Visual acuity.*
  - 0–3 years: fixation behaviour ("fix and follow"), occlusion tolerance (uniocular occlusion test), instrument-based.
  - 3–5 years: LEA symbols or HOTV chart at 3 m or 10 ft.
  - ≥ 5 years: Snellen or LogMAR at 6 m or 20 ft.
  - Always test each eye separately with the other occluded (occlusion paddle, not parental hand).
  - Document at distance and at near.
- *Stereoacuity* with age-appropriate tool (Lang stereotest, Titmus, Randot) where amblyopia or strabismus is suspected.
- *Hirschberg light reflex* (corneal light reflex on penlight); confirms gross alignment.
- *Slit-lamp / direct ophthalmoscope* anterior-segment inspection: cornea (clarity, foreign body, abrasion under fluorescein where available), conjunctiva (injection pattern — diffuse conjunctival vs. ciliary), anterior chamber (depth, cells if uveitis suspected), iris.
- *Fundoscopy.* Direct ophthalmoscopy through undilated pupil to visualise optic disc and posterior pole. Mydriatic dilation is not routine at B1-01 (specialist decision); a clear undilated optic-disc view is enough to screen for gross optic-nerve abnormality. Inability to obtain a view of the disc in either eye after reasonable attempt is itself an indication for §7.3 routine pedo-ophthal review.

### 4.3 Scales and questionnaires

| Scale / measurement | Domain | Age | Cutoff |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Visual acuity (LogMAR)** | Acuity | 5–18 | Refer thresholds per AAP-AAO-2016: 5y < 20/40; 6y < 20/40; ≥ 7y < 20/30; or 2-line difference between eyes [AAP-AAO-2016-Vision] |
| **LEA / HOTV** | Acuity | 3–5 | Refer if < 20/40 (4y) or < 20/30 (5y); 2-line difference between eyes |
| **Welch Allyn Spot Vision Screener** | Refractive + alignment | 1–18 | Manufacturer "refer" output |
| **Snellen / LogMAR with pinhole** | Differentiate refractive vs. non-refractive | ≥ 5 | Pinhole improvement supports refractive cause; lack of improvement supports structural / amblyopia |
| **Stereoacuity (Lang / Randot)** | Binocularity | ≥ 3 | < 100 arc-sec adequate; > 200 arc-sec or absent → ophthalmology workup |
| **Cover test** | Strabismus | ≥ 3 | Any manifest tropia → §7.3 routine; latent phoria within normal limits |
| **Hirschberg** | Gross strabismus | any age | Asymmetry of corneal reflex → §7.3 |
| **Ishihara plates (24-plate test)** | Colour vision | ≥ 8 | ≥ 4 plates incorrect → colour-vision deficiency; counsel + vocational implication |
| **Convergence Insufficiency Symptom Survey (CISS)** | Convergence insufficiency | ≥ 9 | Score ≥ 16 + clinical findings → §6 vision-therapy management or §7.3 routing |
| **Dry eye / digital eye strain symptom checklist** (CVS-Q or local) | Surface disease | ≥ 8 | Scored per instrument; refer if no improvement after 4 weeks of §6 management |

### 4.4 In-clinic device tests

Required:
- *Welch Allyn Spot Vision Screener.* Already deployed at A1; B1-01 re-runs at presentation if A1 result is ≥ 4 weeks old or if findings shift.
- *Autorefractor (handheld or table-top pediatric).* For all refractive-error confirmations. The optometrist conducts this, not the pediatrician.
- *Visual acuity charts.* LEA, HOTV, Snellen, LogMAR — distance and near; 3 m or 6 m as appropriate.
- *Penlight* for Hirschberg, pupil exam, gross external.
- *Direct ophthalmoscope.* For red reflex and undilated fundus exam.
- *Fluorescein strips + cobalt-blue light* where corneal abrasion or foreign body is suspected.
- *Stereotest* (Lang or Randot) for binocularity assessment.
- *Ishihara plates* for colour vision.
- *Tonometer (handheld iCare or equivalent)* — optional; for any IOP-elevation suspicion or as part of routine surveillance in juvenile arthritis (B2-03 cross-care).

Optional / on-indication:
- *Slit lamp* if available — substantially improves anterior-segment assessment.
- *Lensometer* — to read prior-prescription spectacles brought by the family.

### 4.5 Laboratory and imaging

B1-01 is not a laboratory-heavy clinic. Investigations are limited:
- *Conjunctival swab and culture* — for neonatal conjunctivitis (suspected gonococcal / chlamydial — §7 routing) or for any bacterial conjunctivitis not responding to empirical first-line topical antibiotic at 7 days.
- *FBC + ESR / CRP* — only if orbital cellulitis suspected (rare; routes §7.1 immediately).
- *Imaging.* B1-01 does not order imaging. Suspected orbital cellulitis, suspected intracranial cause of papilloedema or cranial nerve palsy → §7.1 to pediatric ER for CT / MRI as the receiving clinician decides.

Do not order routinely:
- Routine HbA1c or fasting glucose for vision changes — diabetic retinopathy is rare in pediatric type 1 diabetes within the typical B1-01 catchment age and is screened in B2-01 Endocrine.
- Routine vitamin A levels. IAP recommends supplementation rather than universal screening; B1-09 Nutrition manages vitamin A status.
- Genetic testing for inherited retinal dystrophy. Routes to §7.3 to pediatric ophthalmology / clinical genetics.

## §5 Severity grading [SACRED SECTION]

B1-01 grades severity per condition. Refractive error and myopia carry the largest case volume; strabismus, amblyopia, and structural disease are diagnostic-not-grading and route per §7. Conjunctivitis and digital eye strain have their own gradings.

### 5.1 Refractive error and myopia

| Severity | Threshold | Intervention tier | Subscription tier match |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mild refractive error** | < ±1.50 D spherical equivalent, no symptoms, acuity within age-appropriate cutoff | §6.1 Light: counselling, no prescription; surveillance | Basic ₹2,999 |
| **Moderate refractive error** | ±1.50 D to ±5.00 D OR symptomatic at any power; acuity below age cutoff; pinhole improves | §6.2 Standard: spectacles dispensed; annual review | Advanced ₹6,999 |
| **High refractive error** | > ±5.00 D spherical equivalent, OR > 2.00 D astigmatism, OR > 1.50 D anisometropia | §6.3 Intensive: spectacles + 6-monthly review; consider §7.3 routine to pediatric ophthalmology for retinal-risk surveillance (especially high myopia > –6.00 D) | Premium ₹12,999 |
| **Progressive myopia** | Documented progression > 0.50 D / year OR onset < 10 years | §6.3 Intensive: myopia-control programme — counselling + behavioural + low-dose atropine 0.01% (escalate to 0.05% if progression continues); 4-monthly review [IMI-2021] | Advanced or Premium |
| **Refractive change unexpected for age** OR **myopia onset < 6 years** OR **high myopia in young child (> –6.00 D before age 10)** | — | §7.3 routine to pediatric ophthalmology (retinal-risk surveillance, syndromic screen) | — |

### 5.2 Allergic conjunctivitis

| Severity | Findings | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Intermittent itching, mild redness, mild watering, no photophobia, no impact on daily activity | §6.1 Light: lubricants + cold compresses + trigger avoidance |
| **Moderate** | Daily symptoms, conjunctival oedema, papillary reaction, sleep disruption | §6.2 Standard: topical antihistamine / mast-cell stabiliser drops [AAO-Conj-2018] |
| **Severe / atypical** | Vernal keratoconjunctivitis (cobblestone tarsal papillae, shield ulcers), giant papillary conjunctivitis, atopic keratoconjunctivitis | §7.3 routine to pediatric ophthalmology |

### 5.3 Infectious conjunctivitis

| Pattern | Intervention tier |
|---|---|
| **Viral (adenoviral most common)** — watery, follicular, often unilateral spreading bilateral, preauricular node | §6.1 Light: lubricants + hygiene counselling; self-limited at 7–14 days; isolation counselling for school. Does not need topical antibiotic. |
| **Bacterial uncomplicated** — purulent discharge, lid sticking on waking, bilateral or unilateral; > 48 hours duration | §6.2 Standard: topical antibiotic (chloramphenicol or moxifloxacin) [AAO-Conj-2018, AAP-Conj-2013] |
| **Hyperacute / suspected gonococcal** (any age, especially neonate) — copious purulent discharge, marked lid swelling, rapid onset | §7.1 Emergency to pediatric ophthalmology / pediatric ER; never managed in B1-01 |
| **Neonatal (any presentation in first 28 days)** — chemical (silver nitrate; rare now), gonococcal, chlamydial, herpetic | §7.1 / §7.2 routing to pediatric ophthalmology + neonatology; never managed in B1-01 alone |
| **Suspected herpetic / dendritic** — fluorescein-positive dendritic ulcer | §7.1 to pediatric ophthalmology; never empirical topical steroid |

### 5.4 Strabismus and amblyopia

These do not have a Mild / Moderate / Severe grading at B1-01. Detection at B1-01 routes per §7:

| Finding | Action |
|---|---|
| **Constant manifest tropia at any age** | §7.3 routine to pediatric ophthalmology, within 4 weeks |
| **Intermittent tropia, > 4 months age** | §7.3 routine, within 4 weeks (most pediatric ophthalmology programmes prefer first review by 6 months for intermittent) |
| **Pseudostrabismus** (epicanthal folds, normal Hirschberg, normal cover test) | Reassure; no referral; document |
| **Amblyopia detected** (≥ 2-line acuity difference between eyes with no refractive cause OR after refractive correction) | §7.3 routine to pediatric ophthalmology, within 2–4 weeks; B1-01 does not initiate amblyopia therapy [PEDIG-Amblyopia, AAP-AAO-2016-Vision] |
| **Anisometropic amblyopia suspected** (refractive correction applied but acuity does not equalise after 6 weeks of full-time wear) | §7.3 routine within 4 weeks |

### 5.5 Convergence insufficiency / digital eye strain

| Severity | Findings | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Symptoms only after prolonged near work; CISS < 16; no near-point of convergence (NPC) deficit | §6.1 Light: 20-20-20 protocol, posture, ergonomics, lubricants |
| **Moderate** | CISS ≥ 16; NPC > 6 cm; symptoms affect near-work tolerance | §6.2 Standard: structured 6-week home-based vision-therapy programme with measurable endpoints; lubricants |
| **Severe / refractory** | No improvement after 6 weeks structured §6.2 management; persistent CISS ≥ 16 | §7.3 routine to pediatric ophthalmology / optometric vision therapy |

### 5.6 External lid disease (mild)

| Finding | Action |
|---|---|
| **Chalazion** (chronic, painless, lid nodule) | §6.1 warm compress + lid massage 4–6 weeks; if persistent, §7.3 routine to pediatric ophthalmology / pedo-ophthalmic plastics |
| **Hordeolum (stye)** (acute, painful, lid margin nodule) | §6.1 warm compress + lid hygiene + topical antibiotic if discharging; if no improvement in 7 days, §7.3 |
| **Blepharitis** (mild — lid-margin scaling, eyelash debris) | §6.1 lid hygiene; if recurrent, §7.3 |
| **Subconjunctival haemorrhage** (atraumatic) | Reassure; resolves in 2 weeks; counsel; no intervention. If trauma context, §7.1. |

### 5.7 Boundary rules

- *Borderline acuity:* if the child's tested acuity is at the cutoff (e.g., 5y old at exactly 20/40), repeat the test with a different chart and at a different time. Persistent borderline acuity over two visits → trial of refractive correction; persistence after correction → §7.3.
- *Re-grading after correction:* re-administer acuity, refraction, and stereoacuity at every B1-01 follow-up. Use the same chart family for repeatability.
- *Severity progression triggers:* myopia progression > 0.50 D / year despite low-dose atropine 0.01% → escalate to 0.05% (per §6.3) and document in PHR. Progression > 1.00 D / year despite 0.05% → §7.3 routine to pediatric ophthalmology for higher-concentration atropine or alternative myopia-control modality (orthokeratology, multifocal soft contact lenses — outside Tier 1 scope).
- *Multi-condition presentation:* a child can have refractive error + allergic conjunctivitis + convergence insufficiency simultaneously. Each is graded and managed independently.
- *Adolescent vision-impact uplift:* if visual symptoms are significantly impacting school performance OR the child is the primary academic-performance driver in the family's framing of the visit, escalate at least to Standard tier even if the technical refractive error is Mild. Document rationale.

## §6 Therapeutic options

### 6.1 Light tier (Mild refractive error, mild conjunctivitis, mild surface disease, mild lid disease)

**Refractive error (Mild — counselling only):**
- Reassure; document acuity baseline; surveillance at 6–12 months.
- Counsel on outdoor time (≥ 2 hours/day of outdoor light exposure is associated with reduced myopia incidence in children) [IMI-2021].
- Counsel on near-work hygiene: 20-20-20 protocol (every 20 minutes of near work, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds), reading distance ≥ 30 cm, adequate ambient illumination, screen position at or below eye level.
- Annual A1 + B1-01 follow-up to detect onset of refractive error.

**Allergic conjunctivitis (Mild):**

| Drug | Pediatric dose | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Carboxymethylcellulose 0.5% lubricating drops | 1 drop each eye QID PRN | Symptomatic |
| Cold compresses | 5–10 minutes 2–4× daily | PRN |

Behavioural: trigger identification (pollens, dust, animal dander); face-washing on returning indoors; avoid eye-rubbing; hand-washing and household allergen-reduction counselling. Cross-reference B1-06 Allergy if multi-system atopic features.

**Bacterial conjunctivitis — viral pattern (no antibiotic):**
- Reassure; lubricants; hygiene; school exclusion until purulent discharge resolves (typically 24 hours after eye is clear, per local school-health policy).

**External lid disease (mild — chalazion / hordeolum / blepharitis):**
- Warm compresses 5–10 minutes 2–4× daily.
- Lid-margin hygiene with diluted baby shampoo on a cotton-tip OR commercial lid-hygiene wipe, once daily.
- Topical antibiotic ointment (e.g., chloramphenicol or erythromycin) at lid margin BD × 7 days for any active hordeolum with discharge.

**Convergence insufficiency / digital eye strain (Mild):**
- 20-20-20 protocol counselling.
- Screen ergonomics — lower screen height, anti-glare positioning, increase ambient light, larger text size.
- Reading-distance and lighting counselling for paper-based work.
- Carboxymethylcellulose lubricating drops 1 drop QID for surface symptoms.

Dispensary stock for §6.1: carboxymethylcellulose 0.5% drops 10 mL bottles; chloramphenicol 1% eye ointment 5 g; erythromycin 0.5% eye ointment 3.5 g; lid-hygiene wipes; printed parent-app QR codes for behavioural counselling videos.

Expected timeline: surface / allergic symptoms improvement in 1–2 weeks; refractive surveillance at next periodic A1 visit.

### 6.2 Standard tier (Moderate refractive error → spectacles, moderate conjunctivitis → drops, etc.)

**Refractive error (Moderate — spectacles dispensed):**

Spectacle prescription:
- Cycloplegic refraction is conducted at the SKIDS clinic by the optometrist using cyclopentolate 1% (1 drop, repeat after 10 minutes; refract at 30–45 minutes) for children < 8 years OR for any child where accommodative spasm is suspected. Tropicamide 1% is an alternative for children where cyclopentolate is contraindicated [NEEDS EXPANSION IN EDITORIAL PASS — confirm cycloplegic agent choice with operations].
- Manifest refraction for children ≥ 8 years where accommodation is mature.
- Prescription ordered with full cycloplegic value for hyperopia ≥ +2.00 D, partial cylinder where Jackson cross-cylinder testing supports symptomatic comfort, and full myopic correction.

Spectacles dispensed in-clinic from the SKIDS frame-and-lens catalogue:
- Frames: pediatric impact-resistant materials; flexible-hinge frames for active children.
- Lenses: polycarbonate (impact resistance) standard; CR-39 or trivex acceptable; high-index lenses for high prescriptions (> ±5.00 D); UV-protective coating standard; anti-reflective coating optional.
- Photochromic / progressive lenses on case-by-case discussion.

Counselling on wear:
- Full-time wear for myopia, anisometropia, accommodative esotropia.
- Reading-only wear for moderate hyperopia where activity-based use is appropriate (per refractionist judgement).

Allied staff: SKIDS optometrist conducts the autorefraction, manifest refraction, and dispensing fitting. Clinic nurse or trained optometric assistant counsels on wear, care, and adaptation expectations.

**Allergic conjunctivitis (Moderate):**

| Drug | Pediatric dose | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Olopatadine 0.1% drops (antihistamine + mast-cell stabiliser) | 1 drop each eye BD | Daily during symptomatic period; up to 8 weeks at a time [AAO-Conj-2018] |
| Ketotifen 0.025% drops (alternative) | 1 drop each eye BD | As above |
| Cetirizine 5–10 mg PO once daily | Add for systemic atopic features | Per B1-06 Allergy if multi-system |

**Bacterial conjunctivitis (uncomplicated, > 48 hours purulent):**

| Drug | Pediatric dose | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Chloramphenicol 0.5% drops | 1 drop each eye 4–6× daily for first 2 days, then QID | 5–7 days [AAO-Conj-2018] |
| Moxifloxacin 0.5% drops (alternative for children > 1 year) | 1 drop each eye TDS | 5–7 days |
| Tobramycin 0.3% drops (alternative) | 1 drop each eye 6× daily | 7 days |

**Convergence insufficiency / digital eye strain (Moderate):**
- Structured 6-week home-based vision-therapy programme with weekly progress checks via parent app:
  - Pencil push-ups: hold pencil at arm's length, slowly bring toward nose, focus until doubling, repeat 20 times, twice daily.
  - Brock string exercises (bead string at varying distances, focus practice).
  - Computer-orthoptic exercises (HTS or equivalent app-based).
- Lubricating drops as §6.1.
- Re-test at 6 weeks; if NPC < 6 cm and CISS < 16, discharge to surveillance; otherwise → §6.3 / §7.3.

Dispensary stock for §6.2: spectacle frames and lenses (catalogue); olopatadine 0.1% 5 mL bottles; ketotifen 0.025% 5 mL bottles; chloramphenicol 0.5% drops 10 mL; moxifloxacin 0.5% drops 5 mL; tobramycin 0.3% drops; cyclopentolate 1% drops 5 mL; tropicamide 1% drops 5 mL.

Expected timeline: spectacle adaptation in 1–2 weeks; allergic / bacterial conjunctivitis improvement in 5–7 days; convergence insufficiency improvement in 6 weeks of consistent exercise.

### 6.3 Intensive tier (High refractive error, progressive myopia, severe conjunctivitis routing)

**High refractive error / high myopia:**
- Spectacles per §6.2 with high-index lenses.
- 6-monthly review (vs. annual for moderate).
- Retinal-surveillance routing: any child with myopia > –6.00 D OR axial length > 26 mm (where SKIDS has access to A-scan biometry) → §7.3 routine to pediatric ophthalmology for dilated fundus exam baseline; subsequent surveillance per ophthalmologist.
- Counsel family on lifelong retinal-tear / detachment risk; symptoms warranting emergency review (sudden flashes, floaters, curtain in vision).

**Progressive myopia (myopia-control programme):**

The programme's three components run together:

1. *Behavioural component:*
   - Increase outdoor time to ≥ 2 hours/day (school-day strategy: outdoor break + after-school outdoor).
   - Reduce continuous near-work; enforce 20-20-20 rule.
   - Reading distance ≥ 30 cm; screen at eye level.
   - Adequate ambient lighting at near-work stations.
   - Sleep adequacy (associated with myopia progression in some studies).

2. *Pharmacological component (low-dose atropine):*

| Indication | Drug | Dose | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| First-line for documented progression > 0.50 D / year OR onset < 10 years | Atropine 0.01% eye drops | 1 drop each eye | Once at bedtime [IMI-2021, Cochrane-CD009078] |
| Step-up if progression continues > 0.50 D / year on 0.01% | Atropine 0.05% eye drops | 1 drop each eye | Once at bedtime |

Counsel on side effects: photophobia (sunglasses if needed), near-vision blur (uncommon at 0.01%, more at 0.05% — bifocals may be discussed), allergic reaction (uncommon).

Reassess every 4 months: cycloplegic refraction + axial length where biometry available. Document progression rate.

Discontinuation: atropine continued through the high-progression period (typically until mid-adolescence) and tapered when progression slows to < 0.25 D / year over two consecutive 6-month intervals.

3. *Spectacle component:*
   - Full myopic correction; updated at every progression.
   - Single-vision standard. Multifocal soft contacts and orthokeratology are evidence-supported alternatives but require pediatric ophthalmology / specialist optometry — outside B1-01 Tier 1 scope; route §7.3 if family requests.

**Severe allergic conjunctivitis (vernal, atopic, GPC suspicion) → §7.3 routing.**

Dispensary stock additions for §6.3: atropine 0.01% drops 5 mL (compounded or commercial); atropine 0.05% drops 5 mL; high-index spectacle lenses; pediatric impact-resistant frames in larger sizes for adolescents.

### 6.4 Specific therapeutic notes

| Therapeutic | Notes |
|---|---|
| Topical steroid eye drops (any) | NOT prescribed at B1-01. Steroid eye drops require slit-lamp surveillance for IOP and corneal-thinning monitoring. Any indication → §7.3 to pediatric ophthalmology. |
| Cyclopentolate / tropicamide for cycloplegic refraction | Used by the SKIDS optometrist only, in-clinic, for refraction. Not dispensed for home use. |
| Atropine therapy | Only at 0.01% and 0.05% concentrations at B1-01. Higher concentrations route §7.3. |
| Spectacles | Dispensed in-clinic from the SKIDS catalogue; fitted by the optometrist or trained assistant. Lens replacement for progression covered per subscription tier. |
| Contact lenses | NOT dispensed at B1-01 Tier 1. Soft contact lens fitting and orthokeratology require specialised infrastructure; route to a partner pediatric optometrist (Tier 3 partnership). |

### 6.5 Colour-vision deficiency (when detected)

A child found Ishihara-positive for colour-vision deficiency at B1-01:
- Counsel family in plain language: most common form is red-green deficiency; X-linked recessive in males (8% prevalence) and rare in females; not progressive; not curable.
- Vocational implications counselling: certain professions (military, transport, electrician trades, some medical fields) have colour-vision standards.
- Document the specific deficiency type if confirmable.
- No further intervention at B1-01; not §7.

### 6.6 Tapering and stopping

- *Spectacles:* not tapered. Re-prescribed at every progression.
- *Allergic conjunctivitis drops:* used during symptomatic period; can be paused during asymptomatic months.
- *Bacterial conjunctivitis antibiotic:* fixed 5–7 day course; do not extend.
- *Atropine for myopia:* continued through progression-active period; tapered when progression slows over two consecutive 6-month intervals.
- *Convergence insufficiency exercises:* discontinued once measurable improvement (NPC < 6 cm, CISS < 16) is sustained for 4 weeks; reinforce maintenance behaviour.

## §7 Hard referral triggers [SACRED SECTION]

The case escalates out of B1-01's scope when ANY of the following is true:

### 7.1 Emergency referral (same day, transport directly to ER or named pediatric ophthalmology emergency)

- [ ] **Suspected leukocoria** — white pupil reflex on photographs or examination, regardless of age. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology + pediatric oncology consult, within 24 hours. Differentials include retinoblastoma, congenital cataract, persistent fetal vasculature, Coats disease, retinal detachment. The cost of missed retinoblastoma is catastrophic; the protocol does not tolerate even uncertainty about whether to refer [AAP-AAO-2016-Vision].
- [ ] **Acute traumatic eye injury** — penetrating injury, hyphaema, suspected globe rupture, chemical injury, large corneal abrasion with foreign body, lid laceration involving canaliculus or tarsus. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology / pediatric ER within 1 hour. For chemical injury, copious irrigation with normal saline or sterile water at B1-01 begins immediately and continues during transfer.
- [ ] **Sudden vision loss (any timeframe)** — acute monocular or binocular vision loss, not accounted for by an obvious refractive cause. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology + pediatric ER, within 1 hour.
- [ ] **Suspected orbital cellulitis** — proptosis, pain on eye movement, restricted eye movement, fever, marked lid swelling extending to deep tissue, RAPD. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER + pediatric ophthalmology, within 1 hour. NOT preseptal cellulitis (which can be managed at B1-19 Infections + B1-01).
- [ ] **Hyperacute purulent conjunctivitis (suspected gonococcal) at any age**, especially neonate. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology + neonatology / pediatric ER, within 1 hour. Empirical IM ceftriaxone may be initiated if delay anticipated [AAP-Conj-2013].
- [ ] **Suspected herpetic keratitis / dendritic ulcer** — fluorescein-positive dendritic pattern, vesicular eyelid eruption with eye involvement. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology, within 24 hours. NEVER initiate empirical topical steroid.
- [ ] **Acute angle closure (rare in pediatric)** — severe eye pain with vomiting, halos around lights, hard globe on palpation, fixed mid-dilated pupil. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER, within 1 hour.
- [ ] **Papilloedema on undilated fundoscopy OR sixth-nerve palsy** — concerning for raised intracranial pressure. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER + pediatric ophthalmology + pediatric neurology, within 1 hour for imaging.
- [ ] **Suspected non-accidental injury with eye findings** — retinal haemorrhages in an infant, periorbital bruising in non-mobile infant, "raccoon eyes" without trauma history. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER + child-protection referral, within 1 hour. Same retention protocol as A1 §7.1 — child not discharged to suspected-perpetrator caregiver.

### 7.2 Urgent referral (within 7 days)

- [ ] **Confirmed amblyopia in child < 7 years** — the critical window for amblyopia therapy is narrowing rapidly with age; earlier intervention is better. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology, within 7 days [PEDIG-Amblyopia, AAP-AAO-2016-Vision].
- [ ] **New-onset strabismus < 6 months age** — congenital esotropia, congenital cranial-nerve palsy, sensory strabismus. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology, within 7 days.
- [ ] **Acquired ptosis with visual axis obstruction in young child** — amblyogenic risk. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology / pedo-ophthalmic plastics, within 7 days.
- [ ] **Suspected uveitis (any presentation)** — photophobia + ciliary injection + ± hypopyon ± synechiae; especially in the context of juvenile idiopathic arthritis (B2-03 Rheumatology cross-care). → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology, within 7 days.
- [ ] **Diplopia with cranial-nerve palsy pattern (third / fourth / sixth)** — neurological cause until proven otherwise. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric neurology + pediatric ophthalmology, within 7 days.
- [ ] **Asymmetric red reflex (Brückner test positive)** — anisometropia, cataract, refractive error. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology, within 7 days.

### 7.3 Routine referral (next available appointment, ≤ 4 weeks)

- [ ] **Constant manifest strabismus at any age** (esotropia, exotropia, hypertropia / hypotropia). → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology, within 4 weeks. Pseudostrabismus does not refer.
- [ ] **Intermittent strabismus > 4 months age.** → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Refractory convergence insufficiency** — CISS ≥ 16 after 6 weeks of structured §6.2 management; near-point of convergence > 6 cm persistent. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology / specialty optometric vision therapy, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Refractive correction not improving acuity to age cutoff** — possible amblyopia, cataract, retinal pathology. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Progressive myopia not controlled on atropine 0.05%** — progression > 1.00 D / year despite step-up. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology, within 4 weeks. Discussion of higher-concentration atropine, orthokeratology, or multifocal soft contacts.
- [ ] **High myopia in young child** (> –6.00 D before age 10). → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology, within 4 weeks. Retinal-risk surveillance baseline.
- [ ] **Vernal / atopic / giant-papillary conjunctivitis** — chronic surface disease beyond mild allergic conjunctivitis. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology, within 4 weeks. Combined-care with B1-06 Allergy.
- [ ] **Persistent chalazion** — > 6 weeks of warm compresses without resolution OR child distress. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology / pedo-ophthalmic plastics, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Recurrent hordeolum / blepharitis** — three episodes within 6 months. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology, within 4 weeks. Underlying systemic cause screen (rosacea, demodex, immunodeficiency).
- [ ] **Suspected congenital nystagmus or new-onset nystagmus.** → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology + pediatric neurology, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Family request for orthokeratology / contact-lens fitting / refractive surgery counselling.** → SKIDS-T3 pediatric optometrist with specialty contact lens / pediatric ophthalmology, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Inability to obtain an undilated fundus view in either eye** despite reasonable attempt. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology, within 4 weeks for dilated fundus exam.
- [ ] **Unilateral or asymmetric refractive error suggesting amblyogenic potential** (anisometropia > 1.50 D). → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ophthalmology, within 4 weeks (concurrent with spectacle correction at B1-01).

### 7.4 Handoff communication

When B1-01 fires a §7 referral, the SKIDS pediatrician (or B1-01 optometrist) must send the receiving clinician (auto-generated by Companion):

1. **Child identification:** name, DOB, child_id, parent contact, parent consent confirmation, current medications, known allergies, atopic / juvenile-arthritis comorbidities (relevant for uveitis screen).
2. **The trigger:** which §7 sub-trigger fired with the underlying clinical finding.
3. **Diagnosis (provisional):** ICD-10 where determined, plain-language description.
4. **Vision assessment data:**
   - Distance and near visual acuity each eye, with and without correction.
   - Pinhole acuity (where relevant).
   - Cycloplegic refraction values each eye (if obtained).
   - Stereoacuity test result.
   - Cover-test result (alternate, simultaneous).
   - Hirschberg result.
   - Red reflex assessment.
   - Pupil examination including RAPD.
   - Anterior-segment findings.
   - Fundus findings (undilated).
5. **Photographs / Welch Allyn output:** any external photos, Welch Allyn instrument output, autorefractor printouts.
6. **Therapies tried at B1-01 and outcomes** (e.g., spectacle wear x 6 weeks with no acuity equalisation; atropine 0.05% × 6 months with continued progression).
7. **Bridge interventions started** at the §7 visit (e.g., topical antibiotic for suspected bacterial conjunctivitis pending ophthalmology review).
8. **Reason for referral, in clinical terms:** one to two sentences from the SKIDS clinician on what they need from the receiving clinician.
9. **Urgency tier and target window:** §7.1 / §7.2 / §7.3 with the time bound.
10. **SKIDS-T3 / clinic** and appointment booking status.

The packet is auto-generated by Companion when the trigger fires. The SKIDS clinician reviews and signs off before transmission. For §7.1 emergencies, the receiving clinician is also called by phone.

## §8 Follow-up schedule

### 8.1 Follow-up frequency

| Severity tier / condition | First follow-up | Subsequent | Annual review |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mild refractive error (no Rx) | per A1 periodicity | as A1 | Yes |
| Moderate refractive error (Rx issued) | 6 weeks (adaptation check) | 12 months | Yes |
| High refractive error / High myopia | 6 weeks | 6 months | Yes |
| Progressive myopia on myopia control | 4 months | 4 months until stable | Yes |
| Allergic conjunctivitis (Mild / Moderate) | 4 weeks if symptomatic; otherwise as A1 | as needed | Yes |
| Bacterial conjunctivitis | day 7 (telephone or photo via app acceptable) | as needed | — |
| Convergence insufficiency Standard | 6 weeks | 12 weeks if not discharged | — |
| Chalazion / hordeolum / blepharitis Mild | 4–6 weeks | as needed | — |

### 8.2 What to recheck at each follow-up

- *Refractive:* visual acuity each eye uncorrected and corrected; refraction (cycloplegic at 6 months minimum where on atropine, otherwise annually); axial length where biometry available; spectacle condition.
- *Myopia control:* progression rate (D/year); axial length; behavioural adherence (outdoor time, near-work); atropine adherence and side-effect screen.
- *Conjunctivitis:* symptom resolution; surface findings; trigger control (allergic).
- *Convergence insufficiency:* CISS, NPC, accommodation.
- *Lid disease:* lesion size and tenderness.

### 8.3 Treatment success criteria

- *Refractive:* acuity at age cutoff with correction; comfortable wear; no eye-strain symptoms.
- *Myopia control:* progression rate < 0.50 D / year (target) on the chosen intervention combination.
- *Allergic conjunctivitis:* symptom-free or near-symptom-free during the symptomatic season on Mild / Moderate-tier therapy.
- *Bacterial conjunctivitis:* clinical clearance by day 7.
- *Convergence insufficiency:* NPC < 6 cm, CISS < 16 sustained for 4 weeks.

### 8.4 Step-down criteria

- Spectacles continue indefinitely; not tapered.
- Atropine for myopia: tapered when progression < 0.25 D / year over two consecutive 6-month intervals.
- Allergic-conjunctivitis topicals: paused during asymptomatic months.
- Convergence insufficiency exercises: discontinued upon sustained improvement.

### 8.5 Step-up criteria (within Tier 1)

- Refractive error progression → updated Rx and 6-monthly review (instead of annual).
- Myopia progression > 0.50 D / year on atropine 0.01% → escalate to 0.05%.
- Allergic conjunctivitis Mild not controlled on lubricants → step up to topical antihistamine.
- Convergence insufficiency Mild not improving on 20-20-20 → step up to structured 6-week home programme.

### 8.6 Step-out criteria

Per §7. Specifically: progression > 1.00 D / year despite atropine 0.05%; CISS persistently ≥ 16 after 6 weeks of structured §6.2; refractive correction not improving acuity to age cutoff; any §7.1 / §7.2 trigger.

### 8.7 Spectacle replacement schedule

- Annual review for all spectacle-wearing children at minimum.
- Replacement covered per subscription tier (Basic out-of-pocket; Advanced one replacement/year; Premium two replacements/year).
- Out-of-cycle replacements (loss / breakage) at out-of-pocket rates regardless of tier.

### 8.8 Disenrolment / loss-to-follow-up

A child active in the myopia-control programme who misses two scheduled 4-monthly follow-ups is contacted by clinic Manager. If no response within 30 days, named ped / optometrist calls. Loss-to-follow-up on active atropine therapy is a §12 quarterly-review trigger (uncertainty about ongoing dosing has clinical and stewardship implications).

## §10 Parent-facing communication

### 10.1 Scripted explanations

When grading is Mild refractive error, no Rx:

> "[Aanya]'s vision check is in the normal range for her age. She doesn't need glasses today. We did identify [some near-sightedness / a small refractive error] that we want to keep an eye on. The two big things you can do at home: at least two hours of outdoor time per day if her routine permits, and breaks from close-up work — every 20 minutes she does near-work, a 20-second look at something far away. We'll re-check at her next routine SKIDS visit."

When grading is Moderate refractive error, Rx issued:

> "[Aanya] is myopic — her eyes focus light a bit short of her retina, so distance vision is blurred. We're going to give her a pair of glasses today; you'll see her in them in [N days]. For school, she should wear them full-time. The first week or two she may notice the glasses; that's normal adaptation. We'll see her back in six weeks to check the adaptation, and again at six months to see if her prescription has changed. Children's eyes do change as they grow, and we'll update the glasses when needed."

When myopia-control programme is started:

> "[Rohit]'s myopia is progressing — his prescription has gotten stronger over [time period]. We want to slow that down, both because we want him to need a milder prescription as an adult and because progressive myopia carries a longer-term risk of retinal problems. We're going to do three things. First: a small drop in each eye every night before bed — a very low concentration of a medication called atropine. Second: a daily target of two hours of outdoor time. Third: a structured break from near-work — the 20-20-20 rule. We'll re-check his prescription every four months and see if his progression slows. The drops are not a cure; they slow progression by about half in the typical case. Most children tolerate them with no side effects."

When grading is Moderate allergic conjunctivitis:

> "[Aanya]'s eyes are itchy and red because she's having an allergic reaction — likely to something in the air at home, school, or outdoors during this season. We're going to use a drop in each eye twice a day for [N weeks]. She'll feel relief within a few days. Cold compresses help — a wet washcloth from the fridge for five minutes when she's particularly itchy. We'll also do a check at the SKIDS Allergy clinic if you'd like — sometimes the eye allergies are part of a wider pattern."

When suspected leukocoria fires §7.1:

> "I noticed something on [Rohit]'s eye exam that I want a children's eye specialist to look at today. There's a finding called the red reflex that should look the same in both eyes; in one of his eyes it doesn't look quite right. There are several possible causes, some of which need urgent specialist assessment. I have already called [partner pediatric ophthalmology] and they are expecting [him] today. We need to go directly there. I will be in touch with the team. Try not to be alarmed by the urgency — this is the right step."

When suspected strabismus fires §7.3:

> "[Aanya]'s eyes are not aligning together perfectly — what's sometimes called a 'lazy eye' or strabismus, though the formal name is what your specialist will discuss. Strabismus needs the children's eye specialist for proper diagnosis and treatment, which can include glasses, eye-patching, or in some cases surgery. Catching this early gives the best treatment outcome. The appointment is in [N weeks]. I'll send across her vision exam, photos, and our findings, so the specialist can hit the ground running."

### 10.2 Scripted intervention-tier explanation

For families with progressive myopia where Advanced or Premium subscription is recommended:

> "[Rohit]'s myopia control means we'll see him every four months for the next year or two, plus the spectacle changes as his prescription updates. The Advanced subscription bundles four visits and one spectacle replacement per year; the Premium bundles unlimited visits and two replacements. Most progressive-myopia children fit the Advanced tier; if his progression remains rapid, Premium becomes worthwhile. The clinical care doesn't change based on tier — only what's included vs. paid per visit."

### 10.5 Cultural sensitivities

- *Spectacle stigma in young children.* Some Indian families (especially for young girls) carry concerns about spectacle wear — appearance, marriageability narratives, perceived weakness. Address explicitly with the family: spectacles in a child are corrective, not stigmatic; uncorrected refractive error has lifelong school-performance and amblyopia consequences. Frame-style choice within the catalogue allows the family to select what they're comfortable with.
- *Eye-drop hesitancy.* Atropine is sometimes refused by families on the basis of unfamiliarity ("we never put drops in his eyes before"); demonstrate one drop at the visit; provide the technique video on the parent app; reassure that 0.01% is an extremely low concentration with minimal side effects. Document refusal if the family declines after counselling.
- *Outdoor-time constraints.* Indian urban families often face genuine barriers to two hours/day outdoor time (heat, air pollution, school timetable, safety concerns, especially for girls). Counsel realistically; partial outdoor time is better than none; school-day strategy and weekend strategy can compensate. Avoid moralising the family for non-adherence.
- *Traditional eye remedies.* Kohl, surma, ghee drops, and other folk applications are still used in some families for "strengthening the eyes." Engage rather than dismiss: most are not harmful in small quantities but kohl with lead has known toxicity. Discuss the specific preparation; recommend cessation only if a known harmful constituent. Do not lecture.
- *Vocational disclosure of colour-vision deficiency.* Adolescent privacy applies — the colour-vision result is the adolescent's information, with vocational implications they can choose to disclose or not. The result is in the PHR; the family is informed only with the adolescent's consent for adolescents ≥ 12 years.
- *Religious and cultural framing of vision changes.* Some families interpret vision changes through religious or fated framings. Engage respectfully; provide the medical framing alongside; do not contest the family's spiritual framework.

## §12 Quality and audit

### 12.1 Outcome metrics

B1-01 is working well when:

- ≥ 85% of children issued first spectacles report comfortable wear at the 6-week adaptation check.
- ≥ 80% of children on the myopia-control programme achieve progression < 0.50 D / year by the second 4-monthly review.
- < 10% of children on atropine 0.05% require step-up to §7.3 within 12 months (primary B1-01 control rate).
- ≥ 90% of A1 vision Orange flags result in a confirmed B1-01 visit within 4 weeks.
- 100% of §7.1 emergency triggers result in same-day handoff to the SKIDS-T3.
- ≥ 95% of suspected amblyopia cases (§7.2) attend the pediatric ophthalmology appointment within 7 days.
- ≥ 85% of allergic conjunctivitis cases achieve symptom control on Mild / Moderate-tier therapy without §7 routing.
- < 5% of bacterial conjunctivitis cases require second-line antibiotic (first-line empirical resistance / non-response rate).
- Spectacle dispensing rate (Rx issued → glasses dispensed in-clinic same visit) ≥ 90% [NEEDS EXPANSION IN EDITORIAL PASS — confirm operational target].
- Loss-to-follow-up rate on active myopia-control programme < 10% per year.

### 12.2 Quarterly case review

The protocol owner reviews every quarter:
- A random sample of 30 B1-01 visits, balanced across refractive / myopia-control / conjunctivitis / referral.
- All §7 referrals (every tier).
- All severity-tier downgrades on Screen 5 (especially Severe → Moderate, Moderate → Mild).
- All atropine 0.05% prescriptions (indication, prior progression rate, adherence).
- All cases of suspected leukocoria handling (was the §7.1 routing correct and timely; was the family communication effective).
- All cases where Rx was changed by ≥ 1.00 D in < 6 months (rapid progression OR refraction-error trigger).
- All loss-to-follow-up cases on active myopia-control programme.

Output: 1-page report to SKIDS clinical operations with recommended protocol updates flagged.

### 12.3 Pediatrician / optometrist certification on this protocol

To deliver B1-01, the SKIDS clinical team for this protocol must include:

**The B1-01 SKIDS optometrist (mandatory):**
- B.Optom or equivalent recognised qualification.
- Pediatric refraction proficiency (cycloplegic refraction in age 0–18; LEA / HOTV / Snellen / LogMAR chart proficiency).
- Manifest and cycloplegic refraction case-vignette assessment (10 cases).
- Spectacle dispensing competency.
- Annual continuing education: 8 hours covering AAPOS updates, IMI annual myopia-control updates.

**The SKIDS pediatrician serving B1-01:**
- Complete the B1-01 onboarding chapter (3 hours).
- Pass a structured case-vignette assessment (10 cases across red-flag conjunctivitis, suspected strabismus, suspected amblyopia, leukocoria).
- Demonstrate red-reflex and Brückner test proficiency (live exam observation).
- Demonstrate Hirschberg, cover-test, and stereotest interpretation.
- Demonstrate fluorescein use for corneal abrasion screening (where slit-lamp or cobalt blue is available).
- Maintain a minimum case load of 30 B1-01 ped-led visits per quarter (mostly conjunctivitis, lid disease, and strabismus / amblyopia routing decisions; refraction is optometrist-led).

### 12.4 The SKIDS Fellowship pathway

Pediatricians delivering B1-01 who wish to advance to credentialed practice may apply for The SKIDS Fellowship in pediatric ophthalmology, where it is offered via partner institutions. See `docs/06-the-skids-fellowship.md`. The credentialed pediatric ophthalmologist would deliver:

- Strabismus and amblyopia management (currently §7.3-routed).
- Higher-concentration atropine and alternative myopia-control modalities (orthokeratology supervision, multifocal soft contact lens fitting).
- Slit-lamp surveillance for any topical-steroid course.
- Pediatric uveitis co-management with B2-03 Rheumatology.
- Procedural pediatric ophthalmology (chalazion incision, foreign-body removal under topical anaesthesia, basic surgical exposure under fellowship training).
- Protocol governance for B1-01 across the SKIDS network.

Pediatric ophthalmology is not currently a B2 protocol in the SKIDS catalogue (which has only Endocrine, Neurology, Rheumatology). The Fellowship may expand B1-01 into a credentialed-tier complex pathway as the network grows; see `docs/04-tier-system.md`.
