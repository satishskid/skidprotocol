# SKIDS Allergy Clinic

> The SKIDS Allergy Clinic manages allergic rhinitis, mild-to-moderate
> food allergy with structured allergen-avoidance and adrenaline-auto-
> injector action plans, acute and mild-chronic urticaria, drug-allergy
> initial assessment, and joint-care for atopic dermatitis with
> food-trigger components. Anaphylaxis at presentation, oral
> immunotherapy candidacy, severe chronic urticaria, suspected
> hereditary angioedema, and complex multi-system atopic disease
> route per §7.

---

## §1 Service definition

**What this clinic does:**
- Diagnoses and manages allergic rhinitis (ARIA-graded) with intranasal corticosteroids, oral antihistamines, and saline nasal hygiene [ARIA-2020].
- Diagnoses food allergy through structured history, skin-prick testing (SPT), specific IgE blood testing (sIgE) where SPT is not feasible, and supervised oral-food-challenge (OFC) within a defined safety protocol [NIAID-Food-2010, EAACI-Food-2014].
- Issues structured anaphylaxis action plans and dispenses adrenaline auto-injectors (300 µg ≥ 25 kg; 150 µg 7.5–25 kg) for any child meeting the §6 dispensing criteria [WAO-Anaphylaxis-2020].
- Co-manages atopic dermatitis-with-food-trigger pattern with B1-05 Skin (B1-05 manages the cutaneous component; B1-06 manages the allergen identification and elimination plan).
- Co-manages cow's-milk-protein allergy (CMPA) with B1-09 Nutrition & Growth (B1-09 manages the nutritional adequacy; B1-06 manages the allergic / immunological component).
- Will co-manage asthma triggers with B1-14 Pulmonology / Asthma when B1-14 is drafted (allergen-avoidance and immunotherapy candidacy components).
- Manages acute urticaria (< 6 weeks) and mild chronic urticaria (≥ 6 weeks; non-impactful); routes severe chronic spontaneous urticaria, autoimmune urticaria suspicion, and physical urticaria refractory to first-line antihistamine to §7.
- Manages drug-allergy initial assessment for non-severe reactions and issues drug-avoidance documentation; severe drug reactions (suspected SJS / TEN / DRESS) are §7.1 emergencies (cross-care with B1-05 §7.1).
- Counsels insect-venom-allergy families with action-plan + adrenaline-auto-injector after a confirmed venom-anaphylaxis episode; venom immunotherapy (VIT) candidacy routes §7.3 to pediatric allergy specialist.
- Initiates aeroallergen allergen immunotherapy (sublingual immunotherapy, SLIT) for selected confirmed monosensitised cases; complex polysensitisation and subcutaneous immunotherapy (SCIT) are out-of-scope and route §7.3.

**What this clinic does not do:**
- Manage acute anaphylaxis at presentation — §7.1 emergency to pediatric ER (with adrenaline IM administered en route per training).
- Initiate oral immunotherapy (OIT) for food allergy — §7.3 to pediatric allergy specialist.
- Manage suspected hereditary angioedema (HAE), C1-esterase deficiency, mast-cell activation syndrome, mastocytosis — §7 routing.
- Diagnose or manage severe drug reactions (SJS / TEN / DRESS, fixed drug eruption complex, anaphylaxis to drug) beyond the immediate stabilisation — §7.1 / §7.3.
- Initiate subcutaneous immunotherapy (SCIT) — requires resuscitation infrastructure beyond Tier 1 scope.
- Manage chronic spontaneous urticaria refractory to first-line antihistamine optimisation, OR severe chronic urticaria affecting daily function (these need omalizumab or specialist input — §7.3).
- Manage primary immunodeficiency-related allergic disease (cross with B1-19 Infections; route §7.3 to immunology).

**Age range covered:** 0 – 18 years.
- 0 – 1 year: CMPA-dominant presentations; eczema-with-food-trigger.
- 1 – 5 years: peanut and tree-nut allergy detection; egg-allergy resolution surveillance; allergic-rhinitis early presentation.
- 5 – 18 years: full ARIA-graded rhinitis management; immunotherapy candidacy decisions; insect-venom and drug-allergy presentations; adolescent self-management of action plan and auto-injector.

**SKIDS subscription tier(s):**
- **Basic ₹2,999** — A1 routing supported. First B1-06 visit, SPT panel, action plan, and adrenaline-auto-injector dispensing on per-visit pricing.
- **Advanced ₹6,999** — up to 4 B1-06 visits per year included; one auto-injector pair per year covered; structured allergen-avoidance counselling.
- **Premium ₹12,999** — unlimited B1-06 visits within the clinically indicated set; two auto-injector pairs per year covered; SLIT programme included where indicated; priority §7 partner referral handoff.

**SKIDS clinical tier:** Tier 1, with a key allied-staff dependence — the SKIDS allergy nurse (trained in SPT administration, action-plan teaching, adrenaline-injector technique demonstration). Without this nurse, B1-06 cannot operate at full capability and degrades to a routing-and-counselling role only. Protocol governance sits with the named pediatric allergist or pediatric pulmonologist with allergy expertise identified in §2 (assignment pending). Complex tier per §7 escalates to Tier 3 partner pediatric allergy specialist or pediatric ER.

## §2 Evidence base

This protocol governs primary-care allergy management at SKIDS clinics. It follows the most current recommendations from:

Governing source for allergic-rhinitis classification, severity grading, and stepped therapy.

**Departures from cited guidelines:**

1. *In-clinic SPT panel scope.* B1-06 conducts SPT for a defined panel of common Indian aeroallergens (house dust mite Dermatophagoides pteronyssinus and D. farinae, cockroach, common moulds — Aspergillus / Alternaria / Cladosporium, common pollens — grass mix and tree mix per region) plus common food allergens (cow's milk, egg, peanut, tree nuts, wheat, soy, sesame, fish, shellfish for adolescents). SPT for less-common allergens routes §7.3 to pediatric allergy specialist. The panel is operationally defined and may expand as the SKIDS clinic capability matures [NEEDS EXPANSION IN EDITORIAL PASS — confirm SPT panel scope and the regional pollen-mix selection].

2. *Oral food challenge (OFC) at B1-06.* Low-risk OFC (e.g., baked-egg challenge in egg-allergic child with negative SPT to baked egg) is performed in-clinic with the resuscitation kit (adrenaline, antihistamine, IV access, oxygen) and a 4-hour observation period. Higher-risk OFC (large-amount peanut, multiple-allergen) routes §7.3 to pediatric allergy specialist with infrastructure. The boundary between low-risk and higher-risk is operationalised in §6 [NIAID-Food-2010].

3. *SLIT initiation in primary care.* B1-06 initiates sublingual immunotherapy (SLIT) for monosensitised house-dust-mite or single-pollen allergic rhinitis in children ≥ 5 years where the rhinitis is moderate-severe and unresponsive to optimised pharmacotherapy. Polysensitisation, food-allergy SLIT (experimental), and any SCIT initiation route §7.3. This is a relatively progressive primary-care position; some Indian centres restrict SLIT to specialist-only [NEEDS EXPANSION IN EDITORIAL PASS — confirm SLIT-in-primary-care position with the named protocol owner].

4. *Universal early peanut introduction at A1 §6.1 CF programme.* B1-06 supports the LEAP-derived guidance for early peanut introduction (4–6 months) in atopic-risk infants — joint with B1-05 (eczema severity assessment) and B1-09 (CF programme delivery). This is a clear Western-data-derived recommendation; IAP-Allergy-2022 framing on Indian-context applicability is being clarified [LEAP-2015, NIAID-Peanut-2017, NEEDS EXPANSION IN EDITORIAL PASS].

5. *Adrenaline auto-injector dispensing in primary care.* B1-06 dispenses adrenaline auto-injectors directly. Some Indian centres restrict to specialist; the supply chain has historically been inconsistent. SKIDS commits to maintaining auto-injector stock and dispensing per protocol; A1 §7.1 anaphylaxis-without-action-plan is a §7.1 trigger that B1-06 closes (every at-risk family leaves with action plan + auto-injector pair after the B1-06 visit) [NEEDS EXPANSION IN EDITORIAL PASS — confirm SKIDS supply-chain commitment for adrenaline auto-injectors across all clinic locations].

6. *Adrenaline auto-injector weight-banding cutoff.* The 25 kg cutoff for 150 mcg → 300 mcg auto-injector transition is a SKIDS operational decision. WAO-2020 uses 25 kg; UK Resuscitation Council and ASCIA use 20 kg; some markets use 30 kg. For children weighing 22–30 kg, clinical judgement applies — document the weight and the dose selected.

No other material departures.

## §4 Diagnostic workflow

### 4.1 Structured history

Ask, in this order:

1. *Presenting complaint detail.* Symptoms (specific — itch, sneeze, congestion, watering, hives, swelling, breathing difficulty, GI), onset, duration, fluctuation, settings (home / outdoor / school), seasonality.
2. *Suspected trigger detail.* For food: what was eaten, how much, time-to-reaction (minutes vs. hours), severity, intervention required (antihistamine / adrenaline / hospital), reproducibility on subsequent exposures. For aeroallergen: pollen / dust / pets / mould; spatial (indoor / outdoor) and temporal (seasonal / perennial) pattern.
3. *Severity of any prior reaction.* Mild (skin only, no airway / circulatory features) vs. moderate (multi-system, no airway compromise) vs. severe / anaphylaxis (airway, breathing, circulation features). The severity of any prior reaction is the strongest predictor of next-reaction severity and drives §6 dispensing decisions.
4. *Atopy comorbidities.* Personal history of eczema (with B1-05 cross-reference), asthma (with B1-14), allergic rhinitis. Family history of atopy in first-degree relatives.
5. *Food history.* Diet recall; foods avoided due to allergy concern (whether confirmed or not — distinguishes confirmed allergy from family-imposed avoidance); current diet adequacy (cross with B1-09 for any restricted-diet child).
6. *Medication history.* All prior reactions to medications: drug name, indication, time-to-reaction, severity, intervention, subsequent re-exposure if any. Specific attention to NSAIDs, antibiotics (penicillin family especially), local anaesthetics, contrast media.
7. *Insect-bite / venom history.* Any prior bee / wasp / hornet / fire-ant sting; reaction severity; subsequent stings.
8. *Action-plan and auto-injector status.* Does the family have a written action plan for any confirmed allergy? Does the family carry adrenaline auto-injectors? Where? Who knows how to use them? When were they last checked / replaced (devices expire)?
9. *School / daycare context.* Does the school know about the allergy? Is there a school action plan on file? Who is the designated trained person at school?
10. *Prior interventions.* Prior allergy testing (where, what panel, results — bring records); prior immunotherapy; prior elimination diets; prior medications used; traditional / complementary interventions.

**Red flags to elicit at history:**
- Any prior anaphylactic reaction (airway, breathing, circulation features) regardless of timing.
- Any reaction requiring emergency department visit or hospitalisation.
- Any reaction requiring adrenaline administration.
- Suspected hereditary angioedema features (recurrent angioedema without hives, family history of angioedema, abdominal-pain attacks, laryngeal episodes).
- Reaction to multiple drug classes (suggesting either misinterpretation or a complex syndrome — refer).
- Suspected mastocytosis (recurrent flushing, urticaria pigmentosa, anaphylaxis without identifiable trigger).
- Severe drug reaction in past (mucosal involvement, sloughing, systemic features) — SJS / TEN / DRESS suspicion.

### 4.2 Examination

Examine for, in this order:

- *General appearance.* Engagement, breathing pattern (rate, retractions), facial features (allergic salutation crease, allergic shiners, mouth breathing).
- *Skin.* Eczematous changes (cross with B1-05); urticarial lesions; angioedema; signs of recent scratching; dermographism (gentle stroke with a tongue depressor — wheal-and-flare response in physical urticaria).
- *Eyes.* Conjunctival injection, cobblestoning of tarsal conjunctiva (vernal keratoconjunctivitis suggestion), tearing.
- *Nasal.* Mucosal pallor / oedema vs. erythema; turbinate swelling; clear vs. purulent discharge; polyps (uncommon in pediatric — significant if seen, raises CF / aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease suspicion in older); nasal crease.
- *Oropharynx.* Cobblestone posterior pharynx (post-nasal drip); dental crowding / dental occlusion (chronic mouth-breathing); tonsillar size (cross with B1-03 for Throat & Airway).
- *Ears.* Tympanic-membrane appearance for OME (allergic-rhinitis-associated middle-ear effusion is common; cross with B1-02 Hearing & Ear).
- *Chest.* Auscultation for wheeze, rhonchi, breath-sound symmetry; respiratory rate; signs of accessory-muscle use. Chronic atopic disease in children often has subclinical asthma — listen carefully.
- *Cardiovascular.* Heart rate, blood pressure (baseline for anaphylaxis-history workup; tachycardia at rest may suggest sub-clinical asthma or anxiety).
- *Lymph node and abdominal.* Hepatosplenomegaly (rule out mastocytosis suspicion); lymphadenopathy.

### 4.3 Scales and questionnaires

| Scale | Domain | Age | Cutoff |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ARIA classification** | Allergic rhinitis | 5–18 (extended down on history) | Intermittent (< 4 days/week or < 4 weeks duration) vs. Persistent; Mild vs. Moderate-Severe (impact on sleep / daily activities / school / work) [ARIA-2020] |
| **TNSS (Total Nasal Symptom Score)** | Rhinitis severity | 5–18 | 0–12 score; ≥ 6 → consider step-up therapy |
| **CARAT (Control of Allergic Rhinitis and Asthma Test)** | Combined rhinitis-asthma control | adolescent | Track score over time; declining score → step up |
| **UAS7 (Urticaria Activity Score over 7 days)** | Chronic urticaria activity | 6–18 (with parent help) | Score 0–42; > 16 indicates active disease, treatment escalation [EAACI-Urticaria-2022] |
| **CU-Q2oL (Chronic Urticaria Quality of Life)** | Chronic urticaria QoL impact | 12–18 | Per scale; high impact suggests step-up |
| **Anaphylaxis severity grading** (Brown / Mueller scale) | Severity of prior reaction | any | Grade I–IV; III–IV = severe / anaphylaxis |
| **Food allergy reaction history score** | Reaction documentation | any | Structured intake at first visit |

### 4.4 In-clinic device tests

Required:
- *Skin-prick testing kit.* Standard pediatric allergen extracts (in-clinic panel per §2 departures); positive control (histamine) and negative control (saline); SPT lancets; ruler; timer (15 minutes for read).
- *Resuscitation kit (mandatory in any room where SPT or OFC is conducted).* Adrenaline (1:1000 IM, drawn-up syringes 0.01 mg/kg with maximum 0.3 mg per dose); pediatric BP cuff; pulse oximeter; bag-and-mask ventilation kit; oxygen source and mask; IV access supplies; antihistamine (diphenhydramine IV/IM and oral chlorpheniramine); methylprednisolone; IV fluid (normal saline). The kit is checked weekly; expiry-tracked.
- *Spirometer (basic).* For any allergic-rhinitis presentation with respiratory symptoms (cross with B1-14 Pulmonology / Asthma when drafted).
- *Peak flow meter.* For asthma-suspected presentations.
- *Oral food challenge room.* A dedicated room with 4-hour observation capability for low-risk OFC (per §6 OFC criteria).

Optional / on-indication:
- *Nasal endoscope (rhinoscope).* For polyposis suspicion or chronic rhinosinusitis workup; B1-06 does not interpret beyond detection — referral to ENT (B1-03 cross-care or §7.3) if abnormal.

### 4.5 Laboratory and imaging

Order:
- *Total IgE.* Of limited diagnostic value for individual allergen identification but useful for atopy quantification and tracking; not routinely ordered unless complex polysensitisation pattern needs confirmation.
- *Specific IgE (sIgE) panel.* Targeted to clinical history. NOT a broad-panel "allergy screen." Order for the specific allergen(s) implicated by history. Examples: sIgE to peanut, tree nuts (almond, walnut, cashew, pistachio), egg white, cow's milk, wheat, soy, fish, shellfish, individual aeroallergens.
- *Component-resolved diagnostics (CRD).* For peanut, cashew, egg, milk, wheat where the major-vs-minor allergen distinction influences clinical decision (e.g., Ara h 2 for peanut anaphylaxis-risk discrimination from oral-allergy-syndrome cross-reactivity). Available where laboratory supports; otherwise route §7.3 [EAACI-Food-2014, NIAID-Food-2010].
- *Tryptase (baseline + acute-event 1–4h post-event).* For any history of anaphylaxis-without-clear-trigger; suspected mastocytosis.
- *FBC + differential.* For any chronic urticaria > 6 weeks; eosinophilia raises atopy / parasitic / hyper-eosinophilic syndrome suspicion.
- *Stool examination + giardia antigen + ova/parasite.* For unexplained eosinophilia in chronic urticaria, especially in geographies with high parasitic load.
- *Thyroid antibodies (TPO, TG-Ab) + TSH.* For chronic spontaneous urticaria (autoimmune urticaria association).
- *C4 + C1-esterase inhibitor level + function.* For suspected hereditary angioedema (recurrent angioedema without hives, family history) — order at B1-06 to expedite §7.3 referral with workup in hand.

Do not order routinely:
- Broad-panel sIgE testing without clinical-history correlation. Broad panels generate false positives and family confusion.
- IgG / IgG4 panels for "food intolerance" — these are not validated for clinical decision-making and are misleading [NIAID-Food-2010].
- Skin biopsy for chronic urticaria — routes via §7.3 if warranted.
- Patch testing for delayed contact-allergic-dermatitis — routes via §7.3 to pediatric dermatology / allergy specialist.

## §5 Severity grading [SACRED SECTION]

### 5.1 Allergic rhinitis (ARIA-graded)

| Severity | ARIA classification | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild intermittent** | Symptoms < 4 days/week or duration < 4 weeks; no impact on sleep / daily activities / school / work | §6.1 Light: saline nasal hygiene + oral H1-antihistamine PRN |
| **Mild persistent** | Symptoms ≥ 4 days/week and duration ≥ 4 weeks; no significant impact | §6.2 Standard: regular oral H1-antihistamine + saline; consider intranasal corticosteroid if not controlling |
| **Moderate-severe intermittent** | Symptoms < 4 days/week or duration < 4 weeks; significant impact (sleep / school / work) | §6.2 Standard: oral H1-antihistamine + intranasal corticosteroid |
| **Moderate-severe persistent** | Symptoms ≥ 4 days/week and duration ≥ 4 weeks; significant impact | §6.3 Intensive: intranasal corticosteroid + oral H1-antihistamine + leukotriene receptor antagonist (consider) + allergen avoidance + SLIT candidacy assessment if indicated [ARIA-2020] |
| **Refractory after 12 weeks of optimised §6.3** | — | §7.3 to pediatric allergy specialist or pediatric ENT for nasal endoscopy / polyp surveillance |

### 5.2 Food allergy (IgE-mediated)

| Severity | Findings | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Suspected food allergy by history alone** | Clear history of immediate-onset reaction (< 2h) with multi-system features | §6.2 Standard: targeted SPT / sIgE; structured elimination if positive; action plan + auto-injector dispensed on history alone if anaphylaxis-pattern |
| **Confirmed food allergy, no anaphylaxis history** | Positive SPT / sIgE matching history; no airway or circulatory features in any prior reaction | §6.2: structured elimination + action plan + auto-injector (≥ 25 kg = 300 µg auto-injector; 7.5–25 kg = 150 µg; < 7.5 kg = adrenaline ampoule + IM kit per pediatric resuscitation plan) [WAO-Anaphylaxis-2020] |
| **Confirmed food allergy WITH anaphylaxis history** | Positive testing + prior anaphylactic reaction | §6.3 Intensive: structured elimination + action plan + 2 auto-injectors at all times (school + home / on-person); annual review; OFC consideration only if low-risk indicators per §6.4 |
| **Multiple food allergy OR rapidly evolving polysensitisation** | ≥ 3 confirmed food allergens with overlapping risk | §7.3 to pediatric allergy specialist (OIT candidacy, comprehensive avoidance planning) |
| **Uncertain food-allergy diagnosis after SPT / sIgE** | History suggestive but testing equivocal | §6.2 OFC if low-risk per §6.4 criteria; otherwise §7.3 |

### 5.3 Atopic dermatitis with suspected food trigger

| Severity | Findings | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild AD with suspected food trigger, no anaphylaxis features** | AD per B1-05 §5.1 + parent-reported food-related flare | §6.2 joint with B1-05: 4-week elimination of suspected trigger + structured re-introduction; SPT / sIgE only if elimination supports a true trigger |
| **Moderate AD with suspected food trigger** | AD per B1-05 §5.1 Moderate + food trigger | §6.2: as above + B1-05 §6.2 management |
| **Severe AD with food-trigger workup** | AD per B1-05 §5.1 Severe + food-trigger workup needed | §6.3: targeted SPT / sIgE; B1-05 §6.3 management; route §7.3 if persistent severe AD with multi-allergen sensitisation |

### 5.4 Cow's milk protein allergy (CMPA)

CMPA grading is jointly defined with B1-09 (which manages the nutritional adequacy of elimination):

| Pattern | Severity | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Non-IgE CMPA, mild-moderate** (loose stools, blood-streaked stools in well infant, mild eczema) | Mild-moderate | §6.2 (B1-06 + B1-09): 2–4 week elimination + structured re-introduction over 4–8 weeks; eHF formula where breastfeeding not possible [ESPGHAN-CF-2017] |
| **Non-IgE CMPA, severe (FPIES — Food Protein-Induced Enterocolitis Syndrome)** | Severe | §7.2 to pediatric GI / pediatric allergy + B1-09 |
| **IgE-mediated CMPA** (urticaria, vomiting within minutes) | — | §6.2: SPT / sIgE confirmation; elimination + action plan + auto-injector if anaphylaxis-pattern |
| **CMPA with anaphylaxis history** | — | §6.3 Intensive + §7.1 if active; never elimination-and-reintroduction without specialist supervision |

### 5.5 Urticaria

#### 5.5.1 Acute urticaria (< 6 weeks)

| Severity | Findings | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Localised hives, no systemic features, no airway involvement | §6.1: oral H1-antihistamine PRN; usually self-limited |
| **Moderate** | Generalised hives, no systemic features | §6.2: oral H1-antihistamine regularly for 1–2 weeks |
| **With angioedema (no airway)** | Generalised hives + facial / lip / extremity angioedema, no airway | §6.2: oral H1-antihistamine + short oral steroid course |
| **Anaphylaxis pattern** | Hives + airway / breathing / circulatory features | §7.1 emergency + B1-06 follow-up for action plan |

#### 5.5.2 Chronic urticaria (≥ 6 weeks)

| Severity | UAS7 | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Well-controlled** | < 7 | §6.1 maintenance H1-antihistamine |
| **Active disease** | 7–16 | §6.2: regular H1-antihistamine; if not controlled, up-dose to 4× standard pediatric dose [EAACI-Urticaria-2022] |
| **Severe / refractory** | > 16 OR significant QoL impact persistent on 4× H1-antihistamine | §7.3 to pediatric allergy specialist for omalizumab or alternative |

### 5.6 Drug allergy

| Severity | Findings | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild non-IgE pattern** | Maculopapular rash days into a course, no mucosal / systemic features | §6.1: document; alternative drug class for future use; avoid the implicated drug |
| **IgE-mediated mild-moderate** | Urticaria within minutes of drug; no anaphylaxis features | §6.2: document; alternative drug class; B1-06 issues drug-allergy alert card; routes §7.3 routine for drug-challenge consideration if alternative classes are limited |
| **Suspected severe drug reaction (SJS / TEN / DRESS / fixed drug eruption complex)** | — | §7.1 emergency (cross with B1-05 §7.1) |
| **Anaphylaxis to drug** | — | §7.1 + §6.3 long-term action plan + auto-injector + drug-avoidance documentation |

### 5.7 Insect venom allergy

| Severity | Findings | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Local reaction only** | Localised swelling, even if large; no systemic features | §6.1 reassure; no auto-injector indicated |
| **Systemic skin reaction (urticaria/angioedema only, no airway/cardiovascular)** | Systemic but skin-limited | §6.2 action plan + consider auto-injector based on family preference and severity (in pediatric, auto-injector is generally not mandated for skin-only systemic reactions per WAO) |
| **Anaphylaxis to insect venom** | Airway / breathing / circulatory features | §6.3 + §7.3 routine to pediatric allergy specialist for venom immunotherapy candidacy assessment [WAO-Anaphylaxis-2020] |

### 5.8 Aeroallergen sensitisation (asymptomatic positive testing)

| Pattern | Action |
|---|---|
| **Sensitised but asymptomatic** (positive SPT / sIgE without clinical symptoms) | §6.1: counsel; no treatment indicated; surveillance for symptom emergence |
| **Sensitised with mild rhinitis** | §6.1 / §6.2 per §5.1 |
| **Sensitised with moderate-severe rhinitis (monosensitised)** | §6.3 includes SLIT candidacy assessment |
| **Sensitised with significant asthma trigger** | Joint with B1-14 (when drafted); avoidance counselling |

### 5.9 Boundary rules

- *Borderline severity:* a child with rhinitis at the boundary between mild persistent and moderate-severe persistent is treated at the higher tier for the first treatment cycle; step-down at follow-up if symptoms support.
- *Re-grading after intervention:* re-administer the same scale; document delta. ARIA / TNSS / UAS7 at every follow-up.
- *Severity-progression triggers:* a child with previously mild persistent rhinitis whose symptoms worsen to moderate-severe persistent over 6 months despite first-line therapy → reassess for new sensitisation, asthma comorbidity, or environmental change.
- *Anaphylaxis-history overrides scale:* any child with a prior anaphylactic reaction is automatically managed at §6.3 with two auto-injectors regardless of subsequent SPT / sIgE result. Negative testing after a clear anaphylactic event does not de-escalate management without specialist OFC input.
- *Family-stress / caregiver-anxiety uplift:* a family with a child whose mild allergy is generating significant caregiver anxiety / restrictive practices is uplifted at least to Standard tier to support structured counselling and adequate-diet maintenance.

## §6 Therapeutic options

### 6.1 Light tier (Mild rhinitis, mild acute urticaria, mild aeroallergen sensitisation, drug-allergy documentation)

**Mild allergic rhinitis — saline + PRN antihistamine:**

| Drug | Indication | Pediatric dose |
|---|---|---|
| Saline nasal spray / drops | First-line, all ages | Several times daily PRN |
| Cetirizine | PRN for symptom relief | 2.5 mg PO once nightly (6m–2y) · 5 mg (2–6y) · 5–10 mg (≥ 6y) |
| Loratadine (alternative) | PRN for symptom relief | 5 mg (2–5y) · 10 mg (≥ 6y) once daily |
| Fexofenadine (alternative) | PRN | 30 mg BD (6m–2y) · 30 mg BD or 60 mg/day (2–11y) · 180 mg/day (≥ 12y) |

Behavioural / environmental:
- Allergen-avoidance counselling (per identified allergen).
- Dust-mite reduction (encasings on pillow / mattress; weekly washing in hot water; HEPA vacuuming; reduce upholstered toys / fabric).
- Pet dander: bathing the pet weekly reduces shedding; bedroom-restriction; HEPA filter.
- Pollen avoidance: window-closed during high-pollen periods; shower after outdoor activity; pollen-app monitoring.

Dispensary stock for §6.1: saline nasal spray; cetirizine syrup and tablets; loratadine syrup and tablets; fexofenadine tablets; allergen-avoidance counselling printed handouts (English / Hindi / regional); parent-app QR codes.

Expected timeline: improvement in 1–2 weeks; surveillance at 3 months for mild persistent.

### 6.2 Standard tier (Moderate rhinitis, confirmed food allergy without anaphylaxis history, moderate urticaria, IgE drug allergy mild-moderate)

**Moderate allergic rhinitis — intranasal corticosteroid + antihistamine:**

| Drug | Indication | Pediatric dose |
|---|---|---|
| Mometasone furoate nasal spray | Intranasal corticosteroid first-line | 50 µg/spray; one spray each nostril once daily ≥ 2 years; titrate up to 2 sprays each nostril if needed |
| Fluticasone furoate nasal spray (alternative) | Intranasal corticosteroid alternative | 27.5 µg/spray; one spray each nostril once daily ≥ 2 years |
| Cetirizine / loratadine / fexofenadine | Continued antihistamine per §6.1 | Regular daily dosing |
| Montelukast | Adjunctive for rhinitis-with-asthma overlap or persistent rhinitis with sleep disturbance | 4 mg PO HS (2–5y), 5 mg HS (6–14y), 10 mg HS (≥ 15y); counsel on neuropsychiatric side-effect black-box warning [Cochrane-CD001936] |

Counsel on nasal-spray technique: aim away from the septum (toward the lateral wall), don't sniff hard, prime the bottle weekly, allow 4–6 weeks for full effect.

**Confirmed food allergy without anaphylaxis history — structured elimination + action plan:**

Allergen-elimination dietetic counselling (joint with B1-09):
- Read-the-label training (English / Hindi / regional language; common cross-contamination considerations).
- Cross-reactivity counselling (peanut + tree nut; cow's milk + goat's milk; egg + chicken meat sensitivity is rare).
- Restaurant / school food safety.
- Travel and family-event planning.

Action plan generation (always written; English + family's primary language):
```
[Child's name] has [allergen] allergy.

Mild reaction (skin / mild oral itch / no airway):
  Give: cetirizine 5 mg / 10 mg PO
  Watch for 1 hour. If worsening, treat as severe.

Severe reaction (any of: difficulty breathing, throat tightness,
swelling of tongue / lips, wheeze, faint / collapse, vomiting + skin):
  Give: adrenaline auto-injector — outer mid-thigh, through clothing
        if needed.
  Call ambulance immediately.
  Lie down with legs raised (unless vomiting — recovery position).
  May give a second adrenaline injection after 5 minutes if no
  improvement.
  Give: cetirizine + asthma reliever inhaler if available
        (after adrenaline, not as substitute for adrenaline).
```

The action plan is signed by the SKIDS pediatrician + parent + adolescent (where applicable). A copy is attached to the school-letter (school-letter-generation per §9).

Adrenaline auto-injector dispensing:
- 7.5–25 kg child: 150 µg auto-injector (e.g., EpiPen Jr equivalent or Indian-market equivalent).
- ≥ 25 kg child: 300 µg auto-injector.
- < 7.5 kg infant: adrenaline 1:1000 ampoule + 1 mL syringe + IM-injection-technique counselling [WAO-Anaphylaxis-2020].
- Two auto-injectors dispensed at all times (school + home / on-person).
- Annual review for expiry; replacement at expiry.

Auto-injector technique demonstration: SKIDS allergy nurse demonstrates with a trainer device; parent + adolescent demonstrate back; competence documented.

**Acute urticaria (Moderate / generalised) and chronic urticaria mild-active:**

| Drug | Indication | Pediatric dose |
|---|---|---|
| Cetirizine | First-line H1-antihistamine | Per §6.1 |
| Loratadine / fexofenadine (alternatives) | If cetirizine causes drowsiness | Per §6.1 |
| Up-dose H1-antihistamine | Chronic urticaria UAS7 7–16 not controlled at standard dose | Up to 4× standard pediatric dose (off-label but EAACI-supported) [EAACI-Urticaria-2022] |
| Short oral corticosteroid course | Acute urticaria with significant angioedema | Prednisolone 1 mg/kg/day × 3–5 days (max 40 mg/day) |

**IgE-mediated mild-moderate drug allergy:**
- Document in PHR; alert flag activated.
- Drug-allergy alert card issued.
- Counsel on drug-class avoidance (e.g., avoid all penicillins in confirmed amoxicillin allergy; consider cephalosporin cross-reactivity counselling — historically overstated, now believed < 5% with selectively avoiding 1st-gen).
- Future drug-challenge consideration only with specialist input — §7.3 routine if needed.

Dispensary stock for §6.2: mometasone nasal spray; fluticasone nasal spray; montelukast tablets and sachets; antihistamines per §6.1 in larger volumes; adrenaline auto-injectors (150 µg and 300 µg — both stocked); adrenaline 1:1000 ampoules + 1 mL syringes; printed action-plan templates; trainer auto-injectors for technique demonstration; drug-allergy alert cards.

### 6.3 Intensive tier

**Moderate-severe persistent allergic rhinitis on triple-modality therapy:**
- Continue intranasal corticosteroid + oral antihistamine.
- Add montelukast (with neuropsychiatric counselling per black-box warning).
- Optimise allergen avoidance with structured environmental review.
- Consider SLIT candidacy if monosensitised (per §6.4).

**Confirmed food allergy WITH anaphylaxis history:**
- All §6.2 measures.
- Two auto-injectors at all times — never one (one in school, one on-person / home).
- Structured anaphylaxis re-education annually (action-plan re-rehearsal, technique re-demonstration).
- Annual review with B1-06; SPT / sIgE trajectory; resolution surveillance for milk / egg / wheat / soy (commonly outgrown); persistent for peanut / tree nut / fish / shellfish (less commonly outgrown).
- OFC for resolution candidacy only per §6.4 criteria.
- Adolescent transition: explicit teaching on self-carry, self-injection, peer-disclosure, restaurant / travel preparation.

**Sublingual immunotherapy (SLIT):**

Indications:
- Age ≥ 5 years.
- Monosensitised house-dust-mite OR single-pollen confirmed allergic rhinitis.
- Moderate-severe persistent rhinitis NOT controlled on optimised pharmacotherapy (intranasal steroid + antihistamine + montelukast for 12 weeks).
- Family commitment to a 3-year course.
- No severe asthma, no eosinophilic oesophagitis, no chronic systemic inflammatory disease, no on-going beta-blocker use.

Initiation:
- First dose given in-clinic with 30-minute observation period (low risk of systemic reaction with SLIT vs. SCIT).
- Daily home dosing; family trained on what-to-do for local reactions (oral itch is common and self-limited) vs. systemic (rare; action plan + auto-injector available).
- Duration: minimum 3 years for full benefit; clinical re-assessment at 6 months and annually.

Polysensitisation, food allergy SLIT (experimental), or any SCIT candidacy → §7.3.

### 6.4 Oral food challenge (OFC) at B1-06

OFC indications at B1-06 (low-risk only):
- Resolution surveillance for previously-confirmed milk / egg / wheat / soy in a child whose SPT / sIgE has decreased significantly.
- Initial cautious confirmation in a child with equivocal history + low-positive sIgE.
- NEVER for: peanut / tree-nut / fish / shellfish in a child with prior anaphylaxis (these route §7.3 to specialist OFC).
- NEVER for: any child with sIgE in the high-positive range (per allergen-specific cutoffs).
- NEVER for: any child with active asthma (FEV1 < 80% predicted on the day of challenge).
- NEVER for: any child < 1 year (specialist).

OFC procedure at B1-06:
- 4-hour observation in dedicated room with full resuscitation kit.
- SKIDS allergy nurse + on-call pediatrician for the duration.
- Incremental dosing starting at 1/100 final dose, doubling at 15-minute intervals to age-appropriate full dose.
- BP, HR, oxygen saturation, peak-flow monitored at every increment.
- Immediate cessation + adrenaline + ER routing on any objective sign of reaction (urticaria > localised, angioedema, wheeze, hypotension, oxygen desaturation).
- 4-hour observation post-final-dose before discharge.
- Family briefed pre-procedure on the protocol, the risks, the plan if reaction occurs, and the discharge plan if successful.
- OFC outcome documented in PHR; pass → continue surveillance with reintroduction; fail → continue elimination + counsel.

### 6.5 Atopy-prevention counselling for at-risk infants

For atopic-risk infants (eczema in infancy and / or family history of food allergy):

- **Peanut introduction per LEAP/NIAID-Peanut-2017:**
  - (a) *Severe eczema or egg allergy:* SPT before home introduction; if SPT positive → supervised OFC before introduction.
  - (b) *Mild-moderate eczema:* introduce peanut around 6 months.
  - (c) *No eczema or food allergy:* introduce freely per family readiness.
- **Egg:** introduce from 4–6 months regardless of eczema severity [ESPGHAN-CF-2017].
- Maintain breastfeeding to ≥ 6 months exclusive where possible.
- No restrictive maternal diet during pregnancy or breastfeeding for allergy prevention (no longer recommended).

### 6.6 Contraindications and precautions

| Therapeutic | Contraindication / precaution | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Oral H1-antihistamine sedating (chlorpheniramine, diphenhydramine) | Generally avoided in pediatric chronic use due to cognitive / school-performance impact | Use second-generation (cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine) for chronic indications |
| Intranasal corticosteroid in infants < 2y | Limited evidence | Specialist input; saline alone is first-line in this age |
| Montelukast | Black-box warning on neuropsychiatric side effects (mood changes, agitation, suicidality) | Mandatory family counselling at initiation; document; monitor for mood changes |
| Adrenaline auto-injector | None — auto-injector is always available for any anaphylaxis-risk child | Universal dispensing per §6 criteria |
| Oral steroid course | Repeated courses → adrenal-axis suppression risk; ≥ 4 courses/year is a concern | Specialist input if frequent steroid use needed |
| SLIT | Severe asthma, eosinophilic oesophagitis, beta-blocker, immunosuppression | Defer; specialist input |
| OFC | Active asthma, recent severe reaction, high-positive sIgE | Defer; specialist input |

### 6.7 Tapering and stopping

- *Antihistamine for chronic rhinitis / urticaria:* maintenance during symptomatic period; pause during asymptomatic months.
- *Intranasal corticosteroid for rhinitis:* continue during symptomatic season; can step down to PRN after 12 weeks of stable control.
- *Action plan and auto-injector:* indefinite for any confirmed allergy with anaphylaxis-risk pattern; annual review.
- *SLIT:* 3-year course; not abruptly stopped.
- *Elimination diet:* continued until OFC-confirmed resolution OR specialist guidance.

## §7 Hard referral triggers [SACRED SECTION]

The case escalates out of B1-06's scope when ANY of the following is true:

### 7.1 Emergency referral (same day, transport directly to ER)

- [ ] **Anaphylaxis at presentation** — airway / breathing / circulatory features with allergen exposure history. → IM adrenaline 0.01 mg/kg (max 0.3 mg) immediately at B1-06; supine position with legs raised; oxygen; IV access if available; transfer to named partner pediatric ER, within 1 hour [WAO-Anaphylaxis-2020]. Adrenaline given EVEN IF the diagnosis is uncertain — undertreated anaphylaxis is the harm-mode.
- [ ] **Suspected severe drug reaction (SJS / TEN / DRESS)** — mucosal involvement, sloughing, systemic features. → Named partner pediatric ER + pediatric dermatology + pediatric allergy specialist, within 1 hour. Immediate cessation of suspect drug. (Cross-reference with B1-05 §7.1.)
- [ ] **Suspected hereditary angioedema acute attack** — angioedema without urticaria, especially with abdominal pain or laryngeal involvement. → Named partner pediatric ER, within 1 hour. C1-esterase inhibitor concentrate administered en route if available.
- [ ] **Severe drug-induced anaphylaxis** — beyond skin-only, with airway / breathing / circulatory. → As anaphylaxis emergency above.
- [ ] **OFC or SPT-induced systemic reaction at B1-06** — in-clinic anaphylaxis from challenge or testing. → IM adrenaline + standard anaphylaxis protocol + transfer to named partner pediatric ER (within network), within 1 hour.

### 7.2 Urgent referral (within 7 days)

- [ ] **Severe chronic spontaneous urticaria (UAS7 > 16) refractory to 4× H1-antihistamine** with significant QoL impact. → Named partner pediatric allergy specialist for omalizumab consideration, within 7 days [EAACI-Urticaria-2022].
- [ ] **Suspected hereditary angioedema (recurrent angioedema without urticaria, family history, abdominal-pain attacks)** with confirmatory C4 / C1-esterase results pending or low. → Named partner pediatric allergy specialist + pediatric immunology, within 7 days.
- [ ] **Suspected mastocytosis** — persistent flushing, urticaria pigmentosa, anaphylaxis without clear trigger, elevated baseline tryptase. → Named partner pediatric allergy specialist + pediatric haematology / oncology, within 7 days.
- [ ] **FPIES (Food Protein-Induced Enterocolitis Syndrome) suspected** — repeated severe vomiting / dehydration after specific food exposure, especially in infants. → Named partner pediatric allergy + pediatric GI + B1-09, within 7 days.
- [ ] **Suspected eosinophilic oesophagitis** — feeding difficulty, dysphagia, food impaction, vomiting in an atopic child. → Named partner pediatric GI for endoscopy + biopsy, within 7 days.

### 7.3 Routine referral (next available appointment, ≤ 4 weeks)

- [ ] **Refractory allergic rhinitis** — moderate-severe persistent symptoms despite 12 weeks of optimised §6.3 with documented adherence. → Named partner pediatric allergy specialist OR pediatric ENT (for nasal endoscopy / polyposis surveillance), within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Multiple food allergy** — ≥ 3 confirmed food allergens with overlapping risk OR rapidly evolving polysensitisation. → Named partner pediatric allergy specialist for OIT candidacy assessment + comprehensive avoidance planning, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Oral immunotherapy (OIT) candidacy** — family request for OIT for peanut / tree-nut / milk / egg. → Named partner pediatric allergy specialist with OIT programme, within 4 weeks. B1-06 does not initiate OIT.
- [ ] **High-risk OFC indication** — peanut / tree-nut / fish / shellfish OFC OR any OFC in child with prior anaphylaxis OR high-positive sIgE. → Named partner pediatric allergy specialist with OFC infrastructure, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Insect venom anaphylaxis history** for venom-immunotherapy candidacy. → Named partner pediatric allergy specialist, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Polysensitisation aeroallergen** beyond monosensitised SLIT scope OR SCIT candidacy. → Named partner pediatric allergy specialist, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Suspected primary immunodeficiency** with allergic-disease comorbidity. → Named partner pediatric immunology + B1-19 Infections cross-care, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Drug-allergy challenge candidacy** — confirmed allergy where alternative drug class is unavailable / unsuitable AND drug-challenge is needed. → Named partner pediatric allergy specialist with challenge infrastructure, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Patch testing for delayed contact-allergic dermatitis** — refractory contact dermatitis cross-care with B1-05. → Named partner pediatric dermatology / allergy specialist, within 4 weeks.

### 7.4 Handoff communication

When B1-06 fires a §7 referral, the SKIDS pediatrician + allergy nurse sends the receiving clinician (auto-generated by Companion):

1. **Child identification:** name, DOB, child_id, parent contact, parent consent confirmation, current medications, all known allergens with severity classification.
2. **The trigger:** which §7 sub-trigger fired with the underlying clinical / biochemical finding.
3. **Diagnosis (provisional or confirmed):** ICD-10 / atopic comorbidities.
4. **Allergy testing data:** SPT results (allergen, wheal size, +/-), sIgE values (allergen, kU/L, allergen-specific reference range), tryptase if obtained, complement / C1-esterase if obtained.
5. **Reaction history:** all prior reactions in chronological order with severity grading.
6. **Therapies tried at B1-06 and outcomes:** specific drug, dose, duration, response.
7. **Action plan and auto-injector status:** action plan attached; auto-injectors dispensed (date, lot, expiry); technique-demonstration competence noted.
8. **Bridge interventions started.**
9. **Reason for referral, in clinical terms.**
10. **Urgency tier and target window.**
11. **Named partner / clinic** + appointment booking status.

The packet is auto-generated by Companion. Reviewed and signed off. For §7.1 emergencies, phone-call confirmation in addition.

## §8 Follow-up schedule

### 8.1 Follow-up frequency

| Severity tier / condition | First follow-up | Subsequent | Annual review |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mild rhinitis | 3 months | A1 cadence | Yes |
| Moderate rhinitis on combination therapy | 6 weeks | every 3 months | Yes |
| Severe persistent rhinitis on §6.3 | 4 weeks | every 6 weeks | Yes |
| Confirmed food allergy without anaphylaxis | 3 months (action-plan reinforcement) | annual SPT / sIgE for outgrowth surveillance | Yes |
| Confirmed food allergy WITH anaphylaxis | 4 weeks (action-plan reinforcement); annual full review | every 12 months | Yes |
| Acute urticaria | 2 weeks if persistent | discharge if resolved | — |
| Chronic urticaria active | 4 weeks | every 6–12 weeks | Yes |
| Drug allergy (mild-moderate) | 1 visit for documentation; alert-card issued | as needed | Yes |
| Insect venom (anaphylaxis history) | 4 weeks | annual | Yes |
| SLIT initiation | 1 week (early-tolerance check) | every 3 months × 1 year then biannual | Yes |
| OFC pass (resolution confirmed) | 4 weeks (re-introduction success) | annual | Yes |
| OFC fail | 4 weeks (counselling + auto-injector technique re-check) | annual SPT / sIgE | Yes |

### 8.2 What to recheck at each follow-up

- *Rhinitis:* TNSS / ARIA classification; medication adherence; allergen-avoidance behaviour; surface-disease cross-care (B1-01 conjunctivitis, B1-02 OME).
- *Food allergy:* action plan re-rehearsed; auto-injector technique re-demonstrated; auto-injector expiry checked; school-letter status; SPT / sIgE trajectory at annual.
- *Urticaria:* UAS7; medication effectiveness at current dose.
- *SLIT:* adherence; local-reaction tolerance; rhinitis symptom score delta.
- *Cross-care:* B1-05 AD status; B1-09 dietary adequacy on elimination; B1-14 asthma control (when active).

### 8.3 Treatment success criteria

- *Rhinitis:* TNSS ≤ 3 or ARIA-classified well-controlled; minimal medication burden; no school / sleep impact.
- *Food allergy:* no accidental exposures requiring adrenaline; family confident in action plan; auto-injectors carried and unexpired; school-letter on file.
- *Urticaria:* UAS7 < 7 sustained for ≥ 4 weeks.
- *SLIT:* TNSS reduction ≥ 30% by 12 months; sustained at 3-year course completion.

### 8.4 Step-down criteria

- Rhinitis: step down intranasal corticosteroid frequency once symptoms controlled for 12 weeks.
- Antihistamine: pause during asymptomatic months.
- SLIT: course completed at 3 years; not tapered.

### 8.5 Step-up criteria (within Tier 1)

- Rhinitis Mild not controlled on antihistamine alone → add intranasal corticosteroid.
- Rhinitis Standard not controlled on combination → add montelukast and SLIT candidacy review.
- Urticaria Standard at standard antihistamine → up-dose to 4× standard.

### 8.6 Step-out criteria

Per §7. Specifically: refractory rhinitis after 12 weeks of optimised §6.3; multi-food allergy; OIT candidacy; severe chronic urticaria refractory to 4× antihistamine; high-risk OFC; SCIT candidacy; suspected HAE / mastocytosis.

### 8.7 Disenrolment / loss-to-follow-up

A child active on B1-06 management with anaphylaxis-risk allergy who misses the annual auto-injector renewal visit is a high-priority loss-to-follow-up — auto-injector expiry without replacement leaves the family unprotected. Manager + named ped contact within 30 days of due date; phone follow-up if no response. Quarterly-review trigger.

### 9.6 Edge cases and overrides

**Anaphylaxis at B1-06.** SOP — IM adrenaline, supine-with-legs-raised, oxygen, ER call; all logged real-time in Companion. The protocol's §7.1 trigger fires; visit becomes a §7.1 visit; full incident report generated for §12 review.

**Resuscitation-kit checklist incomplete before SPT / OFC.** Hard block — Companion does not allow SPT / OFC to commence without the kit-check completion.

**Auto-injector out of stock.** Manager alert; family advised on alternative (adrenaline ampoule + IM kit); referral to alternative pharmacy if needed; flagged for §12 review.

**Family declines auto-injector despite anaphylaxis-risk pattern.** Document refusal with structured reason; family-signed acknowledgement of risk; reassessment at every visit. Continued refusal at second visit → escalate to protocol owner.

**SLIT systemic reaction at first dose.** Adrenaline + supportive care + ER routing; SLIT discontinued; document as adverse event; no further SLIT at B1-06 for this child.

**Offline behaviour.** Action plan generation, auto-injector dispensing record, alert generation locally cached. §7.1 in offline mode — phone-call ER guidance.

## §10 Parent-facing communication

### 10.1 Scripted explanations

When grading is Mild allergic rhinitis:

> "[Aanya]'s nasal symptoms are consistent with allergic rhinitis — her nose reacts to something in the air, most likely [identified allergen]. We're going to use saline rinses and a non-drowsy antihistamine when she's symptomatic. The two big things at home: reduce dust-mite exposure with the encasing-and-washing routine I'll show you in the parent app, and start the antihistamine when symptoms flare. We'll see her back in three months. If she's needing the antihistamine daily, we step up to a nasal steroid spray."

When confirming a food allergy with auto-injector dispensing:

> "[Rohit] has a confirmed allergy to [peanut]. The skin test and his history match. We're going to do three things today. First, the dietitian will show you how to read labels and avoid peanut in cooking and packaged food. Second, I'm writing a clear action plan that tells you exactly what to do for a mild reaction and what to do for a severe one — the severe ones are the ones we have to take seriously. Third, you'll leave today with two adrenaline auto-injectors. Our nurse will demonstrate how to use them; you'll demonstrate back; and we'll send you a video to review at home. Two injectors — one for school, one for home / on-person. Annual replacement; we'll remind you. With the avoidance and the action plan, most children with peanut allergy go through childhood without a major reaction. The action plan is what makes the difference if there is a reaction."

When the family arrives with anaphylaxis history but no action plan (closing the A1 §7.1 gap):

> "I see [Rohit] had a severe reaction last year. From today, that's not happening without preparation. We do three things at this visit: an allergen-confirmation test for what we suspect; an action plan that we sign together; and you leave with the adrenaline injectors and the technique training. The reason I'm being firm about all three: an action plan + adrenaline at hand is the difference between a scary moment and a hospital admission. We'll do this carefully and you'll feel confident at the end."

When discussing SLIT initiation:

> "[Aanya]'s rhinitis isn't fully controlled even with the spray and tablet. We have one more option in this clinic — sublingual immunotherapy. A small dose of the allergen, under her tongue, every day for three years. The body learns not to over-react. About two-thirds of children get significant improvement. The first dose we give here today with thirty minutes of observation. After that, daily at home. There's local mouth-tingling that's normal; serious reactions are rare. It's a three-year commitment, and it's only worth doing if we're confident we'll stick with it. Want to think about it for a week?"

When OFC is scheduled:

> "[Rohit]'s milk allergy may have resolved — his blood test has come down. We're going to do a controlled food challenge here at the clinic. Small amount, escalating gradually, watching closely for four hours. Best case, he tolerates milk and we re-introduce it at home. If there's a reaction, we treat it here immediately — we have everything ready. The pre-test fasting is overnight; bring his favourite distractions; the four hours will feel long. We do this with one nurse and a doctor present the whole time."

When §7.1 anaphylaxis at presentation fires:

> "[Aanya] is having a serious allergic reaction right now. I'm giving her adrenaline. We need to get her to [partner ER] today. Stay with her; lay her back with legs raised; she'll feel better soon. The injection will take effect in minutes. If she's not improving, I have a second injection ready. Once she's stable, the team there will continue monitoring."

### 10.5 Cultural sensitivities

- *"We just won't eat that food" minimisation.* Indian families may underestimate the risk of accidental exposure (cross-contamination at street food, hidden ingredients in spice mixes, religious-festival sweets containing nuts). Counsel explicitly on cross-contamination risk; demonstrate the action plan + auto-injector explicitly.
- *Dietary-restriction stigma.* Vegetarian / Jain families may interpret a child's food allergy as catalogued food preference; non-vegetarian families may dismiss "fussiness" framing. Frame the allergy in objective-test terms (SPT, sIgE) to give it medical legitimacy.
- *Auto-injector hesitancy.* Adrenaline injection is unfamiliar to many Indian families. Demonstrate with the trainer; let the family practise; demonstrate that the device is intramuscular not intravenous, that the needle is hidden, that it's designed for non-medical use. Documented family-disclosed worry.
- *Festival foods and family events.* Peanut, tree-nut, dairy are core to Indian sweets; managing food allergy at family events is socially difficult. Help the family rehearse: the carry-your-own-food strategy; the polite-refusal script; the auto-injector-discreetly-on-person plan; the trusted-relative-trained-on-injector arrangement.
- *Drug-allergy disclosure stigma.* Some families fear that documented drug allergy will limit future medical treatment or insurance. Frame the alert as protective rather than restrictive — the alternative is unsafe re-exposure.
- *Air pollution and rhinitis amplification.* Indian urban air pollution is a real comorbidity; counsel on the combined effect; consider AQI-based avoidance strategies; do not pretend air pollution can be eliminated — that's unrealistic.
- *Religious / traditional remedies.* Honey, mustard-oil application, ayurvedic preparations are commonly used for rhinitis and urticaria. Engage; some are harmless adjuncts (honey for soothing, saline equivalent), some have unpredictable interactions; discuss specific preparations.
- *School-letter responsiveness varies.* Some Indian schools are highly responsive; some are not. Frame the letter as the clinical recommendation; offer to discuss with the school directly if the family wishes. Document school engagement.

## §12 Quality and audit

### 12.1 Outcome metrics

B1-06 is working well when:

- ≥ 80% of allergic rhinitis cases achieve ARIA well-controlled status by 12 weeks of stepped therapy.
- 100% of anaphylaxis-risk families leave the visit with action plan + dispensed auto-injectors + technique-demonstrated competence.
- ≥ 95% of auto-injector replacements happen before expiry (loss-to-follow-up rate < 5%).
- ≥ 70% of moderate-severe persistent rhinitis on §6.3 with SLIT achieve TNSS reduction ≥ 30% by 12 months.
- 100% of in-clinic OFCs are conducted with full resuscitation kit verified pre-procedure (Companion enforcement).
- < 1% of in-clinic OFCs result in severe reaction requiring ER transfer (low-risk-OFC selection metric).
- ≥ 90% of families with previously-confirmed allergy attend the annual review.
- 100% of A1 §7.1 anaphylaxis-without-action-plan cases close at the B1-06 visit (action plan + auto-injector dispensed before discharge).
- ≥ 90% of school-letters generated reach the school within 7 days.
- Drug-allergy documentation is complete on all relevant cases (alert card issued, drug-class avoidance counselled).

### 12.2 Quarterly case review

The protocol owner reviews every quarter:
- A random sample of 30 B1-06 visits balanced across rhinitis / food allergy / urticaria.
- All §7.1 anaphylaxis events (in-clinic and presented).
- All in-clinic OFCs (selection appropriateness, procedural fidelity, outcome).
- All SLIT initiations and discontinuations.
- All auto-injector dispensing and expiry-tracking adherence.
- All cases of confirmed multi-food allergy (routing appropriateness).
- All cases of suspected severe drug reaction.
- Loss-to-follow-up cases on auto-injector renewal.

Output: 1-page report to SKIDS clinical operations.
