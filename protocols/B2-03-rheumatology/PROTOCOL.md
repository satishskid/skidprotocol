# SKIDS Rheumatology Clinic

> The SKIDS Rheumatology Clinic is a Tier 2 service requiring a
> Fellowship-credentialed pediatric rheumatologist. It manages
> juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA), systemic lupus erythematosus
> (SLE), juvenile dermatomyositis, vasculitis, periodic fever
> syndromes, and autoimmune connective-tissue disorders.
> Available only at SKIDS clinics where a credentialed
> rheumatologist is assigned.

---

## §1 Service definition

**What this clinic does:**
- Diagnoses and manages juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA) — all subtypes per ILAR classification: oligoarticular, polyarticular RF− and RF+, systemic (sJIA), enthesitis-related (ERA), psoriatic, undifferentiated. Treatment: NSAIDs, intra-articular corticosteroid injections, methotrexate, biologic agents (etanercept, adalimumab, tocilizumab per subtype).
- Diagnoses and manages childhood SLE: ACR/EULAR criteria, hydroxychloroquine, corticosteroids, immunosuppressives (mycophenolate, azathioprine, cyclophosphamide for nephritis), anti-dsDNA/complement monitoring, renal surveillance.
- Manages juvenile dermatomyositis: Bohan and Peter criteria, CK/aldolase/LDH, MRI of muscles, methotrexate + corticosteroids, IVIG for refractory.
- Manages vasculitis: IgA vasculitis (Henoch-Schönlein purpura) — supportive + renal monitoring; Kawasaki disease acute management coordination with B1-16 (IVIG + aspirin + echo surveillance); polyarteritis nodosa, granulomatosis with polyangiitis (rare — refer to tertiary rheumatology if complex).
- Manages periodic fever syndromes (PFAPA — most common in children): tonsillectomy referral coordination with B1-03 if PFAPA confirmed; familial Mediterranean fever (colchicine); other autoinflammatory syndromes (genetic testing + IL-1 blockade where indicated).
- Manages uveitis surveillance in JIA (oligoarticular JIA — highest uveitis risk): coordinates with B1-01 Vision / pediatric ophthalmologist for slit-lamp screening per ACR schedule.
- Manages medication monitoring: methotrexate (FBC + LFT monthly × 3 months then 3-monthly); biologics (infection screen, TB screen pre-initiation, vaccination status cross B1-22).
- Cross-cares with B1-08 (mood/behavioural impact of chronic illness), B1-09 (nutrition in chronic inflammation), B1-14 (respiratory involvement in SLE/dermatomyositis).

**What this clinic does not do:**
- Operate without a Fellowship-credentialed pediatric rheumatologist.
- Perform joint aspiration (office procedure by rheumatologist) — most SKIDS clinics may not have procedural setup; routes to rheumatology partner facility if needed.
- Manage lupus nephritis requiring renal biopsy (→ §7.3 nephrology for biopsy; B2-03 manages immunosuppression).
- Manage macrophage activation syndrome (MAS) in sJIA — §7.1 ER + tertiary rheumatology (life-threatening).

**Tier 2 credential requirement:** Pediatric rheumatologist with DM Pediatric Rheumatology or equivalent Fellowship.

**When B2-03 is NOT available:** Cases route to named external pediatric rheumatologist partner (Tier 3). There is no Tier 1 B1 rheumatology protocol — B2-03 is the only SKIDS entry point for rheumatological care.

**A1 routing gap:** A1 currently has no explicit screening domain or §6.3 Orange routing for rheumatological presentations. Children with joint swelling, prolonged fever with rash, or autoimmune features enter B2-03 via: (1) clinical suspicion at any B1 visit, (2) GP/external referral, (3) B1-19 FUO workup identifying autoimmune cause. The editorial pass should consider adding an A1 §6.3 row for musculoskeletal/autoimmune concerns routing to B2-03 (where available) or to named rheumatology partner.

## §3–§12 (Tier 2 structure)

**§3:** Receives from: B1-19 §7.3 (FUO with autoimmune features), any B1 visit where joint swelling / prolonged fever / rash / autoimmune features identified, GP/external referral. B1-03 cross (PFAPA tonsillectomy), B1-16 cross (Kawasaki echo surveillance), B1-01 cross (uveitis screening in JIA).

**§4:** Rheumatological examination (joint count — swollen/tender, range of motion, enthesitis assessment, skin — rash distribution, nailfold capillaroscopy where available, muscle strength — Childhood Myositis Assessment Scale). Investigations: ANA, anti-dsDNA, ENA panel, RF, anti-CCP, complement (C3/C4), CK/aldolase, ESR/CRP, FBC, urinalysis (lupus nephritis screening), joint ultrasound/MRI, slit-lamp ophthalmology referral.

**§5 [SACRED]:** JIA severity by joint count + functional limitation (JADAS-27 score); SLE severity by SLEDAI score; MAS diagnostic criteria (2016 ACR/EULAR for sJIA): fever in known/suspected sJIA + ferritin > 684 ng/mL + any 2 of: platelet count ≤ 181 × 10⁹/L, AST > 48 U/L, triglycerides > 156 mg/dL, fibrinogen ≤ 360 mg/dL. MAS = §7.1 emergency — life-threatening; mortality 8–22%.

**§6:** JIA: NSAIDs (naproxen 10–15 mg/kg/day BD) → intra-articular triamcinolone (oligo) → methotrexate 10–15 mg/m²/week (polyarticular) → biologic (etanercept/adalimumab if methotrexate-refractory). SLE: hydroxychloroquine (all patients) + prednisolone (flares) + mycophenolate/azathioprine (maintenance) + cyclophosphamide (nephritis induction). PFAPA: single-dose prednisolone aborts episodes; tonsillectomy (B1-03 cross) is curative in most. Kawasaki: IVIG 2 g/kg single dose + aspirin: high-dose 30–50 mg/kg/day in 4 divided doses during acute inflammatory phase, continued until afebrile for 48–72 hours; then low-dose 3–5 mg/kg/day single daily dose (antiplatelet) for 6–8 weeks if no coronary aneurysm, or indefinitely if coronary aneurysm present; echo at diagnosis, 2 weeks, 6 weeks (B1-16 cross).

**§7 [SACRED]:**
- §7.1: MAS in sJIA (→ ER + tertiary rheumatology — ferritin > 10,000, liver failure, DIC); lupus cerebritis (→ ER); acute renal failure in lupus nephritis (→ ER + nephrology).
- §7.2: New-onset polyarthritis with systemic features → B2-03 within 7 days; lupus nephritis (proteinuria + haematuria in known SLE) → nephrology within 7 days.
- §7.3: JIA not responding to methotrexate → tertiary rheumatology for biologic initiation (if not available at this SKIDS clinic); lupus nephritis biopsy → nephrology; uveitis in JIA → ophthalmology.

## §8 Follow-up framework

### 8.1 Condition-specific follow-up schedule

| Condition | Interval | Key assessments | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| JIA — active disease, on NSAID/methotrexate titration | Every 4 weeks | Joint count (swollen + tender), JADAS-27, ESR/CRP, methotrexate labs (FBC + LFT), medication tolerability, growth | Until JADAS ≤ 1 (inactive disease) on stable dose |
| JIA — inactive disease on stable therapy | Every 3 months | JADAS-27, joint count, ESR/CRP, methotrexate labs (3-monthly), growth, functional status | Ongoing; medication taper discussion at 6–12 months inactive disease |
| JIA — on biologic therapy | Every 3 months | JADAS-27, joint count, ESR/CRP, infection screen, injection-site assessment, growth, vaccination status review (cross B1-22) | Ongoing while on biologic |
| JIA — uveitis screening (high-risk: oligo, ANA+, age < 7, disease duration < 4y) | Every 3 months | Slit-lamp examination by ophthalmologist (cross B1-01); document grade if uveitis present | Until 4 years after disease onset AND age > 7; then every 6 months for 3 years; then annual |
| JIA — uveitis screening (lower risk) | Every 6–12 months | Slit-lamp examination | Per ACR 2019 risk-stratification schedule |
| SLE — active disease / flare management | Every 2–4 weeks | SLEDAI, anti-dsDNA, complement (C3/C4), urinalysis, FBC, renal function, medication tolerability | Until SLEDAI < 4 on stable therapy |
| SLE — maintenance / quiescent | Every 1–3 months | SLEDAI, anti-dsDNA, complement, urinalysis, FBC, renal function, bone density (if on chronic steroids), growth | Ongoing — lifelong |
| Juvenile dermatomyositis — active | Every 4 weeks | Muscle strength (CMAS), CK/aldolase/LDH, skin rash assessment, steroid side-effects, methotrexate labs | Until muscle enzymes normalised and strength improving on stable therapy |
| Juvenile dermatomyositis — maintenance | Every 3 months | CMAS, muscle enzymes, skin, medication monitoring | Ongoing; steroid taper guided by CMAS and enzymes |
| Kawasaki disease — post-acute | 2 weeks, 6 weeks, 6 months, 12 months post-acute | Echo (coronary artery dimensions, Z-scores), ESR/CRP, platelet count, aspirin compliance | If no coronary aneurysm: discharge echo surveillance at 12 months. If aneurysm: ongoing per cardiology (cross B1-16) |
| PFAPA | Every 3 months | Episode frequency diary, growth, tonsillectomy referral status if indicated | Until episodes cease (most resolve by age 10) or tonsillectomy performed |
| IgA vasculitis (HSP) | 2 weeks, 4 weeks, 3 months, 6 months, 12 months | Urinalysis (haematuria, proteinuria), blood pressure, renal function, abdominal symptoms | If no renal involvement at 6 months: discharge. If nephritis: ongoing per nephrology |

### 8.2 Follow-up visit structure

Each follow-up visit includes:
1. Symptom review: joint symptoms, skin, fatigue, fever, new organ involvement.
2. Joint examination (full joint count — swollen and tender) and/or disease-specific examination.
3. Disease activity score calculation (JADAS-27 for JIA, SLEDAI for SLE, CMAS for JDM).
4. Laboratory results review (FBC, ESR/CRP, LFT, renal function, complement, autoantibodies, urinalysis).
5. Medication reconciliation: current drugs, doses, adherence, side-effects, lab monitoring results.
6. Growth and nutritional status (cross B1-09 if growth concern, especially if on chronic steroids).
7. Psychosocial screen: school attendance, mood, peer relationships (cross B1-08 if concern).
8. Uveitis screening compliance check (JIA).
9. Plan adjustment: dose change, escalation (NSAID → methotrexate → biologic), §7 referral if threshold met.
10. Parent counselling per §10.
11. Next follow-up scheduling per §8.1 table.

### 8.3 Loss-to-follow-up protocol

| Condition | Trigger | Action |
|---|---|---|
| JIA on methotrexate | Missed follow-up by 14 days | Manager phone call; medication refill and lab check |
| JIA on biologic | Missed follow-up by 14 days | Manager phone call; biologic injection scheduling |
| SLE on immunosuppression | Missed follow-up by 14 days | Manager + rheumatologist phone; urinalysis status check |
| JIA uveitis screening overdue | Missed by 30 days | Manager phone call; ophthalmology appointment re-scheduling |
| Kawasaki echo overdue | Missed scheduled echo | Manager phone call; echo re-scheduling (cross B1-16) |
| JDM on steroids | Missed follow-up by 14 days | Manager phone call; steroid-tapering status check |

## §9 Companion software workflow

### 9.1 Doctor Module screens

**Screen 1: Visit context**

- Renders referral source (B1-19 FUO, GP, external, B1-03 PFAPA, B1-16 Kawasaki, internal B1 visit) with reason and attached documents.
- Shows previous B2-03 visit history: JADAS / SLEDAI trend, joint count trend, medication history, investigation results, uveitis screening dates.
- Auto-calculates time since diagnosis, time on current therapy, time since last lab monitoring.

**Screen 2: Rheumatological examination**

- Interactive body diagram for joint count: tap each joint to mark swollen (S), tender (T), or limited range of motion (L). Supports 27 joints per JADAS-27.
- Enthesitis assessment (ERA subtype): enthesitis count, specific sites.
- Skin examination: rash distribution (butterfly rash, heliotrope, Gottron's papules, psoriatic), nailfold capillaroscopy findings.
- Muscle strength: CMAS score entry (JDM).
- Systemic features: fever log, serositis symptoms, oral ulcers, alopecia, Raynaud's.
- Growth assessment: height, weight, BMI (auto-plotted; steroid-related growth impact flagged).

**Screen 3: Investigation ordering and review**

- Autoimmune panel: ANA, anti-dsDNA, ENA panel, RF, anti-CCP, complement (C3/C4).
- Inflammatory markers: ESR, CRP, ferritin.
- Organ-specific: urinalysis (dipstick + microscopy), renal function, LFT, FBC with differential.
- Disease-specific: CK/aldolase/LDH (JDM), uric acid (gout mimic screen).
- Methotrexate monitoring: FBC + LFT (auto-prompted per schedule — monthly for 3 months, then 3-monthly).
- Biologic pre-screening: TB screening (Mantoux / IGRA), hepatitis B/C, HIV, varicella immunity (cross B1-22).
- Imaging: joint ultrasound, joint MRI, muscle MRI (JDM), echo (Kawasaki — cross B1-16).
- Genetic testing: autoinflammatory gene panel (FMF, TRAPS, CAPS, MKD) for suspected periodic fever syndromes.

**Screen 4: Diagnosis and disease activity scoring (§5)**

- JIA: subtype classification (ILAR dropdown), JADAS-27 auto-calculated from joint count + physician global + parent global + ESR. Disease status: active / inactive (JADAS ≤ 1) / clinical remission on medication / clinical remission off medication.
- SLE: SLEDAI auto-calculated from clinical and lab inputs. Classification: active flare / low disease activity / remission.
- JDM: CMAS score, enzyme levels, disease status.
- MAS screening: auto-flags when sJIA patient has ferritin > 684 ng/mL; triggers MAS checklist (2016 ACR/EULAR criteria). **MAS = §7.1 emergency — Companion hard-blocks visit completion and forces §7.1 packet.**
- Mandatory disease-activity scoring before proceeding. Override with free-text reason if doctor disagrees.

**Screen 5: Treatment plan**

- NSAID prescribing: naproxen dose calculator (10–15 mg/kg/day BD).
- Intra-articular corticosteroid: joint selection from body diagram, triamcinolone hexacetonide dose by joint size.
- Methotrexate prescribing: dose calculator (10–15 mg/m²/week), route (oral/subcutaneous), folic acid co-prescription auto-added.
- Biologic prescribing: drug selection (etanercept, adalimumab, tocilizumab — subtype-matched recommendation displayed), pre-screening checklist (TB, hepatitis, varicella — Companion blocks prescription until pre-screening complete), injection scheduler.
- SLE treatment: hydroxychloroquine (all patients — auto-added), prednisolone dose and taper schedule, immunosuppressive selection and dose.
- Kawasaki acute: IVIG dose calculator (2 g/kg), aspirin dose calculator (high-dose 30–50 mg/kg/day → low-dose 3–5 mg/kg/day transition), echo scheduling.
- PFAPA: prednisolone single-dose for episode abortion, tonsillectomy referral to B1-03.
- Physiotherapy prescription (JIA — joint mobility, strengthening).

**Screen 6: §7 review (only renders if any §7 trigger fires)**

Pauses the standard flow; renders the §7 handoff packet. MAS in sJIA or lupus cerebritis or acute renal failure = §7.1: mandatory phone-call-confirmed checkbox to receiving ER + tertiary rheumatology. Mandatory acknowledgement before visit completion.

**Screen 7: Follow-up scheduling (§8)**

Auto-suggests next B2-03 visit per §8.1 table. Auto-schedules uveitis screening appointment (ophthalmology cross B1-01) per risk-stratification schedule. Auto-schedules Kawasaki echo per timeline (cross B1-16). Auto-schedules methotrexate lab monitoring per protocol.

### 9.6 Edge cases and overrides

**MAS screening criteria met in sJIA patient.** Companion hard-blocks visit completion. §7.1 packet is rendered immediately. Doctor must confirm phone call to receiving ER and tertiary rheumatology before the visit can close. No override — MAS is life-threatening (mortality 8–22%).

**Biologic pre-screening incomplete.** Companion blocks biologic prescription until TB screen, hepatitis B/C, HIV, and varicella immunity are documented. Doctor cannot override — infection risk on biologics is unacceptable without screening.

**Methotrexate lab results show transaminase elevation > 3x ULN.** Companion auto-flags and displays: "Hold methotrexate. Recheck LFT in 2 weeks. Resume at reduced dose only when transaminases < 2x ULN." Doctor may override with documented reason; override logged for §12 review.

**Doctor disagrees with auto-calculated JADAS.** Doctor uses override on Screen 4 with mandatory free-text reason. Logged for §12 review.

**Offline behaviour.** Doctor Module supports offline B2-03 visits: examination, joint count, diagnosis, prescription all locally cached and synced on reconnection. §7 emergency triggers (MAS) in offline mode trigger a hard-stop with phone-call-required guidance.

## §10 Parent-facing communication

### 10.1 Scripted explanations

**JIA — new diagnosis:**

> "The swelling in [Aanya]'s joints is caused by her immune system attacking the joint lining by mistake. This is called juvenile arthritis. It is not the same as the arthritis older people get — most children do very well with treatment, and many achieve remission, meaning no active disease at all, within a few years. The medicine we use calms the immune system down so the joints can heal and [she] can move and play normally. We will monitor [her] closely to make sure the treatment is working and adjust it if needed."

**JIA — escalation to methotrexate:**

> "The anti-inflammatory medicine alone has not been enough to control [Aanya]'s joint inflammation. We need to add a medicine called methotrexate. I know the name sounds strong — it is also used in cancer treatment, but at much higher doses. The dose we use for arthritis is very low and is one of the best-studied medicines in children's rheumatology. It works by calming the immune system more effectively. We will do regular blood tests — monthly at first, then every three months — to make sure [her] liver and blood counts are fine. Most children tolerate it well. It takes about 6–8 weeks to start working."

**JIA — escalation to biologic:**

> "Methotrexate has helped but [Aanya]'s arthritis is still active. The next step is a biologic medicine — an injection given [weekly / fortnightly] that targets the specific part of the immune system causing the inflammation. These are very effective medicines. Before we start, we need to do some screening tests — a TB test, a blood test for hepatitis, and a check on [her] vaccinations. Once we start, we will monitor [her] regularly for infections, because the medicine lowers one part of the immune system."

**SLE — new diagnosis:**

> "Lupus is a condition where the immune system becomes overactive and can affect many parts of the body — skin, joints, kidneys, blood. It is a lifelong condition, but with the right medication and monitoring, most children with lupus live full, active lives. The medicine does not cure lupus — it controls it, so that the overactive immune system does less damage. We will need to monitor [Aanya]'s kidneys regularly, because that is the part of the body we need to protect most carefully."

**Kawasaki disease — acute phase counselling:**

> "[Aanya] has Kawasaki disease. This is a condition that causes inflammation in blood vessels, and we need to treat it promptly to protect the blood vessels around [her] heart. We are giving a medicine through a drip called IVIG — immunoglobulin — which calms the inflammation, plus aspirin. Most children respond very well within 24–48 hours. We will do heart scans — echocardiograms — at diagnosis, at two weeks, and at six weeks, to check the blood vessels. Most children have no lasting heart effects."

**PFAPA — diagnosis:**

> "[Aanya] has a condition called PFAPA — periodic fever, mouth ulcers, sore throat, and swollen glands that happen like clockwork every few weeks. It is not an infection and it is not contagious. It is the immune system overreacting periodically. The episodes can be stopped with a single dose of prednisolone when they start. Most children grow out of it by age 10. If the episodes are very frequent and disruptive, we can discuss tonsillectomy, which cures it in most children."

**MAS — §7.1 emergency communication:**

> "I am very concerned about [Aanya]. [Her] blood tests show that [her] immune system is in a dangerous state called macrophage activation syndrome. This is a medical emergency and [she] needs to be in hospital today. I have called [partner ER / tertiary rheumatology] and they are expecting [her]. I will send all [her] records across. This is treatable, but it needs intensive-care-level monitoring and treatment that we cannot provide in clinic."

### 10.2 Scripted intervention-tier explanation

> "[Aanya]'s arthritis [or lupus / rheumatology condition] needs regular follow-up — blood tests every [month / three months], medicine refills, physiotherapy, and eye checks. The Premium subscription covers all these visits, the blood tests, and the medicine supply through our clinic. If you have been paying per visit, the subscription is usually more cost-effective with the number of visits [she] needs. The clinic manager can walk you through the comparison."

### 10.5 Cultural sensitivities

- **Diagnostic delay in JIA.** Joint pain in Indian children is frequently dismissed as "growing pains" by families and primary-care physicians (average diagnostic delay 6–12 months). Counter explicitly: "Growing pains are bilateral, in the muscles (not the joints), happen at night, and the child's joints look and move normally in the morning. Arthritis is joint swelling with warmth and limitation of movement — these are different." Educate families on the importance of early treatment to prevent joint damage.
- **Steroid fear.** Indian families often associate steroids with serious side-effects (weight gain, growth stunting, "becoming dependent"). Counter with specific, honest information: name the drug (prednisolone), name the dose, name the duration, name the taper plan. Acknowledge side-effects are real but managed by using the lowest effective dose for the shortest necessary time. Distinguish short-course (Kawasaki, PFAPA episode) from maintenance (SLE, JDM) and explain why each duration is appropriate.
- **Traditional remedies and joint pain.** Ayurvedic and homeopathic treatments for joint pain are common. Counsel as with other protocols: do not dismiss, but request that prescribed medications continue alongside. The risk is delayed treatment leading to joint damage (JIA) or organ damage (SLE).
- **"Arthritis is for old people."** Extended family may not believe a child can have arthritis. Provide written material (§10.4) to share with family members. The school-communication template helps with teachers who may also be sceptical.
- **SLE and marriage concerns.** In adolescent girls, SLE diagnosis may raise marriage-prospects concerns from the extended family. Frame SLE as a manageable lifelong condition — most patients with well-controlled SLE have normal life expectancy and can have children (with planning). Address contraception and pregnancy planning directly with the adolescent and family when age-appropriate. Cross B1-13 for adolescent privacy framework.
- **Biological therapy and "Western medicine" concerns.** Some families may be resistant to biologic injections. Explain mechanism in simple terms, provide data on effectiveness and safety, and address specific fears. Do not force; document refusal and offer closer monitoring with conventional therapy if family declines.

## §12 Quality and audit

### 12.1 Outcome metrics

B2-03 is working well when:

- ≥ 50% of JIA patients achieve inactive disease (JADAS ≤ 1) at 12 months of treatment.
- ≥ 70% of JIA patients on methotrexate achieve clinical response (JADAS improvement ≥ 50%) by 6 months.
- SLE flare rate < 2 per year on maintenance therapy.
- 100% of MAS cases recognised at the B2-03 visit (no missed cases — operationalised as: every sJIA patient with ferritin > 684 has MAS criteria formally assessed and documented).
- ≥ 95% of at-risk JIA patients (oligo, ANA+, age < 7, disease duration < 4 years) have uveitis screening completed per ACR 2019 schedule.
- ≥ 95% of Kawasaki patients receive IVIG within 10 days of fever onset.
- 100% of Kawasaki patients have echo at 2 weeks, 6 weeks, and 6 months per protocol.
- ≥ 95% of methotrexate lab monitoring (FBC + LFT) completed within 14 days of due date.
- 100% of biologic pre-screening (TB, hepatitis B/C, HIV, varicella) documented before first biologic dose.
- ≥ 90% of SLE patients have urinalysis performed at every follow-up visit.
- 100% of §7.1 emergency triggers result in same-day handoff to ER + tertiary rheumatology.
- ≥ 80% of JIA patients diagnosed within 3 months of first SKIDS presentation with joint symptoms (diagnostic-delay metric).

### 12.2 Quarterly case review

The protocol owner (pediatric rheumatologist) reviews every quarter:
- All new JIA diagnoses: subtype classification accuracy, treatment-escalation timeliness, JADAS trend.
- All SLE patients: SLEDAI trend, renal involvement surveillance compliance, immunosuppression adequacy.
- All MAS episodes: recognition timeliness, §7.1 execution, outcome.
- All §7 referrals (every tier): outcome tracking.
- All biologic initiations: pre-screening completeness, response assessment at 3 months.
- All methotrexate lab-monitoring compliance: delays, abnormal results, dose adjustments.
- All uveitis screening: compliance rate, any new uveitis diagnoses, treatment initiated.
- All Kawasaki cases: IVIG timeliness, echo timeline compliance, coronary outcomes.
- A random sample of 20 B2-03 visits across condition categories.
- All loss-to-follow-up cases on immunosuppressive therapy.

Output: 1-page report to SKIDS clinical operations, with any recommended protocol updates flagged.

### 12.3 Pediatric rheumatologist certification on this protocol

To deliver B2-03, a SKIDS pediatric rheumatologist must:
- Hold DM Pediatric Rheumatology or equivalent Fellowship credential.
- Complete the B2-03 onboarding chapter (6 hours).
- Pass a structured case-vignette assessment (20 cases across JIA subtypes, SLE, JDM, vasculitis, PFAPA, MAS).
- Demonstrate JADAS-27 scoring accuracy (10 cases scored against protocol owner's assessment).
- Demonstrate SLEDAI scoring accuracy (5 cases).
- Demonstrate MAS recognition accuracy (5 cases — 100% required; no false negatives permitted).
- Demonstrate joint examination proficiency (5 children examined with joint count agreement within ± 2 joints of protocol owner's count).
- Maintain a minimum case load of 25 B2-03 visits per quarter.
- Complete annual continuing education (8 hours covering ACR/EULAR JIA updates, SLE management updates, biologic safety updates, Kawasaki guidelines).

### 12.4 The SKIDS Fellowship pathway

The SKIDS Pediatric Rheumatology Fellowship IS the credential pathway for this Tier 2 service. Unlike Tier 1 protocols where a junior SKIDS pediatrician delivers under protocol governance, B2-03 requires the Fellowship-credentialed pediatric rheumatologist to be the delivering clinician. See `docs/06-the-skids-fellowship.md`.

The Fellowship-credentialed rheumatologist delivers:
- All conditions in §1 (JIA, SLE, JDM, vasculitis, periodic fever syndromes, autoimmune connective-tissue disorders).
- Uveitis surveillance coordination for JIA (cross B1-01).
- Kawasaki disease acute management coordination (cross B1-16).
- PFAPA management and tonsillectomy referral coordination (cross B1-03).
- Protocol governance for Tier 1 protocols that identify rheumatological presentations (B1-19 FUO workup, B1-05 autoimmune skin features, any B1 visit with joint swelling).
- Quarterly case review per §12.2.
- Training of SKIDS pediatricians on rheumatological red-flag recognition in A1 and B1 protocols.
