# SKIDS Blood Clinic

> The SKIDS Blood Clinic delivers structured evaluation of anaemia beyond IDA (B1-09 manages IDA), thalassemia-trait identification, bleeding-disorder screening, unexplained bruising/petechiae workup, lymphadenopathy evaluation, and malignancy-suspicion triage. Transfusion-dependent thalassemia major, sickle-cell complications, and confirmed malignancy route per §7 to pediatric haematology/oncology.

---

## §1 Service definition

**What this clinic does:**
- Evaluates anaemia not responding to B1-09 oral-iron therapy (Hb not improving after 4 weeks therapeutic iron): peripheral smear, reticulocyte count, iron studies, Hb electrophoresis, B12/folate, LDH/haptoglobin for haemolysis workup.
- Identifies thalassemia trait (alpha and beta) and sickle-cell trait via Hb electrophoresis / HPLC; provides genetic counselling for carrier families; routes thalassemia major/intermedia and sickle-cell disease to §7.3.
- Screens for bleeding disorders: prolonged bleeding after minor trauma, easy bruising, menorrhagia in adolescent girls (cross B1-13), family history — PT, aPTT, platelet count, von Willebrand panel where indicated.
- Evaluates unexplained bruising/petechiae: differentiates ITP (isolated thrombocytopenia, otherwise well child) from leukaemia/aplastic anaemia (pancytopenia, hepatosplenomegaly, bone pain, lymphadenopathy → §7.2).
- Manages immune thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP) in typical presentation (age 2–10, isolated thrombocytopenia, well child, no hepatosplenomegaly): observation if platelet > 20,000 and no active bleeding; routes severe/refractory ITP to §7.3.
- Evaluates persistent lymphadenopathy (> 2 cm, non-tender, > 4 weeks) per A1 §7.2 malignancy-suspicion pathway as bridge to pediatric oncology.
- Provides newborn screening result interpretation: haemoglobinopathy panel, G6PD deficiency confirmation, and family counselling.
- Cross-cares with B1-09 (IDA management), B1-13 (adolescent menorrhagia-related anaemia), B1-17 (anaemia of CKD).

**What this clinic does not do:**
- Manage transfusion-dependent thalassemia major (→ §7.3 haematology centre).
- Manage sickle-cell disease with active complications (→ §7.3).
- Diagnose or manage leukaemia / lymphoma (→ §7.2 oncology).
- Perform bone-marrow aspiration/biopsy (→ §7.3).
- Administer blood transfusion (→ §7.1/§7.2 hospital).
- Manage haemophilia beyond initial screening (→ §7.3 haematology).

**Age range covered:** 0 – 18 years.

**SKIDS subscription tier(s):**
- **Basic ₹2,999** — A1 POC Hb screening; first B1-18 visit on per-visit pricing.
- **Advanced ₹6,999** — anaemia workup; thalassemia-trait identification; up to 4 visits; genetic counselling.
- **Premium ₹12,999** — unlimited visits; bleeding-disorder workup; ITP monitoring; multi-protocol cross-care.

**SKIDS clinical tier:** Tier 1. §7 to pediatric haematology (thalassemia major, SCD, haemophilia), pediatric oncology (malignancy), ER (severe anaemia, active bleeding).

## §4 Diagnostic workflow

### 4.1 Anaemia workup (beyond IDA)
- FBC with peripheral smear (morphology: microcytic / normocytic / macrocytic; target cells, sickle cells, spherocytes, schistocytes).
- Reticulocyte count (low = production failure; high = destruction/blood loss).
- Iron studies (serum iron, TIBC, ferritin) — if not already done at B1-09.
- Hb electrophoresis / HPLC (thalassemia trait, SCD trait, HbH, HbE).
- B12, folate (if macrocytic — cross B1-09).
- LDH, haptoglobin, bilirubin (indirect), Coombs test (if haemolysis suspected).
- If pancytopenia → §7.2 urgent (bone-marrow failure or malignancy).

### 4.2 Thalassemia-trait identification
- Hb electrophoresis / HPLC: HbA2 > 3.5% = beta-thal trait; HbF, HbS, HbE patterns.
- MCV < 80 fL with normal iron studies = thal trait until proven otherwise.
- Genetic counselling: carrier risk, partner testing before marriage/pregnancy, prenatal diagnosis options.
- Thal trait = does NOT need iron (iron is contraindicated if iron stores normal).

### 4.3 Bleeding-disorder screening
- PT, aPTT, platelet count, bleeding time (if available).
- Prolonged aPTT with normal PT → intrinsic pathway (haemophilia screen: factor VIII, IX activity).
- Normal PT/aPTT with prolonged bleeding → von Willebrand disease screen (vWF antigen, vWF activity, factor VIII).
- Platelet function disorders → §7.3 haematology for platelet aggregation studies.

### 4.4 ITP assessment
- Typical: age 2–10; preceded by viral illness; isolated thrombocytopenia (platelet < 100,000); no hepatosplenomegaly; no lymphadenopathy; no blast cells on smear; child otherwise well.
- Atypical features (any) → §7.2: age < 1 or > 10, pancytopenia, hepatosplenomegaly, lymphadenopathy, bone pain, weight loss.
- Age < 1 year: consider non-accidental injury (NAI) in the differential for unexplained bruising/petechiae in infants — mandatory safeguarding screen.

### 4.5 Lymphadenopathy evaluation
- > 2 cm, non-tender, fixed, > 4 weeks → FBC, peripheral smear, ESR, CRP, LDH, uric acid, chest X-ray.
- If suspicious (peripheral blast cells, B-symptoms, hepatosplenomegaly) → §7.2 oncology within 7 days.
- If reactive (tender, mobile, < 2 cm, recent infection) → observe 4 weeks; if persistent → workup.

## §5 Severity grading [SACRED SECTION]

| Severity | Criteria | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Thalassemia trait identified; G6PD deficiency confirmed; isolated mild anaemia responding to workup-guided treatment | §6.1 — counsel + monitor |
| **Moderate** | ITP typical (platelet 20–100K, no bleeding); anaemia workup in progress; von Willebrand trait | §6.2 — observe ITP; treat anaemia per cause |
| **Severe** | ITP with platelet < 20K or active mucosal bleeding; severe anaemia (Hb 5–7); haemolysis confirmed | §6.3 / §7 |
| **Beyond Tier 1** | Thalassemia major; SCD with complications; haemophilia; malignancy; pancytopenia | §7.2 / §7.3 |

### Boundary rules
- Hb < 5 = §7.1 ER. Hb 5–7 = §7.2 + same-week B1-18. Hb 7–cutoff = B1-18 manages.
- Pancytopenia = always §7.2 (never manage at B1-18 without haematology input).
- ITP typical (platelet > 20,000, no mucosal bleeding) = B1-18 observes. ITP with platelet ≤ 20,000 OR mucosal bleeding = §6.2 treatment initiation + §7.3 haematology guidance. ITP with platelet < 10,000 OR severe bleeding = §7.2 urgent haematology.
- Thalassemia TRAIT = B1-18 counsels. Thalassemia MAJOR = §7.3 haematology.

## §6 Therapeutic options

### 6.1 Light tier
- Thalassemia trait: genetic counselling; partner-testing advice; NO iron supplementation if iron-replete.
- G6PD deficiency: avoidance list (fava beans, naphthalene, specific medications — primaquine, dapsone, sulfonamides); parent-app G6PD card activated; school/medication awareness letter.
- Mild anaemia of identified cause: treat per cause (B12 for macrocytic, folate, iron if actually deficient).

### 6.2 Standard tier
- ITP (typical, platelet 20–100K, no bleeding): observation; activity restriction (no contact sports until platelet > 50K); weekly platelet count × 4 weeks; if spontaneous recovery → discharge. If persisting > 3 months → chronic ITP → §7.3.
- ITP (platelet ≤ 20,000 or mucosal bleeding): initiate treatment per haematology guidance (IVIG or short-course prednisolone) — route §7.3 for haematology input before treatment if not emergent; §7.2 if platelet < 10,000 or severe bleeding.
- von Willebrand disease type 1: desmopressin (DDAVP) trial for response assessment → §7.3 haematology for ongoing management plan.

### 6.3 Intensive tier
- Severe anaemia (Hb 5–7, stable): §7.2 same-week to haematology + hospital with transfusion capacity.
- Haemolysis: stabilise; route §7.2.

### 6.4 G6PD management programme
- Avoidance counselling; G6PD card issuance; annual education reinforcement; school letter.

## §7 Hard referral triggers [SACRED SECTION]

### 7.1 Emergency (same day)
- [ ] **Hb < 5 g/dL OR haemodynamic compromise at any Hb** → ER with transfusion capacity.
- [ ] **Active severe bleeding with coagulopathy** → ER.
- [ ] **Acute haemolytic crisis** (jaundice + dark urine + rapidly dropping Hb — G6PD, autoimmune) → ER.

### 7.2 Urgent (within 7 days)
- [ ] **Hb 5–7 g/dL (stable)** → haematology + hospital.
- [ ] **Pancytopenia** → haematology/oncology.
- [ ] **Lymphadenopathy with malignancy features** (B-symptoms, blast cells, hepatosplenomegaly) → oncology.
- [ ] **ITP atypical** → haematology.
- [ ] **ITP platelet < 10,000 OR severe/life-threatening bleeding** → haematology urgent.

### 7.3 Routine (within 4 weeks)
- [ ] **Thalassemia major / intermedia confirmed** → haematology centre.
- [ ] **Sickle-cell disease confirmed** → haematology centre.
- [ ] **Haemophilia confirmed (factor VIII/IX < 40%)** → haematology.
- [ ] **von Willebrand disease (types 2, 3)** → haematology.
- [ ] **Chronic ITP (> 3 months)** → haematology.
- [ ] **Unexplained persistent cytopenias** → haematology for bone marrow.
- [ ] **Persistent lymphadenopathy (> 4 weeks, > 2 cm, workup inconclusive)** → biopsy via surgery/oncology.

### 7.4 Handoff: FBC, peripheral smear, Hb electrophoresis, iron studies, coagulation profile, LDH/haptoglobin, reticulocyte count.

## §8 Follow-up schedule

| Scenario | First follow-up | Subsequent |
|---|---|---|
| ITP (typical, observation) | Weekly platelet count × 4 weeks | Monthly × 3 months; if recovering → discharge; if persistent > 3 months → §7.3 |
| ITP (on treatment — IVIG / prednisolone) | 48–72h post-treatment (platelet count) | Weekly × 4 weeks; then per haematology guidance |
| Thalassemia trait identified | 3 months (counselling reinforcement) | Annual FBC; pre-marital counselling reminder at adolescent visits |
| G6PD deficiency | 3 months (avoidance-list reinforcement) | Annual; education update at school-entry and adolescence |
| Anaemia workup (cause identified, treatment initiated) | 4 weeks (recheck FBC + reticulocyte count) | Per cause: B12/folate — 3-monthly until replete; haemolysis — per haematology |
| Bleeding-disorder screening (vWD type 1 / mild) | Per haematology management plan | Per haematology guidance |
| Lymphadenopathy (reactive, under observation) | 4 weeks | If resolved → discharge; if persistent → workup per §4.5 |

§8.2 What to recheck: FBC with peripheral smear (anaemia, ITP); platelet count (ITP trend); reticulocyte count (haemolysis monitoring); Hb electrophoresis result review (thal trait); G6PD avoidance-list compliance; bleeding symptoms (bruising diary for coagulopathy); lymph-node size and character (lymphadenopathy).

§8.3 Success: ITP — platelet recovery > 100,000 sustained × 2 consecutive months; anaemia — Hb normalised for age and sex; thal trait — family counselled and partner-testing advice documented; G6PD — no haemolytic episodes; bleeding disorder — haematology management plan in place.

§8.7 Loss-to-follow-up: Companion alert for ITP children (platelet trend monitoring essential to detect chronic ITP early) and thalassemia-trait families (genetic counselling follow-through). High-priority re-engagement for ITP.

## §9 Companion software workflow

### 9.1 Doctor Module screens

**Screen 1: Pre-consult summary** — A1 routing context (Hb flag, anaemia flag); prior B1-18 visits; prior B1-09 IDA management history; current Hb; Hb electrophoresis result; ITP platelet trend; G6PD status; B1-09/B1-13/B1-17 cross-references.

**Screen 2: History** — structured form: presenting concern (pallor, bruising, petechiae, bleeding, lymph node, fatigue); bleeding history (duration, triggers, family history); transfusion history; dietary history (B1-09 cross); menstrual history (adolescent girls — B1-13 cross); medication/exposure history (G6PD triggers).

**Screen 3: Examination** — pallor assessment (conjunctival, palmar); bruising/petechiae distribution; lymph-node map (location, size, consistency, tenderness, fixation); hepatosplenomegaly (liver span, spleen tip); growth plotted.

**Screen 4: Investigations** — FBC display with morphology classification (microcytic / normocytic / macrocytic); peripheral smear findings (structured — target cells, sickle cells, spherocytes, blast cells, schistocytes); reticulocyte count; Hb electrophoresis / HPLC result with HbA2, HbF, HbS values; iron studies; B12/folate; haemolysis markers (LDH, haptoglobin, bilirubin, Coombs); coagulation profile (PT, aPTT); platelet trend graph (ITP).

**Screen 5: Severity grading** — auto-populated from Hb level + platelet count + smear findings + clinical features.

**Screen 6: Diagnostic classification** — auto-suggested from investigation pattern (e.g., microcytic + elevated HbA2 = beta-thal trait; isolated thrombocytopenia + well child = typical ITP). Doctor confirms or modifies.

**Screen 7: Therapeutic plan** — G6PD avoidance-list activation; ITP management pathway (observation vs treatment); anaemia treatment per cause; genetic counselling documentation; §7 trigger checklist.

**Screen 8: Follow-up** — ITP platelet monitoring schedule; thal-trait annual FBC reminder; G6PD education update calendar; anaemia recheck scheduler.

- ITP child with upcoming dental procedure: platelet count required pre-procedure; communicate with dentist (B1-04 cross); if platelet < 50,000, defer elective procedures.
- Offline: paper FBC/smear results; upload when connectivity restored.

## §10 Parent-facing communication

### 10.1 Scripted explanations

Thalassemia trait:

> "This is a carrier state — not a disease. [He] is healthy and will remain healthy. The reason we identify it is so that when [he] grows up and chooses a partner, we can test them too. If both partners carry the trait, there is a 25% chance their children could have thalassemia major, which is a serious blood condition requiring regular transfusions. Knowing this now means the family can plan ahead. This is empowering information, not a problem."

ITP:

> "[Her] body is temporarily making antibodies against her own platelets — the cells that help blood clot. This is very common after viral infections in children. The platelet count is low, which is why you see the bruises and small red dots. The good news is that in most children, the platelet count recovers on its own within a few weeks to months. We monitor with weekly blood tests. We only treat with medicine if the count drops very low or there is active bleeding."

Anaemia workup:

> "The iron medicine did not bring [his] blood count up as expected, so we are doing some additional tests to find out exactly what type of anaemia this is. There are several types, and each has a specific treatment. Once we know the cause, we can treat it correctly."

Malignancy concern (lymphadenopathy with suspicious features):

> "The blood test shows some cells that need to be looked at by a specialist. I want to be straightforward with you — we need to rule out a few serious conditions. The specialist will do further tests and give us a clear answer. I am referring [him] urgently so there is no delay."

### 10.5 Cultural sensitivities

- *Thalassemia carrier stigma in Indian marriage context.* Thal-trait disclosure can carry significant social stigma, particularly in communities where arranged marriages are common. Frame as empowerment, not defect: "Knowing your carrier status gives your family the power to plan. Half the population in some regions carries this trait — it is common and it is not a disease." Avoid language that implies the child is "defective" or "unmarriageable."
- *G6PD and Indian households.* Fava beans (less common in Indian diet) and naphthalene mothballs (very common in Indian households for clothing storage) are key triggers. Emphasise the mothball risk specifically — many families store clothes with naphthalene balls in almirahs. Provide a practical avoidance list with Indian-context items.
- *Bleeding-disorder stigma in boys.* Families may worry that a bleeding disorder means the child cannot play, participate in sports, or live normally. Normalise: mild bleeding disorders (vWD type 1) are manageable; most children lead fully active lives with appropriate precautions. The diagnosis helps schools and doctors manage emergencies better.
- *"Low blood" (khooni kamzori).* Anaemia is often described as "khooni kamzori" (blood weakness). Acknowledge the term; reframe with the specific diagnosis. "We have found exactly which type of blood weakness this is, and we have a specific treatment for it."
- *Malignancy fear with lymphadenopathy.* Parents may panic at any lymph-node workup. Reassure that most childhood lymphadenopathy is reactive (infection-related) and benign. The workup is to be thorough, not because cancer is likely. When malignancy IS suspected, be direct but compassionate (§10.1 scripted).

§11.3 Top parent objections:

| Objection | Manager response |
|---|---|
| "Iron didn't work — just give stronger iron." | "The doctor has checked why the iron didn't work. There are different types of anaemia, and some don't respond to iron at all. The blood test tells us the exact type so we can give the right treatment." |
| "Thalassemia trait — does this mean my child is sick?" | "No — carrier status is not a disease. [He] is healthy. We identify it so that future family planning is informed. This is very common in India." |
| "She's bruising — is it leukaemia?" | "Bruising in children is very common and usually not serious. The blood test checks the platelet count and the blood cells. In most cases, it is a temporary immune response. The doctor will explain the results clearly." |
| "Why weekly blood tests for ITP? Can't you just treat it?" | "Most children with ITP recover on their own without medicine. We monitor the platelet count to make sure it is trending upward. Treating when it's not needed exposes [her] to medicine side effects unnecessarily." |
| "G6PD — can he ever eat normally?" | "Yes — most foods are safe. There is a short list of specific foods and medicines to avoid. We give you a card to carry and share with teachers and doctors. Day-to-day eating is not affected." |

§11.4–§11.6: standard manager touchpoints. Thal-trait families get proactive follow-up for partner-testing reminder at adolescent milestones. ITP families get weekly check-in calls during observation phase.

## §12 Quality and audit

- ≥ 95% of thalassemia-trait-identified children receive documented genetic counselling (with partner-testing advice).
- ITP observation-only rate ≥ 70% for typical ITP (platelet > 20,000, no mucosal bleeding) — avoid over-treatment.
- 100% of pancytopenia cases referred via §7.2 within 24 hours.
- 100% of peripheral smear blast-cell findings trigger §7.2 within 24 hours.
- ≥ 95% of §7 triggers result in documented referral with complete handoff packet.
- G6PD avoidance-card issuance rate ≥ 95% for confirmed G6PD-deficient children.
- Anaemia cause identification rate ≥ 90% within 2 visits of B1-18 workup.
- Loss-to-follow-up < 15%.

§12.2 Quarterly: protocol owner reviews all §7 referrals, ITP treatment rates (observation vs intervention — target observation ≥ 70%), thal-trait counselling documentation, pancytopenia referral timeliness, G6PD card issuance, and anaemia workup completion rates.

Annual update (2 hours).
