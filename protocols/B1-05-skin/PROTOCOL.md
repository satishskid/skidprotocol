# SKIDS Skin Clinic

> The SKIDS Skin Clinic manages atopic dermatitis, acne, common
> infectious dermatoses (scabies, impetigo, tinea), reactive
> dermatoses, and benign pediatric skin lesions to severe-tier
> intensity, with §7 escalation for refractory, complex, or
> dermatology-procedure cases.

---

## §1 Service definition

**What this clinic does:**
- Diagnoses and manages atopic dermatitis (AD / eczema) using SCORAD severity grading and a stepped-care kit (emollients, topical corticosteroids, topical calcineurin inhibitors, oral antihistamines for pruritus) [AAD-2014-AD].
- Diagnoses and manages acne vulgaris using the Investigator's Global Assessment for Acne (IGA-Acne) and a stepped kit (topical retinoids, benzoyl peroxide, topical antibiotics, oral antibiotics in moderate-to-severe disease ≥8 years) [AAD-2024-Acne].
- Diagnoses and manages common pediatric infectious dermatoses: impetigo, folliculitis, scabies, pediculosis, tinea (corporis / capitis / cruris / pedis), candidal intertrigo, molluscum contagiosum, viral warts.
- Diagnoses and manages reactive dermatoses: contact dermatitis (irritant and allergic), papular urticaria (insect-bite reactions), pityriasis rosea, mild urticaria.
- Surveils benign pediatric lesions: melanocytic nevi, café-au-lait macules (with NF1 screening triggers), salmon-patch / nevus simplex, infantile hemangiomas without functional risk, keratosis pilaris.
- Counsels and treats common pediatric pigmentation disorders within scope: post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation, mild pityriasis alba, mild melasma in adolescents.
- Escalates per §7 to pediatric dermatology, pediatric ER, or Tier 3 partner for any condition outside the stepped-care kit's scope.

**What this clinic does not do:**
- Manage erythroderma, SJS / TEN, DRESS, eczema herpeticum, or any skin manifestation of suspected systemic disease — these are §7.1 emergencies.
- Initiate isotretinoin, biologics (dupilumab), systemic immunosuppression (methotrexate, ciclosporin), or phototherapy. These require pediatric dermatology supervision and route per §7.
- Excise lesions surgically (any biopsy or excision routes to a Tier 3 partner pedodermatologist or pediatric surgeon).
- Manage genodermatoses (ichthyoses, epidermolysis bullosa, neurocutaneous syndromes beyond initial NF1 screening trigger), connective-tissue-disease skin findings (route to B2-03 Rheumatology), or suspected pediatric melanoma.
- Manage vascular malformations or hemangiomas with periocular, airway, or PHACES-syndrome features — route per §7.

**Age range covered:** 0 – 18 years. Specific lower-age cutoffs apply for individual therapeutics in §6 (e.g., topical tacrolimus from 2 years, oral doxycycline from 8 years).

**SKIDS subscription tier(s):**
- **Basic ₹2,999** — A1 routing here is supported. First B1-05 visit and any infectious-dermatosis course (impetigo, scabies, tinea — typically 1–2 visits) are individually billed at the Basic per-visit rate.
- **Advanced ₹6,999** — up to 4 B1-05 visits per year included. Suitable for moderate AD or acne expected to need 2–4 visits within a flare cycle.
- **Premium ₹12,999** — unlimited B1-05 visits within the clinically indicated set; priority booking for §7 partner-derm referral handoff. Suitable for severe AD or severe acne with multi-month management plans.

**SKIDS clinical tier:** Tier 1. Delivered by every SKIDS pediatrician using the protocol, the dispensary kit listed in §6, and allied-staff support (B1-05 has minimal allied-staff dependence — a clinic nurse for application demonstrations, no separate dermatology-trained staff at Tier 1). Protocol governance sits with the named pediatric dermatologist identified in §2 (assignment pending at editorial pass). Complex tier escalation per §7 routes to a Tier 3 partner pedodermatologist or pediatric ER as appropriate.

## §2 Evidence base

This protocol governs primary-care management of common pediatric skin conditions at SKIDS clinics. It follows the most current recommendations from:

- **[AAD-2014-AD]** — Eichenfield LF, Tom WL, Berger TG, et al. Guidelines of care for the management of atopic dermatitis. Sections 1 and 2. *J Am Acad Dermatol.* 2014;70(2):338–351; 71(1):116–132. The governing source for AD diagnosis, severity grading, and topical therapy.
- **[AAD-2024-AD]** — American Academy of Dermatology atopic dermatitis updated guidelines (2023–2024 publication series; topical, phototherapy, systemic). Used alongside AAD-2014 where the 2024 updates supersede.
- **[AAD-2024-Acne]** — Reynolds RV, Yeung H, Cheng CE, et al. Guidelines of care for the management of acne vulgaris. *J Am Acad Dermatol.* 2024;90(5):1006.e1–1006.e30. Governing source for acne diagnosis, severity grading, and stepped therapy.
- **[IAP-Atopic-2023]** — Indian Academy of Pediatrics. Position Statement on Atopic Dermatitis in Indian Children, 2023. Used in preference to AAD where the two diverge on Indian-context recommendations (cost-conscious therapy, infection-superinfection patterns). [IAP citation AI-verified 2026-05-01; exact title requires IAP website confirmation]
- **[IADVL-Scabies-2021]** — Indian Association of Dermatologists, Venereologists and Leprologists. Guidelines for the management of scabies in India, 2021. Governing source for scabies first-line therapy in the Indian context (permethrin 5%, with oral ivermectin for crusted / outbreak / household).
- **[CDC-Scabies-2024]** — CDC. Scabies clinical guidance, 2024 update. Used for institutional / outbreak management and crusted scabies.
- **[AAD-Tinea-2014]** — Tinea capitis: clinical features and treatment. AAD guideline summary.
- **[NELSON-21:CH656]** — Nelson Textbook of Pediatrics, 21st edition, chapter on diseases of the skin. Reference textbook for the spectrum of pediatric dermatoses.
- **[IAP-TB-6E]** — IAP Textbook of Pediatrics, 6th edition, chapter on pediatric dermatology. Reference for Indian-context epidemiology and first-line therapy.
- **[AAP-BF-4E]** — Bright Futures, 4th edition. Cited for the periodicity-of-skin-examination logic A1 references for B1-05 routing.
- **[Cochrane-CD003871]** — Cochrane review on emollients and moisturisers for eczema. Last verified: pending. Cited in §6 for emollient evidence base.
- **[Cochrane-CD009864]** — Cochrane review on antibiotics for impetigo. Cited in §6 for impetigo therapy.
- **[Stevens-2014-IDSA]** — Stevens DL et al. IDSA Practice Guidelines for the Diagnosis and Management of Skin and Soft Tissue Infections, 2014 update. *Clin Infect Dis.* 2014;59(2):e10–52. Cited for impetigo and cellulitis severity criteria.

**Departures from cited guidelines:**

1. *First-line tinea capitis therapy.* AAD names terbinafine as first-line in T. tonsurans-predominant geographies (US). India's tinea capitis is mixed-isolate with significant T. violaceum and zoophilic Microsporum spp.; griseofulvin remains a reasonable first-line in Indian practice. A1-05 follows IAP Textbook (griseofulvin first-line, terbinafine second-line per organism susceptibility) [IAP-TB-6E]. Confirm with culture / KOH where available.

2. *Oral antibiotic choice for moderate acne.* AAD names doxycycline as first-line tetracycline. India's antimicrobial stewardship environment and adolescent acne volume warrant explicit attention to: (a) duration cap at 3 months at any dose to limit resistance pressure, (b) topical optimisation before oral initiation, (c) tetracycline minimum age 8 years (universal) and 12 years for cosmetic use unless severity dictates otherwise. [TO BE DECIDED BY SKIDS PEDIATRICIAN]

3. *Permethrin vs. ivermectin in scabies.* AAD / CDC name permethrin 5% cream as first-line and oral ivermectin as alternative. IADVL allows oral ivermectin as first-line in epidemic / institutional / household-coverage situations because of compliance advantages. B1-05 follows IADVL: permethrin 5% first-line individual case, oral ivermectin first-line for whole-household treatment in scabies outbreaks [IADVL-Scabies-2021].

4. *Skin biopsy.* Both AAD and IAP support biopsy for diagnostic uncertainty. SKIDS does not biopsy at Tier 1 (no procedural infrastructure); any biopsy indication routes to §7 Tier 3 partner. This is an operational rather than clinical departure.

No other material departures.

| "His face / cheeks are red and rough since infancy." | Infant atopic dermatitis (face-and-extensor pattern). | §4 AD pathway. |
| "She scratches all night and her sleep is broken." | AD with significant pruritus impact, OR scabies, OR papular urticaria. | §4 AD pathway + scabies screen + sleep history. If pruritus dominant, also consider B1-07 Sleep recheck. |
| "His teenage acne is getting worse / leaving marks." | Acne vulgaris, moderate-to-severe. | §4 acne pathway → IGA grade. Adolescent psychosocial impact assessed per B1-13 Adolescent. |

| "His scalp has a circular bald patch with broken hairs." | Tinea capitis. | §4 tinea pathway → KOH / culture if available. |
| "She has yellow crusted spots on her face that are spreading." | Impetigo. | §4 bacterial pathway → topical or oral antibiotic per extent [Stevens-2014-IDSA, Cochrane-CD009864]. |
| "Pearly bumps on his trunk that don't itch much but are spreading." | Molluscum contagiosum. | §4 viral lesion pathway → counsel; intervene only if symptomatic / extensive. |
| "Wart on her hand for months that won't go away." | Verruca vulgaris. | §4 viral lesion pathway → topical salicylic acid / cryotherapy if available. |
| "Fine pink rash all over after starting [drug]." | Drug eruption (mild morbilliform). | §4 drug-eruption pathway → assess for SJS/TEN red flags per §7. |
| "Hives that come and go for weeks." | Acute or chronic urticaria. | §4 urticaria pathway → joint with B1-06 Allergy if chronic / food-trigger pattern. |
| "Birthmark she's had since birth — should we worry?" | Benign vascular or pigmented lesion (salmon patch, café-au-lait, nevus). | §4 lesion-surveillance pathway → measure, photograph, schedule recheck. |
| "Many flat brown patches all over." | Café-au-lait macules — assess for NF1 criteria. | §4 lesion pathway → if ≥6 macules ≥5 mm prepubertal / ≥15 mm postpubertal, route §7 routine to neurology / genetics [NELSON-21:CH656]. |
| "Diaper rash that won't clear." | Irritant diaper dermatitis vs. candidal intertrigo. | §4 diaper-area pathway. |
| "Patches losing pigment." | Vitiligo, pityriasis alba, or post-inflammatory hypopigmentation. | §4 hypopigmentation pathway. Vitiligo → §7 routine to pedoderm. Pityriasis alba and PIH → in-clinic management. |
| "Red painful pimple with fever." | Skin and soft tissue infection — possible cellulitis / abscess. | §4 SSTI pathway → severity per IDSA criteria; §7 routing if systemic features. |

**Routing decisions:**
- If the child arrives via A1 with an Orange skin flag, the flag context is auto-loaded into B1-05 (parent description, A1's photographic capture if any, flag tier).
- If the presenting condition has overlap with another B1 protocol, B1-05 manages the cutaneous component and joint-cares with the overlap protocol explicitly. Examples: AD with food-allergy trigger (joint with B1-06 Allergy), severe acne with significant psychosocial impact (joint with B1-13 Adolescent), recurrent impetigo with underlying immunodeficiency suspicion (joint with B1-19 Infections).
- If §7 fires at presentation (e.g., child arrives with erythroderma, suspected SJS/TEN, eczema herpeticum), B1-05 does not commence stepped therapy; the case routes immediately per §7.

**Coming from SKIDS Screening (A1):**
The following A1 screening flags route automatically to B1-05:
- A1 §5.2 Skin domain Orange flag (any non-emergency rash, lesion, or chronic skin condition not previously diagnosed or not well controlled).
- A1 §5.2 Acne domain Orange flag (IGA-Acne ≥2 in an adolescent not previously seen at B1-05).
- A1 §5.2 Birthmark surveillance Orange (new or growing pigmented lesion; ≥6 café-au-lait macules; vascular lesion with size or distribution concern).

## §4 Diagnostic workflow

### 4.1 Structured history

Ask, in this order:

1. *Onset, duration, distribution.* When did it start? Where on the body did it start? Where is it now? Is it episodic (flare-and-settle) or continuous? Has it spread or stayed local?
2. *Symptoms.* Itch (graded 0–10 by parent / child if ≥7y), pain, burning, sleep disruption, school disruption.
3. *Triggers identified by family.* Specific foods, soaps, fabrics, weather changes, illness, stress, sweat.
4. *Prior treatments tried.* Over-the-counter, prescription, traditional remedies (henna, oils, herbal applications). Specific products used; how long; effect observed; reason for stopping.
5. *Atopy history.* Personal history of asthma, allergic rhinitis, food allergy. Family history of atopy in first-degree relatives.
6. *Infection / contact history.* Recent infections (viral exanthems), antibiotic / drug exposure (drug eruption screen), sick contacts with similar rash (scabies / impetigo / viral exanthem clues), pet exposure (zoophilic tinea, flea bites).
7. *Adolescent-specific.* Cosmetic product use, occlusive hair products (acne distribution clues), menstrual exacerbation pattern (acne), psychosocial impact (acne — bullying, withdrawal, school refusal). Document and joint-care with B1-13 Adolescent if significant.
8. *Acne-specific.* Onset relative to puberty, menstrual cycle pattern, skin-care routine, dietary milk / high-glycaemic-load triggers (limited evidence; do not over-counsel) [AAD-2024-Acne].
9. *Adherence and application technique.* For previously-treated cases, ask the parent to demonstrate or describe how they applied prior topicals — quantity, frequency, duration. Most "treatment failure" is undertreatment (steroid phobia, fingertip-unit ignorance) rather than disease severity.

**Red flags to elicit at history (any presentation):**
- Mucosal involvement (oral, ocular, genital) — SJS/TEN screen.
- Systemic features (fever, myalgia, lymphadenopathy, organ symptoms) with rash — DRESS, viral exanthem, Kawasaki, etc.
- Rapidly spreading erythema with pain — cellulitis, necrotising fasciitis (rare).
- Bullae, sloughing, Nikolsky-positive areas — SJS/TEN, SSSS.
- New rash in immunocompromised child — opportunistic skin infection or systemic disease.
- Rash + unexplained bruising / petechiae — haematological / NAI screen.

### 4.2 Examination

Examine for:

- *Distribution and morphology.* Document with body-region notation (e.g., "antecubital and popliteal fossae, mild on cheeks") and morphology terms (macule / papule / plaque / vesicle / pustule / nodule / scale / lichenification / excoriation / weeping / crusting). Photograph for the PHR per §9.
- *Severity-grading-relevant signs.*
  - For AD: the SCORAD components — extent (rule-of-9s), intensity (erythema, oedema/papulation, weeping/crusting, excoriation, lichenification, dryness — each 0–3), subjective (itch and sleep loss — VAS 0–10) [AAD-2014-AD].
  - For acne: lesion count (closed comedones, open comedones, papules, pustules, nodules) and IGA-Acne score 0–4.
- *Secondary infection signs.* Yellow / honey-coloured crust (impetigo superinfection of AD), warmth, lymphadenopathy.
- *Burrows or webspace involvement.* Scabies (interdigital, wrist, waist, genitals, axillae).
- *Wood's lamp examination* if available — fluorescence patterns for tinea capitis (Microsporum spp. fluoresce green; Trichophyton spp. do not), pityriasis versicolor, vitiligo extent.
- *Hair examination* if scalp involvement — broken hairs, scaling, alopecia patches, nits (pediculosis).
- *Nails* in any tinea or psoriasis suspicion.
- *Mucosa and conjunctivae* if any lesion, rash, or systemic feature — SJS/TEN screen.
- *Lymphadenopathy* in any infectious-dermatosis presentation.
- *Vital signs* — temperature in any SSTI, drug eruption, or new-onset rash with systemic features.

### 4.3 Scales and questionnaires

| Scale | Domain | Age | Cutoff thresholds |
|---|---|---|---|
| **SCORAD** | Atopic dermatitis severity | 0–18 | <25 mild · 25–50 moderate · >50 severe [AAD-2014-AD] |
| **IGA-Acne** | Acne severity | typically ≥10 | 0 clear · 1 almost clear · 2 mild · 3 moderate · 4 severe [AAD-2024-Acne] |
| **POEM** (Patient-Oriented Eczema Measure) | AD impact (parent / child report) | 2–18 | 0–2 clear · 3–7 mild · 8–16 moderate · 17–24 severe · 25–28 very severe |
| **Itch VAS** | Pruritus severity | ≥7 | 0 none · 1–3 mild · 4–6 moderate · 7–10 severe |
| **CDLQI / DLQI** (Children's / Dermatology Life Quality Index) | QoL impact | 4–18 (CDLQI) or ≥16 (DLQI) | 0–1 no impact · 2–6 small · 7–12 moderate · 13–18 very large · 19–30 extremely large |

SCORAD and IGA-Acne are mandatory for the relevant conditions. POEM and CDLQI are administered when the SCORAD or IGA suggests moderate-to-severe disease and at the second visit (to track delta).

### 4.4 In-clinic device tests

Required:
- *Standardised photography.* The Companion-attached camera or clinic phone with Companion-app capture, with the body region, date, and visit_id auto-tagged. Photographs at every visit so that the SKIDS pediatrician can show the family the trajectory and so the §7 referral packet has visual documentation.

Optional / on-indication:
- *Wood's lamp* — for tinea capitis screening, vitiligo extent assessment, pityriasis versicolor.
- *KOH preparation microscopy* — if the clinic has a microscopist, for confirming tinea diagnoses. Otherwise, treat clinically and reassess at 4 weeks.
- *Dermatoscope* — for nevus surveillance and to assess scabies burrows. A handheld dermatoscope is standard B1-05 kit; advanced dermatoscopy training is not required for the screening role it plays at Tier 1.

### 4.5 Laboratory and imaging

Order:
- *KOH microscopy or fungal culture* — for any suspected tinea capitis before initiating systemic antifungal therapy (4–8 week course commitment), where laboratory access permits. Empirical griseofulvin is acceptable in a typical clinical picture [AAD-Tinea-2014, IAP-TB-6E].
- *Bacterial swab and culture* — for impetigo not responding to first-line topical / oral therapy at 7 days, or for any abscess prior to drainage.
- *Total IgE and specific food / aeroallergen IgE panel* — only if AD is moderate-to-severe AND food-trigger or aeroallergen-trigger is suggested by history; route through B1-06 Allergy rather than ordering directly from B1-05 unless co-managed [AAD-2014-AD].
- *Liver function tests* — baseline before initiating oral terbinafine or oral itraconazole. Repeat at week 4 if course is ≥ 4 weeks.
- *Pregnancy test* — for any adolescent female prior to initiating systemic isotretinoin (which is §7 referral, not B1-05) or oral retinoid co-management.

Do not order routinely:
- Routine total IgE for AD diagnosis. AD is a clinical diagnosis; IgE adds little to management and confounds family expectations of allergen-specific cure.
- Routine bacterial swab of every weeping eczema patch. Treat clinically; swab only if not responding.
- Skin biopsy. SKIDS does not biopsy at Tier 1; route any biopsy indication to §7 Tier 3 partner.

## §5 Severity grading [SACRED SECTION]

B1-05 grades severity per condition, mapping to the three intervention tiers (§6.1 Light / §6.2 Standard / §6.3 Intensive) and to §7 if beyond-severe.

### 5.1 Atopic dermatitis — SCORAD-graded

| Severity | SCORAD | Intervention tier | Subscription tier match |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | <25 | §6.1 Light: emollient + low-potency topical steroid PRN | Basic ₹2,999 |
| **Moderate** | 25–50 | §6.2 Standard: emollient + mid-potency topical steroid course + topical calcineurin inhibitor for face/eyelids + sleep-impacted antihistamine | Advanced ₹6,999 |
| **Severe** | >50 | §6.3 Intensive: as Standard + structured wet-wrap therapy + bleach-bath protocol + 4-week scheduled review | Premium ₹12,999 |
| **Refractory beyond Tier 1** | SCORAD ≥40 after 4 weeks of optimised §6.3 *with documented adherence and trigger-control* | §7 routine to pediatric dermatology (consideration of phototherapy, dupilumab, systemic immunosuppression) | — |

### 5.2 Acne vulgaris — IGA-Acne-graded

| Severity | IGA-Acne | Intervention tier | Subscription tier match |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | 1–2 (almost clear / mild) | §6.1: topical retinoid + benzoyl peroxide; reassess at 8–12 weeks | Basic ₹2,999 |
| **Moderate** | 3 | §6.2: topical retinoid + benzoyl peroxide + topical antibiotic OR oral antibiotic (≥8 years) for ≤ 3 months; reassess at 8–12 weeks | Advanced ₹6,999 |
| **Severe** | 4 (severe inflammatory or nodulocystic) | §6.3: oral antibiotic + topical retinoid + benzoyl peroxide; assess at 6 weeks; if no improvement OR scarring → §7 | Premium ₹12,999 |
| **Refractory or scarring** | IGA 4 with scarring at presentation OR IGA ≥3 after 12 weeks of optimised §6.3 | §7 routine to pediatric dermatology for isotretinoin pathway | — |

### 5.3 Infectious dermatoses — extent-and-systemic-features-graded

For impetigo, tinea, scabies, etc., the operational severity grading follows the same Light / Standard / Intensive / Refer logic but is condition-specific:

| Condition | Mild (Light §6.1) | First-line Rx at this tier | Moderate (Standard §6.2) | First-line Rx | Severe (Intensive §6.3) | §7 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Impetigo** | < 5 lesions, no constitutional | Mupirocin 2% topical TDS × 5d | ≥ 5 lesions OR facial | Cephalexin 25 mg/kg/dose BD × 7d (or amoxicillin-clavulanate) | Multifocal OR systemic | §7.2 if cellulitis / MRSA [Stevens-2014-IDSA] |
| **Scabies** | Individual, no household | Permethrin 5% cream overnight × 2 (day 1, 8) | Household coverage | Permethrin 5% all contacts + oral ivermectin 200 mcg/kg × 2 | Crusted suspected | §7.2 if crusted confirmed [IADVL-Scabies-2021] |
| **Tinea corporis/cruris** | < 3 lesions, < 5 cm | Clotrimazole 1% cream BD × 4 weeks | Multiple / > 5 cm | Terbinafine cream BD × 4 weeks | Tinea capitis (any) | Griseofulvin 20 mg/kg/day × 6–8w; §7.3 if refractory |
| **Pediculosis** | Localised | Permethrin 1% lotion × 10–20 min, repeat day 7 | Whole-family | Same + household linen treatment | Pyoderma 2° | §7.3 if resistant to permethrin + malathion |
| **Molluscum/warts** | Few, asymptomatic | Observation (self-limiting 6–18m); cryotherapy if symptomatic | Many / distressing | Salicylic acid 15–17% daily under occlusion × 12w | — | §7.3 if immunocompromised + extensive |
| **Cellulitis** | — | — | — | — | — | All cellulitis: §7.2 non-systemic, §7.1 systemic — B1-05 does not manage |

### 5.4 Reactive dermatoses

| Condition | Mild (Light) | Moderate (Standard) | Severe (Intensive) | §7 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Acute urticaria** | Localised, no systemic features | Generalised, no airway involvement | Recurrent or chronic > 6 weeks (route to B1-06 Allergy) | Anaphylaxis context → §7.1 |
| **Contact dermatitis** | Localised, mild irritant pattern | Generalised or recurrent | Severe with vesiculation | Suspected severe ACD requiring patch testing → §7.3 |
| **Drug eruption** | Mild morbilliform, no mucosal / systemic features | Generalised, no mucosal | Mucosal involvement OR systemic features | Suspected SJS / TEN / DRESS → §7.1 |

### 5.5 Pigmentary and lesion-surveillance categories

These do not have a Light / Standard / Intensive grading; they have a surveillance grade or a §7 referral pattern.

| Finding | Action |
|---|---|
| **Salmon patch / nevus simplex** (face, nape) | Reassure; expected to fade by school age (face) or persist (nape, "stork bite"). No intervention. |
| **Café-au-lait macules** | Count and measure. ≥ 6 macules ≥ 5 mm prepubertal (or ≥ 15 mm postpubertal) → §7.3 routine to neurology/genetics for NF1 evaluation [NELSON-21:CH656]. |
| **Melanocytic nevus** | Photograph + measure. Surveillance at next periodic visit; § 7 routine to pedoderm if any ABCDE feature (asymmetry, border irregularity, colour variation, diameter > 6 mm with growth, evolving). |
| **Infantile hemangioma** without functional risk | Photograph + measure. Surveillance. |
| **Infantile hemangioma** periocular, airway, perineal, large facial (PHACES suspicion), or with ulceration | §7.2 urgent to pediatric dermatology / pedo-ophthalmology / pediatric ENT as appropriate. |
| **Vitiligo** (suspected) | §7.3 routine to pediatric dermatology. B1-05 does not initiate vitiligo-specific therapy. |
| **Pityriasis alba** | Counsel, emollients, sunscreen counselling. In-clinic management. |
| **Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation** | Counsel, sunscreen, treat the underlying inflammatory dermatosis. In-clinic management. |

### 5.6 Boundary rules

- *Borderline severity (within 2 SCORAD points or one IGA-Acne tier):* default to the higher tier for the first treatment cycle. If the patient improves at the lower-tier intensity at follow-up, step down. The cost of starting one tier higher and stepping down is small; the cost of undertreating moderate disease is loss of family confidence in the protocol.
- *Re-grading after intervention:* re-administer SCORAD / IGA at every follow-up using the same scale and the same scorer. Document delta. The delta is the operational measure of treatment response — not an absolute score.
- *Severity progression triggers:* a SCORAD or IGA score that has worsened by ≥ 25% between two consecutive visits despite documented adherence is a "fast-progression flag" — review for missed diagnosis (e.g., AD that is actually contact dermatitis from a missed allergen, or impetigo superinfection masquerading as AD flare), trigger reassessment, and consider §7 routing if no clear cause emerges.
- *Multi-condition presentation:* a child with both AD and acne, or AD with concurrent impetigo, has each condition graded separately and treated per its own severity tier. The PHR records a SCORAD and an IGA and an impetigo-extent measure independently.
- *Adolescent-specific severity uplift:* an IGA-Acne score that would be Mild on lesion count but where the CDLQI is ≥ 13 (very large QoL impact, especially in the bullying / school-refusal context) is treated at one tier higher than the IGA-only grade. Document rationale.

## §6 Therapeutic options

### 6.1 Light tier (Mild AD, Mild acne, mild infectious dermatoses)

**Atopic dermatitis (Mild, SCORAD <25):**

| Drug | Indication | Pediatric dose | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Petroleum jelly OR ceramide-based emollient | First-line maintenance | Apply 30 g/week minimum, all body surface, twice daily and after every bath [Cochrane-CD003871] | Indefinite (life-long for atopics) |
| Hydrocortisone 1% cream / ointment | Active flare areas | Thin layer once daily; fingertip-unit dosed by region | 7–14 days per flare; pause between flares |
| Cetirizine | Sleep-impacted pruritus only | 2.5 mg PO once nightly (6m–2y) · 5 mg (2–6y) · 5–10 mg (≥6y) | PRN flares, ≤ 14 days continuous |

Behavioural / Lifestyle:
- Bath duration ≤ 10 minutes, lukewarm, mild non-soap cleanser. Pat-dry. Apply emollient within 3 minutes of patting dry [AAD-2014-AD].
- Cotton clothing, avoid wool / synthetic next-to-skin layers.
- Identify and remove irritants (perfumed soap, fabric softener, alcohol-based cleansers).
- Nail-cutting weekly to reduce excoriation depth; cotton-mitten overnight in <3y if night-scratching dominates.

Parent-applied:
- Demonstrate fingertip-unit dosing in clinic. One fingertip unit (FTU) covers approximately two adult palm areas. Anti-steroid-phobia counselling: chronic emollient use is not a steroid; topical steroid use at the doses and durations prescribed has not been shown to cause systemic effects in healthy children at recommended courses [AAD-2014-AD].

Allied staff:
- Clinic nurse delivers one structured emollient-application demonstration at the first visit.

Dispensary stock:
- Petroleum jelly 100g jars; ceramide-based emollient 200g pump; hydrocortisone 1% 15g cream / ointment; cetirizine 5 mg/5 mL syrup, 10 mg tablets.

Expected timeline to improvement: SCORAD reduction ≥ 30% by week 4.

**Acne (Mild, IGA 1–2):**

| Drug | Indication | Pediatric dose | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Adapalene 0.1% gel | Comedonal / mild inflammatory acne | Apply thin layer once nightly to whole acne-prone area | 12 weeks minimum to assess [AAD-2024-Acne] |
| Benzoyl peroxide 2.5% gel | Inflammatory papules | Apply once daily (mornings) | 12 weeks |

Behavioural:
- Gentle (non-stripping) cleanser twice daily.
- Discourage picking and squeezing (causes scarring and PIH).
- Sunscreen daily (especially with retinoid use — increased photosensitivity).
- Reassurance: most adolescent acne improves with consistent use of the regimen by 12 weeks; the family should expect 8 weeks before visible improvement.

Dispensary stock: Adapalene 0.1% gel 30g; benzoyl peroxide 2.5% gel 30g; gentle facial cleanser.

Expected timeline to improvement: visible improvement by 8–12 weeks.

### 6.2 Standard tier (Moderate AD, Moderate acne, scabies, tinea capitis, etc.)

**Atopic dermatitis (Moderate, SCORAD 25–50):**

In addition to all of §6.1:

- Photosensitivity with doxycycline — sunscreen mandatory; cap-and-clothing counselling.
- GI upset with doxycycline — take with food and a full glass of water; avoid lying down immediately after dose.
- Topical retinoids during pregnancy — formal pregnancy counselling per B1-13 Adolescent for any sexually active adolescent; tretinoin oral is not used at SKIDS Tier 1.

### 6.3 Intensive tier (Severe AD, Severe acne, scabies — crusted suspicion, etc.)

**Atopic dermatitis (Severe, SCORAD >50):**

In addition to all of §6.1 and §6.2:

|---|---|

| Bleach-bath protocol | As §6.2 but twice weekly mandatory during the severe flare |
| Trigger investigation | Joint with B1-06 Allergy if food / aeroallergen trigger pattern is suspected; specific IgE panel ordered through B1-06 |
| Scheduled review at 4 weeks | Mandatory; if SCORAD ≥ 40 at the 4-week review with documented adherence and trigger control, route §7.3 routine to pedoderm |
| Higher-potency topical steroid (betamethasone valerate 0.1%) | Reserved for thick lichenified plaques on extremities only; 7-day course, then step down to mid-potency. Never face / eyelids / genitals / flexures. [AAD-2014-AD] |

**Acne (Severe, IGA 4):**

| Intervention | Detail |
|---|---|
| Oral doxycycline + topical retinoid + benzoyl peroxide | Per §6.2 dosing; assess at 6 weeks. If no improvement OR active scarring at presentation → §7.3 routine for isotretinoin pathway |
| Psychosocial co-management | CDLQI mandatory. Joint-care with B1-13 Adolescent if CDLQI ≥ 13. |

Dispensary stock additions: betamethasone valerate 0.1% cream 15g (restricted access; B1-05 supply only).

**Scabies (Crusted Norwegian — suspected):**

Crusted scabies is NOT managed at B1-05 alone. Initial workup at B1-05; routes to §7.2 urgent for:
- Confirmation of crusted disease.
- Combination ivermectin + topical permethrin + keratolytic (salicylic acid 5–10%) regimen.
- Hospitalisation if institutional-source / immunocompromised host.

### 6.4 Contraindications and precautions

| Therapeutic | Contraindication / precaution | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Topical corticosteroid on face / eyelids / genitals / flexures (mid- and high-potency) | Skin atrophy, telangiectasia, glaucoma, perioral dermatitis | Restrict mid- and high-potency to body / trunk / limbs. Use hydrocortisone 1% (low-potency) on face, OR topical calcineurin inhibitor [AAD-2014-AD] |
| Tacrolimus / pimecrolimus | Age <2 years (off-label below this); active viral skin infection (HSV, varicella, molluscum extensive) | Hold during active viral infection; do not initiate <2y without dermatology supervision |
| Doxycycline | Age <8 years (tooth staining, enamel hypoplasia); pregnancy; photosensitivity | Use erythromycin alternative <8y for moderate acne (rarely needed at this age); pregnancy test in adolescent female before initiation; sunscreen counselling mandatory |
| Topical retinoid | Pregnancy (limited evidence of harm; precaution); photosensitivity | Counsel; not contraindicated in non-pregnant adolescents |
| Ivermectin oral | Weight <15 kg | Use permethrin 5% topical exclusively |
| Permethrin 5% | Age <2 months | Use sulfur 6% precipitated in petrolatum (older Indian-context first-line for neonatal scabies) [IADVL-Scabies-2021] |
| Griseofulvin | Severe liver disease; porphyria | Switch to terbinafine after baseline LFT |
| Terbinafine oral | Severe liver disease; concurrent QT-prolonging drugs | Baseline LFT; review medication list |
| Cephalexin / cloxacillin | Documented penicillin / cephalosporin allergy | Substitute clindamycin or azithromycin per culture; route §7.3 if recurrent SSTI in beta-lactam-allergic child for B1-19 input |
| Bleach baths | Open weeping wounds (sting); concentrated bleach (skin damage) | Strict dilution counselling; do not exceed 0.005% |

### 6.5 Lesion surveillance and proactive maintenance

For pigmented and vascular lesions under surveillance per §5.5:
- Photograph at baseline + measure with adhesive ruler / dermatoscope reticule.
- Re-photograph and re-measure at every B1-05 visit and at least annually under A1 surveillance.
- Counsel family on the ABCDE features warranting unscheduled return.

Proactive maintenance for AD with frequent flares (≥ 4 flares/year):
- Twice-weekly mid-potency topical steroid OR tacrolimus to relapse-prone sites, on top of daily emollient. Documented to reduce flare frequency [AAD-2014-AD].

### 6.6 Tapering and stopping

- *Topical corticosteroids:* once flare is controlled (typically 7–14 days of daily use), step down to either: (a) intermittent flare-only use as needed, OR (b) twice-weekly proactive maintenance for relapse-prone sites. Do not continue daily topical steroid indefinitely.
- *Topical calcineurin inhibitors:* maintenance use twice-weekly is acceptable long-term; not associated with the steroid-side-effect profile.
- *Oral antibiotics for acne:* 3-month maximum at any single course. After course completion, continue topical retinoid + benzoyl peroxide indefinitely; if recurrence requires another oral course, separate by ≥ 3 months and consider §7 for isotretinoin pathway.
- *Oral antifungals for tinea capitis:* continue 2 weeks past clinical clearance; do not stop on first appearance of regrowth.
- *Anti-scabies:* fixed two-application schedule; do not extend.

## §7 Hard referral triggers [SACRED SECTION]

The case escalates out of B1-05's scope when ANY of the following is true:

### 7.1 Emergency referral (same day, transport directly to ER)

- [ ] **Suspected Stevens-Johnson Syndrome / Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis** — any rash with mucosal involvement (oral, ocular, genital), bullae, sloughing, Nikolsky-positive areas, or suspected drug-induced epidermal detachment. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER + pediatric dermatology consult, within 1 hour. Discontinue all suspect drugs immediately.
- [ ] **Suspected Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome** — any age but especially infants/young children with diffuse erythema, fever, sloughing in flexures or peri-orifices. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER, within 1 hour.
- [ ] **Suspected DRESS** — drug eruption with high fever, lymphadenopathy, eosinophilia, hepatitis, or other organ involvement. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER, within 1 hour. Discontinue suspect drugs.
- [ ] **Eczema herpeticum suspected** — atopic dermatitis with rapid eruption of monomorphic punched-out vesicles / pustules, often with systemic features (fever, lymphadenopathy). → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER for IV aciclovir within 1 hour; do NOT continue topical steroid at the affected site until ruled out.
- [ ] **Anaphylaxis context** — any child presenting with urticaria + airway / cardiovascular features, or with prior anaphylaxis without an action plan and adrenaline auto-injector. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER + B1-06 Allergy hand-off; adrenaline IM administered same-day if active anaphylaxis.
- [ ] **Suspected non-accidental injury** — bruise patterns inconsistent with developmental ability or with caregiver explanation; burns with immersion or pattern indicators; unusual cigarette-burn distribution; healed parallel marks suggestive of repeated injury. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER + child-protection referral, within 1 hour. Same retention protocol as A1 §7.1 — child not discharged to suspected-perpetrator caregiver.
- [ ] **Cellulitis with systemic features** — extending erythema with fever, tachycardia, lethargy, lymphangitic streaks. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER for IV antibiotics within 1 hour [Stevens-2014-IDSA].
- [ ] **Necrotising fasciitis suspicion** — pain disproportionate to skin findings, rapidly extending erythema, dusky / violaceous discolouration, bullae, crepitus, or systemic toxicity. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER and pediatric surgeon within 1 hour.
- [ ] **Erythroderma at any age** — confluent erythema covering > 80% body surface area, regardless of suspected cause. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER + pediatric dermatology, within 1 hour.

### 7.2 Urgent referral (within 7 days)

- [ ] **Cellulitis without systemic features** — extending erythema, warmth, lymphadenopathy, no fever / tachycardia. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric infectious disease / pediatric ER, within 24–48 hours; B1-19 Infections may co-manage if same-network. Empirical oral cephalexin or clindamycin started at B1-05 as a bridge if appointment > 24 hours away.
- [ ] **Suspected crusted (Norwegian) scabies** — hyperkeratotic plaques with very high mite burden, often in immunocompromised, neurologically impaired, or institutional contacts. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric dermatology, within 7 days; isolation precautions; combination ivermectin + topical regimen initiated under specialist guidance [CDC-Scabies-2024].
- [ ] **Hemangioma at high-risk site** — periocular (vision threat), perinasal / airway, perineal (ulceration risk), large facial (>5 cm or PHACES suspicion), multifocal cutaneous (visceral hemangiomatosis screen). → SKIDS-T3 pediatric dermatology / pedo-ophthalmology / pediatric ENT, within 7 days. Propranolol initiation occurs at the specialist, not at B1-05.
- [ ] **New-onset purpura without trauma** — palpable purpura suggests vasculitis (HSP, etc.); non-palpable suggests thrombocytopenia (ITP, leukaemia). → SKIDS-T3 pediatric haematology + same-week B1-18 Blood as a bridge.
- [ ] **Confirmed BP elevation co-presenting with skin findings** (e.g., neurofibromatosis with hypertension, vasculitis with hypertension). → SKIDS-T3 pediatric nephrology + B1-17 Kidney & Urology as a bridge; A1's BP-elevation §7 protocol applies.

### 7.3 Routine referral (next available appointment, ≤ 4 weeks)

- [ ] **Refractory atopic dermatitis** — SCORAD ≥ 40 after 4 weeks of optimised §6.3 with documented adherence and trigger control. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric dermatology, within 4 weeks. Send: SCORAD trajectory (3 sequential scores), documented therapy with quantities used, adherence assessment, photographs, current regimen, IgE / aeroallergen / food panel results from B1-06 if any [AAD-2014-AD].
- [ ] **Refractory or scarring acne** — IGA ≥ 3 after 12 weeks of optimised §6.3, OR scarring at presentation, OR ≥ 2 prior 3-month oral antibiotic courses. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric dermatology for isotretinoin pathway, within 4 weeks. Pregnancy test in adolescent female before referral [AAD-2024-Acne].
- [ ] **Suspected vitiligo** — confirmed depigmentation (not pityriasis alba). → SKIDS-T3 pediatric dermatology, within 4 weeks. B1-05 does not initiate vitiligo-specific therapy.
- [ ] **Café-au-lait macule count meeting NF1 screening criteria** — ≥ 6 macules ≥ 5 mm prepubertal (or ≥ 15 mm postpubertal). → SKIDS-T3 pediatric neurology / clinical genetics, within 4 weeks [NELSON-21:CH656].
- [ ] **Pigmented lesion with ABCDE features** — asymmetry, border irregularity, colour variation, diameter > 6 mm with growth, evolving features. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric dermatology, within 2–4 weeks for dermoscopy and possible biopsy. (Pediatric melanoma is rare but the threshold for referral is low.)
- [ ] **Recurrent impetigo / SSTI / boils requiring decolonisation protocol or immunological workup** → B1-19 Infections (within SKIDS network; not §7) for the workup, with §7.3 to pediatric immunology only if PID criteria met [B1-19].
- [ ] **Suspected genodermatosis** — ichthyoses, epidermolysis bullosa suggestive findings, neurocutaneous syndromes (TS, NF, Sturge-Weber). → SKIDS-T3 pediatric dermatology + clinical genetics, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Connective tissue disease cutaneous findings** — discoid rash, malar rash, dermatomyositis-pattern findings, scleroderma-pattern. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric rheumatology (or B2-03 if available), within 4 weeks; B1-05 does not manage CTD skin findings beyond initial assessment.
- [ ] **Cosmetic or psychosocial-priority referral** — keloid scarring after acne resolution; significant facial PIH causing distress; congenital nevus removal request from family. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric dermatology / pedoderm cosmetic procedure, within 4–8 weeks. Joint-care with B1-13 Adolescent for psychosocial dimension.

### 7.4 Handoff communication

When B1-05 fires a §7 referral, the SKIDS pediatrician must send the receiving clinician (auto-generated by Companion):

1. **Child identification:** name, DOB, child_id, parent contact, parent consent confirmation, current medications, known allergies (especially to topical / oral agents), atopic comorbidities.
2. **The trigger:** which §7 sub-trigger fired with the underlying clinical finding.
3. **Diagnosis (provisional or confirmed):** ICD-10 code where determined, plain-language description.
4. **Severity grade trajectory:** all available SCORAD / IGA / extent-and-systemic scores in chronological order; quantified delta from initial presentation to current.
5. **Therapies tried and outcomes:** drug, dose, duration, adherence, response. Include both effective and ineffective trials.
6. **Photographs:** minimum three (initial presentation, mid-treatment, current) attached as image files.
7. **Investigations done at SKIDS:** KOH / culture / bacterial swab / LFT / IgE results.
8. **Bridge interventions started:** any therapy initiated at the §7 visit pending the appointment (e.g., oral cephalexin started for cellulitis pending pediatric-ID review).
9. **Reason for referral, in clinical terms:** one or two sentences from the SKIDS pediatrician on what they need from the receiving clinician.
10. **Urgency tier and target window:** §7.1 / §7.2 / §7.3 with the time bound.
11. **SKIDS-T3 / clinic** and appointment booking status.

The packet is auto-generated by Companion when the trigger fires. The SKIDS pediatrician reviews and signs off before transmission. For §7.1, the receiving clinician is also called by phone — the Companion packet records, but does not substitute for, the verbal handoff.

## §8 Follow-up schedule

### 8.1 Follow-up frequency

| Severity tier | First follow-up | Subsequent | Annual review |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mild AD | 4 weeks | every 3 months until stable then every 6 months | Yes |
| Moderate AD | 4 weeks | every 6–8 weeks until controlled | Yes |
| Severe AD | 2 weeks | every 4 weeks until SCORAD <25 then space out | Yes |
| Mild acne | 8–12 weeks | every 12 weeks | Yes |
| Moderate / Severe acne | 6 weeks | every 6 weeks during active oral antibiotic; every 12 weeks once stable | Yes |
| Impetigo | day 7 (telephone or photo via app acceptable) | as needed | — |
| Scabies | 4 weeks | as needed (re-treatment or contact tracing) | — |
| Tinea capitis | 4 weeks (mid-course); 8 weeks (end-of-course) | confirm clearance with KOH if available | — |
| Lesion surveillance (nevi, café-au-lait, hemangioma) | 6 months | 12 months | Yes |

### 8.2 What to recheck at each follow-up

- *AD:* SCORAD, POEM (≥2y), itch VAS (≥7y), CDLQI; review of trigger diary; emollient quantity used (the family should be using ≥30 g per week of body emollient in moderate disease — measured against the 250–500 g monthly dispensing).
- *Acne:* IGA-Acne, lesion count (5-region scoring), CDLQI ≥ 16y or DLQI ≥ 16y (or family-impact scale below 16y).
- *Infectious dermatoses:* clearance documented clinically; KOH at end of tinea capitis course where available; contact-tracing completion for scabies and pediculosis.
- *Lesion surveillance:* photograph + measure; ABCDE re-assessed for nevi.

### 8.3 Treatment success criteria

- *AD:* SCORAD reduction ≥ 50% from baseline AND patient/family satisfaction reported, with sustainable maintenance regimen identified.
- *Acne:* IGA reduction by ≥ 1 grade by 12 weeks; ≥ 2 grades by 6 months. Sustained on topical maintenance.
- *Impetigo:* clinical clearance by day 7.
- *Scabies:* no new burrows by week 4; pruritus may persist (counsel).
- *Tinea capitis:* mycological clearance (KOH negative if available) at 8 weeks; clinical regrowth by 12–16 weeks.
- *Lesion surveillance:* lesion stable in size, colour, surface; no ABCDE features.

### 8.4 Step-down criteria

- Step down topical steroid potency once flare is controlled.
- Move from active treatment to twice-weekly proactive maintenance for AD when ≥ 8 weeks of stable control achieved.
- Stop oral antibiotic at 12 weeks for acne; continue topical maintenance.
- Discharge from B1-05 active follow-up to A1 surveillance once disease has been stable for ≥ 6 months on maintenance regimen.

### 8.5 Step-up criteria (within Tier 1)

- AD SCORAD increase by ≥ 25% between consecutive visits despite documented adherence → escalate intervention tier; reassess for missed trigger or superinfection.
- Acne IGA increase by ≥ 1 grade at the 12-week review → escalate intervention tier; review adherence and consider §7.

### 8.6 Step-out criteria

- Per §7. Specifically: SCORAD ≥ 40 after 4 weeks of optimised §6.3 (AD); IGA ≥ 3 after 12 weeks of optimised §6.3 (acne); failure of culture-confirmed therapy (tinea, scabies); any §7.1 / §7.2 trigger fires at any review.

### 8.7 Disenrolment / loss-to-follow-up

A child active on B1-05 management who misses two scheduled follow-ups for an active moderate-severe condition is contacted by clinic Manager (WhatsApp, then phone). If no response within 30 days from the second missed appointment, named pediatrician calls. Loss-to-follow-up with active oral antibiotic (acne, tinea capitis, impetigo) is a §12 quarterly-review trigger because of antimicrobial-stewardship implications.

## §10 Parent-facing communication

### 10.1 Scripted explanations

When grading is Mild AD:

> "[Aanya] has a mild form of eczema. Her skin barrier doesn't hold moisture as well as it should, so it gets dry, itchy, and red in patches. We're going to do two things: a thick moisturiser applied generously, twice a day and after every bath, all over her body — even on the areas that look fine — and a small amount of medicated cream on the active patches for one to two weeks until they calm down. The moisturiser is for life; the medicated cream is for flares. Most children with mild eczema have it well-controlled with this routine. We'll see her back in four weeks to check how she's doing."

When grading is Moderate AD:

> "[Aanya] has moderate eczema. We're going to use a stronger medicated cream — still a small amount, only on the active patches — for about two weeks, and add another medicated cream specifically for her face and eyelids that doesn't have steroid in it, so you can use it longer if needed. The moisturiser routine is even more important now: at least two big tubes a month, generously applied. We'll also start a flare-trigger diary on the parent app for two weeks so we can identify what makes her flares worse. We'll see her back in two to four weeks."

When grading is Severe AD:

> "[Aanya]'s eczema is severe. We're going to do everything we've discussed — the moisturiser, the medicated creams — plus a technique called wet-wraps for a week. The clinic nurse will demonstrate how to do them, and you'll continue at home. We'll also discuss whether food triggers might be involved and get our allergy clinic to look at that. I'll see her back in two weeks. If at four weeks she's not significantly better, I'll send her to the children's skin specialist for additional treatment options. This is not because we are giving up — it's because severe eczema sometimes needs treatments that are best started by a specialist."

When grading is Moderate or Severe acne:

> "[Rohit]'s acne is at the [moderate / severe] level. We're going to do a combination treatment: a cream applied at night, another in the morning, plus an oral antibiotic for about three months. The reason for the antibiotic is to control the inflammation faster than creams alone can. The treatment isn't an instant fix — it takes about six weeks before you see clear improvement and three to four months for the major changes. Don't pick at the spots; that's what causes the marks that last after the spot itself has gone. Use sunscreen daily — the medication makes skin more sun-sensitive. We'll see [him] back in six weeks."

When §7.1 emergency fires:

> "I'm worried about [name]'s rash. The way [his/her] skin and mouth are looking is something that needs to be seen at the hospital today, urgently. I've already called [partner ER] and they are expecting [him/her]. The medication [he/she] was on may be causing this; we need to stop it right now. Let's get [him/her] there. I will be in touch with the team."

When §7.3 routine fires (refractory AD or acne):

> "[Aanya / Rohit]'s [eczema / acne] hasn't improved enough on the treatment we've tried. That doesn't mean nothing will work — it means [he/she] needs the next level up, which the children's skin specialist can offer. I've sent across all of [his/her] history, photographs, and what we've tried, so the specialist won't start from scratch. The appointment is [date]. I'll stay involved — once the specialist's plan is clear, we coordinate it from this clinic."

### 10.5 Cultural sensitivities

- *Steroid phobia.* Indian families often refuse or under-apply topical steroid because of widespread "steroid is dangerous" narratives. Counter explicitly with: (a) named drug (mometasone), named potency class (mid), named anatomic restriction (body only, not face), named duration (7–14 days per flare). Address the specific brand-name fears the family raises. Document the discussion. Re-assess adherence at follow-up before assuming treatment failure.
- *Traditional applications and home remedies.* Henna, mustard oil, coconut oil, neem paste, turmeric, sandalwood pastes are commonly applied. Engage rather than dismiss: most are not harmful (some — neem, turmeric — have weak antimicrobial activity), but the active ingredient is inert against AD or moderate-severe acne. The risk is delaying effective therapy. Discuss adding the topical steroid / retinoid / antibiotic *alongside* the family's chosen home remedy if the family insists, rather than demanding cessation. Document.
- *Adolescent acne and marriageability narratives.* Acne in adolescent girls is sometimes framed by extended family in marriage-prospects terms, which intensifies psychosocial impact. Address adolescent privately for HEEADSSS-style screening per A1 / B1-13. Avoid using marriage-prospects framing in clinical conversation; redirect family conversations to clinical and self-image-impact framing.
- *Diet-and-acne discourses.* Eliminate-this-food advice circulates widely. Counsel: aside from a possible weak association with high-glycaemic-load and skim milk, dietary intervention has limited evidence in acne management. Do not endorse extensive dietary restriction; the family's nutritional adequacy comes first [AAD-2024-Acne].
- *Skin colour and pigmentation.* Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation is more visible and persistent in skin-of-colour. Counsel proactively that PIH is expected to fade over months, sunscreen helps prevent worsening, and pigmentation does not equal active disease.
- *Privacy in adolescent skin examinations.* Sensitive areas (genital, breast, gluteal) are examined with a chaperone (parent or clinic staff member of same gender as the adolescent), with the adolescent's verbal consent, and only when clinically necessary.

## §12 Quality and audit

### 12.1 Outcome metrics

B1-05 is working well when:

- ≥ 70% of Moderate AD cases achieve SCORAD reduction ≥ 50% by week 8 of §6.2 management.
- ≥ 80% of Mild and Moderate impetigo achieve clinical clearance by day 7 with first-line therapy (no second-line escalation).
- ≥ 90% of scabies cases achieve clearance with the two-application permethrin or two-dose ivermectin protocol; whole-household treatment completed in ≥ 95% of household-pattern cases.
- ≥ 80% of Moderate / Severe acne cases achieve IGA reduction ≥ 1 grade by week 12.
- < 5% of all oral antibiotic courses extend beyond 12 weeks (acne) or beyond 8 weeks (tinea capitis) — antimicrobial-stewardship metric.
- 100% of high-potency topical steroid prescriptions (betamethasone valerate or higher) are reviewed at the next §12 quarterly audit; clinically appropriate use rate ≥ 95%.
- 100% of §7.1 emergency triggers result in same-day handoff to the SKIDS-T3.
- ≥ 95% of refractory AD / acne (§7.3) referrals attend the pediatric dermatology appointment within the protocol-specified window.
- Steroid-phobia-related under-treatment (operationalised as: families dispensed < 100 g emollient/month for moderate AD or skipping topical steroid in > 50% of prescribed days at adherence assessment) is addressed in ≥ 80% of identified cases by re-counselling or reassessment within 2 weeks. ### 12.4 The SKIDS Fellowship pathway

Pediatricians delivering B1-05 who wish to advance to credentialed practice may apply for The SKIDS Fellowship in pediatric dermatology, where it is offered via partner institutions. See `docs/06-the-skids-fellowship.md`. The credentialed pediatric dermatologist would then deliver:

- Refractory AD currently routing to §7.3 (phototherapy decision, biologic / dupilumab initiation, systemic immunosuppression).
- Refractory or scarring acne (isotretinoin pathway with REMS-equivalent monitoring).
- Vitiligo, psoriasis, alopecia areata, and other conditions B1-05 currently routes out.
- Procedural dermatology (cryo, electrocautery, biopsy, basic surgical excision).
- Protocol governance for B1-05 across the SKIDS network.

Pediatric dermatology is not currently a B2 protocol in the SKIDS catalogue (which has only Endocrine, Neurology, Rheumatology). The Fellowship may expand B1-05 into a credentialed-tier complex pathway as the network grows; see `docs/04-tier-system.md` for the framework.
