# SKIDS Gastrointestinal Clinic

> The SKIDS Gastrointestinal Clinic delivers structured evaluation
> and management of recurrent abdominal pain (functional and
> organic), GORD, coeliac disease screening, chronic diarrhoea
> workup, Helicobacter pylori testing, NAFLD co-management with
> B1-10, and routing to pediatric gastroenterology for IBD, hepatic
> disease, and endoscopy. Functional constipation routes to B1-21;
> B1-15 manages organic/refractory constipation.

---

## §1 Service definition

**What this clinic does:**
- Evaluates recurrent abdominal pain (RAP) using Rome IV criteria to distinguish functional abdominal pain disorders (FAPD: functional dyspepsia, irritable bowel syndrome, abdominal migraine, functional abdominal pain NOS) from organic causes [Rome-IV-2016].
- Manages gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) in infants and children: distinguishes physiological GOR (common, self-limiting) from pathological GORD (with complications); initiates conservative management and acid-suppression therapy where indicated [NASPGHAN-GORD-2018].
- Screens for coeliac disease with tissue transglutaminase IgA (tTG-IgA) + total IgA in children with chronic diarrhoea, growth faltering, iron-deficiency anaemia refractory to oral iron, or family history; routes confirmed coeliac to §7.3 for endoscopic biopsy confirmation and dietitian-led gluten-free diet [ESPGHAN-Coeliac-2020].
- Evaluates chronic diarrhoea (> 2 weeks) with structured differential: infectious, post-infectious, coeliac, CMPA (cross B1-06), lactose intolerance, toddler's diarrhoea, IBD (§7.3), and rare causes.
- Tests for Helicobacter pylori in children with dyspeptic symptoms and epigastric pain; treats confirmed infection per ESPGHAN/NASPGHAN guidelines (triple therapy) [NASPGHAN-Hp-2017].
- Co-manages NAFLD with B1-10 Obesity: B1-10 manages lifestyle intervention; B1-15 manages hepatological workup when ALT persistently elevated > 2× ULN (liver ultrasound, further LFTs, §7.3 if fibrosis suspected).
- Manages acute gastroenteritis (AGE) follow-up: post-AGE secondary lactose intolerance (cross B1-09), post-AGE IBS, persistent diarrhoea post-AGE.
- Evaluates GI bleeding (upper and lower): haematemesis, melena, haematochezia — stratifies urgency and routes per §7.
- Manages organic/refractory constipation that does not respond to B1-21 functional-constipation management (Rome IV) after 3 months, or constipation with alarm features (blood in stool, failure to thrive, delayed meconium passage, absent anal wink).
- Cross-cares with B1-09 (malabsorption-driven nutritional deficiency), B1-06 (CMPA, food allergy with GI manifestation), B1-10 (NAFLD), and B1-14 (GORD as asthma trigger).

**What this clinic does not do:**
- Perform upper or lower GI endoscopy (§7.3 to pediatric gastroenterology partner).
- Manage confirmed IBD (Crohn's disease, ulcerative colitis) — §7.3 to pediatric GI for induction and maintenance therapy.
- Manage hepatic disease beyond NAFLD screening (Wilson disease, autoimmune hepatitis, cirrhosis → §7.3).
- Manage GI bleeding requiring endoscopic intervention — §7.1/§7.2 ER.
- Manage surgical GI conditions (appendicitis, intussusception, volvulus, pyloric stenosis) — §7.1 ER + pediatric surgery.
- Manage neonatal jaundice — §7.1 per A1 protocol.
- Manage cystic fibrosis GI manifestations — §7.3 to pediatric pulmonology + GI.

**Age range covered:** 0 – 18 years.

**SKIDS subscription tier(s):**
- **Basic ₹2,999** — A1 GI routing; first B1-15 assessment on per-visit pricing.
- **Advanced ₹6,999** — up to 4 B1-15 visits per year; coeliac screening included; H. pylori testing; GORD management.
- **Premium ₹12,999** — unlimited B1-15 visits; multi-protocol cross-care (B1-15 + B1-09 + B1-10 + B1-06); priority pediatric GI partner referral.

**SKIDS clinical tier:** Tier 1. Every SKIDS pediatrician manages GORD, RAP, and chronic diarrhoea under this protocol. Pediatric gastroenterology partner (Tier 3) provides endoscopy, IBD management, hepatology, and complex GI workup. Protocol governance with named pediatric gastroenterologist.

**Departures from cited guidelines:**

1. *Coeliac no-biopsy pathway.* ESPGHAN 2020 permits a no-biopsy diagnosis when tTG-IgA > 10× ULN + positive EMA-IgA + symptomatic + HLA-DQ2/DQ8 positive. SKIDS follows this pathway where HLA testing is available; where not, routes to §7.3 for endoscopic biopsy. This is a pragmatic departure — many Indian centres cannot perform HLA-DQ2/DQ8 [NEEDS EXPANSION IN EDITORIAL PASS].

2. *H. pylori test-and-treat not routine.* NASPGHAN/ESPGHAN 2017 recommends against test-and-treat in children (unlike adults). SKIDS tests only for dyspeptic symptoms, not asymptomatic carriage. This is guideline-concordant but worth emphasising because some Indian practitioners test-and-treat broadly.

3. *PPI duration limited.* SKIDS limits empirical PPI use to 4–8 weeks with mandatory reassessment. Long-term PPI in children has safety concerns (bone density, C. difficile, nutrient malabsorption). Step-down to H2RA or discontinuation is attempted at each review.

## §4 Diagnostic workflow

### 4.1 Recurrent abdominal pain assessment

**Rome IV FAPD criteria (must be met for ≥ 2 months, at least 4 episodes/month):**

| FAPD type | Key features |
|---|---|
| **Functional dyspepsia** | Epigastric pain/burning, early satiety, postprandial fullness; NOT relieved by defaecation |
| **Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS)** | Abdominal pain related to defaecation; change in stool frequency or form |
| **Abdominal migraine** | Paroxysmal, intense, periumbilical pain lasting ≥ 1h; associated with anorexia, nausea, vomiting, pallor; stereotyped pattern; between episodes the child is well |
| **Functional abdominal pain NOS** | Does not fit other FAPD categories |

**Alarm features that exclude functional diagnosis (investigate for organic cause):**
- Weight loss or growth faltering.
- Blood in stool (gross or occult).
- Dysphagia.
- Persistent vomiting.
- Night-time pain that wakes the child.
- Family history of IBD, coeliac, or peptic ulcer disease.
- Perianal disease (fistula, fissure, skin tags).
- Fever.
- Joint symptoms (IBD-associated arthritis).
- Delayed puberty.
- Right upper quadrant or right lower quadrant localisation.

### 4.2 Dyspepsia / H. pylori assessment

- Dyspeptic symptoms (epigastric pain, burning, postprandial discomfort) in child ≥ 6 years.
- H. pylori testing indicated ONLY if dyspeptic symptoms present — NOT for asymptomatic children [NASPGHAN-Hp-2017].
- **Test:** H. pylori stool antigen (HpSA) — preferred non-invasive test. Urea breath test (UBT) where available.
- **Do NOT use:** H. pylori serology (not reliable in children for active infection).
- Must be off PPI ≥ 2 weeks and antibiotics ≥ 4 weeks before testing.

### 4.3 GORD assessment

**Infants (< 12 months):**
- Physiological GOR: frequent effortless regurgitation; happy spitter; gaining weight; no distress → reassurance; not GORD.
- GORD: regurgitation + poor weight gain, OR feeding refusal, OR back-arching (Sandifer syndrome), OR respiratory symptoms (chronic cough, apnoea, wheeze), OR haematemesis → investigate.

**Children / adolescents:**
- Heartburn, chest pain, epigastric pain, acid brash, chronic cough (cross B1-14), dental erosion (cross B1-04).
- GORD questionnaire (GORD-Q where validated).

### 4.4 Chronic diarrhoea workup (> 2 weeks)

Structured differential:

| Category | Key features | Investigations |
|---|---|---|
| Infectious | Travel, exposure, daycare, immunocompromised | Stool routine + culture + ova/parasites + C. difficile toxin |
| Post-infectious | Following AGE; persistent loose stools | Secondary lactose intolerance trial; stool reducing substances |
| Coeliac | Growth faltering, bloating, pallor, diarrhoea/steatorrhoea | tTG-IgA + total IgA (§4.5) |
| CMPA (non-IgE) | Infant; bloody mucoid stools; eczema cross (B1-05/B1-06) | Elimination + re-challenge per B1-06 protocol |
| Toddler's diarrhoea | 6m–5y; well, growing, non-bloody loose stools; excessive juice/fluid | Dietary history; reduce juice; increase fat/fibre |
| IBD | Bloody diarrhoea, weight loss, growth faltering, perianal disease, fever | FBC + ESR + CRP + albumin + faecal calprotectin → §7.3 if elevated |
| Immunodeficiency | Recurrent infections, FTT, chronic diarrhoea | Immunoglobulins, lymphocyte subsets → §7.3 |

### 4.5 Coeliac screening

**Who to screen:**
- Chronic diarrhoea or steatorrhoea.
- Growth faltering without other cause.
- Iron-deficiency anaemia refractory to oral iron (cross B1-09).
- First-degree relative with coeliac disease.
- Type 1 diabetes (cross B2-01).
- Down syndrome, Turner syndrome, autoimmune thyroid disease.
- Dental enamel defects (cross B1-04).
- Recurrent aphthous ulcers (cross B1-04).

**Test:** tTG-IgA + total IgA (to exclude IgA deficiency, which gives false-negative tTG).
- tTG-IgA negative + normal total IgA → coeliac unlikely.
- tTG-IgA positive → §7.3 for confirmation (EMA-IgA + HLA-DQ2/DQ8 for no-biopsy pathway per ESPGHAN 2020, or endoscopic biopsy).
- tTG-IgA negative + low total IgA → check tTG-IgG or deamidated gliadin peptide IgG.

**Important:** child must be on a gluten-containing diet for ≥ 6 weeks before testing. Do not test during a gluten-free trial.

### 4.6 GI bleeding assessment

| Feature | Most likely cause | Urgency |
|---|---|---|
| Painless rectal bleeding (bright red, coating stool) in child 2–8y | Juvenile polyp (most common), anal fissure | §7.3 pediatric GI for colonoscopy (polyp) or B1-21 (fissure) |
| Bloody mucoid stools in infant | CMPA (most common), infectious colitis | B1-06 CMPA workup; stool culture |
| Haematemesis (vomiting blood) | Oesophagitis (GORD), Mallory-Weiss, variceal (portal hypertension) | §7.1 if haemodynamically significant; §7.2 if stable |
| Melena (black tarry stools) | Upper GI bleed | §7.1/§7.2 |
| Haematochezia (red blood mixed with stool) + weight loss + diarrhoea | IBD | §7.2 → pediatric GI |
| Painless massive rectal bleeding (child 2–10y) | Meckel diverticulum | §7.1 → ER + pediatric surgery |

### 4.7 Organic / refractory constipation

Children arriving from B1-21 after 3 months of refractory functional constipation, or children with alarm features at presentation:

**Alarm features for organic constipation:**
- Delayed meconium passage (> 48h) → Hirschsprung disease suspicion.
- Ribbon-like stools.
- Failure to thrive.
- Abdominal distension.
- Blood in stool without fissure.
- Absent anal wink or abnormal sacral exam.
- Lower limb neurological abnormality.

If alarm features present → §7.3 pediatric GI/surgery for anorectal manometry, rectal biopsy (Hirschsprung), or spinal imaging.

### 4.8 NAFLD hepatological workup

From B1-10 escalation (ALT > 2× ULN after 6 months lifestyle):
- Liver ultrasound (hepatic steatosis assessment).
- Full LFT panel (ALT, AST, GGT, ALP, bilirubin, albumin).
- Hepatitis B and C serology (exclude viral hepatitis).
- Ceruloplasmin + 24h urine copper (exclude Wilson disease if ALT persistently elevated in child ≥ 3 years).
- Autoimmune hepatitis screen (ANA, anti-smooth muscle antibody, IgG) if non-obese child with elevated transaminases.
- If fibrosis suspected (AST/ALT ratio, FIB-4 score) → §7.3 pediatric hepatology/GI for elastography or biopsy.

### 4.9 In-clinic investigations

| Test | Indication |
|---|---|
| Stool routine + occult blood | Chronic diarrhoea, GI bleeding screen |
| Stool culture + ova/parasites | Infectious diarrhoea, chronic diarrhoea |
| Stool reducing substances | Suspected lactose malabsorption |
| H. pylori stool antigen (HpSA) | Dyspeptic symptoms |
| Faecal calprotectin | Suspected IBD (elevated > 200 mcg/g supports inflammation) |
| tTG-IgA + total IgA | Coeliac screening |

### 4.10 Decision tree

```
Child presents with GI complaint
        │
        ▼
Recurrent abdominal pain → §4.1 Rome IV assessment
        ├── No alarm features → FAPD → §6.1 / §6.2
        ├── Alarm features → organic workup → §7.3 if IBD/structural
        │
GORD → §4.3
        ├── Physiological GOR (infant) → §6.1 reassurance
        ├── Pathological GORD → §6.2 acid suppression
        │

        ├── Infectious → treat + B1-09 cross
```

## §5 Severity grading [SACRED SECTION]

### 5.1 Recurrent abdominal pain

| Severity | Criteria | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild FAPD** | Rome IV criteria met; no alarm features; minimal school/activity impact | §6.1 Light — reassurance + dietary/lifestyle |
| **Moderate FAPD** | Rome IV criteria met; school absence ≥ 1 day/week; parental anxiety significant | §6.2 Standard — structured management + possible pharmacotherapy |
| **Severe FAPD** | Significant functional impairment; school refusal; pain behaviour dominating family life | §6.2 + B1-08 cross (pain psychology) |
| **Organic suspected** | Alarm features present | Investigate; §7.3 if confirmed organic |

### 5.2 GORD severity

| Severity | Features | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Physiological GOR** | Regurgitation without complications; thriving | §6.1 — position, feed modification |
| **Mild GORD** | Regurgitation + mild feeding reluctance; weight gain borderline | §6.2 — conservative + reassess |
| **Moderate GORD** | Feeding refusal, poor weight gain, back-arching, respiratory symptoms | §6.2 — acid suppression trial |
| **Severe / complicated GORD** | Haematemesis, stricture suspicion, Barrett suspicion (adolescent), apnoea | §7.3 → pediatric GI for endoscopy |

### 5.3 Chronic diarrhoea severity

| Severity | Features | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Well child, thriving, non-bloody (toddler's diarrhoea) | §6.1 — dietary adjustment |
| **Moderate** | Weight deceleration, nutritional deficiency emerging, or persistent > 4 weeks | §6.2 — investigate + treat cause |
| **Severe** | FTT, bloody, systemic features (fever, joint symptoms, perianal disease) | Investigate urgently; §7.2/§7.3 |

### 5.4 Boundary rules

- Functional constipation (Rome IV, no alarm features) → B1-21. Refractory after 3 months or alarm features → B1-15.
- Recurrent abdominal pain WITHOUT alarm features = FAPD until proven otherwise. Do NOT order imaging or endoscopy for FAPD without alarm features.
- CMPA with GI symptoms → B1-06 owns the diagnosis pathway (elimination/challenge); B1-15 co-manages if GI symptoms persist after diet modification.
- Iron-deficiency anaemia refractory to oral iron → B1-09 flags for B1-15 coeliac screening; B1-15 screens.
- Neonatal jaundice → §7.1 per A1, NOT B1-15 scope.
- Abdominal mass on examination → §7.2 to oncology, NOT B1-15 management.

## §6 Therapeutic options

### 6.1 Light tier

**Physiological GOR (infant):**
- Feed thickening (cereal-thickened feeds or pre-thickened formula if formula-fed).
- Upright positioning 20–30 minutes post-feed.
- Smaller, more frequent feeds.
- Reassurance: resolves in most infants by 12–18 months.

**Toddler's diarrhoea:**
- Reduce excessive juice/fluid intake (≤ 120 mL juice/day).
- Increase dietary fat and fibre.
- Reassurance: benign; child is well and growing.

**Mild FAPD:**
- Reassurance: name the diagnosis ("functional abdominal pain"); explain the brain-gut connection in age-appropriate terms.
- Dietary: adequate fibre; regular meals; reduce gas-producing foods if bloating.
- Lifestyle: regular sleep; physical activity; stress management.
- Parent-app FAPD resources activated.

### 6.2 Standard tier

**GORD acid-suppression trial (pathological GORD):**
- **PPI (omeprazole):** 1 mg/kg/day (max 20 mg) PO once daily before breakfast × 4–8 weeks [NASPGHAN-GORD-2018].
- Alternative: ranitidine 2 mg/kg/dose BD (if PPI not available/tolerated) [NEEDS EXPANSION — confirm ranitidine availability post-NDMA recall; famotidine as alternative].
- Reassess at 4–8 weeks: if symptoms resolved → taper and stop. If symptoms recur → restart; if recurrence × 2 → §7.3 for endoscopy.
- Do NOT continue PPI > 8 weeks without documented reassessment.

**Moderate FAPD (IBS-type):**
- Dietary: low-FODMAP trial supervised by dietitian (B1-09 cross) × 4–6 weeks; if response, systematic reintroduction.
- Pharmacological: peppermint oil capsules (≥ 8 years); hyoscine butylbromide 10 mg PO TDS PRN for spasm; amitriptyline 0.2 mg/kg at bedtime for visceral hypersensitivity (adolescent, off-label — use cautiously per B1-08 prescribing discipline).
- Psychological: pain-coping strategies; cognitive-behavioural approach; B1-08 cross-care if functional impairment significant.

**H. pylori eradication (confirmed HpSA-positive with dyspeptic symptoms):**
- Triple therapy × 14 days [NASPGHAN-Hp-2017]:
  - PPI (omeprazole 1 mg/kg/day) + amoxicillin 50 mg/kg/day (max 1 g BD) + clarithromycin 15 mg/kg/day (max 500 mg BD).
  - Alternative (clarithromycin resistance suspected): PPI + amoxicillin + metronidazole 20 mg/kg/day BD.
- Confirm eradication: HpSA or UBT ≥ 4 weeks after completing treatment (≥ 2 weeks off PPI).
- If eradication fails → §7.3 pediatric GI for culture-guided therapy.

**Coeliac management (confirmed diagnosis from §7.3):**
- Strict gluten-free diet (GFD) — lifelong. SKIDS dietitian leads dietary education and monitoring (cross B1-09).
- Monitor: tTG-IgA at 6 months and 12 months post-GFD initiation; should decline. Persistent elevation → adherence review.
- Nutritional monitoring: iron, B12, folate, vitamin D, calcium (malabsorption correction).
- B1-15 follow-up: 6-monthly × 2 years, then annually.

### 6.3 Intensive tier

- Severe GORD not responding to PPI trial → §7.3 for endoscopy + pH-impedance study.
- IBD confirmed at §7.3 → pediatric GI manages induction/maintenance; B1-15 monitors between GI visits; B1-09 manages nutritional support.
- Hepatological workup (§4.8) completed → §7.3 if fibrosis or non-NAFLD liver disease.

### 6.4 Contraindications and precautions

| Therapeutic | Contraindication / Precaution | Action |
|---|---|---|
| PPI (long-term) | > 8 weeks without reassessment | Mandatory step-down trial |
| Clarithromycin | QT-prolonging drugs | Use metronidazole alternative |
| Low-FODMAP diet | < 6 years; eating disorder risk | Do not use; dietary counselling only |
| Amitriptyline (low-dose) | Cardiac history; concurrent QT-prolonging drugs | ECG before initiation; B1-08 cross |
| Gluten-free diet | NOT for coeliac screening phase | Maintain gluten-containing diet until tTG-IgA drawn |

### 6.5 Dispensary stock

- Omeprazole 10 mg, 20 mg capsules; dispersible tablets where available.
- Famotidine 10 mg, 20 mg tablets.
- Amoxicillin suspension and capsules (shared with B1-03).
- Clarithromycin 125 mg/5 mL suspension; 250 mg tablets.
- Metronidazole 200 mg/5 mL suspension; 200 mg, 400 mg tablets.
- Hyoscine butylbromide 10 mg tablets.
- Oral rehydration salts (ORS).
- Stool-test collection kits (routine, culture, calprotectin, HpSA).

## §7 Hard referral triggers [SACRED SECTION]

### 7.1 Emergency referral (same day)

- [ ] **Acute abdomen** — severe abdominal pain + guarding + rigidity + signs of perforation / peritonitis. → ER + pediatric surgery.
- [ ] **Haemodynamically significant GI bleeding** — haematemesis with hypotension / tachycardia, or massive rectal bleeding (Meckel diverticulum suspicion). → ER.
- [ ] **Bilious vomiting in infant** — green vomiting in neonate/infant = midgut volvulus until proven otherwise. → ER + pediatric surgery immediately.
- [ ] **Abdominal mass** — any palpable abdominal mass. → §7.2 per A1 §7.2 oncology pathway.
- [ ] **Intussusception suspected** — intermittent severe colicky pain, currant-jelly stool, sausage-shaped abdominal mass, lethargy. → ER.

### 7.2 Urgent referral (within 7 days)

- [ ] **Suspected IBD** — bloody diarrhoea + weight loss + elevated faecal calprotectin (> 200 mcg/g) + raised CRP/ESR + low albumin. → Pediatric GI within 7 days.
- [ ] **Haematemesis (stable)** — non-massive, child haemodynamically stable. → Pediatric GI for endoscopy within 7 days.
- [ ] **Suspected Wilson disease** — low ceruloplasmin + elevated urine copper + Kayser-Fleischer rings (adolescent). → Pediatric hepatology within 7 days.
- [ ] **Autoimmune hepatitis suspected** — elevated transaminases + positive autoantibodies + elevated IgG. → Pediatric hepatology.

### 7.3 Routine referral (within 4 weeks)

- [ ] **Coeliac disease tTG-IgA positive** → Pediatric GI for confirmation (endoscopic biopsy or no-biopsy pathway).
- [ ] **GORD not responding to 2 courses of PPI (8 weeks each)** → Pediatric GI for endoscopy + pH-impedance.
- [ ] **H. pylori eradication failure** → Pediatric GI for culture-guided therapy.
- [ ] **Refractory constipation with alarm features** (delayed meconium, absent anal wink, ribbon stools, FTT) → Pediatric GI/surgery for Hirschsprung workup (rectal biopsy, anorectal manometry).
- [ ] **Painless rectal bleeding (juvenile polyp suspected)** → Pediatric GI for colonoscopy.
- [ ] **Chronic diarrhoea without identified cause after B1-15 workup** → Pediatric GI for endoscopy + biopsy.
- [ ] **NAFLD with suspected fibrosis** (elevated FIB-4, persistently elevated transaminases after 12 months lifestyle) → Pediatric hepatology for elastography/biopsy.
- [ ] **Suspected eosinophilic oesophagitis** (dysphagia + food impaction + GORD-unresponsive symptoms) → Pediatric GI for endoscopy + biopsy.
- [ ] **Perianal disease** (fistula, abscess, complex skin tags) in child/adolescent → Pediatric GI (IBD workup) + pediatric surgery.

### 7.4 Handoff communication

Standard packet:
1. Rome IV classification (FAPD type, if applicable).
2. Laboratory results (tTG-IgA, faecal calprotectin, H. pylori, LFTs, FBC + inflammatory markers).
3. GORD treatment history (PPI course dates, response).
4. Dietary history (dietitian-generated).
5. Growth trajectory (B1-09 cross-reference).
6. Companion-generated referral summary.

## §8 Follow-up schedule

| Scenario | First follow-up | Subsequent |
|---|---|---|
| Physiological GOR (infant) | 4–6 weeks (reassurance check) | PRN; resolves by 12–18 months |
| GORD on PPI | 4–8 weeks (trial endpoint) | 3-monthly during taper |
| FAPD (mild) | 4–6 weeks | PRN; annual at A1 |
| FAPD (moderate-severe) | 2–4 weeks | Monthly × 3; then 3-monthly |
| H. pylori treatment | 6 weeks post-treatment (eradication test) | PRN if eradicated |
| Coeliac (confirmed, on GFD) | 3 months | 6-monthly × 2 years; annual thereafter |
| Chronic diarrhoea (under investigation) | 2–4 weeks | Per diagnosis |
| NAFLD (hepatological workup) | Per §4.8 investigation timeline | Per pediatric GI guidance |

§8.2 What to recheck: symptom diary; growth parameters; dietary adherence (coeliac); PPI step-down attempt; stool pattern; lab monitoring per condition.

§8.3 Success: FAPD symptoms controlled with functional improvement; GORD symptoms resolved with PPI stopped; H. pylori eradicated; coeliac tTG-IgA normalising on GFD; growth trajectory improving.

§8.7 Loss-to-follow-up: Companion alert for coeliac children (GFD adherence depends on ongoing engagement) and GORD children on PPI (prevent indefinite unsupervised PPI use).

## §9 Companion software workflow

### 9.1 Doctor Module screens

### 9.6 Edge cases

- Parent insists on endoscopy for FAPD without alarm features: counsel that Rome IV criteria predict functional diagnosis accurately; endoscopy is invasive and rarely changes management without alarm features. Document refusal to accept functional diagnosis.
- H. pylori testing in asymptomatic child (parent request): do NOT test. Counsel per NASPGHAN — treatment of asymptomatic carriage is not recommended in children.
- Gluten-free diet started before coeliac testing: advise 6-week gluten challenge before tTG-IgA testing; explain that testing on GFD gives false-negative results.
- Offline: paper symptom diary; upload when connectivity restored.

## §10 Parent-facing communication

### 10.1 Scripted explanations

Functional abdominal pain:

> "[Aanya]'s tummy pain is real — it is not imaginary. It is caused by the nerves in the gut being more sensitive than usual. This is very common in children and is called 'functional abdominal pain.' The good news is that there is no damage or disease in the gut. The not-so-good news is that it takes time and some lifestyle changes to settle down. We work on diet, sleep, activity, and stress management. Most children improve significantly over 3–6 months."

GORD:

> "[Arjun] has reflux — stomach acid coming up into the food pipe, which causes the pain and the sour taste. We will try a medicine that reduces acid production for [4–8 weeks]. At the same time, we adjust feeding patterns: smaller meals, no lying down immediately after eating, no late-night snacking. Most children respond well, and we aim to stop the medicine once things settle."

Coeliac:

> "The blood test suggests [Aanya] may have coeliac disease — her body reacts to gluten (a protein in wheat, barley, and rye) by damaging the lining of her gut. This explains the [symptoms]. We need to confirm the diagnosis with [a biopsy / additional blood tests], and then she will need a gluten-free diet. This is a lifelong change, but the good news is that once gluten is removed, the gut heals completely and she will feel much better. Our dietitian will help your family make this transition."

### 10.5 Cultural sensitivities

- *"It's just gas."* Many Indian families attribute all abdominal symptoms to "gas" (acidity). Acknowledge the concept; reframe with the specific diagnosis. "Gas" is not a medical diagnosis — the assessment tells us exactly what's happening.
- *Wheat centrality in Indian diet.* Coeliac diagnosis is particularly challenging in India where roti/chapati is a staple. Dietitian must provide rice-based, millet-based, and other gluten-free Indian alternatives. The family needs practical recipes, not just a list of foods to avoid.
- *Home remedies for stomach pain.* Ajwain (carom seeds), jeera (cumin) water, hing (asafoetida) — these are common Indian remedies. They are not harmful and may help with mild bloating. Do not dismiss; integrate where safe.
- *PPI reluctance.* Some families are wary of "strong medicines for the stomach." Explain the mechanism simply: "This medicine turns down the acid level so the lining can heal. It's a short course — [4–8 weeks] — not lifelong."
- *Endoscopy anxiety.* Parents fear endoscopy (especially under sedation). Explain when it is truly needed (alarm features, failed PPI, coeliac confirmation) and when it is NOT needed (functional pain without alarm features).

## §12 Quality and audit

§12.1 Metrics:
- ≥ 90% of RAP evaluations include documented Rome IV classification and alarm-feature screen.
- PPI stewardship: ≤ 5% of B1-15 children on PPI > 8 weeks without documented reassessment.
- Coeliac screening: ≥ 95% of tTG-IgA-positive children referred to §7.3 within 4 weeks.
- H. pylori: 100% of treated children have eradication test at 6 weeks post-treatment.
- Alarm-feature recognition: 100% of children with alarm features are investigated (not managed as FAPD).
- ≥ 95% of §7 triggers result in documented referral with complete handoff packet.
- Coeliac GFD adherence: ≥ 80% of coeliac children have declining tTG-IgA at 12-month follow-up.
- Loss-to-follow-up: < 15%.

§12.2 Quarterly: protocol owner reviews all §7 referrals, PPI prescribing audit, coeliac monitoring adherence, H. pylori eradication rates, alarm-feature recognition audit (random sample of 30 RAP visits).

§12.3 Certification: every SKIDS pediatrician delivering B1-15 must complete Rome IV FAPD training (4 hours); GORD management training (2 hours); coeliac-screening interpretation (2 hours); H. pylori testing and treatment protocol (2 hours). Annual update (2 hours).

§12.4 Fellowship: no specific SKIDS GI Fellowship pathway. Protocol governance sits with the named pediatric gastroenterologist.
