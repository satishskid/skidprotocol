# SKIDS Nutrition & Growth Clinic

> The SKIDS Nutrition & Growth Clinic manages growth faltering up to
> moderate severity, common nutritional deficiencies (iron, vitamin
> D, vitamin A, B12, zinc), feeding difficulties, breastfeeding and
> complementary feeding support, and adolescent nutrition. Severe
> acute malnutrition, refractory failure to thrive, suspected
> malabsorption, suspected eating disorder, and suspected endocrine
> short stature route per §7.

---

## §1 Service definition

**What this clinic does:**
- Manages growth concerns to moderate severity using WHO 0–5y and IAP 5–18y growth references with structured nutritional rehabilitation [WHO-2024-Growth, IAP-Growth-2015].
- Diagnoses and treats iron deficiency and iron deficiency anaemia (Hb between 7.0 g/dL and the WHO age-specific cutoff) with weight-based oral iron and dietary counselling [WHO-2011-Anaemia].
- Diagnoses and treats vitamin D deficiency (25-OH vitamin D < 20 ng/mL) with structured therapeutic dosing.
- Manages vitamin A deficiency clinical and biochemical with high-dose vitamin A per WHO / IAP schedule.
- Manages B12 deficiency with oral or IM cyanocobalamin / methylcobalamin.
- Provides breastfeeding support — latch, supply, milk transfer, and management of common breastfeeding problems (sore nipples, engorgement, mastitis routing) — in collaboration with the SKIDS dietitian / IYCF counsellor.
- Provides complementary feeding (CF) introduction support per IYCF / WHO-IYCF guidance, with a structured 6-month-onwards CF programme.
- Manages picky eating, food-refusal patterns, and behavioural feeding difficulties up to moderate severity with the SKIDS dietitian.
- Manages cow's milk protein allergy (CMPA) initial elimination and re-introduction in collaboration with B1-06 Allergy.
- Manages secondary lactose intolerance post-gastroenteritis with temporary lactose-restriction.
- Counsels on adolescent nutrition (sports / vegetarian / weight-management — distinct from B1-10 Obesity & Metabolic which manages obesity-tier cases).
- Initiates and oversees prophylactic supplementation prescribed at A1 (vitamin D, iron) and steps up to therapeutic dosing where deficiency is confirmed.

**What this clinic does not do:**
- Manage severe acute malnutrition (SAM — MUAC < 11.5 cm OR weight-for-height < −3 SD OR bilateral pitting oedema) — these are §7.1 to a SAM ward [WHO-SAM-2013].
- Manage severe anaemia (Hb < 7 g/dL OR any signs of haemodynamic compromise) — §7.1 / §7.2 to pediatric haematology and B1-18 Blood.
- Diagnose or manage suspected endocrine causes of short stature — these route to B2-01 Endocrine, with bridge supplementation only.
- Diagnose or manage suspected coeliac disease, inflammatory bowel disease, cystic fibrosis, or other malabsorptive disorders — §7.3 to pediatric gastroenterology with B1-15 GI as a bridge.
- Manage anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, ARFID, or other eating disorders — §7.3 to pediatric psychiatry with B1-08 Behavioural and B1-13 Adolescent as bridges.
- Manage obesity (BMI ≥ 95th percentile) as primary obesity care — that is B1-10. B1-09 manages the undergrowth-and-deficiency end of the spectrum.

**Age range covered:** 0 – 18 years. Specific age-band considerations:
- 0 – 6 months: exclusive breastfeeding support; supplementation per IAP / IYCF (vitamin K newborn, vitamin D from age 0–14 days for breastfed, iron from 6 months).
- 6 – 12 months: complementary feeding introduction; iron-rich CF emphasis; vitamin D and iron dosing per IAP schedule.
- 1 – 5 years: catch-up nutrition for any prior faltering; vitamin A schedule; deworming per IAP.
- 5 – 12 years: balanced-diet counselling; deficiency screening at age 5 and selectively thereafter.
- 12 – 18 years: adolescent nutrition; adolescent menstruating-female iron status; sports nutrition; vegetarian-adequacy review.

**SKIDS subscription tier(s):**
- **Basic ₹2,999** — A1 routing supported; first B1-09 visit on per-visit pricing; supplementation dispensed at SKIDS rates.
- **Advanced ₹6,999** — up to 4 B1-09 visits per year included; dietitian sessions included up to 4/year; supplementation dispensary covered. Suitable for managed iron deficiency anaemia, mild-moderate growth faltering, and CF / breastfeeding support programmes.
- **Premium ₹12,999** — unlimited B1-09 visits within the clinically indicated set; unlimited dietitian sessions; priority §7 partner referral for any escalation. Suitable for moderate growth faltering on rehabilitation, multi-deficiency profiles, and any concurrent multi-protocol management (B1-09 + B1-06 + B1-15, etc.).

**SKIDS clinical tier:** Tier 1, with a key allied-staff dependence — the SKIDS dietitian is integral to B1-09 operations, paralleling the optometrist dependency in B1-01. Without an in-clinic dietitian, B1-09 cannot operate at full capability. Protocol governance sits with the named subspecialist identified in §2 (assignment pending). Complex tier per §7 escalates to pediatric gastroenterology (Tier 3 partner), pediatric haematology (Tier 3), pediatric endocrinology (B2-01 if available, otherwise Tier 3), or named SAM-ward inpatient unit.

## §2 Evidence base

This protocol governs primary-care management of pediatric nutrition and growth at SKIDS clinics. It follows the most current recommendations from:

- **[IAP-Growth-2015]** — Khadilkar V, Yadav S, Agrawal KK, et al; IAP Growth Charts Committee. Revised IAP Growth Charts for height, weight and body mass index for 5–18-year-old Indian children. *Indian Pediatr.* 2015;52(1):47–55. Governing source for growth in 5–18 years in the Indian context.
- **[IAP-Period-2024]** — IAP Periodicity / Growth, Development and Behavioural Surveillance Recommendations. Already referenced from A1; B1-09 inherits A1's anaemia-screening and supplementation cadences.

**Departures from cited guidelines:**

1. *Universal anaemia screening at age 5 in addition to 9–12 months.* AAP recommends 12 months only. IAP recommends 9–12 months and 5 years given high Indian prevalence. B1-09 follows IAP. Adolescent menstruating females are screened annually (an additional Indian-context departure from AAP universal once-in-adolescence) [WHO-2011-Anaemia, IAP-Period-2024].

2. *Vitamin D therapeutic dosing.* IAP-VitaminD-2017 schedule used preferentially; AAP 600 IU/day prophylactic still applies for children with adequate sun exposure but is uncommon in urban Indian children. Indian-context default is supplementation across all healthy children at age-appropriate prophylactic doses via A1; therapeutic dosing for confirmed deficiency follows IAP [IAP-VitaminD-2017].

3. *Iron supplementation duration in IDA.* WHO recommends 3 months total; AAP recommends until Hb normalises plus 2–3 months. B1-09 follows AAP-aligned duration (until Hb normalises plus 3 months at therapeutic dose, then continue 1 mg/kg/day prophylactic at A1 cadence) given high Indian relapse rates and dietary iron inadequacy [Cochrane-CD009085, IAP-IDA-2021].

4. *Cow's milk protein allergy management.* Standard ESPGHAN-aligned 2–4 week elimination + structured re-introduction; co-managed with B1-06 Allergy for the IgE-mediated component if any. Departure: SKIDS dispenses extensively hydrolysed formula (eHF) where breastfeeding is not possible, rather than amino-acid formula (AAF) at first line — the latter is reserved for eHF failure or anaphylaxis history per B1-06 [ESPGHAN-CF-2017].

5. *Severe acute malnutrition.* B1-09 does not manage SAM. All SAM cases route §7.1. This is an operational departure — some primary-care services do manage outpatient SAM via WHO outpatient therapeutic programme (OTP). The SKIDS Tier 1 boundary deliberately routes SAM out because the OTP requires specific RUTF (ready-to-use therapeutic food) supply, daily monitoring infrastructure, and inpatient-fallback on appetite-test failure that exceeds B1-09 Tier 1 scope [NEEDS EXPANSION IN EDITORIAL PASS — confirm whether SKIDS clinics partner with public-health OTP centres and the referral pathway].

No other material departures.

## §3 Presenting concerns

A child arrives at SKIDS B1-09 Nutrition & Growth via one of these paths:

| Parent language | Clinical translation | Routing |
|---|---|---|
| "He's not growing like his cousin / brother / classmates." | Suspected growth faltering / short stature concern. | §4 growth pathway → §5.1 grade. |
| "She doesn't eat anything we give her." | Picky eating / food refusal. | §4 feeding-behaviour pathway. |
| "He's been pale and tired." | Possible anaemia. | §4 anaemia pathway → POC Hb. |
| "She's exclusively breastfed and I'm worried she isn't getting enough." | Breastfeeding adequacy concern. | §4 breastfeeding pathway → SKIDS dietitian / IYCF counsellor co-visit. |
| "He's six months old and I don't know how to start solids." | Complementary feeding initiation. | §4 CF pathway → structured CF programme. |
| "Every time he drinks milk he gets diarrhoea." | Possible CMPA or lactose intolerance. | §4 CMPA / lactose pathway, joint with B1-06. |
| "She's vegetarian and I worry about her nutrition." | Vegetarian diet adequacy. | §4 dietary-adequacy pathway → dietitian. |
| "He's very thin but otherwise active." | Constitutional thinness vs. faltering. | §4 growth pathway → review trajectory. |
| "We were told at the hospital his B12 was low." | Confirmed B12 deficiency referral. | §4 B12 pathway. |
| "Her teacher says she falls asleep in class." | Possible deficiency-related fatigue (iron, B12, vitamin D). | §4 anaemia + deficiency screen. |
| "He's adolescent, very active in sports, and tired all the time." | Adolescent sports nutrition + iron status. | §4 adolescent nutrition pathway. |
| "She's eating less, losing weight, and exercising a lot." | Possible eating disorder. | §4 with eating-disorder screen → §7.3 if criteria. |
| "We had gastroenteritis last week and now he can't tolerate milk." | Secondary lactose intolerance. | §4 → §6 lactose-restriction trial. |
| "She had measles / persistent diarrhoea / measles outbreak exposure." | Vitamin A status concern. | §4 → vitamin A schedule per WHO. |
| "He won't drink water; only juice / sugary drinks." | Hydration and added-sugar concern. | §4 dietary counselling. |

**Routing decisions:**
- A1 Orange growth flag, A1 Orange feeding flag, A1 confirmed POC Hb below cutoff, A1 vaccination-visit incidental nutritional concern → all route to B1-09 within 2 weeks (per A1 §6.3 routing matrix).
- A1 Red flags (MUAC < 11.5 cm SAM, Hb < 7 g/dL, Hb < 5 with compromise) bypass B1-09 and route §7.1 / §7.2 directly per A1 §7.
- B1-09 may co-manage with B1-06 (CMPA, food allergy), B1-15 (constipation, GORD, suspected GI cause of faltering), B1-08 (feeding difficulties with behavioural component), B1-13 (adolescent nutrition with psychosocial component), B1-18 Blood (anaemia workup beyond IDA).
- Clinical and sub-clinical hypothyroidism workup with growth-failure component: B1-09 orders TSH; if elevated → §7.3 to B2-01 Endocrine. B1-09 does not initiate thyroid replacement.

**Coming from SKIDS Screening (A1):**
- A1 §5.2 Growth domain Yellow / Orange flag (weight or height < −2 SD, crossing centiles, or specific BMI flags).
- A1 vaccination visit POC Hb at 9–12m or age 5 below WHO cutoff.
- A1 §3 parent-presenting growth or feeding concern.
- A1 §5.2 Acute malnutrition Yellow flag (MUAC 12.5–13.5 cm) — routes here for nutrition rehabilitation; Red MUAC < 11.5 routes §7.

## §4 Diagnostic workflow

### 4.1 Structured history

Ask, in this order:

1. *Birth and neonatal history.* Birth weight; gestational age; small-for-gestational-age vs. appropriate; admission to NICU; need for parenteral nutrition; congenital conditions identified at birth.
2. *Feeding history (chronological).*
   - Breastfeeding: initiation timing (within first hour ideal), exclusive duration, current status, frequency, duration per feed, transfer indicators (audible swallowing, contented post-feed), wet diapers per day (≥ 6 = adequate intake in young infant).
   - Formula: brand, dilution per scoop, frequency, total volume per day.
   - Complementary feeding: age started, foods introduced, refusal patterns, family-meal participation.
   - Current diet: 24-hour recall (verbatim) plus typical-day pattern; vegetarian / non-vegetarian; restrictive practices (Jain / vegan); milk intake (volume/day); sweet beverages and snacks volume.
3. *Growth trajectory.* Bring previous growth records / prior weights / prior heights / clinical notes. Plot all available points on the WHO 0–5 or IAP 5–18 chart in chronological order. Centile crossing matters more than absolute centile for individual children.
4. *Symptoms suggestive of malabsorption.* Chronic diarrhoea, fatty stools, abdominal distension, recurrent vomiting, oral aversion. Suggest possible coeliac, IBD, CF, or chronic infection.
5. *Symptoms of deficiency.* Pallor, irritability (iron); bone pain, weakness, frequent fractures (vitamin D); night blindness, dry eyes (vitamin A); glossitis, peripheral neuropathy (B12); skin and hair changes (zinc, biotin, multi).
6. *Activity and developmental status.* Energy level, school performance, sports participation. (Decrement here is often the first-noticed symptom of deficiency.)
7. *Family history.* Parental heights (mid-parental height calculation), constitutional thinness in family, celiac / IBD / thyroid disease in family, adolescent menarche timing in mother and sisters (relevant for late-bloomer assessment).
8. *Psychosocial.* Caregiver structure; meal-time atmosphere; food security at household level (a real and under-asked question — Indian middle-class clinics often miss food-insecurity in lower-middle-class families); religious or fasting practices in older children.
9. *Adolescent-specific.* HEEADSSS — eating in front of others, weight-perception, exercise patterns, body-image (eating-disorder screen). Menstrual history and pad / tampon use (heavy menstrual loss as anaemia driver). Substance use (smoking, alcohol affect nutrition).
10. *Current and recent illnesses.* Acute or chronic illnesses cause appetite reduction and growth faltering; often the relevant intervention is the underlying illness, not "nutrition."
11. *Previous deficiency treatments.* Iron syrup tried (which one, dose, duration, response), vitamin D given, multivitamin, herbal / Ayurvedic / Unani preparations.

**Red flags to elicit at history (any presentation):**
- Weight loss in any timeframe (vs. plateau).
- Loss of previously-attained skills / regression.
- Fever, bone pain, night sweats.
- Recurrent infections beyond age-typical pattern.
- Polyuria, polydipsia (T1DM screen).
- Goitre or thyroid symptoms.
- Significant constipation or diarrhoea > 4 weeks.
- Eating-disorder behaviours (food-restriction with weight loss, self-induced vomiting, excessive exercise, body-image disturbance).
- Suspected food insecurity at household level.
- Pica beyond age 2.

### 4.2 Examination

Examine for:

- *Anthropometry.* Weight, length (< 24 months) or height, head circumference (< 36 months), MUAC (6m–5y), BMI, waist circumference (≥ 5y if obesity-screen relevant). Use a calibrated electronic scale, infantometer, stadiometer, and MUAC tape.
- *Plot on the appropriate chart* (WHO 0–5 or IAP 5–18) — every measurement, every visit, in chronological order.
- *Z-score / percentile auto-calculation* — done by Companion §9.
- *Mid-parental height* — relevant for short-stature assessment.
   - Boys: (mother's height + father's height + 13) / 2.
   - Girls: (mother's height + father's height − 13) / 2.
   - Target height range = mid-parental height ± 8.5 cm.
- *General* — pallor (conjunctiva, palms, tongue), jaundice, oedema (bilateral pitting in dependent areas — calf, dorsum of foot — is a SAM red flag and routes §7.1), hydration.
- *Hair* — colour (depigmentation in protein-energy malnutrition), texture, easy pluckability.
- *Skin* — rough/dry (vitamin A), pellagra-pattern (B3/niacin), seborrheic-dermatitis-like (zinc, biotin), petechiae (vitamin C).
- *Mouth* — angular cheilitis (riboflavin, iron, B12), atrophic glossitis (B12, iron, folate), bleeding gums (vitamin C), dental caries (B1-04 cross-care).
- *Eyes* — Bitot's spots (vitamin A), conjunctival xerosis, pallor.
- *Cardiovascular* — heart rate, murmurs (anaemia-related flow murmur), hepatomegaly (in severe nutritional disease).
- *Abdomen* — distension, hepatosplenomegaly, masses.
- *Musculoskeletal* — rachitic features (rickets — frontal bossing, rachitic rosary, bow legs / knock knees, wrist widening), muscle bulk / wasting (gluteal flattening in malnutrition).
- *Neurological* — tone, age-appropriate motor milestones, peripheral-neuropathy signs (B12).
- *Pubertal staging (≥ 8y female, ≥ 9y male)* — Tanner staging where consent obtained. Pubertal delay relative to age and bone age is a consideration for §7.3 routing.

### 4.3 Scales and questionnaires

| Scale / measurement | Domain | Age | Cutoff |
|---|---|---|---|
| **WHO Z-score (W/A, H/A, W/H, BMI/A)** | Growth | 0–5 | mild faltering −1 to −2 SD, moderate −2 to −3 SD, severe < −3 SD or oedema → §7 [WHO-2024-Growth] |
| **IAP percentile (W/A, H/A, BMI/A)** | Growth | 5–18 | mild < 10th OR > 90th, moderate < 3rd OR > 97th, severe < 0.4th OR > 99.6th [IAP-Growth-2015] |
| **MUAC** | Acute malnutrition | 6m–5y | 12.5–13.5 cm Yellow (moderate), 11.5–12.5 cm Orange (severe), < 11.5 cm Red → §7.1 [WHO-SAM-2013] |
| **Hb cutoff (WHO age-specific)** | Anaemia | 6m–18y | 6m–5y < 11.0 g/dL · 5–12y < 11.5 · 12–15y < 12.0 · ≥ 15y female non-pregnant < 12.0 · ≥ 15y male < 13.0 [WHO-2011-Anaemia] |
| **24-hour dietary recall** | Intake | 2–18 | Calorie / protein / iron / calcium adequacy assessed against ICMR-RDA-2020 |
| **Food-frequency questionnaire (FFQ)** | Habitual intake | 5–18 | Used for vitamin A, iron, calcium dietary risk assessment |
| **SCOFF eating-disorder screen** | ED screen | 11–18 | ≥ 2 positive answers → §7.3 routine to pediatric psychiatry |
| **Pediatric Picky Eating Score (or local equivalent)** | Picky eating | 2–8 | Score-based; structured behavioural-feeding programme if elevated |
| **Mid-parental height** | Short-stature context | any | Target ± 8.5 cm |
| **Parental concern about growth (open Likert)** | Family context | any | Extreme parental concern with normal growth → counselling-emphasis visit |

### 4.4 In-clinic device tests

Required:
- *Calibrated electronic infant scale* (≤ 24 months) and *child / adult scale* (≥ 24 months); calibration weekly.
- *Infantometer* (length board) for ≤ 24 months.
- *Stadiometer* for ≥ 24 months; SECA or equivalent calibrated.
- *MUAC tape* (WHO standard).
- *POC haemoglobinometer* (HemoCue or equivalent) — for routine 9–12m, age 5, adolescent female, and any clinical anaemia suspicion.
- *Glucometer* — capillary glucose where T1DM is on the differential (polyuria + weight loss).

Optional / on-indication:
- *BIA (bioelectrical impedance analyser)* — for BMI ≥ 85th percentile (cross-care to B1-10 Obesity & Metabolic), or for suspected sarcopenic / kwashiorkor-like patterns.
- *Salter / digital scale appetite-test setup* — for SAM-trigger borderline appetite test if integrated with public-health OTP partnership [NEEDS EXPANSION IN EDITORIAL PASS].

### 4.5 Laboratory and imaging

Order at baseline workup of moderate growth faltering or specific deficiency suspicion:
- **Full blood count + reticulocyte count + peripheral smear** — for any confirmed anaemia (POC Hb below cutoff).
- **Serum ferritin + serum iron + total iron-binding capacity (TIBC) + transferrin saturation (TSAT)** — for anaemia workup. Ferritin < 12 μg/L (under 5y) or < 15 μg/L (5y+) is diagnostic of iron-deficient erythropoiesis [WHO-2011-Anaemia, IAP-IDA-2021]. Caveat: ferritin is an acute-phase reactant; check CRP if recent illness and re-test if elevated.
- **25-OH vitamin D** — for any rachitic features; for BMI < 5th percentile or > 95th percentile; for any suspected deficiency. Therapeutic threshold: < 20 ng/mL deficient; 20–30 ng/mL insufficient; > 30 ng/mL sufficient [IAP-VitaminD-2017].
- **Serum calcium, phosphate, ALP** — for rickets workup.
- **Serum vitamin B12** — for any megaloblastic anaemia (high MCV); for vegetarian / vegan adolescents with fatigue or neurological symptoms; for confirmed deficiency.
- **Serum zinc** — for clinical zinc-deficiency suspicion (acrodermatitis-like rash, growth failure with diarrhoea).
- **Serum vitamin A (retinol)** — for clinical xerophthalmia or post-measles status.
- **Thyroid function (TSH)** — for any height < −2 SD or crossing centile downward; for weight gain disproportionate to height.
- **Urinalysis** — for any failure to thrive workup (renal cause screen).
- **Stool routine + occult blood + ova/parasite + giardia antigen** — for any suspected malabsorption or chronic GI symptoms; for unexplained iron-deficiency-anaemia not responding to oral iron.
- **Coeliac serology (anti-tissue-transglutaminase IgA + total IgA)** — for chronic diarrhoea + growth failure, or unexplained IDA in school-age + adolescent. If positive, route §7.3 to pediatric GI.
- **HIV ELISA** — for any unexplained chronic growth faltering with infections; offer with pre- and post-test counselling per national policy.
- **Bone age (left wrist X-ray)** — for short stature workup (height < −2 SD), to differentiate constitutional growth delay (bone age delayed = catch-up potential) from genetic short stature (bone age = chronological) from pathological causes (bone age varies).

Do not order routinely:
- Serum proteins / albumin in well children with mild growth faltering (a normal albumin does not rule out malnutrition; a low albumin is non-specific).
- Bone densitometry (DEXA) — pediatric indications are narrow; route to specialist where indicated.
- Genetic / metabolic panels — these route to clinical genetics / metabolic clinic via §7.3.
- Routine stool biochemistry (faecal elastase, calprotectin) — these route via pediatric GI.

## §5 Severity grading [SACRED SECTION]

B1-09 grades severity per pathway. The growth pathway and the deficiency pathways are graded against established cutoffs; the behavioural feeding pathway has a graded behavioural rubric.

### 5.1 Growth faltering / short stature / wasting

| Severity | Threshold | Intervention tier | Subscription tier match |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | W/A or H/A or W/H Z-score −1 to −2 SD (0–5y) OR W/A or H/A 3rd–10th percentile (5–18y); MUAC 12.5–13.5 cm | §6.1 Light: dietitian counselling, household-level diet review, recheck at 3 months | Basic ₹2,999 |
| **Moderate** | Z-score −2 to −3 SD OR < 3rd percentile OR MUAC 11.5–12.5 cm OR crossing two major centiles downward over ≥ 6 months | §6.2 Standard: structured 12-week nutritional rehabilitation; deficiency screen ordered; family meal-pattern intervention; recheck at 4 weeks | Advanced ₹6,999 |
| **Severe** | — | §7.1 SAM ward referral. Z-score < −3 SD OR MUAC < 11.5 cm OR bilateral pitting oedema [WHO-SAM-2013] |
| **Refractory beyond moderate Tier 1** | No catch-up after 12 weeks of structured §6.2 with documented adherence; OR worsening on rehabilitation | §7.3 routine to pediatric gastroenterology + endocrinology workup | — |

For short stature specifically (height-for-age):
- Constitutional growth delay (bone age delayed, family history of late puberty, normal growth velocity) — surveillance only at B1-09; no §7.
- Pathological short stature — height < −2 SD with growth velocity < −1 SD per year, OR height < −3 SD, OR height significantly below mid-parental target — routes §7.3 to B2-01 Endocrine after B1-09 baseline workup.

### 5.2 Acute malnutrition (specific MUAC-based grading from A1)

| MUAC | Grade | Action |
|---|---|---|
| ≥ 13.5 cm | Green | No acute malnutrition; routine surveillance |
| 12.5–13.5 cm | Yellow (moderate) | §6.1 Light tier rehabilitation |
| 11.5–12.5 cm | Orange (severe) | §6.2 Standard tier rehabilitation in B1-09; close monitoring with weekly MUAC for 4 weeks; if MUAC drops < 11.5 cm at any review → §7 |
| < 11.5 cm | Red — SAM | §7.1 immediately to SAM ward; B1-09 does not manage |
| Any MUAC + bilateral pitting oedema | Red — SAM (kwashiorkor pattern) | §7.1 immediately to SAM ward |

### 5.3 Iron deficiency anaemia (IDA)

| Severity | Hb (per WHO age-specific cutoff applied) | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Latent / iron-deficient erythropoiesis** | Hb at or just below cutoff with ferritin < 12 μg/L (under 5y) or < 15 μg/L (5y+); MCV mild reduction | §6.1 dietary intervention + 1 mg/kg/day prophylactic iron continuation |
| **Mild IDA** | Hb 1.0 g/dL below cutoff (e.g., 10.0 g/dL in 6m–5y); microcytic, hypochromic | §6.2 Standard: 3 mg/kg/day elemental iron PO until Hb normalises + 3 months; recheck Hb at 4 weeks (response check) and at 12 weeks |
| **Moderate IDA** | Hb 7.0 g/dL to 1.0 g/dL below cutoff | §6.2 / §6.3: 3–6 mg/kg/day elemental iron PO; same recheck schedule; consider parallel ferrous-rich dietary programme |
| **Severe IDA** | Hb < 7.0 g/dL with no haemodynamic compromise | §7.2 urgent to pediatric haematology + B1-18 Blood as bridge |
| **Severe IDA with compromise** | Hb < 7.0 g/dL with tachycardia, capillary refill > 3s; OR Hb < 5.0 g/dL at any presentation | §7.1 emergency to inpatient unit with transfusion capacity |

For non-iron-deficient anaemia (normal ferritin, microcytic — thalassemia trait suspected; or macrocytic — B12 / folate; or normocytic — chronic disease, etc.) — workup at B1-09 baseline (Hb electrophoresis where thalassemia trait suspected, B12 / folate where macrocytic) but management routes to B1-18 Blood for the specific workup pathway.

### 5.4 Specific deficiencies

| Deficiency | Threshold | Severity | Intervention |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Vitamin D** | 25-OH-D 20–30 ng/mL | Insufficient | §6.1 — increase prophylactic dose, sun-exposure counselling [IAP-VitaminD-2017] |
| | 25-OH-D < 20 ng/mL | Deficient | §6.2 — therapeutic dose; recheck 25-OH-D at 12 weeks |
| | < 20 ng/mL with rachitic features OR symptomatic hypocalcaemia OR severe bony deformity | Symptomatic | §7.3 routine to pediatric endocrinology / orthopaedics for bony deformity assessment |
| **Vitamin B12** | Serum B12 100–200 pg/mL with megaloblastic features | Borderline-deficient | §6.1 oral cyanocobalamin |
| | Serum B12 < 100 pg/mL OR macrocytic anaemia + low B12 | Deficient | §6.2 — high-dose oral OR IM cyanocobalamin per §6 |
| | B12 deficiency + neurological symptoms | Symptomatic | §7.3 routine to pediatric neurology + B1-09 management |
| **Vitamin A** | Clinical xerophthalmia (Bitot's spots, conjunctival xerosis) | Confirmed deficiency | §6 — high-dose vitamin A per WHO schedule |
| | Post-measles or persistent diarrhoea > 2 weeks | At-risk | §6 vitamin A schedule per WHO/IAP |
| | Active corneal involvement (corneal xerosis, ulceration) | Sight-threatening | §7.1 emergency to pediatric ophthalmology + IM vitamin A |
| **Zinc** | Clinical signs (acrodermatitis-like rash, persistent diarrhoea > 14 days, growth failure) | Suspected | §6 zinc supplementation; serum zinc to confirm where uncertain |
| **Folate** | Serum / RBC folate below cutoff (lab-specific) | Confirmed | §6 oral folate |

### 5.5 Picky eating / behavioural feeding (Mild / Moderate)

| Severity | Findings | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Limited variety, age-typical food refusals, no growth faltering, no nutritional deficiency, family stress only mild | §6.1 — counselling + dietitian one session + parent-app feeding programme |
| **Moderate** | Significant variety restriction, mealtime distress, parental coercion patterns, growth at lower end of normal but tracking | §6.2 — structured 6–12 week behavioural-feeding programme with dietitian, weekly check-ins, mealtime observation |
| **Severe** | Growth faltering, sensory-feeding difficulty (texture / colour / temperature aversion), gagging / choking pattern, oral-motor dysfunction suspected | §7.3 routine to multidisciplinary feeding clinic (typically pediatric GI + speech-language / feeding therapist + occupational therapy) |
| **ARFID / suspected eating disorder** | SCOFF ≥ 2 with clinical features OR weight-for-height < −1 SD with restrictive feeding pattern | §7.3 routine to pediatric psychiatry + B1-08 + B1-13 |

### 5.6 CMPA and lactose intolerance

| Condition | Severity | Intervention |
|---|---|---|
| **Suspected CMPA, non-IgE mediated, mild-moderate symptoms** (loose stools, blood-streaked stools in well infant, eczema with milk trigger) | Standard | §6.2 — 2–4 week elimination + structured re-introduction; eHF / soy formula (≥ 6m) where breastfeeding not possible. Joint with B1-06 [ESPGHAN-CF-2017] |
| **Suspected CMPA, IgE-mediated** (urticaria, vomiting within minutes of milk ingestion) | — | B1-06 Allergy primary management; B1-09 supports the dietary plan |
| **CMPA with anaphylaxis history** | — | §7.1 / B1-06 with named pediatric allergist; never elimination-and-reintroduction at B1-09 |
| **Secondary lactose intolerance post-gastroenteritis** | Self-limited | §6.1 — 2–4 weeks lactose-restricted diet; reintroduce in stages |
| **Suspected primary congenital lactase deficiency / galactosaemia** (rare; neonatal failure to thrive with diarrhoea) | — | §7.1 to pediatric metabolic / gastroenterology |

### 5.7 Breastfeeding / complementary feeding support

These do not have a Mild / Moderate / Severe grading; they have a structured-programme grade.

| Scenario | Action |
|---|---|
| **Breastfeeding question, otherwise well baby** | §6.1 IYCF counselling, single session |
| **Breastfeeding difficulty (poor latch, low supply concern, sore nipples)** | §6.2 structured breastfeeding-support programme — 2–4 sessions over 4 weeks with SKIDS IYCF counsellor |
| **Possible breast / bottle confusion, supplementation concerns** | §6.2 IYCF programme |
| **Exclusive breastfeeding faltering with weight loss > 10% birth weight in newborn** | §7.1 — newborn-readmission for feeding evaluation per A1 §7.1 |
| **CF initiation at 6 months** | §6.1 structured CF programme — 2 sessions + parent-app modules |
| **CF refusal or significant difficulty** | §6.2 — structured behavioural-feeding programme (see §5.5) |
| **Maternal mastitis suspected** | Refer mother to her physician; B1-09 supports continued breastfeeding [WHO-IYCF-2023] |

### 5.8 Adolescent nutrition

| Scenario | Action |
|---|---|
| **Vegetarian adolescent with nutritional adequacy concerns** | §6.1 dietitian-led adequacy review + targeted supplementation if needed |
| **Adolescent sports nutrition** | §6.1 / §6.2 dietitian programme; iron status check for any female adolescent in endurance sport |
| **Adolescent overweight (BMI 85–95th percentile)** | Cross-care to B1-10 Obesity & Metabolic; B1-09 supports the dietary component if §11 indicates joint care |
| **Adolescent eating-disorder screen positive** (SCOFF ≥ 2 + clinical features) | §7.3 to pediatric psychiatry + B1-08 + B1-13 |

### 5.9 Boundary rules

- *Plot before grade.* A single anthropometric measurement is graded against the chart, but the trajectory across visits is what determines management. A child at the 5th percentile who has been on the 5th percentile across all visits is constitutionally small; a child who has dropped from the 25th to the 5th percentile in 6 months is faltering and is one tier worse than the absolute number suggests.
- *Re-grade after intervention:* re-anthropometry at every visit; re-administer Hb / 25-OH-D / ferritin / B12 at the protocol-specified intervals (§8); re-administer SCOFF in adolescents at every visit.
- *Severity progression triggers:* worsening Z-score by ≥ 0.5 SD between visits despite documented adherence is a fast-progression flag → reassess for missed cause (organic disease, household food insecurity, feeding-environment problem) and consider §7.3 routing.
- *Multi-deficiency presentation:* iron + vitamin D + B12 deficient adolescent female is common in Indian practice. Treat all confirmed deficiencies concurrently; re-test the dominant deficiency at the appropriate interval to confirm response.
- *Family-stress uplift:* a child with mild growth faltering whose family is experiencing significant distress about feeding can be moved to Standard tier even if anthropometrics suggest Light tier — the family-stress component is itself a clinically significant intervention target.

## §6 Therapeutic options

### 6.1 Light tier (Mild faltering, mild deficiency, mild picky eating, single-session IYCF / CF)

**Mild growth faltering or constitutional thinness:**
- Dietitian counselling — single session — covering 24-hour recall analysis, ICMR-RDA targets for the child's age, family-meal observation prompts, and a 4-week food diary via the parent app.
- Continue all A1 prophylactic supplementation (vitamin D, iron) at A1 doses [IAP-VitaminD-2017, IAP-Period-2024].
- Recheck anthropometry at 3 months (or at the next periodic A1, whichever comes first).
- Counsel: most mild faltering tracks back to normal with environmental adjustment; do not pathologise.

**Latent iron deficiency (ferritin low, Hb at or just below cutoff):**
- Continue 1 mg/kg/day prophylactic iron (already at A1 §6.1) for an extended period (12 months) and confirm iron-rich-food intake on diet review.
- Recheck Hb + ferritin at 12 weeks.

**Vitamin D insufficiency (20–30 ng/mL):**

| Drug | Dose | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Cholecalciferol (vitamin D3) | 1000 IU/day < 1y; 1500–2000 IU/day 1–18y | 8–12 weeks; recheck 25-OH-D at 12 weeks; transition to prophylactic dose if normalised [IAP-VitaminD-2017] |

**Borderline B12 (100–200 pg/mL):**
- Cyanocobalamin / methylcobalamin 500 μg PO once daily × 4 weeks.
- Address dietary cause (vegetarian / vegan adolescents): counsel sources (dairy, eggs, fortified cereals) and consider continued prophylactic supplementation.

**Mild picky eating:**
- Single dietitian session.
- Parent-app modules: "Picky Eater Reset" 30-day programme.
- Family-meal counselling: shared meals, no screens at meals, one new food at a time, no coercion or rewards-with-food.

**CF initiation (single session at 6 months):**
- Structured 60-minute IYCF / dietitian visit.
- Parent-app CF programme: week-by-week food introduction guide, allergen-introduction guidance per ESPGHAN (early introduction of allergens including egg and peanut from 4–6 months in atopic-risk infants per allergist input — joint with B1-06) [ESPGHAN-CF-2017].
- Supplementation continued.

**Breastfeeding question, well baby:**
- Single IYCF session: latch demonstration if any concern, transfer assessment, discussion of typical feeding patterns, milk-supply reassurance, weight check on calibrated scale.

**Dispensary stock for §6.1:** vitamin D drops (400 IU and 1000 IU per drop); cholecalciferol oral solution (60,000 IU sachet for therapeutic doses); iron syrup (ferrous fumarate or ferrous sulfate, 25 mg elemental iron/5 mL); cyanocobalamin / methylcobalamin tablets (500 μg, 1500 μg); printed parent-app QR codes for nutrition videos.

Expected timeline: mild faltering catch-up over 3 months; vitamin D normalisation in 8–12 weeks.

### 6.2 Standard tier (Moderate faltering, IDA, vitamin D deficiency, behavioural feeding programme, IYCF programme, CMPA elimination)

**Moderate growth faltering / wasting (12-week structured nutritional rehabilitation):**

- Calorie and protein target: 130–150% of ICMR-RDA for age (catch-up requires excess) [ICMR-RDA-2020].
- Energy density: aim for 0.8–1.0 kcal/g of food via familiar high-energy foods (ghee, oils, full-fat dairy where culturally acceptable, paneer, eggs in non-vegetarians).
- Iron, vitamin D, vitamin A, zinc supplementation at therapeutic doses (see specific entries below) given documented or likely co-deficiency.
- Family-meal pattern intervention: 3 main meals + 2–3 snacks; ≥ 80% of meals taken with family.
- Dietitian sessions: weekly for the first month, then biweekly.
- Anthropometry weekly for 4 weeks, then biweekly until trajectory established.
- Treat any concurrent infection, deworming if indicated, address parasitic load (giardia, ascaris where stool-positive).

**IDA Mild / Moderate:**

| Drug | Dose | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Ferrous fumarate / ferrous sulfate (oral) | 3 mg/kg/day elemental iron PO once daily on empty stomach OR with vitamin C-containing food (orange juice; not with milk / tea / antacid) | Until Hb normalises + 3 months at therapeutic dose; transition to 1 mg/kg/day prophylactic at A1 cadence [IAP-IDA-2021, Cochrane-CD009085] |
| Iron polymaltose complex (alternative for GI intolerance) | Dose-equivalent to ferrous fumarate | As above |

Recheck Hb at 4 weeks (response check — Hb should rise by ≥ 1 g/dL). If no response at 4 weeks, reassess: adherence (most common cause), wrong diagnosis (re-screen ferritin, consider thalassemia trait, occult bleeding), or absorption issue — and consider §7.3 routine to B1-18 Blood for fuller workup.

Counsel: stool darkening expected; constipation common (encourage hydration + fibre, treat per B1-21 if persistent); take with vitamin C-rich food; do not take with calcium-rich food / tea / coffee / antacid.

**Vitamin D deficiency (< 20 ng/mL, no rachitic features):**

| Age | Dose | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| 0–1 year | Cholecalciferol 2000 IU/day | 6 weeks |
| 1–18 years | Cholecalciferol 60,000 IU PO once weekly | 6 weeks; OR 2000 IU/day × 6 weeks [IAP-VitaminD-2017] |

After 6 weeks, transition to prophylactic dose. Recheck 25-OH-D at 12 weeks.

**B12 deficiency (< 100 pg/mL OR macrocytic anaemia + low B12):**

| Drug | Dose | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Cyanocobalamin / methylcobalamin oral | 1000–2000 μg PO once daily | 4–8 weeks; recheck B12 at 8 weeks |
| Cyanocobalamin IM (where oral not feasible / severe) | 1000 μg IM weekly × 4 weeks, then monthly | Per response |

Dietary counselling for ongoing prevention (essential for vegetarian / vegan continuation).

**Vitamin A deficiency (clinical xerophthalmia or post-measles, no corneal involvement):**

WHO age-banded high-dose schedule:

| Age | Dose | Schedule |
|---|---|---|
| < 6 months | 50,000 IU oral | day 0, day 1, day 14 |
| 6–12 months | 100,000 IU oral | day 0, day 1, day 14 |
| > 12 months | 200,000 IU oral | day 0, day 1, day 14 |

Corneal involvement (xerosis, ulceration) → §7.1 to pediatric ophthalmology + IM vitamin A.

**Zinc deficiency (clinical or biochemical):**

| Age | Dose | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| 0–6 months | 10 mg/day elemental zinc | 14 days for diarrhoea-context; 8 weeks for confirmed deficiency |
| > 6 months | 20 mg/day elemental zinc | As above |

**Moderate picky eating / behavioural feeding (12-week structured programme):**
- Dietitian sessions weekly × 4, then biweekly × 4.
- Mealtime observation by dietitian (in-clinic or video-uploaded).
- Parent training: structure (regular timing of meals and snacks), no coercion, no food rewards, food-exposure progression (15+ exposures often needed for acceptance), parent-modelling of food enjoyment, avoid mealtime conflict.
- Weight surveillance every 2 weeks until stable trajectory.
- Re-assess at 12 weeks; if no improvement or weight faltering, route §7.3 to multidisciplinary feeding clinic.

**CMPA elimination + re-introduction:**
- 2–4 week trial of cow's-milk-protein elimination (mother's diet if breastfeeding; eHF if formula-fed; broader animal-protein elimination only if indicated).
- Symptom resolution documented at 4 weeks → structured re-introduction with milk ladder over 4–8 weeks.
- Cross-care with B1-06 Allergy throughout.
- If symptoms recur on re-introduction → continue elimination; reassess at 12 months for tolerance development.

**Breastfeeding-difficulty programme (4-session × 4 weeks):**
- Latch and positioning correction.
- Milk-transfer and supply assessment.
- Pumping support if needed.
- Mastitis prevention; nipple-care for sore nipples.
- Bottle / breast confusion management.
- Galactagogue counselling: domperidone is occasionally used but B1-09 does not initiate without specialist input; herbal (fenugreek, shatavari) acceptable as family-decision.

Dispensary stock for §6.2: ferrous fumarate / ferrous sulfate syrup and tablets; iron polymaltose complex syrup; cholecalciferol 60,000 IU sachets; cyanocobalamin / methylcobalamin oral and IM (at clinics with IM capability); vitamin A capsules (50,000 / 100,000 / 200,000 IU); zinc gluconate / acetate syrup and tablets; eHF formula (one or two named brands stocked); breastfeeding accessories (silicone nipple shields, breast pads, lanolin nipple cream).

### 6.3 Intensive tier

**Severe IDA (Hb < 7.0) → §7.2 / §7.1 routing.** B1-09 does not deliver intensive-tier IDA management.

**Moderate growth faltering not responding at 12 weeks → §7.3 routing to pediatric GI + endocrinology.** B1-09 does not extend nutritional rehabilitation beyond 12 weeks without specialist input.

**Severe vitamin D deficiency with rachitic features or symptomatic hypocalcaemia:**
- 60,000 IU weekly × 8–12 weeks under closer monitoring; calcium supplementation (500–1000 mg/day age-appropriate); recheck calcium, phosphate, ALP, 25-OH-D at 4 weeks.
- §7.3 to pediatric endocrinology / orthopaedics for any bony deformity assessment.

**B12 deficiency with neurological symptoms** → §7.3 routine to pediatric neurology + B1-09 management of replacement.

### 6.4 Contraindications and precautions

| Therapeutic | Contraindication / precaution | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Oral iron | Documented thalassemia major; haemochromatosis | Withhold; route to B1-18 Blood for haematology decision |
| Oral iron in IDA | GI intolerance (nausea, constipation, abdominal pain) | Try iron polymaltose complex or alternate-day dosing; if persists, B1-18 input |
| High-dose vitamin D | Hypercalcaemia; granulomatous disease (sarcoidosis, TB) | Withhold; specialist input |
| High-dose vitamin A | Pregnancy in adolescent | Pregnancy test before any dose > 10,000 IU in adolescent female |
| eHF formula | Anaphylaxis history to milk protein | AAF formula with B1-06 Allergy supervision |
| Galactagogue (domperidone) | B1-09 does not initiate | Specialist (typically lactation consultant or pediatrician) decision |
| Catch-up high-energy feeding | Suspected refeeding syndrome (severe malnutrition) | Catch-up rates managed in inpatient SAM ward, not B1-09 |

### 6.5 Tapering and stopping

- *Iron in IDA:* therapeutic dose until Hb normalises + 3 months; then prophylactic 1 mg/kg/day at A1 cadence.
- *Vitamin D therapeutic course:* 6 weeks at therapeutic dose; then prophylactic per IAP cadence; recheck 25-OH-D annually in groups at risk.
- *B12 therapeutic course:* 4–8 weeks; then dietary management or maintenance dose (especially in vegans).
- *Vitamin A:* fixed schedule (day 0, 1, 14); not tapered.
- *Zinc:* 8-week course; not tapered.
- *eHF formula:* until tolerance demonstrated at 12-month re-challenge or per allergist plan.
- *Breastfeeding-difficulty programme:* time-limited 4 sessions; discharge if breastfeeding established.

## §7 Hard referral triggers [SACRED SECTION]

The case escalates out of B1-09's scope when ANY of the following is true:

### 7.1 Emergency referral (same day, transport directly to inpatient unit)

- [ ] **Severe acute malnutrition** — MUAC < 11.5 cm OR weight-for-height < −3 SD OR bilateral pitting oedema (kwashiorkor pattern). → Named partner SAM ward / pediatric inpatient unit within 4 hours [WHO-SAM-2013]. SKIDS does not deliver outpatient-therapeutic-programme SAM management at Tier 1; this is a hard out-of-scope trigger.
- [ ] **Severe anaemia with haemodynamic compromise** — Hb < 7 g/dL with tachycardia disproportionate to age + capillary refill > 3s; OR Hb < 5 g/dL at any presentation. → Named partner pediatric inpatient unit with transfusion capacity, within 4 hours [WHO-2011-Anaemia].
- [ ] **Vitamin A deficiency with corneal involvement** — corneal xerosis, corneal ulceration, suspected keratomalacia. → Named partner pediatric ophthalmology, within 1 hour, with simultaneous IM high-dose vitamin A administered en route if available.
- [ ] **Newborn with weight loss > 10% birth weight + feeding failure** — overlapping with A1 §7.1 newborn red flags. → Named partner neonatal unit within 4 hours.
- [ ] **Suspected refeeding syndrome** — in any child being fed after a period of starvation, signs of cardiac, respiratory, or neurological deterioration after refeeding initiation. → Named partner pediatric inpatient unit within 1 hour.
- [ ] **Suspected new-onset diabetes mellitus** — POC capillary glucose ≥ 200 mg/dL with weight loss and polyuria / polydipsia, OR signs of DKA. → Named partner pediatric ER (overlap with A1 §7.2 / B2-01 if available).
- [ ] **Suspected child neglect / food insecurity contributing to growth failure** — caregiver disclosure, environmental signs, severely deficient food access at household level. → Named partner pediatric ER + child-protection authority + B1-09 nutrition rehabilitation under ongoing safeguarding supervision.

### 7.2 Urgent referral (within 7 days)

- [ ] **Hb 5–7 g/dL without haemodynamic compromise.** → Named partner pediatric haematology + same-week B1-18 Blood [WHO-2011-Anaemia].
- [ ] **Suspected pediatric malignancy presenting as growth failure with B-symptoms** — unexplained pallor + weight loss + lymphadenopathy + hepatosplenomegaly + bone pain. → Named partner pediatric oncology + same-week B1-18 Blood as bridge.
- [ ] **Suspected primary endocrine cause of growth failure** with biochemistry already supporting (TSH abnormal, growth velocity < −2 SD, bone age severely advanced or delayed, dysmorphic features). → Named partner pediatric endocrinology (or B2-01 Endocrine if available) within 7 days.
- [ ] **Suspected eating disorder** with rapid weight loss, electrolyte abnormality, bradycardia, postural hypotension, or psychiatric crisis features. → Named partner pediatric psychiatry + pediatric medicine, within 7 days.
- [ ] **Suspected coeliac disease with active diarrhoea + weight loss** — anti-tTG IgA positive at high titre + clinical features. → Named partner pediatric gastroenterology + B1-15 GI as bridge, within 7 days.

### 7.3 Routine referral (next available appointment, ≤ 4 weeks)

- [ ] **Refractory growth faltering** — no catch-up after 12 weeks of structured §6.2 with documented adherence and no apparent organic cause identified at B1-09 baseline workup. → Named partner pediatric gastroenterology + pediatric endocrinology, within 4 weeks. Send: anthropometry trajectory, dietary recall, full workup results (CBC, ferritin, 25-OH-D, B12, TSH, urinalysis, stool, coeliac serology).
- [ ] **Pathological short stature workup** — height < −2 SD with growth velocity < −1 SD per year, OR height < −3 SD, OR significant divergence from mid-parental height target. → Named partner pediatric endocrinology (or B2-01), within 4 weeks. Send: growth history, parental heights, bone age, baseline TSH and IGF-1 if available.
- [ ] **Suspected eating disorder (SCOFF positive without crisis features)** — adolescent with restrictive eating, body-image concerns, BMI dropping but stable, no electrolyte abnormality. → Named partner pediatric psychiatry + B1-08 Behavioural + B1-13 Adolescent within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Suspected coeliac disease** without active crisis — anti-tTG IgA elevated. → Named partner pediatric gastroenterology, within 4 weeks. Do not initiate gluten-free diet at B1-09 before confirmatory biopsy.
- [ ] **Suspected inflammatory bowel disease** — chronic GI symptoms + growth failure + family history or other systemic features. → Named partner pediatric gastroenterology + B1-15 GI, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Suspected cystic fibrosis** in any child with chronic respiratory + GI + growth-failure pattern. → Named partner pediatric pulmonology / cystic-fibrosis centre + B1-14 Pulmonology, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Suspected metabolic disease** in any unexplained growth-failure pattern with metabolic decompensation history, organomegaly, or developmental regression. → Named partner pediatric metabolic clinic / clinical genetics, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Refractory CMPA** — symptoms persisting on eHF or recurring on re-introduction across 12 months. → Named partner pediatric allergy / GI + B1-06 / B1-15 within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Severe behavioural-feeding difficulty** — no improvement after 12 weeks structured §6.2, OR sensory / oral-motor difficulty suspected. → Named multidisciplinary feeding clinic (pediatric GI + speech-language / feeding therapy + occupational therapy), within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Vitamin D deficiency with significant bony deformity** — bowing, frontal bossing severe, fractures. → Named partner pediatric orthopaedics + endocrinology, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **B12 deficiency with persistent neurological symptoms despite replacement.** → Named partner pediatric neurology, within 4 weeks.

### 7.4 Handoff communication

When B1-09 fires a §7 referral, the SKIDS pediatrician (with dietitian input) sends the receiving clinician (auto-generated by Companion):

1. **Child identification:** name, DOB, child_id, parent contact, parent consent confirmation, current medications and supplements (specific brands and doses), known allergies.
2. **The trigger:** which §7 sub-trigger fired with the underlying clinical / biochemical finding.
3. **Diagnosis (provisional or confirmed):** ICD-10 where determined, plain-language description.
4. **Anthropometry trajectory:** all available weight, height, MUAC, BMI plotted in chronological order, with Z-scores / percentiles.
5. **Mid-parental height** and target range.
6. **Dietary recall:** 24-hour recall, family-meal pattern, food security context.
7. **Investigations done:** CBC + reticulocyte + smear; ferritin / iron studies; 25-OH-D + Ca / P / ALP; B12 / folate; thyroid panel; urinalysis; stool; coeliac serology; bone age (where ordered).
8. **Therapies tried at B1-09 and outcomes:** specific drug, dose, duration, adherence, response.
9. **Bridge interventions started** (e.g., empirical iron pending haematology review for severe IDA).
10. **Reason for referral, in clinical terms:** one to two sentences from the SKIDS clinician on what they need from the receiving clinician.
11. **Urgency tier and target window:** §7.1 / §7.2 / §7.3 with the time bound.
12. **Named partner / clinic** and appointment booking status.

The packet is auto-generated by Companion; clinician reviews and signs off. For §7.1 emergencies, phone-call confirmation in addition to the packet.

## §8 Follow-up schedule

### 8.1 Follow-up frequency

| Severity tier / condition | First follow-up | Subsequent | Annual review |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mild faltering | 3 months | A1 cadence | Yes |
| Moderate faltering on rehabilitation | 4 weeks | every 2–4 weeks for 12 weeks; then 3-monthly | Yes |
| MUAC 11.5–12.5 cm (Orange) | weekly × 4 | biweekly until MUAC > 13.5 for 4 weeks | Yes |
| Mild IDA on iron | 4 weeks (Hb response) | 12 weeks | — |
| Moderate IDA on iron | 4 weeks | 12 weeks | — |
| Vitamin D deficiency | 12 weeks | annual recheck if at-risk | Yes |
| B12 deficiency | 8 weeks | per maintenance plan | Yes |
| Picky eating Standard programme | weekly × 4, biweekly × 4 | 3 months | Yes |
| Breastfeeding programme | per session schedule | discharged when breastfeeding established | — |
| CF programme | as scheduled | discharged at 12 months | — |
| CMPA elimination + reintroduction | 4 weeks (elimination check) | 4 weeks (reintroduction); 12 months (tolerance retest) | — |

### 8.2 What to recheck at each follow-up

- *Anthropometry:* weight, height, head circumference (< 36m), MUAC (6m–5y), BMI; plot.
- *Growth velocity:* calculated.
- *Adherence to supplementation:* counted-pills / measured-syrup / parent-app-log.
- *Diet recall delta:* what's improved, what hasn't.
- *Specific deficiency labs at the protocol-specified intervals.*
- *Dietitian session feedback.*
- *Family-stress / feeding-environment delta.*
- *For breastfeeding programme:* latch and transfer reassessment; weight on calibrated scale.

### 8.3 Treatment success criteria

- *Mild / Moderate faltering:* sustained catch-up to within ± 1 SD of birth-percentile or genetic target (mid-parental height); growth velocity within ± 1 SD for age.
- *IDA:* Hb normalised + maintained; ferritin > 30 μg/L; haematological indices normalised.
- *Vitamin D deficiency:* 25-OH-D > 30 ng/mL on two consecutive measurements 6 months apart; no rachitic features.
- *Picky eating:* increased food variety per dietitian assessment; mealtime stress reduced; weight tracking.
- *Breastfeeding:* effective transfer documented; weight gain on track; mother satisfied.

### 8.4 Step-down criteria

- Step down from therapeutic to prophylactic dosing once deficiency normalises.
- Discharge from B1-09 active follow-up to A1 surveillance once trajectory stable for 6 months.

### 8.5 Step-up criteria (within Tier 1)

- Worsening Z-score by ≥ 0.5 SD between visits despite adherence → escalate intervention tier; reassess for missed cause.
- IDA Hb not rising by ≥ 1 g/dL at 4-week recheck despite documented adherence → reassess and consider §7.3 to B1-18.

### 8.6 Step-out criteria

Per §7. Specifically: failure to catch up after 12 weeks of §6.2; severe IDA at any presentation; SAM at any presentation; suspected eating disorder; suspected pathological short stature.

### 8.7 Disenrolment / loss-to-follow-up

A child active on B1-09 management who misses two consecutive scheduled follow-ups for moderate-severity rehabilitation is contacted by Manager, then named pediatrician / dietitian. Loss-to-follow-up on active SAM-bridging or active oral iron course is a §12 quarterly-review trigger.

## §10 Parent-facing communication

### 10.1 Scripted explanations

When grading is Mild faltering (no acute concern):

> "[Aanya]'s weight is at the lower end of the normal range for her age. The good news is that her trajectory is holding steady — she's not losing ground, she's growing along her own pattern. Some children are constitutionally smaller, and that's okay if they're healthy and active, which she is. We're going to do two things. First, our dietitian will sit with you for half an hour to look at what she eats in a typical day and suggest some small changes. Second, we'll see her again in three months to plot her growth again. If her trajectory holds, she's just a slower-growing child; if she dips further, we'll do more."

When grading is Moderate faltering (12-week rehabilitation):

> "[Aanya]'s weight has dropped further than I'd like over the last [time period]. We need to do a proper rehabilitation programme — not because she's malnourished in the severe sense, but because we don't want her to drift further. Three things: a structured eating plan that the dietitian builds with you, weekly with her for the first month and biweekly after that. Some specific supplements — iron and vitamin D, very likely, plus we may add others depending on a small blood test. And weekly weight checks here so we can see what's working. The plan is twelve weeks. Most children at this stage catch up well. If she's not catching up by twelve weeks, then we'd send her to a children's GI specialist for a fuller look."

When IDA is diagnosed:

> "[Rohit]'s blood test shows iron deficiency anaemia — his haemoglobin is below where it should be for his age, and his iron stores are low. The good news: it's a fixable problem. We're going to start him on iron syrup, three milligrams per kilogram per day, every morning on an empty stomach with a glass of orange juice — the vitamin C helps absorption. Avoid taking it with milk or tea. His stool will turn dark — that's normal, not bleeding. Some children get a bit constipated; if so, push fluids and fibre. We'll recheck his blood at four weeks; if it's responding, we continue. Total course is about three months at the treatment dose, then a maintenance dose after."

When SAM trigger fires (§7.1):

> "[Aanya]'s muscle and fat measurements are below the level we manage in this clinic. She needs admission to the children's ward at [partner hospital] today for a structured malnutrition treatment programme. They have the right team and the right specialised feeds. I'm calling them now; they're expecting us. The treatment is well-defined and most children recover well; this is the right place for her. We will be in touch with the team, and once she's discharged, the rehabilitation continues here in our clinic."

When suspected eating disorder fires (§7.3):

> "[I'd like to talk to you about something Rohit and I discussed.] Some of [his/her] eating patterns and exercise patterns over the last [time period] suggest something that the children's mental-health and pediatric medicine teams should look at. This isn't something we manage in this clinic alone. I've referred [him/her] to [named pediatric psychiatry partner], and they'll see [him/her] within four weeks. In the meantime, we'll see [him/her] here for weight monitoring and supportive care. Eating concerns at this age are most successfully treated when caught early, which is why we want the specialist input now."

### 10.5 Cultural sensitivities

- *Force-feeding traditions.* Many Indian families practise some degree of force-feeding (pinched-cheek, distraction-with-screens, threats / bargaining) — sometimes with grandparental authority backing the practice. Engage rather than condemn: explain that force-feeding tends to entrench refusal long-term; demonstrate non-coercive alternatives; involve grandparents in the dietitian session where possible.
- *Cow's milk centrality and CMPA hesitancy.* Cow's milk is a near-universal Indian dietary cornerstone; CMPA elimination disrupts this and is met with disbelief or resistance. Frame elimination as time-limited, structured, and reversible; involve B1-06 in the framing if family scepticism is high.
- *Vegetarianism and dietary adequacy.* Vegetarian diets in Indian children can meet ICMR-RDA targets but require attention to iron, B12, zinc, and protein. Avoid the framing "your child needs to eat meat" — that's not the goal; the goal is dietary adequacy within the family's chosen pattern. Demonstrate adequacy through 24-hour recall analysis with the dietitian.
- *Religious and fasting practices.* Adolescent religious fasting (Ramadan, Navratri, etc.) is the family / adolescent's choice; B1-09 supports adequate nutrition during fasting periods rather than challenging the practice. Adjust supplementation timing to non-fasting hours where indicated.
- *Food insecurity disclosure.* Many Indian middle-class families experience episodic food insecurity that they will not disclose unprompted. Ask explicitly and matter-of-factly during §4.1 history. If positive, refer to social-care / public-programme support; do not gate clinical care on payment.
- *Eating-disorder framing.* Indian adolescent eating-disorder presentations are increasingly common but family recognition is low; mothers and grandmothers may interpret restrictive eating as discipline or beauty-management, not pathology. Frame the SCOFF screen for the adolescent in privacy; communicate with the family in pathology terms only with the adolescent's consent unless §7.1 crisis criteria fire.
- *Body-shaming traditions.* "Healthy" and "chubby" are commonly conflated as positive; "thin" is sometimes pathologised even at constitutional thinness. Avoid using these family-loaded terms; refer to anthropometric data plainly.

## §12 Quality and audit

### 12.1 Outcome metrics

B1-09 is working well when:

- ≥ 70% of moderate-faltering rehabilitation cases achieve sustained catch-up (Z-score improvement ≥ 0.5 SD by 12 weeks).
- ≥ 80% of mild / moderate IDA cases achieve Hb response ≥ 1 g/dL by 4-week recheck.
- ≥ 90% of vitamin D deficiency cases normalise 25-OH-D by 12-week recheck.
- ≥ 85% of B12 deficiency cases normalise serum B12 by 8-week recheck.
- 100% of SAM-trigger cases (§7.1) achieve same-day handoff to the named SAM ward.
- ≥ 95% of severe IDA cases (§7.2) attend the pediatric haematology appointment within 7 days.
- ≥ 80% of moderate picky-eating cases show measurable variety improvement by 12-week programme end.
- ≥ 75% of CMPA elimination + reintroduction cases achieve a clear management decision (continue elimination, established tolerance, or routed to specialist) within 6 months of initiation.
- ≥ 70% of A1 vaccination-visit POC Hb screens result in same-day or within-2-week B1-09 visit when below cutoff.
- Loss-to-follow-up rate during active rehabilitation < 10% per quarter.
- Food-insecurity-positive families connected to social-care / public-programme support within 30 days of disclosure ≥ 90%.

### 12.2 Quarterly case review

The protocol owner reviews every quarter:
- A random sample of 30 B1-09 visits, balanced across rehabilitation / deficiency / IYCF / behavioural feeding.
- All §7.1 SAM referrals (timing, handoff packet quality, family communication, post-discharge follow-up).
- All §7.2 severe IDA referrals.
- All cases of refractory rehabilitation (§7.3 to pediatric GI).
- All severity-tier downgrades on Screen 5.
- All food-insecurity disclosures and the social-care follow-up taken.
- All cases of suspected eating disorder.
- All loss-to-follow-up cases on active rehabilitation.

Output: 1-page report to SKIDS clinical operations.

### 12.3 Pediatrician / dietitian certification on this protocol

**The B1-09 SKIDS dietitian (mandatory):**
- Recognised qualification in nutrition / dietetics (M.Sc. Nutrition or equivalent).
- Pediatric nutrition training (formal coursework or supervised pediatric placement).

### 12.4 The SKIDS Fellowship pathway

Pediatricians delivering B1-09 who wish to advance to credentialed practice may apply for:

- **The SKIDS Fellowship in Pediatric Gastroenterology** — credentialed pediatric GI delivers refractory faltering, suspected coeliac / IBD / malabsorption, and the multidisciplinary feeding clinic that B1-09 currently routes to §7.3.
- **The SKIDS Fellowship in Developmental-Behavioural Pediatrics** — DBP-credentialed pediatrician deepens the behavioural-feeding component of B1-09 and the cross-care with B1-08 Behavioural.

Pediatric GI is not currently a B2 protocol in the SKIDS catalogue (which has only Endocrine, Neurology, Rheumatology). The Fellowship may expand B1-09's complex tier into a credentialed in-network pathway as the network grows; see `docs/04-tier-system.md` and `docs/06-the-skids-fellowship.md`.
