# SKIDS Infections Clinic

> The SKIDS Infections Clinic delivers structured evaluation of recurrent infections (frequency thresholds for primary immunodeficiency screening), common pediatric infection management with antimicrobial stewardship, TB screening coordination with NTEP, fever-of-unknown-origin workup, and post-infection follow-up. Suspected PID, severe invasive infections, and tropical infections requiring hospitalisation route per §7.

---

## §1 Service definition

**What this clinic does:**
- Evaluates recurrent infections using the "10 Warning Signs of PID" framework (Jeffrey Modell Foundation): ≥ 4 new ear infections/year, ≥ 2 serious sinus infections/year, ≥ 2 months on antibiotics with little effect, ≥ 2 pneumonias/year, failure to thrive in infant, recurrent deep skin/organ abscesses, persistent thrush or fungal skin infections > 1 year, need for IV antibiotics, ≥ 2 deep-seated infections (meningitis, osteomyelitis, sepsis), family history of PID.
- Manages common pediatric infections within antimicrobial-stewardship principles: viral URTI (no antibiotics), bacterial URTI already covered by B1-03 (pharyngitis) and B1-02 (AOM); B1-19 covers infections not managed by other B1 protocols — skin/soft-tissue infections beyond B1-05 scope, lymphadenitis, periorbital cellulitis (non-orbital), urinary infections beyond B1-17 scope.
- Coordinates TB screening and NTEP referral: Mantoux/TST interpretation, IGRA ordering, chest X-ray interpretation, identification of TB contact history; confirmed TB routes to NTEP DOTS per A1 §7.3 [NTEP-2020].
- Evaluates fever of unknown origin (FUO): documented fever > 38.3°C for > 1 week without diagnosis after initial workup — structured differential (infectious, autoimmune, malignancy, drug fever).
- Manages post-infection syndromes: reactive arthritis, post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis surveillance, post-infectious IBS (cross B1-15).
- Provides antimicrobial-stewardship education: narrow-spectrum first, appropriate duration, no antibiotics for viral illness, antibiogram-guided therapy where culture data available.
- Screens for immunodeficiency in context of recurrent infections: immunoglobulin levels (IgG, IgA, IgM, IgE), lymphocyte subsets, complement (C3, C4, CH50), vaccine-response titres → §7.3 to pediatric immunology if PID confirmed.
- Manages common tropical infections relevant to Indian pediatrics: dengue (clinical management, platelet monitoring, fluid management for dengue with warning signs → §7.1 if severe), malaria (diagnosis, treatment per national protocol, P. falciparum → §7.2), typhoid (culture-confirmed, oral antibiotic).

**What this clinic does not do:**
- Manage severe invasive infections requiring hospitalisation (sepsis, meningitis, osteomyelitis, severe pneumonia → §7.1 ER).
- Manage HIV (→ ART centre per B1-13 §7).
- Manage confirmed primary immunodeficiency beyond initial screening (→ §7.3 pediatric immunology).
- Manage TB treatment (→ NTEP DOTS centre; B1-19 screens and refers).
- Manage severe dengue with shock (→ §7.1 ER).
- Manage cerebral malaria (→ §7.1 ER).

**Age range covered:** 0 – 18 years.

**SKIDS subscription tier(s):**
- **Basic ₹2,999** — A1 infection screening; first B1-19 visit on per-visit pricing.
- **Advanced ₹6,999** — recurrent-infection evaluation; up to 4 visits; FUO workup; antimicrobial-stewardship counselling.
- **Premium ₹12,999** — unlimited visits; PID screening; tropical-infection management; multi-protocol cross-care.

**SKIDS clinical tier:** Tier 1 with scope ceiling at PID and severe invasive infections. §7 to ER (sepsis, severe dengue, cerebral malaria), pediatric immunology (PID), NTEP (TB), ART centre (HIV).

## §4 Diagnostic workflow

### 4.1 Recurrent-infection evaluation
- Quantify infections: number, type, sites, organisms (if known), hospitalisations, antibiotic courses in past 12 months.
- Apply JMF 10 Warning Signs: ≥ 2 present → PID screening indicated.
- **PID screening labs:** FBC with differential (lymphopenia, neutropenia), immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM, IgE), vaccine-response titres (tetanus, pneumococcal), complement (C3, C4, CH50).
- If PID screening abnormal → §7.3 to pediatric immunology.
- If PID screening normal → consider anatomical cause (ENT evaluation for recurrent sinusitis/AOM → B1-02/B1-03 cross), allergy (B1-06 cross), or normal childhood infection frequency in daycare setting.

### 4.2 Antimicrobial-stewardship assessment
- Review antibiotic use in past 12 months: appropriate indications? appropriate duration? appropriate spectrum?
- Counsel: colds do not need antibiotics; green mucus does not mean bacterial infection; completing the course matters.

### 4.3 FUO workup
Documented fever > 38.3°C for > 1 week without diagnosis:
- Phase 1 (initial): FBC, ESR, CRP, blood culture × 2, urinalysis + culture, chest X-ray, Mantoux/IGRA, peripheral smear (malaria), liver function, ANA, LDH.
- Phase 2 (if Phase 1 negative): echocardiography (endocarditis), abdominal ultrasound (abscess, lymphoma), bone-marrow aspiration (§7.3).
- Common causes in Indian children: TB, enteric fever, brucellosis, endocarditis, JIA, lymphoma.

### 4.4 TB screening
- Mantoux (TST): ≥ 10 mm induration in immunocompetent; ≥ 5 mm in immunocompromised or TB contact.
- IGRA (QuantiFERON or T-SPOT) where available — preferred in BCG-vaccinated children.
- Chest X-ray if TST/IGRA positive or clinical suspicion.
- If TB confirmed (clinical + radiological + microbiological) → NTEP DOTS centre per A1 §7.3. B1-19 provides the screening; NTEP provides the treatment.

### 4.5 Dengue management
Per WHO-Dengue-2009:
- **Dengue without warning signs:** home management; daily platelet + haematocrit; oral hydration; paracetamol (NO NSAIDs, NO aspirin); daily SKIDS review during febrile phase.
- **Warning signs** (abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, fluid accumulation, mucosal bleeding, lethargy, hepatomegaly > 2 cm, rising haematocrit with falling platelets): → §7.2 hospital admission for IV fluid management.
- **Severe dengue** (shock, severe bleeding, organ impairment): → §7.1 ER immediately.

### 4.6 Malaria management
- Diagnosis: rapid diagnostic test (RDT) + peripheral smear for species identification.
- P. vivax (uncomplicated): chloroquine 10 mg/kg day 1 + 10 mg/kg day 2 + 5 mg/kg day 3; + primaquine 0.25 mg/kg/day × 14 days for radical cure. G6PD status MUST be confirmed before primaquine dispensing — Companion hard-block: primaquine cannot be prescribed without documented G6PD-normal result in PHR. If G6PD status unknown, order G6PD test and defer primaquine until result available. G6PD-deficient children: do NOT give primaquine; use chloroquine alone for P. vivax [cross B1-18 for G6PD management].
- P. falciparum (uncomplicated): artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT) per NVBDCP — artesunate-SP or artemether-lumefantrine × 3 days; + single-dose primaquine 0.75 mg/kg day 2.
- P. falciparum complicated / cerebral malaria → §7.1 ER.

## §5 Severity grading [SACRED SECTION]

| Severity | Criteria | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Single infection, non-recurrent; dengue without warning signs; malaria uncomplicated vivax | §6.1 — manage + antimicrobial stewardship |
| **Moderate** | Recurrent infections (JMF < 2 warning signs); FUO under investigation; dengue with warning signs | §6.2 — workup + §7.2 if dengue warning |
| **Severe** | JMF ≥ 2 warning signs (PID suspected); FUO > 3 weeks; severe dengue; complicated malaria | §7 |
| **Beyond Tier 1** | Confirmed PID; severe invasive infection; TB confirmed; HIV | §7 |

### Boundary rules
- Normal childhood infection frequency (6–8 URTIs/year in daycare-age children) ≠ recurrent infections. Context matters.
- Dengue warning signs = §7.2 admission. Severe dengue = §7.1 ER.
- TB screening at B1-19; TB treatment at NTEP — never at B1-19.
- PID screening at B1-19; PID management at §7.3 immunology.

## §6 Therapeutic options

### 6.1 Light tier
- Viral URTI: supportive care; NO antibiotics. Counsel family.
- Dengue without warning signs: home monitoring protocol (daily platelet/Hct, hydration, paracetamol, daily clinic review).
- Uncomplicated malaria: appropriate anti-malarial per species.

### 6.2 Standard tier
- Recurrent-infection workup: PID screening labs; anatomical/allergy evaluation.
- FUO Phase 1 workup.
- Lymphadenitis not responding to 2-week oral antibiotic → consider atypical mycobacteria, cat-scratch, fungal; further workup or §7.3.
- Typhoid (culture-confirmed): azithromycin 10–20 mg/kg/day × 7 days OR cefixime 15–20 mg/kg/day × 14 days (based on local sensitivity; fluoroquinolone resistance high in India).

### 6.3 Intensive tier
- FUO Phase 2 → §7.3 for bone marrow, biopsy.
- PID confirmed → §7.3 immunology.

### 6.4 Dispensary stock
- Paracetamol; ORS; RDT kits (dengue NS1 + IgM/IgG; malaria pLDH/HRP2).
- Chloroquine tablets; ACT (artemether-lumefantrine); primaquine.
- Azithromycin; cefixime.
- Mantoux antigen (PPD); IGRA kits (if stocked; otherwise referral).

## §7 Hard referral triggers [SACRED SECTION]

### 7.1 Emergency (same day)
- [ ] **Sepsis / meningitis** (fever + altered consciousness + petechial rash OR neck stiffness) → ER.
- [ ] **Severe dengue** (shock, severe bleeding, organ impairment) → ER.
- [ ] **Cerebral malaria** (P. falciparum + altered consciousness) → ER.
- [ ] **Severe pneumonia** (RR ↑↑, SpO₂ < 92%, chest indrawing) → ER.

### 7.2 Urgent (within 7 days)
- [ ] **Dengue with warning signs** → hospital admission for IV fluid.
- [ ] **P. falciparum malaria with complications** (severe anaemia, jaundice, renal impairment) → hospital.
- [ ] **FUO > 3 weeks without diagnosis** → pediatric infectious disease / haematology.
- [ ] **Periorbital cellulitis with proptosis or limited eye movement** (orbital cellulitis) → ER + ophthalmology.

### 7.3 Routine (within 4 weeks)
- [ ] **PID confirmed on screening** → pediatric immunology.
- [ ] **TB confirmed** → NTEP DOTS centre [NTEP-2020].
- [ ] **Persistent lymphadenopathy (workup inconclusive)** → biopsy via surgery/oncology.
- [ ] **Recurrent deep-seated infections (osteomyelitis, liver abscess) after initial management** → pediatric infectious disease.
- [ ] **Atypical mycobacterial lymphadenitis not responding** → ENT/surgery for excision + ID consultation.

### 7.4 Handoff: infection log (dates, organisms, sites, treatments), PID screening results, Mantoux/IGRA, dengue daily monitoring log, malaria smear results.

## §8 Follow-up schedule

### 8.1 Follow-up frequency

| Condition | First follow-up | Subsequent | Annual review |
|---|---|---|---|
| Viral URTI (managed with stewardship counselling) | As needed (no scheduled follow-up) | — | Via A1 periodic |
| Dengue without warning signs | Daily during febrile phase (platelet + Hct) | Day 7 post-defervescence recheck; day 14 if platelet recovery incomplete | — |
| Dengue post-warning-signs (discharged from hospital) | 48 hours post-discharge | Weekly × 2 weeks; then as needed | — |
| Malaria (uncomplicated) | Day 3 (smear clearance confirmation) | Day 7, day 28 (relapse screen) | — |
| Recurrent-infection evaluation (PID screening) | 2 weeks (results review) | If PID confirmed → §7.3; if normal → 3 months then discharge to A1 | Yes (if recurrence pattern persists) |
| FUO | Weekly until resolved or diagnosed | Phase 2 workup if unresolved at 3 weeks → §7.3 | — |
| Typhoid (culture-confirmed) | Day 7 (clinical response) | Day 14 (end-of-course); repeat blood culture if febrile at day 7 | — |
| TB screening (referred to NTEP) | Post-NTEP referral: 4-week check that NTEP enrolment occurred | Quarterly coordination with NTEP for treatment adherence | Yes |
| Lymphadenitis under workup | 2 weeks (antibiotic response) | 4 weeks if persistent → further workup or §7.3 | — |

### 8.2 What to recheck at each follow-up

- *Dengue:* platelet count trend (graphed), haematocrit trend, warning-sign checklist, hydration status, appetite recovery.
- *Malaria:* peripheral smear (day 3 clearance mandatory for P. falciparum); G6PD status confirmed before primaquine; day 28 smear for P. vivax relapse.
- *Recurrent infections:* infection log update (new infections since last visit), PID screening lab results, anatomical/allergy evaluation status.
- *FUO:* fever curve, Phase 1 lab results, differential narrowing, weight trajectory.
- *Typhoid:* defervescence timeline, appetite, antibiotic adherence.

### 8.3 Treatment success criteria

- *Dengue:* platelet recovery to > 100,000/µL, no warning signs, appetite normalised, afebrile ≥ 48 hours.
- *Malaria:* smear clearance by day 3; no relapse at day 28.
- *Recurrent infections:* PID excluded on screening labs; infection frequency reduced to age-appropriate norms (< 6–8 URTIs/year for daycare-age).
- *FUO:* diagnosis established and treated; or fever resolved spontaneously with no concerning features at 4-week follow-up.
- *Typhoid:* afebrile by day 7; completed antibiotic course; no relapse at 4 weeks.

### 8.4 Step-down and discharge criteria

- Dengue: discharge from B1-19 active follow-up once platelet > 100,000 and afebrile ≥ 72 hours.
- Malaria: discharge after day 28 negative smear. P. vivax: counsel on relapse risk; re-present if febrile.
- Recurrent infections with normal PID screening: discharge to A1 surveillance after 3-month review with no new significant infections.
- FUO resolved: discharge to A1 with documentation of final diagnosis and treatment.

### 8.5 Disenrolment / loss-to-follow-up

A child on active dengue daily monitoring or active malaria treatment who misses a scheduled follow-up is contacted by the clinic Manager immediately (phone). For dengue, same-day phone contact is mandatory. Loss-to-follow-up during active antimicrobial therapy (typhoid, malaria) is a §12 quarterly-review trigger.

## §10 Parent-facing communication

### 10.1 Scripted explanations

When evaluating recurrent infections:

> "Most children who seem to be 'always sick' have a normal immune system — they are simply exposed to many viruses, especially if they attend daycare. Children in group care settings get 6 to 8 colds a year, and that is normal. The tests we're doing check whether the immune system is working properly. In most cases, it is. We'll have the results in about two weeks."

When managing dengue without warning signs:

> "Dengue is a viral illness transmitted by mosquitoes. Most children recover fully in 7 to 10 days. The critical period is when the fever breaks — that's when we watch most carefully for warning signs. [Child] needs to come in every day while the fever lasts so we can check the platelet count and blood concentration. Give paracetamol for fever — no ibuprofen, no aspirin. Push fluids. If [he/she] develops severe stomach pain, persistent vomiting, bleeding from the gums or nose, or becomes very drowsy, come to the clinic immediately — don't wait for tomorrow's appointment."

When prescribing antimicrobial stewardship counselling:

> "Antibiotics work against bacteria, not viruses. Most childhood fevers and colds are caused by viruses. Using antibiotics when they're not needed creates resistant bacteria that are harder to treat later. I know it can feel like we should 'do something,' and we are — we're making sure [child] stays hydrated, comfortable, and monitored. If at any point this turns bacterial, we will start antibiotics immediately."

When §7.1 fires (sepsis / severe dengue / cerebral malaria):

> "I'm very concerned about [child]'s condition. [He/She] needs to be at the hospital right now. I've already called [partner ER] and they are expecting [him/her]. Let's get [him/her] there immediately. I'll stay in touch with the hospital team."

### 10.4 Cultural sensitivities

- *Antibiotic expectation for every fever.* The single biggest antimicrobial-stewardship challenge in Indian outpatient practice. Families expect a prescription; leaving without one feels like an incomplete visit. Counter with: explicit explanation of why antibiotics are withheld, what to watch for, and the specific threshold at which antibiotics would be started. Document the stewardship conversation.
- *Dengue fear and platelet-count anxiety.* Families fixate on the platelet number. Counsel explicitly: platelet count alone does not determine severity; the warning signs (clinical) matter more than the number. A platelet count of 80,000 with no warning signs is safer than 120,000 with persistent vomiting and abdominal pain.
- *Malaria prophylaxis myths.* No vaccine or prophylaxis is needed for endemic-area residents; focus on mosquito-bite prevention (bed nets, repellents, elimination of standing water). Dispel myths about specific foods or drinks preventing malaria.
- *TB stigma.* Families may resist TB screening or NTEP referral due to social stigma. Frame TB as a treatable infection, not a character failing. Emphasise that early detection and DOTS treatment leads to complete cure.
- *Traditional fever remedies.* Sponging with cold water, herbal preparations, religious remedies — engage respectfully; most are not harmful. The risk is delaying medical evaluation for a child with warning signs. Counsel that home remedies can continue alongside medical monitoring.

## §12 Quality and audit

### 12.1 Outcome metrics

B1-19 is working well when:

- Antibiotic-prescribing appropriateness ≥ 85%: no antibiotic prescribed for viral URTI without documented bacterial complication.
- Dengue warning-sign recognition rate = 100%: every child with dengue has daily warning-sign checklist completed; no child with warning signs managed at home.
- TB screening completion rate ≥ 95%: all children with identified TB contacts have Mantoux/IGRA completed and documented.
- PID screening initiated for 100% of children meeting ≥ 2 JMF criteria within 7 days of identification.
- Malaria smear clearance confirmed at day 3 for 100% of P. falciparum cases.
- G6PD status documented before primaquine dispensing = 100% (Companion hard-block enforces this).
- Dengue daily monitoring adherence ≥ 95% (no missed daily visits during febrile phase).
- §7.1 emergency triggers result in same-day handoff in 100% of cases.
- Antimicrobial stewardship override rate < 5%: fewer than 5% of viral URTI visits result in an antibiotic prescription with override.

### 12.2 Quarterly case review

The protocol owner reviews every quarter:
- A random sample of 30 B1-19 visits, balanced across infection types.
- All §7 referrals (every tier).
- All antimicrobial prescriptions — indication, spectrum, duration, culture-guided where applicable.
- All antimicrobial stewardship overrides (antibiotic for viral illness with documented reason).
- All dengue cases — daily monitoring compliance, warning-sign recognition, appropriate §7.2/§7.1 escalation.
- All PID screening cases — JMF criteria met, labs ordered, referral if abnormal.
- All loss-to-follow-up cases on active antimicrobial therapy.

Output: 1-page report to SKIDS clinical operations, with any recommended protocol updates flagged.

### 12.3 Pediatrician certification on this protocol

To deliver B1-19, a SKIDS pediatrician must:
- Complete the B1-19 onboarding chapter (4 hours).
- Pass a structured case-vignette assessment (15 cases across infection types: recurrent infections, dengue, malaria, FUO, TB screening, typhoid).
- Demonstrate dengue warning-sign assessment proficiency (5 simulated cases scored against protocol owner).
- Demonstrate malaria smear interpretation and treatment protocol knowledge.
- Demonstrate antimicrobial-stewardship counselling skills (observed family conversation).
- Maintain a minimum case load of 30 B1-19 visits per quarter.
- Complete annual continuing education (4 hours covering antimicrobial-resistance updates, dengue/malaria national protocol updates, PID screening advances).

### 12.4 The SKIDS Fellowship pathway

Pediatricians delivering B1-19 who wish to advance may apply for The SKIDS Fellowship in pediatric infectious disease, where offered via partner institutions. See `docs/06-the-skids-fellowship.md`. The credentialed infectious-disease specialist would then deliver:
- Complex PID management currently routing to §7.3.
- Hospital-based management of severe infections currently routing to §7.1/§7.2.
- Antimicrobial stewardship programme governance across the SKIDS network.
- Protocol governance for B1-19.
