# SKIDS Sleep Clinic

> The SKIDS Sleep Clinic manages behavioural insomnia of childhood,
> sleep hygiene problems, mild-to-moderate sleep-onset insomnia,
> nightmares, and screening for obstructive sleep apnoea. Confirmed
> moderate-severe OSA, narcolepsy suspicion, parasomnia complex
> patterns, and refractory insomnia route per §7. Melatonin is used
> sparingly per IAP cautious-use framing.

---

## §1 Service definition

**What this clinic does:**
- Manages behavioural insomnia of childhood (BIC — sleep-onset association type and limit-setting type) with structured sleep-hygiene counselling and parent-training programmes (joint with B1-08 Behavioural for moderate-severe family-system components) [AAP-Sleep-2016].
- Manages adolescent sleep-onset insomnia and delayed sleep-phase patterns with sleep hygiene, light-exposure counselling, and time-limited melatonin where indicated.
- Screens for obstructive sleep apnoea using the Pediatric Sleep Questionnaire (PSQ) and overnight pulse oximetry (loaner device); routes confirmed OSA to §7.3 to ENT or pediatric sleep medicine.
- Manages nightmares, sleep terrors, sleep-walking (mild patterns) with parental education and trigger-management; complex parasomnia routes §7.3.
- Manages restless legs syndrome (mild) with iron-status check (joint with B1-09) and sleep-hygiene; severe cases route §7.3.
- Cross-cares with B1-09 Nutrition (iron status, food before sleep), B1-08 Behavioural (sleep-anxiety overlap), B1-02 Hearing & Ear (recurrent OME with sleep impact), B1-03 Throat & Airway (adenotonsillar hypertrophy with snoring → ENT routing).
- Issues structured sleep diaries via parent app and reviews them in clinic visits.

**What this clinic does not do:**
- Confirm OSA diagnosis with polysomnography (PSG) — PSG routes §7.3 to a pediatric sleep laboratory partner.
- Manage moderate-severe OSA, post-PSG decisions on adenotonsillectomy, CPAP / BiPAP initiation — §7 routing.
- Initiate non-melatonin sleep pharmacotherapy (zolpidem, clonidine for sleep, antihistamine sedatives chronically) — §7.3 to specialist.
- Manage narcolepsy / cataplexy / hypersomnolence syndromes — §7.3 to pediatric sleep medicine / neurology.
- Manage complex parasomnia (frequent confusional arousals, REM-behaviour disorder suspicion) — §7.3.
- Manage circadian rhythm disorders beyond simple delayed sleep-phase — §7.3.

**Age range covered:** 4 months – 18 years.
- 4 months – 24 months: settling and night-waking patterns; sleep-association BIC; safe-sleep counselling.
- 2 – 5 years: limit-setting BIC; transition out of co-sleeping (where family chooses); preschool-age nightmares.
- 5 – 12 years: school-age sleep hygiene; OSA screening with PSQ; sleep terrors and sleep-walking.
- 12 – 18 years: adolescent delayed sleep-phase; screen-time and sleep onset; insomnia.

**SKIDS subscription tier(s):**
- **Basic ₹2,999** — A1 routing supported. First B1-07 visit on per-visit pricing.
- **Advanced ₹6,999** — up to 4 B1-07 visits per year included; oximetry loaner included; structured behavioural-sleep programme.
- **Premium ₹12,999** — unlimited B1-07 visits within the clinically indicated set; priority §7 partner sleep-laboratory referral.

**SKIDS clinical tier:** Tier 1, with allied-staff dependency on the SKIDS sleep-trained nurse / counsellor (often the same staff person as the B1-08 counsellor) for behavioural-sleep programme delivery. Without that staff, B1-07 degrades to medical-screening-and-routing only. Protocol governance with a named pediatric sleep specialist or DBP with sleep training. Complex tier per §7 escalates to pediatric sleep medicine, ENT (B1-03 cross-care for adenotonsillar OSA), or pediatric pulmonology / neurology.

## §2 Evidence base

This protocol governs primary-care pediatric sleep management at SKIDS clinics. It follows the most current recommendations from:

- **[AAP-Sleep-2016]** — Paruthi S, Brooks LJ, D'Ambrosio C, et al. Recommended Amount of Sleep for Pediatric Populations: A Consensus Statement of the American Academy of Sleep Medicine. *J Clin Sleep Med.* 2016;12(6):785–786. Governing source for age-banded sleep duration targets.
- **[AAP-2012-OSA]** — Already referenced from A1 §4. Marcus CL, Brooks LJ, Draper KA, et al. Diagnosis and management of childhood obstructive sleep apnea syndrome. *Pediatrics.* 2012;130(3):576–584. Governing source for OSA screening and routing.
- **[AASM-OSA-2012]** — American Academy of Sleep Medicine. Practice parameters for the respiratory indications for polysomnography in children, 2012. Used alongside AAP-2012-OSA.
- **[Owens-2005-BEARS]** — Owens JA, Dalzell V. Use of the BEARS sleep screening tool. Already in citations.bib via A1.
- **[IAP-Sleep-2020]** — Indian Academy of Pediatrics, Adolescent Health Academy + Pediatric Sleep Medicine Chapter. Position statement on pediatric sleep in the Indian context, 2020 (with subsequent updates). [IAP citation AI-verified 2026-05-01; exact title requires IAP website confirmation]
- **[AAP-Melatonin-2022]** — American Academy of Pediatrics. Health Advisory on Melatonin Use in Children, 2022. Used for the cautious-use framing in §6.
- **[Mindell-Sleep-2017]** — Mindell JA, Williamson AA. Benefits of a bedtime routine in young children. *Sleep Med Rev.* 2018;40:93–108. Cited for behavioural-sleep evidence.
- **[NELSON-21:CH31]** — Nelson Textbook of Pediatrics, 21st edition, sleep chapter.
- **[IAP-TB-6E]** — IAP Textbook of Pediatrics, 6th edition.
- **[AAP-BF-4E]** — Bright Futures, 4th edition. Cited for periodicity of sleep counselling at A1.

**Departures from cited guidelines:**

1. *Melatonin cautious-use framing.* CLAUDE.md §7.2 explicitly notes melatonin for sleep is to be used cautiously. AAP-2022 advisory raised concerns about pediatric melatonin use (over-the-counter availability with variable dosing accuracy in non-pharmaceutical-grade products; lack of long-term data). B1-07 follows IAP cautious-use position: melatonin not first-line; reserved for documented sleep-onset insomnia in adolescents > 12 years OR for delayed-sleep-phase pattern OR for ASD-related sleep-onset insomnia (joint with B1-08); short-term courses (≤ 12 weeks); pharmaceutical-grade preparations only [AAP-Melatonin-2022; TO BE DECIDED BY SKIDS PEDIATRICIAN].

2. *Overnight pulse oximetry as OSA screening.* AAP-2012-OSA recommends PSG as confirmatory; oximetry has lower sensitivity for OSA but higher accessibility. B1-07 uses oximetry as a screening adjunct to PSQ (high PSQ + abnormal oximetry → §7.3 PSG); does not use oximetry as a confirmatory test. Where PSQ is high but oximetry is normal, the clinical picture drives the §7.3 decision [AAP-2012-OSA].

3. *Behavioural-sleep first-line for all behavioural insomnia.* No pharmacological alternative is offered at Tier 1 except in specific narrow indications. This is conservative and aligns with all major guidelines.

4. *In-clinic delivery of behavioural-sleep programme.* Behavioural sleep change requires family-system intervention; B1-07 delivers this through the SKIDS sleep-trained counsellor and the parent-app behavioural-sleep modules. Some Indian centres rely on take-home guidance only. SKIDS commits to structured in-clinic delivery to improve adherence.

No other material departures.

## §3 Presenting concerns

A child arrives at SKIDS B1-07 Sleep via one of these paths:

| Parent language | Clinical translation | Routing |
|---|---|---|
| "She wakes up multiple times at night needing me to settle her." | Sleep-association behavioural insomnia. | §4 → §6 behavioural programme |
| "He takes hours to fall asleep at bedtime." | Sleep-onset insomnia or limit-setting BIC. | §4 → §6 |
| "She refuses to go to bed without a fight." | Limit-setting BIC. | §4 → §6 parent-training |
| "He snores loudly every night." | Possible OSA. | §4 → PSQ + oximetry |
| "She gasps or stops breathing in sleep." | OSA red flag. | §4 → §7.2 / §7.3 |
| "He has nightmares many nights a week." | Nightmare disorder. | §4 → trigger review + sleep hygiene |
| "She sleepwalks / talks in her sleep." | Parasomnia (NREM). | §4 → safety counselling + observation |
| "He wakes up screaming and inconsolable." | Sleep terror. | §4 → reassurance + observation; complex → §7.3 |
| "She can't stay awake in school." | Excessive daytime sleepiness — assess sleep duration, OSA, narcolepsy suspicion. | §4 → if not duration-related, §7.3 |
| "He's a teenager who falls asleep at 3am and can't wake for school." | Adolescent delayed sleep-phase. | §4 → §6 + light therapy + cautious-use melatonin |
| "She has restless legs / kicks all night." | RLS suspicion. | §4 → ferritin (joint with B1-09) + §6 |
| "He sleeps too much, all the time." | Hypersomnolence — assess depression (B1-08 cross), narcolepsy. | §4 → §7.3 |
| "Bed-wetting affecting sleep." | Cross with B1-20 Bedwetting. | §4 + B1-20 |
| "Night cough / wheeze waking him up." | Possible asthma trigger; cross with B1-14 (when active). | §4 + cross-care |

**Routing decisions:**
- A1 Orange sleep flag (BEARS positive ≥ 2 domains, parent-reported significant sleep concern, snoring + daytime symptoms) → B1-07 within 4 weeks.
- A1 Red — witnessed apnoea + cyanosis or cardiopulmonary compromise → §7.1 directly.
- B1-07 cross-cares with: B1-08 (behavioural overlap; school refusal), B1-09 (iron status for RLS; bedtime nutrition), B1-02 (recurrent OME), B1-03 (adenotonsillar hypertrophy → ENT routing), B1-14 (asthma night-symptoms), B1-20 (bedwetting overlap), B1-13 (adolescent sleep + psychosocial overlap).

**Coming from SKIDS Screening (A1):**
- A1 §3 parent-presenting sleep concerns.
- A1 §4.3 BEARS positive ≥ 2 domains.
- A1 §4.3 PSQ score > 0.33 with overnight oximetry desat < 90% > 5% → §7.2 (per A1 §7.2 trigger list).

## §4 Diagnostic workflow

### 4.1 Structured history

Ask, in this order:

1. *Sleep patterns.* Bedtime; sleep latency (time to fall asleep); number of awakenings; duration of awakenings; what is needed for the child to settle (parent presence, feeding, rocking, screen, etc.); morning wake time; total sleep duration (24h); naps (timing, duration); weekend / school-day variation.
2. *Sleep environment.* Room shared / individual; co-sleeping pattern (with which family member; whether by family choice or by child's demand); light, noise, temperature; screen presence in bedroom.
3. *Bedtime routine.* Existence of routine; consistency; duration; activities (reading, song, prayer, screen).
4. *Snoring and breathing pattern in sleep.* Frequency (nights/week); volume; observed apnoea; gasping; mouth-breathing; sweating; restless sleep; unusual sleep positions; choking.
5. *Daytime symptoms.* Sleepiness; school attention / behaviour; mood; growth (cross with B1-09); morning headaches; appetite.
6. *Parasomnia history.* Nightmares (frequency; content recall — nightmares are remembered; sleep terrors are not); sleep-walking; sleep-talking; teeth-grinding; bed-wetting.
7. *Restless legs symptoms.* Discomfort relieved by movement; bedtime worsening; family history of RLS.
8. *Mood / anxiety / attention.* Brief screen — many sleep concerns overlap with B1-08 issues.
9. *Caffeine and screen exposure.* Caffeinated beverages (chai, coffee, energy drinks in adolescents); screen time and timing (within 1 hour of bedtime is the key risk).
10. *Family history.* Sleep disorders (OSA, RLS, narcolepsy); psychiatric conditions.
11. *Medical history.* Atopic disease, asthma, allergic rhinitis (sleep-disrupting), recurrent OME, recurrent tonsillitis (cross with B1-02 / B1-03); seizures (any nocturnal events); chronic pain; ADHD (cross with B1-08).
12. *Prior interventions.* Sleep training attempts (which method, duration, response); medications (any prior melatonin, antihistamines for sleep, herbal preparations); traditional remedies.

**Red flags to elicit at history:**
- Witnessed apnoea + cyanosis or behavioural change.
- Cataplectic episodes (sudden loss of muscle tone with emotion, suggesting narcolepsy).
- Excessive daytime sleepiness despite adequate sleep duration.
- Nocturnal seizure-like activity.
- Sudden-onset severe sleep change with mood change (B1-08 cross-care).
- Acting out dreams (REM behaviour disorder; rare in pediatric).
- Cardiac symptoms during sleep (palpitations, chest pain).

### 4.2 Examination

Examine for:

- *Growth and BMI.* Cross-tier overlap — obesity and OSA are linked; failure-to-thrive can occur with severe OSA.
- *Tonsillar size (Brodsky / Friedman scale 1–4).* Grade 3+ in a snoring child raises adenotonsillar OSA suspicion.
- *Adenoidal facies.* Mouth-breathing, long face, dental crowding, nasal obstruction history.
- *Allergic-rhinitis stigmata.* Cross with B1-06.
- *Cardiac.* Heart rate, BP, murmur (cor pulmonale rare but possible in severe long-standing OSA).
- *Neurological screen.* Tone, gait, alertness, eye-movement abnormalities (subtle indicators of neurological causes of sleep complaints).
- *Skin.* Atopic eczema (cross with B1-05; itch-disrupted sleep is common).
- *Iron deficiency stigmata.* Pallor; cross with B1-09 (RLS-iron link).
- *Obesity examination.* Cross with B1-10 if BMI ≥ 85th centile.

### 4.3 Scales and questionnaires

| Scale | Domain | Age | Cutoff |
|---|---|---|---|
| **BEARS** | Sleep screening (broad) | 2–18 | Per A1 §4.3; positive in any domain triggers detailed history [Owens-2005-BEARS] |
| **Pediatric Sleep Questionnaire (PSQ) — sleep-related-breathing-disorder subscale** | OSA screening | 2–18 | Score > 0.33 (mean of 22 yes/no items rated 1) raises OSA suspicion → oximetry + §7 routing as appropriate [AAP-2012-OSA] |
| **Sleep diary** | Pattern documentation | any | 14-day diary at first visit baseline; supports behavioural-sleep formulation |
| **CSHQ (Children's Sleep Habits Questionnaire)** | Comprehensive sleep behaviour | 4–10 | Total > 41 = clinical concern |
| **ESS (Epworth Sleepiness Scale, modified pediatric)** | Daytime sleepiness | adolescent | ≥ 10 = excessive daytime sleepiness — investigate cause |
| **Adolescent Sleep Hygiene Scale** | Sleep hygiene assessment | adolescent | Per scale; identifies specific behaviours for change |

### 4.4 In-clinic device tests

Required:
- *Overnight pulse oximetry loaner device.* Pediatric finger / toe probe; 8-hour recording capability. Family takes home for 2 nights; returns; data analysed for desaturation events (≥ 4% drops, duration, frequency, mean SpO₂).
- *BP cuff with paediatric sizes.*
- *Otoscope.* For OME screening.

Optional:
- *Spirometry / peak flow* if asthma-trigger sleep disturbance suspected.

### 4.5 Laboratory and imaging

Order on indication:
- *Ferritin* — for restless-legs symptoms or periodic limb movements; threshold for replacement is ferritin < 50 ng/mL in pediatric RLS (lower than the IDA threshold given the iron-and-CNS-restless-legs link).
- *FBC* — if anaemia signs.
- *TSH* — for hypersomnolence or daytime fatigue without clear sleep cause.
- *Lateral neck X-ray (adenoidal-thickness)* — historically used; B1-07 does not order routinely; the clinical picture + PSQ + oximetry + ENT examination drives the §7.3 decision.

Do not order routinely:
- Polysomnography at B1-07 — routes §7.3 to sleep laboratory partner.
- Routine CBC / metabolic panel for sleep complaints.
- Brain imaging — only if neurological signs.

### 4.6 Decision tree

```
Sleep presentation
        │
        ▼
History (§4.1) + examination (§4.2) + scales (§4.3) + sleep diary
        │
        ├── Acute red flag (witnessed apnoea + compromise)

        │
```

## §5 Severity grading [SACRED SECTION]

### 5.1 Behavioural insomnia of childhood (BIC)

| Severity | Findings | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Frequent night-waking but family functional; mild daytime impact | §6.1 — sleep-hygiene counselling, parent-app modules, 4-week recheck |
| **Moderate** | Significant family-sleep deprivation; child daytime functional impact | §6.2 — structured 4–6 week behavioural-sleep programme with SKIDS counsellor |
| **Severe** | Family-system distress; comorbid behavioural problems; failure of multiple prior interventions | §6.2 + cross-care with B1-08; if no response in 6 weeks → §7.3 |

### 5.2 Sleep-onset insomnia (school-age + adolescent)

| Severity | Findings | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Sleep latency 30–60 min, occasional, no daytime impact | §6.1 — sleep hygiene, screen-time counselling |
| **Moderate** | Sleep latency > 60 min ≥ 3 nights/week, daytime impact | §6.2 — structured sleep-hygiene programme; cognitive techniques in adolescents; melatonin only if §6 criteria met |
| **Severe / refractory** | Sustained insomnia despite 6 weeks structured §6.2 | §7.3 to pediatric sleep medicine; reassess for psychiatric comorbidity (B1-08) |

### 5.3 OSA suspicion

| Severity | Findings | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Low risk** | Snoring < 3 nights/week, no daytime symptoms, PSQ < 0.33, normal oximetry | §6.1 surveillance |
| **Moderate suspicion** | Snoring ≥ 3 nights/week + PSQ > 0.33 + normal / borderline oximetry; ± tonsillar grade 2 | §7.3 ENT (B1-03 cross-care) for adenotonsillar evaluation; PSG if ENT recommends |
| **High suspicion** | PSQ > 0.33 + oximetry desaturation < 90% > 5% recording time OR clinical features of severe OSA (witnessed apnoea, growth failure, daytime cor-pulmonale features) | §7.2 to pediatric sleep medicine + ENT, within 7 days for PSG and adenotonsillectomy decision |
| **Witnessed apnoea + compromise** | Cyanosis, behavioural change, growth failure | §7.1 emergency |

### 5.4 Parasomnia

| Type | Severity | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Nightmares (mild)** | < 1/week, no avoidance behaviour | §6.1 reassure |
| **Nightmares (moderate-severe)** | Frequent + daytime sleep-anxiety + bedtime resistance | §6.2 + B1-08 cross-care |
| **Sleep terrors / sleep-walking (mild)** | Infrequent, no injury risk | §6.1 safety counselling + observation; usually resolves |
| **Sleep terrors / sleep-walking (frequent)** | Multiple nights/week, sustained for > 3 months, age > 12 | §7.3 |
| **Suspected complex parasomnia or REM behaviour disorder** | — | §7.3 |

### 5.5 Restless legs syndrome

| Severity | Findings | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Symptoms ≤ 2 nights/week, mild | §6.1 — ferritin check; sleep hygiene; iron supplementation if ferritin < 50 ng/mL (joint with B1-09) |
| **Moderate** | ≥ 3 nights/week, sleep impact | §6.2 — iron supplementation (joint with B1-09) + sleep hygiene; reassess at 12 weeks |
| **Severe / refractory** | No improvement with iron + sleep hygiene | §7.3 to pediatric neurology / sleep medicine |

### 5.6 Adolescent delayed sleep-phase

| Severity | Findings | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Sleep-onset 12–1 am; weekday sleep deficit < 2h | §6.1 — sleep hygiene; light exposure morning; restrict evening screen |
| **Moderate** | Sleep-onset 1–3 am; weekday sleep deficit > 2h; school refusal pattern | §6.2 — chronotherapy (graduated bedtime advance); morning light therapy; cautious-use melatonin per §6.4 |
| **Severe / refractory** | Persistent despite §6.2 at 6 weeks; significant school / mood impact | §7.3 to pediatric sleep medicine + B1-08 |

### 5.7 Boundary rules

- *Borderline severity:* uplift to higher tier for first treatment cycle; step down at follow-up.
- *Re-grading:* re-administer sleep diary + scales at every visit.
- *Family-system uplift:* family-stress dimension warrants Standard tier even with Mild scales.
- *PSQ + oximetry combined:* high PSQ with normal oximetry is still moderate-suspicion OSA — clinical drives the §7 decision; oximetry-normal does not exclude OSA.
- *Iron-RLS link:* a child with RLS pattern has ferritin ordered even without anaemia — RLS-relevant ferritin threshold is < 50 ng/mL.

## §6 Therapeutic options

### 6.1 Light tier (Mild presentations across pathways)

**Universal sleep hygiene counselling:**

| Age | Sleep duration target |
|---|---|
| 4–12 months | 12–16 hours total (including naps) |
| 1–2 years | 11–14 hours |
| 3–5 years | 10–13 hours |
| 6–12 years | 9–12 hours |
| 13–18 years | 8–10 hours [AAP-Sleep-2016] |

Counsel:
- Consistent bedtime and wake-time (within 1 hour across weekdays and weekends).
- Bedtime routine (predictable, calming, 20–45 min).
- Bedroom environment: dark, quiet, cool, no screen, child sleeps in their own sleep space (whatever the family chooses for that — own room, own bed in shared room, co-sleeping with safe-sleep practices for infants).
- No screens in bedroom; no screen 1 hour before bed.
- Limit caffeine after midday in school-age and adolescents; avoid caffeine altogether in young children.
- Daytime activity ≥ 1 hour outdoor where feasible.
- Exposure to morning daylight to anchor circadian rhythm.

**Mild BIC, mild sleep-onset insomnia, mild parasomnia:**
- Single counselling session at SKIDS counsellor or pediatrician.
- Parent-app: "Sleep Foundations" 14-day programme (age-banded).
- 4-week recheck with sleep-diary review.

**Mild RLS:**
- Ferritin check (joint with B1-09); iron supplementation if ferritin < 50 ng/mL.
- Sleep hygiene; warm bath before bed; gentle stretching.

**Mild OSA suspicion (low risk):**
- Surveillance; recheck at 3 months with PSQ; if symptoms progress, escalate.

Dispensary stock: parent-app QR codes for sleep content; printed sleep-diary templates; oximetry-loaner devices (with cleaning protocol).

### 6.2 Standard tier (Moderate presentations — structured behavioural programmes)

**Moderate BIC — structured 4–6 week behavioural-sleep programme:**

For sleep-association BIC (most common 4m–24m):
- Family chooses one of three approaches and commits:
  1. *Graduated extinction (modified):* parent checks at increasing intervals (3, 5, 10, 15 minutes) without picking up; reassures verbally; leaves. Progress over 3–7 nights typically.
  2. *Bedtime-fading:* delay bedtime to current actual sleep-onset; build sleep pressure; once sleep falls within 15 minutes, advance bedtime by 15 min every 2 nights to target.
  3. *Camping out (parent-presence-fading):* parent stays in room without engagement; gradually moves further from cot over nights; eventually parent leaves before sleep onset.
- Counsel that all evidence-based methods involve some short-term distress; the trade-off for long-term sleep integrity is family choice.
- SKIDS counsellor delivers 4–6 sessions weekly through the programme; parent-app daily check-in.

For limit-setting BIC (toddler / preschool):
- Bedtime pass system (one or two passes per night for legitimate needs; no engagement otherwise).
- Visual schedule (bedtime routine illustrated for child).
- Consistent firm bedtime + reward chart for staying-in-bed.

**Moderate sleep-onset insomnia (school-age + adolescent):**
- Structured sleep-hygiene programme.
- Stimulus-control techniques (bed = sleep only; if not asleep in 20 min, get out of bed, do a calm activity, return when sleepy).
- Cognitive techniques in adolescents (reframing pre-sleep worries; thought-stopping).
- 4–6 sessions; sleep-diary daily.

**Moderate adolescent delayed sleep-phase:**
- Chronotherapy: bedtime advance 15 min every 2–3 nights toward target; weekend consistency required.
- Morning light therapy: 30 min bright light within 30 min of waking; outdoor light or 10,000-lux light box.
- Evening blue-light restriction: warm-tone settings on screens; no screen 1 hour before target bedtime.
- Cautious-use melatonin per §6.4.

**Moderate parasomnia (nightmares):**
- Trigger review (recent stress, scary content exposure, eating before bed).
- Imagery rehearsal therapy (rewrite the nightmare during the day; rehearse the new ending; older children).
- Reassurance + B1-08 cross-care if anxiety component dominates.

**Moderate RLS:**
- Iron supplementation if ferritin < 50 ng/mL (3 mg/kg/day elemental iron PO; 12-week course; recheck ferritin and clinical response at 12 weeks; joint with B1-09).
- Sleep hygiene + RLS-specific bedtime rituals (warm bath, calf stretches).
- Avoid RLS-aggravating medications (some antihistamines, antidopaminergic).

### 6.3 Intensive tier

Standard + close-interval review; if no response at 6 weeks → §7.3.

### 6.4 Melatonin — cautious-use criteria

Melatonin is NOT first-line. Use only when ALL of the following are met:

- Age ≥ 6 years (and ideally ≥ 12 for non-ASD indications).
- Indication is sleep-onset insomnia OR delayed sleep-phase OR ASD-related sleep-onset insomnia (joint with B1-08).
- Failed 4–6 weeks of optimised behavioural / sleep-hygiene approach.
- Pharmaceutical-grade preparation available (not over-the-counter variable-content products).
- Family counselling on time-limited use, expected response, side-effect profile.

| Indication | Dose | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Adolescent (≥ 12y) sleep-onset insomnia OR delayed sleep-phase | 0.5–3 mg PO 30–60 min before target bedtime; start low (0.5 mg) | 4–12 weeks; reassess; do not extend without specialist input |
| ASD-related sleep-onset insomnia (joint with B1-08) | 0.5–6 mg PO 30 min before bedtime; titrate as tolerated | 12 weeks; specialist input if continued use |
| Pediatric < 6y | NOT used at B1-07 | Specialist input only |

Counsel: melatonin may cause vivid dreams, headache, daytime sleepiness, mood changes. Stop if no response in 4 weeks. Reassess at every follow-up. Long-term safety data in children is limited [AAP-Melatonin-2022].

### 6.5 Sleep medications NOT used at B1-07

- Antihistamines (diphenhydramine, hydroxyzine) for chronic sleep — no.
- Clonidine for sleep — specialist only.
- Zolpidem / Z-drugs — not used in pediatrics outside specialist.
- Benzodiazepines — not used.
- Trazodone — specialist only.

Any of these route §7.3 if specialist consideration warranted.

### 6.6 Contraindications

| Therapeutic | Contraindication / precaution | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Melatonin | Age < 6y at B1-07; pregnancy; immunosuppression | Specialist input |
| Iron for RLS | Documented thalassemia major; haemochromatosis | Withhold; B1-18 input |
| Behavioural programmes | Family system unable to implement (severe parental discord, mental-health crisis) | Address family system first; B1-08 referral |

### 6.7 Tapering and stopping

- Behavioural programmes: structured course completion; maintenance via parent-app reminders.
- Melatonin: do not extend beyond 12 weeks at B1-07 without specialist review; taper if continued (gradual reduction over 1–2 weeks).
- Iron for RLS: continue until ferritin > 50 ng/mL sustained.

## §7 Hard referral triggers [SACRED SECTION]

### 7.1 Emergency referral

- [ ] **Witnessed apnoea with cyanosis or behavioural change** (severe OSA features) at presentation. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER + sleep medicine, within 1 hour.
- [ ] **Suspected nocturnal seizure activity** — abnormal nocturnal movements suggestive of seizure. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER + neurology, within 1 hour.

### 7.2 Urgent referral (within 7 days)

- [ ] **High-suspicion OSA** — PSQ > 0.33 + oximetry desat < 90% > 5% of recording OR severe clinical features. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric sleep medicine + ENT for PSG + adenotonsillectomy decision, within 7 days [AAP-2012-OSA].
- [ ] **Suspected narcolepsy / cataplexy** — excessive daytime sleepiness + cataplectic episodes. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric sleep medicine, within 7 days.

### 7.3 Routine referral (within 4 weeks)

- [ ] **Refractory insomnia** despite 6 weeks structured §6.2 with adherence. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric sleep medicine, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Moderate-suspicion OSA** for PSG / ENT evaluation. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric sleep medicine OR ENT, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Complex parasomnia** — frequent sleep terrors, sleep-walking with injury risk, REM-behaviour disorder suspicion, age > 12 with persistent NREM parasomnia. → Pediatric sleep medicine, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Refractory RLS** — no response to iron + sleep hygiene at 12 weeks. → Pediatric neurology / sleep medicine, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Refractory delayed sleep-phase** — no response to §6.2 at 6 weeks. → Pediatric sleep medicine + B1-08, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Hypersomnolence not explained by sleep duration** — depression-screen-negative, OSA-screen-negative excessive sleepiness. → Pediatric sleep medicine, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Suspected circadian rhythm disorder beyond simple delayed phase** (non-24-hour, advanced phase, irregular). → Pediatric sleep medicine, within 4 weeks.

### 7.4 Handoff communication

Standard packet: child ID, trigger, sleep-diary trajectory, scale scores, oximetry data attached, prior interventions, reason, urgency tier, SKIDS-T3.

## §10 Parent-facing communication

### 10.1 Scripted explanations

Mild BIC:

> "Your baby's night-waking pattern is what we'd call a sleep-association problem — they've learnt to fall asleep with [feeding / rocking / parent-presence], and they look for the same when they briefly wake at night. This is very common and treatable. We'll set up a structured plan you choose between three options — graduated waiting, bedtime fading, or parent-presence-fading. The first three to seven nights are usually the hardest. Most families see real change in two weeks. Our counsellor is on the journey with you weekly."

Moderate snoring with PSQ-positive:

> "[Rohit]'s snoring pattern and the questionnaire suggest his airway may be partially blocked at night — usually from enlarged tonsils or adenoids. This is something a children's ENT will look at. I'm sending him for that evaluation; the ENT will decide whether he needs adenotonsillectomy. While we wait, we'll do an overnight oxygen recording at home — a simple finger sensor for two nights — to see how much desaturation he's having. If the oxygen drops are significant, we expedite the ENT visit."

Adolescent delayed sleep-phase + cautious-use melatonin:

> "[Aanya]'s body clock has shifted late — common in teenagers. We're going to do three things. Morning light: 30 minutes within half an hour of waking, ideally outdoors. Evening: no screens within 60 minutes of target bedtime, dim lights, warm-tone settings. And a small dose of melatonin — 0.5 mg about an hour before target bedtime — for a few weeks while the schedule shifts. Melatonin isn't a sleeping pill; it's a body-clock signal. We'll review at 4 weeks; if it's working, we continue for another 4–8 weeks. If not, we stop and reassess."

Witnessed apnoea (§7.1):

> "What you've described — [Rohit] stopping breathing during sleep — needs urgent specialist review. I'm sending him to [partner ER and sleep medicine]. Take this letter; they'll see him today."

### 10.5 Cultural sensitivities

- *Co-sleeping.* Indian co-sleeping is common, multi-generational, and not pathological. Counsel safe-sleep within whatever sleep arrangement the family chooses.
- *"Sleep-training is harsh."* Frame the choice between methods; respect family preference; emphasise that all evidence-based methods cause some short-term distress.
- *Joint family bedtime.* Multiple-caregiver bedtime routines need consistency across caregivers — engage the actual bedtime caregiver.
- *Adolescent autonomy on sleep.* Adolescent buy-in is necessary; engage adolescent privately on sleep goals.
- *Religious / cultural bedtime practices.* Prayer-before-bed, family rituals — engage these as part of the routine, not against them.
