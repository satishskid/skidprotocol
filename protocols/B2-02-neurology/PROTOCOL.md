# SKIDS Neurology Clinic

> The SKIDS Neurology Clinic is a Tier 2 service requiring a
> Fellowship-credentialed pediatric neurologist. It manages
> epilepsy, headache disorders, neurodevelopmental regression
> workup, movement disorders, neuromuscular disease, and
> complex neurological presentations referred from B1-23
> Developmental Surveillance, B1-08 Behavioural (tic disorders),
> and A1 §7.2 neurological emergencies.

---

## §1 Service definition

**What this clinic does:**
- Manages epilepsy: seizure classification (ILAE 2017), EEG interpretation, anti-seizure medication (ASM) initiation and titration (levetiracetam, valproate, carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, lamotrigine, ethosuximide per seizure type), drug-level monitoring, breakthrough-seizure management, driving/activity counselling for adolescents.
- Evaluates and manages headache disorders: migraine (with/without aura) per ICHD-3 criteria, tension-type headache, secondary headache (red flags → neuroimaging), cluster headache (rare in children). Migraine management: lifestyle triggers, acute treatment (ibuprofen, sumatriptan for ≥ 12y), prophylaxis (propranolol, amitriptyline, topiramate).
- Investigates developmental regression: metabolic screening (TMS, urine organic acids, lactate, ammonia), neuroimaging (MRI brain + MRS), EEG (Landau-Kleffner syndrome, epileptic encephalopathy), genetic testing (whole-exome sequencing where indicated). Receives from B1-23 §7.2 bridge.
- Manages neuromuscular disease: Duchenne/Becker muscular dystrophy (from B1-23 CK screening), spinal muscular atrophy, myasthenia gravis, hereditary neuropathies. Coordinates with multidisciplinary team (physiotherapy, respiratory, cardiology, orthopaedics).
- Manages movement disorders: tic disorders (chronic motor/vocal tics, Tourette syndrome — from B1-08 §7.3), chorea, dystonia, tremor.
- Evaluates and manages cerebral palsy: classification (GMFCS), spasticity management (physiotherapy, botulinum toxin, baclofen), comorbidity surveillance (epilepsy, feeding, hip surveillance, cognitive), orthopaedic coordination.
- Manages febrile seizures: counselling (simple febrile seizure = benign; no ASM needed), complex febrile seizure (prolonged, focal, recurrent in 24h → EEG + follow-up), status epilepticus in febrile seizure (→ §7.1 ER).
- Evaluates neonatal seizures: referred from NICU / neonatal unit; classification, EEG, metabolic workup, neuroimaging.

**What this clinic does not do:**
- Operate without a Fellowship-credentialed pediatric neurologist.
- Manage acute status epilepticus beyond recognition and benzodiazepine first-aid (→ §7.1 ER).
- Perform neurosurgery (epilepsy surgery, VP shunt → §7.3 neurosurgery).
- Manage acute stroke (→ §7.1 ER + interventional neurology).

**Tier 2 credential requirement:** Pediatric neurologist with DM Pediatric Neurology or equivalent Fellowship.

**When B2-02 is NOT available:** A1 / B1-23 §7 triggers route to named external pediatric neurologist partner (Tier 3).

## §3–§12 (Tier 2 structure)

**§3:** Receives from: A1 §7.1 (acute neurological event), A1 §7.2 (developmental regression), B1-23 §7.2/§7.3 (developmental regression, seizures, neuromuscular), B1-08 §7.3 (tic disorders, encephalopathy post-pertussis vaccine cross B1-22), B1-20 §7.3 (spinal dysraphism with enuresis).

**§4:** Neurological examination (cranial nerves, tone, power, reflexes, coordination, gait, fundoscopy), EEG ordering + interpretation, MRI brain ordering + interpretation, nerve-conduction studies, metabolic screening panels, genetic testing coordination.

**§5 [SACRED]:** Epilepsy severity by ASM response (well-controlled = seizure-free ≥ 12 months on ASM; drug-resistant epilepsy: failure of adequate trials of two tolerated, appropriately chosen, and correctly used anti-seizure medication schedules to achieve sustained seizure freedom [ILAE 2010 definition]. Note: the definition rests on ASM failure, not seizure frequency — drug-resistant epilepsy → §7.3 for epilepsy-surgery evaluation). Headache severity by frequency/disability (MIDAS score). CP severity by GMFCS level (I–V).

**§6:** ASM prescribing (weight-based, seizure-type-matched): Valproate: first-line for generalised epilepsies (absence, JME, generalised tonic-clonic). CRITICAL: teratogenic — do NOT use as first-line in adolescent girls of childbearing potential; use levetiracetam or lamotrigine instead. Carbamazepine/oxcarbazepine: first-line for focal epilepsies. CONTRAINDICATED in generalised epilepsies (can worsen absence and myoclonic seizures). Ethosuximide: first-line for childhood absence epilepsy specifically. Migraine acute + prophylactic treatment, botulinum toxin for spasticity, GH-therapy coordination for DMD (cross B2-01), physiotherapy prescriptions, orthopaedic device prescriptions (AFOs, standing frames).

**§7 [SACRED]:**
- §7.1: Status epilepticus (→ ER); acute neurological deterioration with raised ICP (→ ER + neurosurgery); acute stroke (→ ER).
- §7.2: New-onset seizures with focal features or abnormal neuroimaging → tertiary neurology centre.
- §7.3: Drug-resistant epilepsy (≥ 2 appropriate ASM failed) → epilepsy-surgery centre evaluation; neurodegenerative disease confirmed → tertiary metabolic/genetics centre; DMD/SMA → neuromuscular centre; VP shunt malfunction → neurosurgery.

## §8 Follow-up framework

### 8.1 Condition-specific follow-up schedule

| Condition | Interval | Key assessments | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Epilepsy — newly diagnosed, ASM titration phase | Every 4 weeks | Seizure diary review, ASM tolerability, drug levels (trough) at steady state, weight (dose recalculation) | Until seizure-free and on stable dose (typically 3–6 months) |
| Epilepsy — well-controlled on stable ASM | Every 3 months | Seizure diary, growth, school performance, drug levels (annual or if breakthrough), side-effect screen | Ongoing; ASM taper discussion at 2 years seizure-free |
| Epilepsy — drug-resistant (pre-§7.3 referral) | Every 4–6 weeks | Seizure frequency, ASM combination review, EEG if seizure type change, nutritional status | Until §7.3 epilepsy-surgery evaluation initiated |
| Migraine — acute management only | Every 3 months | Headache diary, MIDAS score, trigger review, acute-medication overuse screen | 12 months minimum; step down if MIDAS < 5 for 6 months |
| Migraine — on prophylaxis | Every 6–8 weeks during titration; then every 3 months | Headache frequency, MIDAS, drug tolerability, weight (topiramate), mood (amitriptyline) | Prophylaxis trial minimum 8–12 weeks at therapeutic dose before declaring failure |
| Cerebral palsy | Every 6 months | GMFCS level, hip X-ray (annual if GMFCS III–V), spasticity assessment, feeding/nutrition (cross B1-09), communication, epilepsy status, orthopaedic device review | Ongoing — lifelong |
| Febrile seizures — complex | 3 months, then 6 months, then annual | Seizure recurrence, developmental milestones, EEG result review | Discharge at age 6 if no recurrence and no epilepsy features |
| Neuromuscular disease (DMD/SMA) | Every 3–4 months | Motor function (North Star Ambulatory Assessment for DMD), respiratory function (FVC), cardiac (echo annual cross B1-16), scoliosis screen, growth, steroid side-effects | Ongoing — lifelong |
| Movement disorders (tics/Tourette) | Every 3 months | Tic severity (YGTSS), CBIT/HRT adherence, medication review if on pharmacotherapy, comorbidity screen (ADHD, OCD — cross B1-08) | Step down to 6-monthly if stable for 12 months |
| Developmental regression — under investigation | Every 4–6 weeks | Investigation results (metabolic, genetic, imaging), developmental trajectory, feeding, seizure surveillance | Until diagnosis established and management plan in place |

### 8.2 Follow-up visit structure

Each follow-up visit includes:
1. Seizure diary / headache diary / symptom log review (parent-logged in Companion).
2. Medication reconciliation: current ASM doses, adherence, side-effects, drug-level results.
3. Neurological examination (targeted to condition).
4. Growth and nutritional status (cross B1-09 if growth concern).
5. School performance and psychosocial screen (cross B1-08 if concern).
6. Investigation review (EEG, MRI, lab results received since last visit).
7. Plan adjustment: dose change, investigation ordering, §7 referral if threshold met.
8. Parent counselling per §10.
9. Next follow-up scheduling per §8.1 table.

### 8.3 Loss-to-follow-up protocol

| Condition | Trigger | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Epilepsy on ASM | Missed follow-up by 14 days | Manager phone call; ASM refill check |
| Epilepsy on ASM | Missed follow-up by 30 days | Pediatric neurologist phone review; ASM discontinuation risk counselling |
| CP (GMFCS III–V) | Missed 6-month review | Manager + physiotherapy team outreach |
| Neuromuscular disease | Missed follow-up by 30 days | Manager phone call; respiratory function check scheduling |
| Migraine on prophylaxis | Missed follow-up by 30 days | Manager WhatsApp; drug-refill status check |

## §9 Companion software workflow

### 9.1 Doctor Module screens

**Screen 1: Visit context**

- Renders referral source (A1, B1-23, B1-08, B1-20, external) with reason and attached documents.
- Shows previous B2-02 visit history: seizure diary summary, ASM list with doses and levels, investigation results, GMFCS level (CP), MIDAS score history (headache).
- Auto-calculates time since last seizure, time on current ASM, time since last EEG.

**Screen 2: Neurological examination**

- Structured examination template: consciousness, cranial nerves (I–XII checklist), tone (upper/lower, axial), power (MRC grading per muscle group), reflexes (biceps, triceps, supinator, knee, ankle, plantar), coordination (finger-nose, heel-shin, rapid alternating), gait (normal, spastic, ataxic, waddling, Trendelenburg), fundoscopy findings.
- Head circumference (auto-plotted on growth chart for < 5 years).
- Developmental assessment fields (for regression workup).

**Screen 3: Investigation ordering and review**

- EEG: order with clinical indication (seizure type, frequency, last EEG date); attach EEG report PDF; structured summary fields (normal / epileptiform / focal / generalised / specific syndrome pattern).
- MRI brain: order with clinical indication; attach report; structured summary (normal / structural lesion / white-matter abnormality / metabolic pattern).
- Drug levels: order by ASM name; result entry with therapeutic range auto-displayed.
- Metabolic panel: order specific tests (TMS, urine organic acids, lactate, ammonia, CK); result entry.
- Genetic testing: order type (karyotype, microarray, whole-exome, specific gene panel); status tracking (sent / result pending / result received).

**Screen 4: Diagnosis and severity grading (§5)**

- Epilepsy: seizure type (ILAE 2017 classification dropdown), epilepsy syndrome (if identifiable), ASM response status (well-controlled / partial response / drug-resistant per ILAE 2010 definition).
- Headache: ICHD-3 classification, MIDAS score (auto-calculated from headache diary), frequency tier.
- CP: GMFCS level (I–V with visual reference), associated comorbidities checklist.
- Mandatory severity grading before proceeding. Override with free-text reason if doctor disagrees with auto-calculated tier.

**Screen 5: Treatment plan**

- ASM prescribing: drug selection (seizure-type-matched recommendation auto-displayed), weight-based dose calculator, titration schedule generator.
- **CRITICAL HARD-BLOCK:** Valproate prescribed to adolescent girl of childbearing potential → Companion blocks prescription and displays: "Valproate is teratogenic. Use levetiracetam or lamotrigine as first-line for adolescent girls. Override requires documented counselling and contraception plan." Override requires two-step confirmation with documented reason.
- Migraine treatment: acute (ibuprofen dose calculator, sumatriptan eligibility check for ≥ 12y), prophylaxis (drug selection, dose, titration).
- CP management: physiotherapy prescription, botulinum toxin scheduling, orthopaedic device prescription (AFO type, standing frame).
- NMD management: steroid protocol (DMD), respiratory review schedule, cardiac review schedule.

**Screen 6: §7 review (only renders if any §7 trigger fires)**

Pauses the standard flow; renders the §7 handoff packet; mandatory phone-call-confirmed checkbox for §7.1 (status epilepticus, acute neurological deterioration, acute stroke); mandatory acknowledgement before visit completion.

**Screen 7: Follow-up scheduling (§8)**

Auto-suggests next B2-02 visit per §8.1 table. For ASM titration phase, auto-schedules the 4-week follow-up with drug-level ordering reminder. For CP, auto-schedules 6-month review with hip X-ray reminder (GMFCS III–V).

### 9.6 Edge cases and overrides

**Valproate prescribed to adolescent girl.** Companion hard-blocks this prescription. Override requires: (1) documented counselling on teratogenicity, (2) documented contraception plan or documentation that patient is not sexually active with acknowledgement that this must be reassessed, (3) two-step confirmation. Override logged for §12 audit.

**Drug-resistant epilepsy threshold met.** Companion auto-flags when two appropriate ASMs have failed (per ILAE 2010 definition) and prompts §7.3 referral for epilepsy-surgery evaluation. Doctor may defer with documented reason; deferral logged for §12 review.

**Seizure diary: parent reports status epilepticus at home.** Companion triggers immediate alert to pediatric neurologist and displays emergency guidance to parent (call ambulance, administer rescue benzodiazepine if prescribed).

**Offline behaviour.** Doctor Module supports offline B2-02 visits: examination, diagnosis, prescription all locally cached and synced on reconnection. §7 emergency triggers in offline mode trigger a hard-stop with phone-call-required guidance.

## §10 Parent-facing communication

### 10.1 Scripted explanations

**Epilepsy — new diagnosis:**

> "[Aanya] has epilepsy. This means her brain has a tendency to produce seizures. Epilepsy is one of the most common brain conditions in children — about 1 in 100 children has it — and it is very treatable. Most children with epilepsy live completely normal lives with the right medicine. The medicine prevents seizures; it does not cure the underlying tendency, which is why the course is usually two years seizure-free before we consider stopping. We are starting a medicine called [levetiracetam / valproate / carbamazepine] at a low dose and will increase it gradually over the next few weeks."

**Epilepsy — well-controlled, discussing ASM taper:**

> "[Aanya] has been seizure-free for two years now on [medicine]. That is the point at which we consider whether we can slowly reduce and eventually stop the medicine. I want to be clear: there is about a 30% chance that seizures come back during or after the taper. If they do, we restart the same medicine and it almost always works again. We will reduce very slowly — over 3 to 6 months — and monitor carefully. If you are not comfortable with this, we can continue the medicine longer. There is no wrong answer."

**Epilepsy — drug-resistant, referral for epilepsy-surgery evaluation:**

> "[Aanya]'s epilepsy has not responded to two appropriate medicines. This is called drug-resistant epilepsy, and it happens in about 30% of children with epilepsy. It does not mean nothing will work — it means we need to investigate whether surgery is an option. Surgery for epilepsy in children has a very good success rate when the seizure focus can be identified. I am referring [her] to [epilepsy surgery centre] for evaluation. This does not mean surgery will happen — it means we are checking whether it could help."

**Migraine — diagnosis:**

> "The headaches [Rohit] is having are migraines. Migraine in children is common and is not 'just a headache.' It is a brain condition that causes intense pain, often with nausea and sensitivity to light and sound. Children's migraines are often shorter than adults' — sometimes just an hour — but just as intense. We can treat the attacks when they happen and, if they happen often enough, prevent them with a daily medicine."

**Cerebral palsy — diagnosis discussion:**

> "[Aanya] has cerebral palsy. This means the part of the brain that controls movement developed differently, and that affects how [her] muscles work — [she] may be stiffer than other children, or have difficulty with balance and coordination. Cerebral palsy is not a disease that gets worse over time — the brain injury itself does not change. What changes is the body growing, and we need to help [her] body grow as well as possible. That means physiotherapy, the right equipment, and careful monitoring of [her] hips, spine, and nutrition. Many children with cerebral palsy lead full, active lives."

**Febrile seizure — parental counselling:**

> "[Aanya] had a febrile seizure. This is the most common type of seizure in children — about 1 in 25 children has one. A febrile seizure is triggered by fever, not by the illness causing the fever. It does not mean [she] has epilepsy, and it does not damage the brain. About 1 in 3 children who have one febrile seizure will have another, usually in the next 1–2 years. If it happens again: lay [her] on [her] side, do not put anything in [her] mouth, time the seizure, and if it lasts more than 5 minutes, call an ambulance."

### 10.5 Cultural sensitivities

- **Epilepsy stigma.** Epilepsy carries severe stigma in India — families may hide the diagnosis from school, extended family, and marriage prospects. Frame epilepsy explicitly as a medical condition, treatable and common. Counter supernatural and contagion myths directly: "Epilepsy is not caused by spirits, past life events, or anything the family did. It is a brain condition. It is not contagious." Provide school-communication template that the family controls. Do not disclose diagnosis to school without family consent.
- **Activity restrictions.** Families often over-restrict children with epilepsy (no sports, no school trips). Counsel: once seizures are controlled, children can participate in all activities except unsupervised swimming and unprotected heights. Document the counselling.
- **Traditional remedies.** Some families seek Ayurvedic or homeopathic treatment for epilepsy and may discontinue ASM. Counsel firmly but without dismissing: "We respect your interest in other approaches, but stopping the medicine suddenly is dangerous — it can cause a prolonged seizure. If you want to explore other therapies, please keep the medicine going and let us know, so we can monitor safely." Document.
- **Medication-refusal families.** Some families resist long-term medication for children. Counsel on natural history: untreated epilepsy has a 70–80% recurrence rate after a second unprovoked seizure. The ASM course is finite (typically 2 years seizure-free). Document refusal if it occurs; do not discharge — offer closer follow-up.
- **CP and disability framing.** Avoid deficit-only language. Emphasise what the child can do and how therapy helps the child reach their potential. Address the extended family's "will my child be normal?" question directly and compassionately.

## §12 Quality and audit

### 12.1 Outcome metrics

B2-02 is working well when:

- ≥ 60% of newly diagnosed epilepsy patients achieve seizure freedom at 12 months on ASM.
- ≥ 50% of migraine patients on prophylaxis achieve ≥ 50% reduction in headache frequency at 3 months (MIDAS improvement).
- ≥ 90% of patients with a first unprovoked seizure have an EEG obtained within 4 weeks.
- ≥ 90% of CP patients (GMFCS III–V) have annual hip X-ray surveillance completed on schedule.
- ≥ 95% of CP patients have documented physiotherapy attendance of ≥ 75% of prescribed sessions.
- 100% of drug-resistant epilepsy cases (ILAE 2010 definition met) are referred for epilepsy-surgery evaluation within 3 months of classification.
- 100% of §7.1 emergency triggers result in same-day handoff to ER.
- 0% of adolescent girls of childbearing potential are started on valproate as first-line ASM without documented counselling and override justification.
- ≥ 90% of ASM refills are dispensed within 7 days of due date (no gaps in therapy).
- ≥ 80% of febrile-seizure families receive documented seizure first-aid counselling at the first visit.

### 12.2 Quarterly case review

The protocol owner (pediatric neurologist) reviews every quarter:
- All new epilepsy diagnoses: seizure classification accuracy, ASM choice appropriateness, EEG timeliness.
- All drug-resistant epilepsy cases: ASM trials documented, §7.3 referral timeliness.
- All §7 referrals (every tier): outcome tracking (was the referral appropriate, was the patient seen, what was the outcome).
- All valproate prescriptions to adolescent girls: counselling documentation, override justification review.
- All CP patients: GMFCS stability/progression, hip surveillance compliance, physiotherapy attendance, nutritional status.
- A random sample of 20 B2-02 visits across condition categories.
- All loss-to-follow-up cases on active ASM.
- All seizure-diary-triggered breakthrough alerts: response timeliness and management adequacy.

Output: 1-page report to SKIDS clinical operations, with any recommended protocol updates flagged.

### 12.3 Pediatric neurologist certification on this protocol

To deliver B2-02, a SKIDS pediatric neurologist must:
- Hold DM Pediatric Neurology or equivalent Fellowship credential.
- Complete the B2-02 onboarding chapter (6 hours).
- Pass a structured case-vignette assessment (20 cases across epilepsy, headache, CP, NMD, movement disorders, developmental regression).
- Demonstrate EEG interpretation proficiency (10 EEGs scored against protocol owner's interpretation).
- Demonstrate ILAE 2017 seizure classification accuracy (10 cases).
- Demonstrate GMFCS classification accuracy (5 CP cases).
- Maintain a minimum case load of 30 B2-02 visits per quarter.
- Complete annual continuing education (8 hours covering ILAE updates, ASM updates, CP management updates, NMD treatment advances).

### 12.4 The SKIDS Fellowship pathway

The SKIDS Pediatric Neurology Fellowship IS the credential pathway for this Tier 2 service. Unlike Tier 1 protocols where a junior SKIDS pediatrician delivers under protocol governance, B2-02 requires the Fellowship-credentialed pediatric neurologist to be the delivering clinician. See `docs/06-the-skids-fellowship.md`.

The Fellowship-credentialed neurologist delivers:
- All conditions in §1 (epilepsy, headache, CP, NMD, movement disorders, developmental regression workup).
- Protocol governance for all Tier 1 protocols that route into B2-02 (B1-23 Developmental Surveillance, B1-08 Behavioural — tic disorders, B1-20 Bedwetting — spinal dysraphism).
- Quarterly case review per §12.2.
- Training and certification of SKIDS pediatricians on neurological red-flag recognition in A1 and B1 protocols.
