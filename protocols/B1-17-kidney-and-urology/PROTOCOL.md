# SKIDS Kidney & Urology Clinic

> The SKIDS Kidney & Urology Clinic delivers hypertension secondary-cause evaluation (co-referred with B1-16), UTI diagnosis and management, proteinuria/haematuria workup, renal ultrasound interpretation, urodynamic referral coordination for B1-20 enuresis escalation, and routing to pediatric nephrology/urology for structural anomalies, CKD, nephrotic syndrome, and surgical conditions.

---

## §1 Service definition

**What this clinic does:**
- Evaluates confirmed hypertension (BP ≥ 95th centile on ≥ 3 occasions, co-referred from B1-16): secondary-cause workup including renal ultrasound, urinalysis, serum creatinine, electrolytes, renin-aldosterone (where indicated) [AAP-2017-HBP].
- Diagnoses and manages uncomplicated UTI in children: urine dipstick + culture, appropriate antibiotic therapy, imaging per AAP UTI guidelines (renal ultrasound for first febrile UTI in 2–24 months; VCUG for recurrent or atypical UTI) [AAP-UTI-2011].
- Evaluates isolated proteinuria (persistent on first-morning urine × 2) and isolated haematuria (confirmed microscopic haematuria × 2 on first-morning specimen): structured workup to differentiate benign (orthostatic proteinuria, thin-basement-membrane disease) from pathological (glomerulonephritis, nephrotic syndrome).
- Manages uncomplicated nephrotic syndrome (steroid-responsive, first episode): initiates prednisolone per ISKDC protocol (2 mg/kg/day × 6 weeks, then 1.5 mg/kg alternate day × 6 weeks); routes steroid-resistant or frequently-relapsing NS to §7.3 pediatric nephrology.
- Coordinates urodynamic evaluation for B1-20 enuresis escalation: non-monosymptomatic or refractory enuresis → B1-17 for bladder-function assessment.
- Manages recurrent UTI prevention: identifies vesicoureteral reflux (VUR) candidacy, antibiotic prophylaxis where indicated, behavioural management (timed voiding, adequate fluids, constipation treatment cross B1-21).
- Screens for congenital anomalies of kidney and urinary tract (CAKUT): hydronephrosis follow-up from antenatal ultrasound, posterior urethral valves suspicion, renal agenesis/ectopy.
- Co-manages obesity-related hypertension with B1-10 and B1-16.

**What this clinic does not do:**
- Manage CKD stages 3–5 or dialysis (→ §7.3 pediatric nephrology centre).
- Perform renal biopsy (→ §7.3).
- Manage steroid-resistant nephrotic syndrome (→ §7.3).
- Perform urological surgery (circumcision, orchidopexy, hypospadias repair, pyeloplasty → §7.3 pediatric urological surgeon, Tier 3).
- Manage acute kidney injury (→ §7.1 ER + nephrology).

**Age range covered:** 0 – 18 years.

**SKIDS subscription tier(s):**
- **Basic ₹2,999** — A1 BP screening + urinalysis; first B1-17 visit on per-visit pricing.
- **Advanced ₹6,999** — UTI management programme; hypertension monitoring; renal ultrasound coordination; up to 4 visits.
- **Premium ₹12,999** — unlimited visits; nephrotic syndrome management; multi-protocol cross-care (B1-17 + B1-16 + B1-20 + B1-10).

**SKIDS clinical tier:** Tier 1. §7 escalates to pediatric nephrology (NS, CKD, biopsy), pediatric urology (surgical conditions), and ER (AKI, hypertensive emergency).

## §4 Diagnostic workflow

### 4.1 Hypertension secondary-cause workup
- Renal ultrasound (renal size, echogenicity, hydronephrosis, structural anomaly).
- Urinalysis + urine protein:creatinine ratio.
- Serum creatinine, electrolytes, calcium, phosphate.
- FBC (anaemia of CKD).
- Renin, aldosterone (where indicated — renovascular HTN suspicion).
- TSH, cortisol (endocrine causes — rare).
- If all normal + obesity → primary/essential HTN; B1-10 lifestyle management; B1-16 BP monitoring.
- If abnormal → §7.3 pediatric nephrology.

### 4.2 UTI management
- **Diagnosis:** urine dipstick (leucocyte esterase + nitrites) + urine culture (catheter or clean-catch; NOT bag urine for culture).
- **Treatment:** empirical antibiotic pending culture: cephalexin 25 mg/kg/dose BD or co-amoxiclav × 7–10 days (febrile UTI); × 3–5 days (afebrile/uncomplicated cystitis). Adjust per culture sensitivity.
- **Imaging:** renal ultrasound for first febrile UTI in child 2–24 months [AAP-UTI-2011]. VCUG for recurrent febrile UTI or abnormal ultrasound → §7.3.
- **Prophylaxis:** trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole 2 mg/kg/day (TMP component) at bedtime for VUR grade III+ or recurrent UTI, per nephrology guidance.

### 4.3 Proteinuria workup
- Confirm on first-morning urine × 2 (eliminates orthostatic proteinuria).
- Urine protein:creatinine ratio (UPCR): < 0.2 (normal); 0.2–2.0 (nephrotic-range if ≥ 2.0); > 2.0 with hypoalbuminaemia = nephrotic syndrome.
- If persistent proteinuria: serum albumin, serum creatinine, FBC, C3/C4, ANA, renal ultrasound → §7.3 if nephritic features.

### 4.4 Haematuria workup
- Confirm microscopic haematuria on × 2 first-morning specimens (> 5 RBC/HPF).
- Urine RBC morphology (dysmorphic = glomerular; isomorphic = lower tract).
- Urine culture (exclude UTI), urine calcium:creatinine ratio (hypercalciuria), serum creatinine, C3/C4, ASO titre.
- Renal ultrasound.
- If nephritic features (HTN + haematuria + proteinuria + oedema + reduced GFR) → §7.2 urgent nephrology.

### 4.5 Nephrotic syndrome
- Triad: oedema + proteinuria (UPCR ≥ 2.0) + hypoalbuminaemia (< 2.5 g/dL).
- Age 1–10 years, first episode, no haematuria/HTN/renal impairment → likely minimal-change disease → initiate ISKDC prednisolone at B1-17.
- Atypical features (age < 1 or > 10, haematuria, HTN, low C3, renal impairment) → §7.3 nephrology for biopsy before steroids.

### 4.6 CAKUT follow-up
- Antenatal hydronephrosis: renal ultrasound at 1 week and 4–6 weeks postnatal. If persistent → §7.3 pediatric urology/nephrology for VCUG + MAG3.
- Posterior urethral valves suspicion (bilateral hydronephrosis + thick-walled bladder in boy) → §7.2 urgent urology.

### 4.7 Enuresis urodynamic workup (from B1-20)
- Non-monosymptomatic enuresis or refractory PMNE after alarm + desmopressin → uroflowmetry, post-void residual ultrasound. If abnormal → §7.3 for formal urodynamics.

## §5 Severity grading [SACRED SECTION]

| Severity | Criteria | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Isolated microscopic haematuria or orthostatic proteinuria; single uncomplicated UTI | §6.1 — treat UTI; monitor |
| **Moderate** | Confirmed HTN (Stage 1); recurrent UTI; persistent proteinuria | §6.2 — workup + management |
| **Severe** | Nephrotic syndrome; Stage 2 HTN; nephritic features; AKI signs | §6.3 / §7 |
| **Beyond Tier 1** | Steroid-resistant NS; CKD ≥ stage 3; structural anomaly needing surgery | §7.3 |

### Boundary rules
- First uncomplicated UTI = B1-17 manages. Recurrent UTI = B1-17 manages + imaging + prophylaxis decision. Complex UTI with structural anomaly = §7.3.
- First-episode steroid-responsive NS (typical age, no atypical features) = B1-17. Anything atypical = §7.3 before treatment.
- HTN Stage 1 with normal workup + obesity = co-managed B1-17 + B1-16 + B1-10. HTN with abnormal workup = §7.3.

## §6 Therapeutic options

### 6.1 Light tier
- UTI: appropriate antibiotic per culture; ensure adequate hydration; constipation management (B1-21 cross).
- Isolated haematuria/proteinuria: monitor with 3-monthly urinalysis + BP.

### 6.2 Standard tier
- HTN Stage 1 (normal workup): lifestyle (DASH diet, exercise, sodium reduction) × 6 months; if persistent → §7.3 for antihypertensive.
- Recurrent UTI: prophylaxis (TMP-SMX at bedtime); timed voiding; adequate fluids; constipation treatment.
- Nephrotic syndrome (first episode, typical): prednisolone 2 mg/kg/day (max 60 mg) × 6 weeks → 1.5 mg/kg alternate day (max 40 mg) × 6 weeks → taper. Monitor: daily weight, urine protein dipstick, BP, glucose.

### 6.3 Intensive tier
- NS relapse: prednisolone re-induction per ISKDC; if frequently relapsing (≥ 2 relapses in 6 months or ≥ 4 in 12 months) → §7.3 for steroid-sparing agents.
- AKI signs (oliguria, rising creatinine, fluid overload) → §7.1 ER.

### 6.4 Dispensary stock
Prednisolone 5 mg tablets; cephalexin suspension; co-amoxiclav suspension; TMP-SMX suspension; urine dipstick strips; urine collection kits.

## §7 Hard referral triggers [SACRED SECTION]

### 7.1 Emergency (same day)
- [ ] **Hypertensive emergency** (Stage 2 + symptoms) → ER.
- [ ] **Acute kidney injury** (oliguria, rising creatinine, fluid overload, hyperkalaemia) → ER + nephrology.
- [ ] **Nephrotic syndrome with peritonitis** (abdominal pain + fever + NS) → ER.
- [ ] **Posterior urethral valves** (bilateral hydronephrosis + urinary retention in male infant) → ER + urology.

### 7.2 Urgent (within 7 days)
- [ ] **Nephritic syndrome** (haematuria + HTN + proteinuria + oedema + reduced GFR) → nephrology.
- [ ] **Stage 2 HTN** (asymptomatic) → nephrology/cardiology.
- [ ] **NS with atypical features** → nephrology for biopsy consideration.

### 7.3 Routine (within 4 weeks)
- [ ] **Steroid-resistant NS** → nephrology.
- [ ] **Frequently relapsing NS** → nephrology for steroid-sparing.
- [ ] **Persistent HTN Stage 1 after 6 months lifestyle** → nephrology/cardiology for pharmacotherapy.
- [ ] **CAKUT requiring surgical evaluation** → pediatric urology.
- [ ] **Cryptorchidism** (undescended testis not palpable by 6 months) → pediatric urology.
- [ ] **Recurrent UTI with VUR grade IV–V** → pediatric urology.
- [ ] **CKD suspected (persistent elevated creatinine, small kidneys)** → nephrology.
- [ ] **Renal calculi** → pediatric urology/nephrology.
- [ ] **Enuresis urodynamic abnormality** → pediatric urology.

### 7.4 Handoff: BP log, urinalysis results, renal ultrasound, serum creatinine, urine culture history, NS treatment log.

## §8 Follow-up schedule

| Scenario | First follow-up | Subsequent |
|---|---|---|
| Uncomplicated UTI | 48–72h (if not improving); urine culture at 4 weeks post-treatment | PRN; annual urinalysis at A1 |
| Recurrent UTI on prophylaxis | 4 weeks | 3-monthly × 1 year; then 6-monthly |
| Nephrotic syndrome (induction phase) | Weekly (weight + urine protein dipstick) | Weekly × 6 weeks induction; then monthly during taper |
| Nephrotic syndrome (post-taper) | Monthly × 3 months | 3-monthly × 1 year; then 6-monthly |
| NS relapse | Weekly during re-induction | Per induction schedule |
| HTN Stage 1 (lifestyle management) | Monthly BP × 3 months | 3-monthly if stable |
| Isolated haematuria / proteinuria | 3 months | 3-monthly urinalysis + BP × 1 year; then 6-monthly if stable |
| CAKUT follow-up (postnatal hydronephrosis) | Per ultrasound schedule (1 week, 4–6 weeks) | Per nephrology/urology guidance |
| Enuresis urodynamic workup (from B1-20) | Per urology guidance | Per urology guidance |

§8.2 What to recheck: urine dipstick (protein, blood, leucocytes); BP (at every visit); weight (NS children — daily by parent, weekly at clinic during induction); serum creatinine (if CKD risk); renal ultrasound (per investigation timeline); urine culture (UTI children — confirm clearance); NS treatment compliance (steroid diary).

§8.3 Success: UTI resolved (sterile urine culture at 4 weeks); NS in remission (urine protein dipstick negative × 3 consecutive days); HTN normalised on lifestyle (BP < 90th centile); haematuria/proteinuria stable or resolved; no §7 triggers met.

§8.7 Loss-to-follow-up: Companion alert for NS children (steroid course interruption risks relapse) and recurrent UTI children on prophylaxis (discontinuation without review risks breakthrough UTI). High-priority re-engagement.

## §10 Parent-facing communication

### 10.1 Scripted explanations

UTI:

> "[Aanya] has a urine infection. We confirm this with a urine test. The treatment is a course of antibiotics for [7–10 days]. It is important to complete the full course. We will re-test the urine after treatment to make sure the infection has cleared. We also do an ultrasound to check that the kidneys and urinary tract are normal."

Nephrotic syndrome:

> "[Arjun]'s kidneys are leaking protein into the urine, which is causing the puffiness around his eyes and legs. This condition is called nephrotic syndrome. The good news is that most children respond very well to the medicine — prednisolone. It reduces the protein leak, and you will see the puffiness go down, usually within 1–2 weeks. The full course is about 3 months — it is very important to complete it and not stop early, even when [he] looks better."

Hypertension workup:

> "The blood test and urine test show us whether [his] blood pressure is coming from the kidneys or from another cause. In most children with high blood pressure and a normal weight, the kidneys are the first thing we check. If everything is normal, we manage the blood pressure with diet and exercise changes."

### 10.2 Subscription tier explanation: standard.

### 10.5 Cultural sensitivities

- *Steroid fear in nephrotic syndrome.* "Steroids are dangerous" is a common concern. Explain: prednisolone used here is a different type of steroid from bodybuilding steroids. It is a short course with a gradual taper. Side effects (weight gain, mood changes, increased appetite) are temporary and resolve after the course is completed. The alternative — untreated NS — leads to serious complications including infections and blood clots.
- *Circumcision and undescended testis.* In families requesting circumcision, if the child also has an undescended testis, address sequentially — orchidopexy (bringing the testis down) takes clinical priority. Circumcision is elective and can be planned afterwards. Frame positively: "We want to take care of the more important thing first."
- *"Kidney problem means dialysis."* Reassure: most childhood kidney conditions managed at B1-17 (UTI, NS, mild HTN) do not progress to kidney failure. NS in children has an excellent prognosis — most respond to treatment and outgrow it.
- *Urine-collection reluctance.* Some families find catheterised urine collection distressing. Explain that bag urine is unreliable for culture and may lead to incorrect treatment. Clean-catch is preferred where feasible; catheter is quick and accurate.

## §11 Manager handoff

§11.1 Manager Console: kidney/urology diagnosis; NS status (remission / induction / relapse); BP trend; UTI history; prophylaxis status; §7 referral status; subscription tier and coverage.

§11.2 Tiering: UTI management (single episode) = Basic/Advanced; recurrent UTI with imaging + prophylaxis = Advanced; HTN workup + NS management + multi-protocol cross-care (B1-17 + B1-16 + B1-20 + B1-10) = Premium.

§11.3 Top parent objections:

| Objection | Manager response |
|---|---|
| "It's just a urine infection — why does she need an ultrasound?" | "For children under 2 with a fever and urine infection, the ultrasound checks that the kidneys and tubes are normal. It is a painless scan — no needles, no radiation. The doctor follows national guidelines for this." |
| "Steroids for 3 months — isn't that too long?" | "The 3-month course is the standard treatment used worldwide for this condition. Stopping early makes the condition come back. The doctor tapers the dose gradually so the body adjusts. Side effects are temporary." |
| "His BP is high — does he need medicine for life?" | "In children, we first look for the cause. If the cause is treatable, the BP often normalises. Even when lifestyle changes are needed, children respond well. Medicine is only added if lifestyle alone doesn't work after 6 months." |
| "We want a second opinion from a kidney specialist." | "Absolutely — and the doctor may recommend that too. The workup we have done gives the specialist all the information they need to advise quickly." |

§11.4–§11.6: standard manager touchpoints. NS families get proactive re-engagement during taper phase to ensure course completion and early relapse detection.

## §12 Quality and audit

§12.1 Metrics:
- ≥ 95% of UTI diagnoses are culture-confirmed before antibiotic initiation.
- NS remission (urine protein negative) achieved within 2 weeks of prednisolone initiation in ≥ 80% of first-episode typical NS.
- ≥ 90% of febrile UTI in children 2–24 months receive renal ultrasound per AAP guidelines.
- ≥ 95% of §7 triggers result in documented referral with complete handoff packet.
- NS steroid course completion rate ≥ 90% (no premature discontinuation without medical reason).
- UTI prophylaxis adherence (where prescribed): ≥ 85% (measured by refill counts).
- Loss-to-follow-up < 15%.

§12.2 Quarterly: protocol owner reviews all §7 referrals, NS treatment outcomes (remission rate, relapse rate, frequently-relapsing rate), UTI culture-before-antibiotic compliance, imaging compliance for febrile UTI, and BP monitoring adherence for HTN children.

§12.3 Certification: every SKIDS pediatrician delivering B1-17 must complete UTI diagnosis and management training (4 hours); nephrotic syndrome ISKDC protocol training (4 hours); pediatric BP measurement and classification (2 hours); renal ultrasound interpretation basics (2 hours). Annual update (2 hours).

§12.4 Fellowship: no specific SKIDS Nephrology Fellowship pathway. Protocol governance sits with the named pediatric nephrologist.
