# SKIDS Pulmonology / Asthma Clinic

> The SKIDS Pulmonology / Asthma Clinic delivers GINA-based stepwise
> asthma management, recurrent-wheeze evaluation in under-5s,
> chronic-cough workup, peak-flow and spirometry monitoring,
> asthma-action-plan generation, spacer-technique training, and
> joint trigger-management with B1-06 Allergy for atopic-march
> children. Active status epilepticus, respiratory failure, and
> suspected cystic fibrosis or structural airway disease route
> per §7.

---

## §1 Service definition

**What this clinic does:**
- Diagnoses and manages pediatric asthma using the GINA 2024 stepwise approach (Steps 1–4 within Tier 1 scope; Step 5 escalates per §7.3) [GINA-2024].
- Evaluates recurrent wheeze in children < 5 years using the modified Asthma Predictive Index (mAPI) to stratify risk of persistent asthma.
- Performs pre- and post-bronchodilator spirometry (children ≥ 6 years capable of reproducible technique) and peak-flow monitoring to assess airflow limitation and bronchodilator reversibility.
- Generates individualised written asthma action plans (traffic-light format: green / yellow / red zones) for every child with confirmed asthma.
- Trains children and parents on spacer + pMDI technique; reassesses technique at every follow-up.
- Co-manages allergic asthma triggers with B1-06 Allergy: B1-06 owns trigger identification (skin-prick testing, specific IgE, aeroallergen panel) and allergen immunotherapy candidacy; B1-14 owns airway management, controller therapy, and reliever prescription.
- Manages chronic cough (> 4 weeks) with structured workup: post-nasal drip (joint with B1-06 allergic rhinitis), cough-variant asthma, protracted bacterial bronchitis (PBB), habit/psychogenic cough.
- Screens for obstructive sleep apnoea in children with adenotonsillar hypertrophy and nocturnal symptoms (cross with B1-07 Sleep for OSA pathway; cross with B1-03 Throat & Airway when drafted for adenotonsillectomy routing).
- Manages acute asthma exacerbation (mild-moderate) in clinic with SABA + systemic corticosteroid; severe exacerbation triggers §7.1.
- Communicates FEV1/asthma-control status to B1-06 for SLIT candidacy assessment and OFC scheduling — severe asthma (FEV1 < 80% predicted) is a hard contraindication to SLIT initiation and OFC at B1-06.
- Ensures every child with co-morbid asthma + food allergy (B1-06) has a reliever inhaler prescribed and accessible, as B1-06's anaphylaxis action plan assumes its availability.

**What this clinic does not do:**
- Manage GINA Step 5 severe refractory asthma (high-dose ICS + LABA + OCS-dependent or biologic-candidate). → §7.3 to pediatric pulmonology subspecialist.
- Manage bronchiectasis, cystic fibrosis, primary ciliary dyskinesia, or structural airway disease. → §7.3 to pediatric pulmonology subspecialist.
- Manage pediatric pulmonary tuberculosis beyond initial respiratory-symptom workup (A1 §7.3 routes confirmed TB to NTEP DOTS centre per NTEP-2020; B1-14 may identify TB during chronic-cough workup and routes per §7.3).
- Manage respiratory failure, status asthmaticus, or life-threatening exacerbation. → §7.1 ER.
- Perform bronchoscopy or CT-guided biopsy.
- Initiate or manage biologic therapy (omalizumab, mepolizumab, dupilumab). → §7.3.

**Age range covered:** 0 – 18 years.

**SKIDS subscription tier(s):**
- **Basic ₹2,999** — A1 respiratory-flag routing; first B1-14 visit on per-visit pricing.
- **Advanced ₹6,999** — up to 6 B1-14 visits per year included; peak-flow diary monitoring; asthma action plan; spacer + pMDI kit included at initiation.
- **Premium ₹12,999** — unlimited B1-14 visits; spirometry at each visit; cross-care with B1-06 for trigger management included; priority §7 partner referral.

**SKIDS clinical tier:** Tier 1 with allied-staff dependency on the SKIDS respiratory therapist or trained nurse for spirometry, spacer-technique training, and peak-flow diary coaching. Protocol governance with named pediatric pulmonologist (may share governance with B1-06 per §12.4). Complex tier per §7 escalates to pediatric pulmonology subspecialist for GINA Step 5, structural disease, or diagnostic uncertainty.

**Departures from cited guidelines:**

1. *GINA Step 5 is out of Tier 1 scope.* GINA permits primary-care clinicians to manage through Step 4. B1-14 limits Tier 1 to Steps 1–4; Step 5 (high-dose ICS + LABA + add-on controller + biologic candidacy assessment) routes to §7.3 pediatric pulmonology subspecialist. This is conservative; the departure protects junior pediatricians from managing complex severe asthma without subspecialty oversight.

2. *ICS-formoterol as MART.* GINA 2024 recommends ICS-formoterol as the preferred reliever and MART (maintenance and reliever therapy) from Step 3 in adolescents ≥ 12 years. SKIDS follows this where ICS-formoterol combination inhalers are available in India. Where not available, SABA as reliever + separate ICS controller remains acceptable [NEEDS EXPANSION IN EDITORIAL PASS — confirm ICS-formoterol availability in Indian market].

3. *Spirometry not available in all SKIDS clinics.* Where spirometry is not available (smaller clinics), peak-flow monitoring + clinical assessment is used for control evaluation. Spirometry is a Premium-tier benefit and a clinic-operations dependency. The protocol specifies which decisions require spirometry (e.g., confirming diagnosis in children ≥ 6 years; OFC clearance for B1-06) and which can be made on clinical + peak-flow grounds alone.

## §4 Diagnostic workflow

### 4.1 Structured history

Ask, in this order:
1. **Symptom pattern.** Wheeze, cough, dyspnoea, chest tightness — frequency, timing (nocturnal? exercise? seasonal?), duration of episodes, interval symptoms.
2. **Trigger identification.** Viral URI, exercise, cold air, allergen exposure (dust, pollen, pets, mould), tobacco-smoke exposure (passive), pollution, emotional triggers.
3. **Atopic history.** Personal history of atopic dermatitis (B1-05), allergic rhinitis (B1-06), food allergy (B1-06), eosinophilic oesophagitis. Family history of asthma, atopy.
4. **Previous therapy.** Current medications (ICS, LABA, SABA, montelukast, antihistamines), technique, adherence, controller refill history.
5. **Exacerbation history.** Number of exacerbations in past 12 months, ER visits, hospitalisations, ICU admissions, intubation history.
6. **Impact.** School absence, activity limitation, sleep disturbance, quality of life (parent and child perception).
7. **Environmental.** Home environment (carpets, pets, mould, cooking fuel, incense, tobacco), school environment, outdoor air quality.
8. **Comorbidities.** Allergic rhinitis (B1-06), GORD, obesity (B1-10), anxiety (B1-08), OSA (B1-07).

Red flags to elicit:
- History of ICU admission or intubation for asthma → increased near-fatal asthma risk.
- Frequent SABA use (≥ 3 canisters/year or daily use) → poorly controlled; assess urgently.
- Continuous oral corticosteroid use → Step 5 candidate; §7.3.

### 4.2 Examination

- **Respiratory.** RR, SpO₂, chest expansion, percussion, auscultation (wheeze, prolonged expiration, air entry). Note: normal examination between episodes does not exclude asthma.
- **Upper airway.** Nasal mucosa (pale, boggy = allergic rhinitis; nasal polyps → §7.3), post-nasal drip, adenotonsillar size.
- **Skin.** Atopic dermatitis signs (flexural, dry skin, lichenification) — flag for B1-05 cross-care.
- **Chest shape.** Barrel chest (chronic air trapping), Harrison sulcus (chronic severe asthma or rickets), pectus deformity.
- **Growth.** BMI and height plotted — ICS effect on growth is minimal at low-medium doses but monitor.
- **Clubbing.** Present → NOT asthma; consider bronchiectasis, CF, or structural disease → §7.3.

### 4.3 Scales and control assessment

| Scale | Domain | Cutoffs |
|---|---|---|
| **GINA symptom control** (4-item) | Asthma control ≥ 6 years | Well-controlled (0 yes); Partly controlled (1–2 yes); Uncontrolled (3–4 yes) |
| **C-ACT (Childhood Asthma Control Test)** | Control 4–11 years | ≥ 20 = well-controlled; 13–19 = not well-controlled; ≤ 12 = very poorly controlled |
| **ACT (Asthma Control Test)** | Control ≥ 12 years | ≥ 20 = well-controlled; 16–19 = not well-controlled; ≤ 15 = very poorly controlled |
| **CARAT** | Combined rhinitis + asthma control | Shared with B1-06 for rhinitis-asthma overlap tracking |
| **mAPI (modified Asthma Predictive Index)** | Persistent asthma risk < 5 years | Positive = ≥ 3 episodes wheeze in past year + ≥ 1 major criterion (parental asthma, atopic dermatitis, aeroallergen sensitisation) OR ≥ 2 minor criteria (food allergy, ≥ 4% peripheral eosinophilia, wheeze apart from colds) |

GINA control assessment items (for ≥ 6 years, in past 4 weeks):
1. Daytime symptoms > 2 times/week?
2. Any night waking due to asthma?
3. Reliever needed for symptoms > 2 times/week?
4. Any activity limitation due to asthma?

### 4.4 In-clinic device tests

**Spirometry (children ≥ 6 years capable of reproducible technique):**
- Pre-bronchodilator FEV1, FVC, FEV1/FVC ratio.
- Post-bronchodilator (SABA 400 mcg via spacer; re-test at 15 minutes).
- **Bronchodilator reversibility:** ≥ 12% and ≥ 200 mL improvement in FEV1 = positive (diagnostic for variable airflow limitation) [GINA-2024].
- **FEV1 < 80% predicted** = significant airflow limitation; informs severity grading and communicates to B1-06 (SLIT contraindication; OFC contraindication).
- **FEV1/FVC < 0.90** in children = obstructive pattern.

**Peak-flow monitoring:**
- Teach peak-flow technique; provide peak-flow meter.
- Record personal best over 2 weeks of good control.
- Diurnal peak-flow variability > 13% (average of daily amplitude % mean over 1–2 weeks) = positive for variable airflow limitation [GINA-2024].
- Peak-flow zone assignment for action plan: Green ≥ 80% personal best; Yellow 50–80%; Red < 50%.

**Pulse oximetry:**
- At every visit; at every exacerbation assessment.
- SpO₂ < 92% on room air = severe exacerbation → §7.1.
- SpO₂ 92–95% = moderate exacerbation → §6.7 in-clinic management with close monitoring.

**Fractional exhaled nitric oxide (FeNO):** [NEEDS EXPANSION IN EDITORIAL PASS — confirm whether SKIDS clinics will stock FeNO devices. FeNO > 35 ppb in children supports eosinophilic airway inflammation and ICS responsiveness; useful for phenotyping and ICS dose titration. If not available, clinical + spirometry assessment is sufficient.]

### 4.5 Chronic cough workup (cough > 4 weeks)

Differential in children:
1. **Cough-variant asthma.** Dry cough, worse at night/exercise, atopic history, bronchodilator-responsive. Trial of ICS × 4 weeks; response confirms diagnosis.
2. **Post-nasal drip / upper airway cough syndrome.** Wet cough, throat clearing, nasal congestion, allergic rhinitis (cross with B1-06). Treat rhinitis; reassess.
3. **Protracted bacterial bronchitis (PBB).** Wet cough > 4 weeks without specific cause; responds to extended antibiotic course (amoxicillin-clavulanate 25 mg/kg/dose BD × 14 days; extend to 4 weeks if incomplete response). If > 3 episodes/year or no response → §7.3 for bronchiectasis workup [IAP-Asthma-2021].
4. **GORD-related cough.** Cough with feeds, postural, with other GORD symptoms. Joint with B1-15 GI when drafted.
5. **Habit / psychogenic cough.** Absent during sleep; adolescent; staccato barking quality. Joint with B1-08 if psychosocial dimension significant.
6. **TB.** Chronic cough + fever + weight loss + contact history → chest X-ray + Mantoux/IGRA → NTEP referral per A1 §7.3.
7. **Foreign-body aspiration.** Acute onset, unilateral wheeze, choking history → §7.1 ER for rigid bronchoscopy.
8. **Structural / rare.** Tracheomalacia, vascular ring, bronchiectasis, CF, PCD → §7.3.

### 4.6 Decision tree

```
Child presents with wheeze / cough / dyspnoea
        │
```

## §5 Severity grading [SACRED SECTION]

### 5.1 Asthma severity (initial assessment, treatment-naive)

Based on symptom frequency and lung function before controller therapy [GINA-2024]:

| Severity | Daytime symptoms | Night symptoms | FEV1 (≥ 6 years) | Exacerbations | GINA Step | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Intermittent** | ≤ 2 days/week | ≤ 2 nights/month | ≥ 80% predicted | 0–1 /year | Step 1 | Light §6.1 |
| **Mild persistent** | > 2 days/week, not daily | 3–4 nights/month | ≥ 80% predicted | ≥ 2 /year | Step 2 | Light §6.1 |
| **Moderate persistent** | Daily | > 1 night/week | 60–80% predicted | ≥ 2 /year | Step 3 | Standard §6.2 |
| **Severe persistent** | Throughout the day | Nightly | < 60% predicted | Frequent | Step 4 | Intensive §6.3 |
| **Beyond Tier 1** | Uncontrolled on Step 4 | — | — | — | Step 5 | §7.3 referral |

### 5.2 Asthma control (ongoing assessment, on controller therapy)

Use GINA 4-item control assessment (§4.3) at every follow-up:

| Control level | Criteria (past 4 weeks) | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Well-controlled** | 0 of 4 items positive | Maintain current step; consider step-down after 3 months |
| **Partly controlled** | 1–2 of 4 items positive | Review technique + adherence first; if optimised, consider step-up |
| **Uncontrolled** | 3–4 of 4 items positive | Step-up after confirming technique + adherence; if already Step 4 → §7.3 |

### 5.3 Exacerbation severity (acute presentation)

| Severity | Clinical features | SpO₂ | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Speaking in sentences; RR mildly ↑; no accessory muscles | ≥ 95% | §6.7 SABA × 3 doses; reassess |
| **Moderate** | Speaking in phrases; RR ↑; some accessory muscle use | 92–95% | §6.7 SABA + systemic steroid; observe 1–2h |
| **Severe** | Speaking in words; RR ↑↑; accessory muscles; agitated | < 92% | §7.1 emergency |
| **Life-threatening** | Drowsy/confused; silent chest; cyanosis | < 90% | §7.1 immediate ER; adrenaline if anaphylaxis coexists |

### 5.4 Recurrent wheeze in < 5 years

| Pattern | mAPI | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Episodic viral wheeze (< 3 episodes/year) | Negative | §6.1 SABA PRN; reassess at 5 |
| Recurrent wheeze (≥ 3 episodes/year) | Negative | §6.1 SABA PRN + consider intermittent ICS during URI; reassess at 5 |
| Recurrent wheeze (≥ 3 episodes/year) | Positive | Probable asthma; initiate low-dose ICS per §6.2 |

### 5.5 Chronic cough severity

| Pattern | Action |
|---|---|
| Cough-variant asthma confirmed (ICS-responsive) | §6.2 |
| PBB (wet cough, antibiotic-responsive) | §6.4 |
| PBB recurrent (> 3 episodes/year) | §7.3 for bronchiectasis workup |
| Chronic cough unresponsive to ICS trial + PBB trial | §7.3 |

### 5.6 Boundary rules

- A child presenting with first-ever wheeze and no prior atopic history: treat acute episode; do NOT label as asthma on first presentation. Schedule B1-14 follow-up at 4–6 weeks for diagnostic confirmation.
- Borderline control (ACT 19–20): review technique and adherence before step-up.
- A child on GINA Step 4 whose control remains poor after 3 months with confirmed good technique + adherence → §7.3, not further escalation within B1-14.
- Severity is assessed on initial presentation; control is assessed at every follow-up. They are different dimensions — do not conflate.
- FEV1 < 80% predicted → communicate to B1-06 (blocks SLIT and OFC).
- Notify B1-06 when a previously severe-asthma child achieves well-controlled status on current step → SLIT candidacy can be reassessed.

## §6 Therapeutic options

### 6.1 Light tier (Intermittent / Mild persistent — GINA Steps 1–2)

**Step 1 (Intermittent):**
- SABA (salbutamol 100 mcg/puff via pMDI + spacer, 2–4 puffs PRN) for symptom relief.
- GINA 2024 preferred: as-needed low-dose ICS-formoterol (≥ 12 years where available) OR as-needed ICS taken whenever SABA is taken (ICS-reliever approach for all ages).
- If SABA-only approach used: maximum SABA use without escalation = ≤ 2 days/week.

**Step 2 (Mild persistent):**
- Low-dose ICS daily controller:
  - Beclometasone 100–200 mcg/day via pMDI + spacer (or equivalent).
  - Budesonide 200–400 mcg/day via nebuliser for < 5 years if pMDI not feasible.
  - Fluticasone propionate 50–100 mcg/day via pMDI + spacer.
- SABA PRN for relief.
- Alternative: montelukast 4 mg (2–5 years) / 5 mg (6–14 years) / 10 mg (≥ 15 years) daily — less effective than ICS; acceptable if family refuses ICS or ICS not tolerated.

**Spacer technique training (mandatory at initiation):**
1. Shake pMDI.
2. Attach to spacer (with mask for < 4 years; mouthpiece for ≥ 4 years).
3. Actuate one puff.
4. Breathe tidally through spacer × 5–6 breaths (mask) OR single slow deep breath + hold 10 seconds (mouthpiece).
5. Wait 30 seconds; repeat for second puff if prescribed.
6. Rinse mouth after ICS.

**Dispensary stock:**
- Salbutamol 100 mcg pMDI.
- Beclometasone 50 mcg / 100 mcg pMDI.
- Fluticasone propionate 50 mcg pMDI.
- Spacer devices (with mask: infant, small child; mouthpiece: ≥ 4 years).
- Peak-flow meters (paediatric and adult scales).
- Montelukast 4 mg / 5 mg / 10 mg chewable tablets.

### 6.2 Standard tier (Moderate persistent — GINA Step 3)

**Step 3:**
- **Children 6–11 years:** Low-dose ICS + LABA (e.g., fluticasone/salmeterol 50/25 mcg 1 puff BD via pMDI + spacer) OR medium-dose ICS (beclometasone 200–400 mcg/day).
- **Adolescents ≥ 12 years:** Low-dose ICS-formoterol as MART (maintenance and reliever therapy) — budesonide/formoterol 200/6 mcg 1 puff BD + PRN for relief [GINA-2024]. Where ICS-formoterol not available: low-dose ICS + LABA with separate SABA for relief.
- **Children < 6 years:** Medium-dose ICS (budesonide 400–800 mcg/day nebulised or beclometasone 200–400 mcg/day pMDI + spacer). Consider add-on montelukast.
- Add: allergen-avoidance measures (cross with B1-06 trigger workup), exercise premedication if exercise-induced bronchoconstriction (SABA 15 min before exercise).
- CARAT scale for rhinitis-asthma overlap tracking (shared with B1-06).

### 6.3 Intensive tier (Severe persistent — GINA Step 4)

**Step 4:**
- **Children 6–11 years:** Medium-dose ICS + LABA. Add tiotropium (Respimat 2.5 mcg 2 puffs OD) if still uncontrolled [GINA-2024].
- **Adolescents ≥ 12 years:** Medium/high-dose ICS-formoterol MART. Add tiotropium if still uncontrolled.
- **Children < 6 years:** Refer to specialist if medium-dose ICS + montelukast inadequate → §7.3.
- Review and optimise: technique (re-check at every visit), adherence (refill counts), trigger avoidance (B1-06 allergen assessment), comorbidities (rhinitis, GORD, obesity, anxiety).

**If uncontrolled on optimised Step 4 after 3 months → §7.3 to pediatric pulmonology subspecialist for Step 5 assessment (high-dose ICS, biologic candidacy, phenotyping).**

### 6.4 Protracted bacterial bronchitis

- Amoxicillin-clavulanate 25 mg/kg/dose PO BD × 14 days (extend to 4 weeks if incomplete response at 14 days).
- If confirmed PBB with > 3 recurrences/year → §7.3 for bronchiectasis workup (HRCT chest).
- Antimicrobial stewardship: do not extend beyond 4 weeks without specialist input.

### 6.5 Contraindications and precautions

| Therapeutic | Contraindication / Precaution | Action |
|---|---|---|
| ICS | Oral thrush (prevention: mouth rinsing) | Continue ICS; treat thrush with oral nystatin |
| ICS (high-dose) | Growth velocity reduction (monitor height) | Use minimum effective dose; plot height at every visit |
| LABA | Monotherapy (never without ICS) | Always prescribe as combination ICS + LABA |
| Montelukast | Neuropsychiatric effects (mood change, sleep disturbance, suicidal ideation) | Counsel parent; monitor; discontinue if symptoms emerge; notify B1-08 if behavioural concern [GINA-2024] |
| Tiotropium | Not approved < 6 years | Do not use in < 6 years |
| Systemic corticosteroids | Frequent courses (> 2/year) | Indicates poor control; escalate step or §7.3 |

### 6.6 Asthma action plan generation

Every child with confirmed asthma receives a written asthma action plan (traffic-light format):

**Green zone (doing well):**
- No symptoms; peak flow ≥ 80% personal best.
- Action: continue daily controller as prescribed.

**Yellow zone (getting worse):**
- Increasing symptoms; peak flow 50–80% personal best; waking at night; needing SABA more often.
- Action: SABA 2–4 puffs via spacer; repeat at 20 min if needed × 3 doses. Double ICS dose OR add prednisolone 1 mg/kg/day (max 40 mg) × 3–5 days if not improving after SABA. Contact SKIDS clinic.

**Red zone (medical alert):**
- Severe symptoms; peak flow < 50% personal best; SABA not helping after 3 doses; difficulty speaking; lips blue.
- Action: SABA 6 puffs via spacer immediately; start prednisolone; call emergency services / proceed to ER.

Action plan is printed from Companion; copy to parent, copy to school (with parent consent), copy retained in PHR.

### 6.7 Acute exacerbation management in clinic

**Mild-moderate exacerbation (SpO₂ ≥ 92%):**
1. Salbutamol 4–6 puffs via pMDI + spacer (or 2.5–5 mg nebulised). Repeat every 20 min × 3 doses.
2. If partial response after 3 doses: add ipratropium bromide 4–8 puffs (or 250–500 mcg nebulised).
3. Prednisolone 1–2 mg/kg PO (max 40 mg) as single dose.
4. Monitor SpO₂, RR, clinical response for 1–2 hours.
5. If good response (SpO₂ ≥ 95%, symptom resolution, sustained ≥ 1 hour): discharge with prednisolone × 3–5 days + step-up review at 48–72h.
6. If partial/poor response: §7.1 ER transfer.

**Severe exacerbation (SpO₂ < 92%) or life-threatening features → §7.1 immediately.**

### 6.8 Tapering / stepping down

- Step down only when asthma well-controlled for ≥ 3 months.
- Reduce ICS dose by 25–50% per step; maintain for 3 months before further step-down.
- Step 3 → Step 2: discontinue LABA; continue low-dose ICS.
- Step 2 → Step 1: consider ICS-reliever approach (PRN low-dose ICS with SABA) OR seasonal ICS if seasonal pattern.
- Do NOT discontinue ICS entirely if ever had severe exacerbation requiring OCS or ER.
- If symptoms recur after step-down: step back up immediately; reassess after 3 months.

## §7 Hard referral triggers [SACRED SECTION]

### 7.1 Emergency referral (same day)

- [ ] **Severe acute asthma exacerbation** — SpO₂ < 92% on room air, speaking in words only, accessory-muscle use, agitation. → Nebulised SABA + ipratropium + systemic steroid in clinic + ER transfer.
- [ ] **Life-threatening asthma** — drowsy/confused, silent chest, cyanosis, SpO₂ < 90%, poor respiratory effort. → ER immediately; call emergency services.
- [ ] **Suspected foreign-body aspiration** — acute-onset unilateral wheeze, choking history. → ER for rigid bronchoscopy.
- [ ] **Anaphylaxis with bronchospasm** (co-morbid food allergy per B1-06) — adrenaline IM immediately per B1-06 §6.6 + ER.
- [ ] **Acute pneumothorax / pneumomediastinum** (sudden pleuritic chest pain, subcutaneous emphysema, haemodynamic compromise). → ER.

### 7.2 Urgent referral (within 7 days)

- [ ] **Post-exacerbation with persistent hypoxia** — SpO₂ 92–94% at 4h post-treatment despite in-clinic management. → Pediatric pulmonology or hospital admission.
- [ ] **Frequent severe exacerbations** — ≥ 2 exacerbations requiring OCS or ER in past 6 months despite Step 3–4 therapy. → Pediatric pulmonology subspecialist.
- [ ] **Suspected TB during chronic cough workup** — chronic cough + fever + weight loss + positive Mantoux/IGRA or CXR findings. → NTEP DOTS centre per A1 §7.3 [NTEP-2020].

### 7.3 Routine referral (within 4 weeks)

- [ ] **GINA Step 5 candidate** — uncontrolled asthma on optimised Step 4 (confirmed technique + adherence + comorbidity management) after 3 months. → Pediatric pulmonology subspecialist for phenotyping + biologic assessment.
- [ ] **Clubbing** → NOT asthma. → Pediatric pulmonology for bronchiectasis / CF / structural workup.
- [ ] **Recurrent PBB (> 3 episodes/year)** → Pediatric pulmonology for HRCT + bronchiectasis evaluation.
- [ ] **Chronic cough unresponsive to ICS trial + PBB trial** after 8 weeks → Pediatric pulmonology for further workup.
- [ ] **Suspected structural airway disease** (stridor, fixed monophonic wheeze, abnormal CXR). → Pediatric pulmonology.
- [ ] **Nasal polyps in child / adolescent** — atypical; consider CF or aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease. → Pediatric pulmonology + ENT.
- [ ] **Persistent FEV1 < 60% predicted despite optimised therapy** → Pediatric pulmonology.
- [ ] **Diagnostic uncertainty** — features atypical for asthma (productive cough, haemoptysis, digital clubbing, failure to thrive, recurrent pneumonia). → Pediatric pulmonology.

### 7.4 Handoff communication

Standard packet:
1. Spirometry results (all pre/post-bronchodilator records).
2. Peak-flow diary (if maintained).
3. Current GINA step and medications with doses.
4. Exacerbation history (number, severity, ER visits, OCS courses).
5. GINA control score / ACT / C-ACT trajectory.
6. Trigger profile (B1-06 allergy workup results if available).
7. Asthma action plan (current version).
8. Companion-generated referral summary.

## §8 Follow-up schedule

| Scenario | First follow-up | Subsequent |
|---|---|---|
| New asthma diagnosis (any step) | 4–6 weeks | Every 3 months until well-controlled × 2 consecutive visits; then 6-monthly |
| Step-up | 4–6 weeks | Every 3 months |
| Post-exacerbation | 48–72h (phone or in-person) | 2 weeks (in-person); then resume schedule |
| Step-down trial | 3 months | 3 months; step back up if symptoms recur |
| Episodic viral wheeze (< 5 years) | 3 months | 6-monthly; reassess diagnosis at age 5 |
| PBB treatment | End of antibiotic course (14–28 days) | 3 months; §7.3 if recurrent |
| Chronic cough workup | 4 weeks (after ICS trial / PBB trial) | Per diagnosis |

§8.2 What to recheck: spacer technique (every visit); GINA control assessment (every visit); peak-flow diary review; spirometry (6-monthly if available, or at step-up/step-down decisions); medication adherence (refill counts); trigger exposure review; growth (height plotted); B1-06 asthma-status communication.

§8.3 Success: well-controlled asthma (ACT ≥ 20 or GINA 0/4) for ≥ 3 months; no exacerbations requiring OCS or ER; normal activity participation; normal sleep; FEV1 ≥ 80% predicted; reliever use ≤ 2 days/week; stable or improving peak-flow.

§8.7 Loss-to-follow-up: Companion alert to manager + parent if B1-14 follow-up missed. Asthma children are high-priority re-engagement because interrupted controller therapy increases exacerbation risk.

## §10 Parent-facing communication

### 10.1 Scripted explanations

New asthma diagnosis:

> "[Aanya]'s airways are sensitive — they narrow when they are irritated by triggers like colds, dust, or exercise. That is what causes the cough and wheeze. Asthma is very common in children and very manageable. We are going to give [her] two types of medicine: a daily preventer inhaler that keeps the airways calm, and a reliever inhaler for when symptoms flare. The preventer is the important one — it works by preventing attacks, not just treating them. Most children with asthma live completely normal lives, play sports, and outgrow the worst of it."

Spacer technique:

> "The inhaler works much better with this spacer device — it gets more medicine into the lungs and less into the mouth. Let me show you how to use it. [Demonstrate.] Now you try. [Observe and correct.] We will check this at every visit because technique matters more than the dose."

Steroid concerns:

> "The preventer inhaler contains a very small dose of steroid that goes directly into the airways. It is not the same as steroid tablets or injections. The dose is measured in micrograms — thousandths of a milligram. At this dose, the medicine stays in the airways and does not affect [her] growth, bones, or immune system. The risk of not using the preventer — more asthma attacks, more ER visits, more missed school — is much greater than the risk of the inhaler."

### 10.2 Subscription tier explanation

> "Your Basic plan covers the initial assessment. The Advanced plan includes regular follow-ups, a peak-flow meter and spacer kit, and your asthma action plan. The Premium plan adds spirometry testing at each visit and coordinates with the allergy clinic to identify and manage [his] specific triggers."

### 10.5 Cultural sensitivities

- *"Steroids are bad."* Address directly: the inhaled dose is micrograms, not milligrams; it stays in the airways; it is not the same as systemic steroids used in bodybuilding or cricket. Quantify the risk-benefit.
- *Nebuliser preference over pMDI + spacer.* Some Indian families associate nebulisers with "stronger" treatment. In fact, pMDI + spacer delivers drug more efficiently with fewer systemic side effects. Counsel; demonstrate; allow time for adoption. If family insists on nebuliser for young child unable to use spacer, prescribe nebulised equivalent.
- *Inhaler stigma at school.* Some children refuse to use inhalers at school due to peer stigma. Pre-exercise SABA can be taken at home before school if exercise is predictable. Discuss with school nurse (where available) for discreet administration.
- *Alternative-medicine preferences.* Acknowledge; do not dismiss. Ask the family to continue the evidence-based controller while trying complementary approaches. The controller is non-negotiable for persistent asthma; the complementary approach is the family's choice.
- *Cost concern.* Generic beclometasone pMDI is the least expensive ICS option in India (₹80–150 per canister). Spacer devices cost ₹200–500. The cost of an ER visit for an uncontrolled exacerbation far exceeds the annual cost of controller therapy — quantify this for the family.

## §11 Manager handoff

§11.1 Manager Console: asthma control status (well-controlled / partly / uncontrolled); current GINA step; exacerbation count; action-plan status; cross-care with B1-06 (trigger management status); subscription tier and coverage.

§11.2 Tiering: first assessment at A1 routing = Basic; regular follow-up + spacer kit + action plan = Advanced; spirometry + B1-06 cross-care = Premium. Multi-protocol cross-care (B1-14 + B1-06) automatically triggers Premium recommendation.

§11.3 Top parent objections:

| Objection | Manager response |
|---|---|
| "It's just a cold — he doesn't have asthma." | "Many children wheeze with colds. The doctor's assessment tells us whether it is a pattern that needs a preventer. The preventer stops the colds from triggering bad episodes." |
| "Inhalers are addictive / for life." | "Inhalers are not addictive. Many children step down or stop as they grow. The preventer is used to keep the airways calm — like brushing teeth prevents cavities. The doctor reviews every 3 months whether [he] still needs it." |
| "We can't afford the inhaler and spacer every month." | "The preventer canister lasts 1–2 months and costs about ₹100–150. The spacer lasts years. The Advanced plan includes the spacer kit. An ER visit for an asthma attack costs much more." |
| "Can't he just use the reliever when he needs it?" | "Reliever-only treatment puts [him] at higher risk of bad attacks. The preventer stops attacks from happening. The doctor has tailored this plan to [his] severity." |
| "We want to try homoeopathy / ayurveda first." | "The doctor respects your choices. We ask that you continue the preventer alongside any complementary treatment so [he] is protected while you explore other options." |

§11.4–§11.6: standard manager touchpoints. Asthma children get seasonal pre-engagement (e.g., before winter/monsoon) to reinforce controller adherence during high-trigger periods.

## §12 Quality and audit

§12.1 Metrics:
- ≥ 80% of B1-14 children achieve well-controlled asthma (ACT ≥ 20 / C-ACT ≥ 20 / GINA 0/4) within 3 months of initiating or stepping-up therapy.
- ≥ 90% of children with confirmed asthma have a current written asthma action plan (updated within past 12 months).
- Spacer-technique satisfactory rate ≥ 85% at follow-up visits.
- Exacerbation rate requiring OCS or ER ≤ 1 per child per year (target).
- SABA over-reliance: < 10% of B1-14 children using > 3 SABA canisters/year.
- ≥ 95% of §7 triggers result in documented referral with complete handoff packet.
- B1-06 asthma-status communication: 100% of children on both B1-14 + B1-06 have `asthma_status` updated at every B1-14 visit.
- Loss-to-follow-up < 15%.

§12.2 Quarterly: protocol owner reviews 30 B1-14 visits (control assessment completeness, step appropriateness, technique re-check documentation), all §7 referrals, all exacerbation events, and B1-06 cross-care communication logs.

§12.3 Certification: every SKIDS pediatrician delivering B1-14 must complete GINA-aligned asthma management training (8 hours); spirometry interpretation training (4 hours); spacer-technique assessment (pass/fail practical); annual update (2 hours covering GINA updates and new inhaler devices).

§12.4 Fellowship: SKIDS Pediatric Pulmonology / Allergy Fellowship pathway. Protocol governance for B1-06 + B1-14 across the SKIDS network is shared by the named pediatric pulmonologist with allergy expertise — the same governance owner for both protocols.
