# SKIDS Bedwetting Clinic

> The SKIDS Bedwetting Clinic delivers structured assessment and
> management of nocturnal enuresis (primary and secondary) in
> children ≥ 5 years, using enuresis alarms as first-line, desmopressin
> for situational/refractory cases, bladder diary analysis, and
> exclusion of urological / neurological causes. Daytime incontinence
> and secondary enuresis with alarm features route per §7.

---

## §1 Service definition

**What this clinic does:**
- Classifies enuresis: primary monosymptomatic nocturnal enuresis (PMNE — never dry ≥ 6 consecutive months, no daytime symptoms), non-monosymptomatic (with daytime symptoms), and secondary enuresis (previously dry ≥ 6 months, now wetting) [ICCS-2014].
- Excludes organic causes: UTI (urine dipstick + culture), diabetes mellitus (glucosuria), diabetes insipidus (polyuria workup), constipation (cross B1-21 — full rectum compresses bladder), OSA (cross B1-07), neurological (spinal dysraphism signs), and renal anomaly (ultrasound if indicated).
- Delivers first-line enuresis alarm therapy with structured family training (12–16 week programme; success defined as 14 consecutive dry nights) [NICE-Enuresis-2010].
- Prescribes desmopressin (DDAVP) for situational use (sleepovers, camps) and as second-line for alarm-refractory PMNE (desmopressin melt 120–240 mcg sublingual at bedtime) [ICCS-2014].
- Manages constipation-associated enuresis with B1-21 cross-care (treat constipation first; enuresis often resolves).
- Screens for psychosocial impact: self-esteem, social avoidance, bullying, family stress (cross B1-08 if significant).
- Counsels families on developmental trajectory: PMNE resolves spontaneously at ~15%/year; treatment accelerates resolution.

**What this clinic does not do:**
- Manage daytime urinary incontinence as the primary concern (→ B1-17 Kidney & Urology for urodynamic workup).
- Manage secondary enuresis with alarm features (haematuria, dysuria, new-onset polyuria/polydipsia, neurological signs) beyond initial assessment (→ §7.3).
- Prescribe imipramine (tricyclic — cardiotoxicity risk; not first or second line at SKIDS).
- Manage neurogenic bladder (→ §7.3 pediatric urology/neurology).

**Age range covered:** 5 – 18 years (enuresis diagnosed ≥ 5 years per ICCS; A1 routes at ≥ 7 years for nocturnal enuresis).

**SKIDS subscription tier(s):**
- **Basic ₹2,999** — A1 elimination screening; first B1-20 assessment on per-visit pricing.
- **Advanced ₹6,999** — enuresis alarm programme (device loaner included); up to 6 B1-20 visits; bladder diary monitoring.
- **Premium ₹12,999** — unlimited visits; desmopressin management; multi-protocol cross-care (B1-20 + B1-21 + B1-07 + B1-08).

**SKIDS clinical tier:** Tier 1. Protocol governance with named pediatric nephrologist or DBP. §7 escalates to B1-17 Kidney & Urology (urodynamics, structural), B2-02 Neurology (spinal/neurological), or B2-01 Endocrine (DI, DM).

## §4 Diagnostic workflow

### 4.1 History
1. Wetting pattern: frequency (nights/week), volume (large/small), timing (early/late night), ever dry ≥ 6 months?
2. Daytime symptoms: urgency, frequency, incontinence, holding postures, dribbling.
3. Fluid intake: amount, timing (evening intake), caffeine.
4. Bowel pattern: constipation screen (Bristol stool scale; cross B1-21).
5. Sleep: depth of sleep, snoring (cross B1-07 for OSA), difficulty waking.
6. Family history: enuresis in parents/siblings (strong genetic component — 77% if both parents, 44% if one parent).
7. Psychosocial: impact on self-esteem, social avoidance, family response (punitive?), bullying, secondary enuresis triggers (new sibling, school change, trauma).
8. Prior treatment: alarms tried? desmopressin tried? dose, duration, response.
9. Red flags: new-onset polydipsia/polyuria (DM, DI), dysuria/haematuria (UTI, structural), neurological symptoms (gait, bowel incontinence, back pain).

### 4.2 Examination
- General: growth, BP.
- Abdomen: palpable faecal mass (constipation), palpable bladder (retention).
- Lumbosacral: sacral dimple, hair tuft, lipoma (spinal dysraphism → §7.3).
- Lower limb neurological: tone, reflexes, gait, sensation (screen for neurogenic bladder).
- Genital: meatal position (boys), labial adhesion (girls — urinary trapping).
- Perianal: anal wink (intact = S2-4 intact).

### 4.3 Investigations
- **Urine dipstick** (all): glucose (DM), protein, blood, leucocytes, nitrites (UTI), specific gravity (concentrating ability).
- **Urine culture** if dipstick positive or recurrent UTI history.
- **Bladder diary** (2 weeks): fluid intake, voiding times, voided volumes, wet/dry nights.
- **Renal ultrasound**: if secondary enuresis with alarm features, recurrent UTI, daytime symptoms, or abnormal examination.
- **Further investigations** (not at B1-20; at §7.3): urodynamics (B1-17), spinal MRI (B2-02), water-deprivation test (B2-01).

### 4.4 Decision tree

```
Child ≥ 5 years with bedwetting
        │
        ├── Red flags (polyuria/polydipsia, neuro signs, haematuria) → §7.3
        ├── Daytime incontinence primary → B1-17
        ├── Constipation identified → B1-21 first; return if enuresis persists
        │
        ├── PMNE confirmed → §5.1 → §6 alarm / desmopressin
        ├── Secondary enuresis, no alarm features → §5.2 → §6 + psychosocial support
        ├── Non-monosymptomatic → B1-17 cross + B1-20 co-manage
        │
        ▼
§6 management → follow-up → §7 if refractory
```

## §5 Severity grading [SACRED SECTION]

### 5.1 PMNE severity

| Severity | Frequency | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | 1–2 nights/week | §6.1 — education + motivational support; alarm if family ready |
| **Moderate** | 3–5 nights/week | §6.2 — alarm therapy programme |
| **Severe** | 6–7 nights/week | §6.2 — alarm therapy; §6.3 if alarm fails after 12 weeks |

### 5.2 Secondary enuresis

| Pattern | Action |
|---|---|
| Recent onset, identifiable psychosocial trigger, no alarm features | §6.1 + B1-08 cross if needed |
| Recent onset, alarm features (polyuria, haematuria, neuro signs) | §7.3 |

### 5.3 Boundary rules
- Age < 5: not diagnosed as enuresis; developmental normal. Reassure.
- Age 5–6: counselling + reassurance; active treatment only if family requests and psychosocial impact significant.
- Age ≥ 7: A1 flags; active treatment indicated.
- Constipation must be treated first (B1-21 cross-care) before enuresis-specific intervention.
- Daytime incontinence = B1-17, not B1-20.

## §6 Therapeutic options

### 6.1 Light tier (education + motivational support)
- Normalise: explain prevalence (5–10% at age 7; 1–2% at age 15), genetic component, spontaneous resolution rate.
- Fluid management: adequate daytime intake; avoid large volumes 2h before bed; avoid caffeine.
- Bladder diary: track wet/dry nights; reward dry nights (sticker chart; NOT punishment for wet nights).
- Lifting/waking: parent wakes child to void at parent's bedtime — provides some dry nights but does not cure; bridge to alarm.

### 6.2 Standard tier (alarm therapy programme)

**Enuresis alarm protocol (12–16 weeks):**
1. SKIDS loans bedwetting alarm device (pad-and-bell or body-worn sensor).
2. Parent training session: how alarm works (sensor detects moisture → alarm sounds → child wakes, stops stream, goes to toilet, changes, resets).
3. Early weeks: parent must wake child if alarm doesn't wake them; child participates in cleanup (non-punitive).
4. Target: 14 consecutive dry nights = success; then withdraw alarm.
5. Overlearning phase (after 14 dry nights): child drinks extra fluid before bed for 2 weeks while using alarm — reduces relapse.
6. If no improvement after 4 weeks of correct alarm use → reassess (technique? constipation? daytime symptoms missed?).
7. If partial response at 12 weeks → extend to 16 weeks.
8. If no response at 16 weeks → §6.3.

**Success rate:** ~65–70% with correct use. Relapse rate ~30%; re-treat with alarm if relapse.

### 6.3 Intensive tier (desmopressin / refractory)

**Desmopressin (second-line after alarm failure, or situational):**
- Desmopressin melt (lyophilisate) 120 mcg sublingual at bedtime; increase to 240 mcg if no response after 2 weeks.
- OR desmopressin tablet 200 mcg at bedtime; increase to 400 mcg.
- Fluid restriction: ≤ 240 mL from 1h before desmopressin until 8h after (water-intoxication prevention).
- Trial × 4 weeks: if dry → continue 3 months → structured withdrawal (1 week on, 1 week off).
- If relapse on withdrawal → restart × 3 months → re-attempt withdrawal.
- **Situational use:** single doses for sleepovers, camps, travel (no need for 3-month course; use as needed).

**Combination alarm + desmopressin:** for severe refractory cases; use both simultaneously × 12 weeks.

**If refractory to alarm + desmopressin → §7.3 for urodynamic evaluation at B1-17.**

### 6.4 Contraindications

| Therapeutic | Contraindication | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Desmopressin | Habitual polydipsia; hyponatraemia risk; renal disease | Do not prescribe; alarm only |
| Desmopressin | Concurrent NSAID / carbamazepine (hyponatraemia risk) | Avoid combination |
| Imipramine | Any (SKIDS policy) | Not prescribed at Tier 1 |

## §7 Hard referral triggers [SACRED SECTION]

### 7.1 Emergency referral (same day)
- [ ] **Hyponatraemic seizure on desmopressin** — confusion, seizure, headache with desmopressin use. → ER immediately; stop desmopressin; IV normal saline.

### 7.2 Urgent referral (within 7 days)
- [ ] **New-onset polyuria/polydipsia with enuresis** → B2-01 Endocrine (DM/DI workup).
- [ ] **Haematuria + dysuria + enuresis** → B1-17 Kidney (UTI/structural workup).

### 7.3 Routine referral (within 4 weeks)
- [ ] **Non-monosymptomatic enuresis with daytime symptoms not responding to B1-21 constipation management** → B1-17 Kidney for urodynamics.
- [ ] **Refractory enuresis (alarm + desmopressin failed after adequate trials)** → B1-17 Kidney / pediatric urology.
- [ ] **Sacral/spinal abnormality on examination** → B2-02 Neurology for spinal MRI.
- [ ] **Secondary enuresis with suspected abuse / trauma** → B1-08 / child-protection pathway per B1-13.

### 7.4 Handoff communication
Standard packet: bladder diary, treatment history (alarm weeks, desmopressin dose/response), urine results, ultrasound (if done), examination findings.

## §10 Parent-facing communication

### 10.1 Scripted explanations

> "[Arjun]'s bedwetting is very common — about 1 in 10 children his age still wets the bed. It is not his fault, not laziness, and not a behaviour problem. His bladder and his brain's wake-up signal are still maturing. We can speed up that maturation with a bedwetting alarm — a device that trains his brain to wake up when his bladder is full. Most children become dry within 3–4 months."

### 10.5 Cultural sensitivities
- *"He'll grow out of it."* True for most — 15%/year spontaneous resolution. But treatment prevents years of psychosocial impact and is more effective than waiting.
- *Punishment for bedwetting.* Common in Indian households. Counsel explicitly: punishment makes bedwetting worse, not better. The child cannot control it.
- *Stigma / shame.* Frame as a medical condition, not a character flaw. Use the word "bedwetting" plainly; do not euphemise.
- *Sharing beds / sleeping arrangements.* Many Indian children share beds; alarm use may wake siblings. Problem-solve: body-worn sensor rather than pad; sibling-management strategies.
