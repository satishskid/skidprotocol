# SKIDS Developmental Surveillance

> The SKIDS Developmental Surveillance protocol delivers structured
> developmental monitoring using ASQ-3, Trivandrum DST, and CDC
> milestones at every A1 periodic visit, formal developmental
> assessment when A1 flags trigger, autism screening coordination
> with B1-08 (M-CHAT-R/F), early-intervention programme initiation,
> and routing for formal diagnostic assessment (ADOS, psychometric
> testing) to DBP / pediatric neurology. Developmental regression
> is a §7.2 emergency bridge to neurology.

---

## §1 Service definition

**What this clinic does:**
- Receives children flagged at A1 with developmental Orange flags (ASQ-3 below cutoff in any domain, Trivandrum DST ≥ 2 delay items or any warning sign) for structured developmental assessment within 2 weeks [CDC-Milestones-2022, AAP-BF-4E].
- Performs detailed developmental assessment across the five domains: gross motor, fine motor, communication/language, problem-solving/cognitive, personal-social/adaptive.
- Administers domain-specific developmental tools beyond A1 screening: Bayley Scales interpretation (when performed at partner centre), DASII (Developmental Assessment Scales for Indian Infants), VSMS (Vineland Social Maturity Scale), and Denver II where indicated.
- Coordinates autism screening with B1-08: M-CHAT-R/F is administered at A1 (16–30 months); positive M-CHAT-R/F routes to B1-08 for behavioural assessment AND to B1-23 for developmental-delay assessment if concurrent developmental delay features exist. B1-23 does NOT perform formal autism diagnosis — that routes to §7.3 (DBP or pediatric psychiatry for ADOS/clinical diagnosis).
- Initiates early-intervention referrals: speech-language therapy (B1-11), occupational therapy, physiotherapy, special education, and sensory-integration therapy through named early-intervention partners.
- Monitors developmental trajectory in children with established diagnoses (global developmental delay, Down syndrome, cerebral palsy) who are receiving early-intervention services — B1-23 provides the SKIDS-side longitudinal monitoring and coordinates with the therapy team.
- Assesses developmental impact of chronic medical conditions: hearing loss (B1-02 cross), vision impairment (B1-01 cross), chronic illness, prematurity, NICU graduates.
- Bridges developmental regression to neurology: A1 §7.2 fires for milestone regression; B1-23 provides same-week assessment and documentation while the child awaits the named pediatric neurologist / B2-02.
- Counsels families on developmental expectations, realistic goal-setting, early-intervention importance, and navigates the Indian early-intervention service landscape.

**What this clinic does not do:**
- Perform formal autism diagnostic assessment (ADOS-2, ADI-R) — §7.3 to DBP or pediatric psychiatrist.
- Perform formal psychometric / IQ testing — §7.3 to clinical psychologist.
- Diagnose intellectual disability (requires formal psychometric testing at §7.3).
- Manage cerebral palsy rehabilitation directly — B1-23 monitors; physiotherapy/OT team delivers.
- Manage genetic syndromes beyond developmental monitoring — genetic diagnosis and management at §7.3 clinical genetics.
- Manage epilepsy or neurodegenerative disease — §7.2/§7.3 to pediatric neurology / B2-02.
- Prescribe developmental pharmacotherapy (e.g., no nootropics, no unproven supplements for developmental delay).

**Age range covered:** 0 – 6 years (primary developmental-surveillance window); monitoring continues to 18 years for children with established developmental diagnoses.

**SKIDS subscription tier(s):**
- **Basic ₹2,999** — A1 developmental screening at periodic visits (ASQ-3, Trivandrum DST); first B1-23 visit on per-visit pricing.
- **Advanced ₹6,999** — structured developmental assessment; early-intervention coordination; up to 4 B1-23 visits per year.
- **Premium ₹12,999** — unlimited B1-23 visits; multi-protocol cross-care (B1-23 + B1-08 + B1-11 + B1-02); priority early-intervention partner referral; longitudinal monitoring for established diagnoses.

**SKIDS clinical tier:** Tier 1 with allied-staff dependency on the SKIDS developmental therapist / early-intervention coordinator (may share role with B1-08 counsellor). Protocol governance with named DBP (may share with B1-08 governance). Complex tier per §7 escalates to DBP for formal diagnosis, pediatric neurology / B2-02 for regression and neurological conditions, clinical psychologist for psychometric testing, and clinical genetics for syndromic evaluation.

**Departures from cited guidelines:**

1. *ASQ-3 + Trivandrum DST dual-track.* AAP recommends ASQ-3 as the primary screening tool. SKIDS uses both ASQ-3 and Trivandrum DST because the Trivandrum DST is validated for Indian populations and captures some Indian-context developmental variations that ASQ-3 may miss. The more concerning result from either tool drives the flag tier.

2. *Formal autism diagnosis out of B1-23 scope.* AAP 2020 recommends that primary-care clinicians can make a clinical autism diagnosis in clear cases. SKIDS routes all formal autism diagnoses to §7.3 (DBP or pediatric psychiatrist with ADOS-2) to ensure diagnostic quality and avoid premature labelling. B1-23 provides the developmental-delay assessment that often co-occurs with autism but does not make the autism diagnosis itself.

3. *DASII over Bayley in Indian infants.* Where formal developmental assessment is needed for Indian infants < 30 months, SKIDS prefers DASII (culturally adapted) over Bayley (which requires trained administration and Western norms). Bayley results from external centres are accepted and interpreted.

| "She used to talk but has stopped." | Developmental regression — §7.2 bridge. | §4 + §7.2 neurology |
| "He's not doing what other kids his age do." | Parental concern about developmental pace. | §4 |
| "The crèche says she's behind." | External-observer concern. | §4 |

| "NICU graduate — developmental follow-up." | NICU graduate surveillance. | §4 high-risk monitoring |
| "She has cerebral palsy — how is she progressing?" | CP longitudinal monitoring. | §4 + therapy-team coordination |
| "He doesn't point or share toys (18m)." | Autism-related developmental concern. | §4 + B1-08 M-CHAT-R/F coordination |

**Routing decisions:**
- A1 Orange developmental flag (ASQ-3 below cutoff OR Trivandrum DST ≥ 2 delay items) → B1-23 within 2 weeks.
- A1 §7.2 developmental regression (loss of milestones) → B1-23 same-week bridge + named pediatric neurologist / B2-02 within 7 days.
- M-CHAT-R/F positive at A1 → B1-08 for behavioural assessment; B1-23 co-assessment if concurrent developmental delay features.
- Speech-language delay isolated → B1-11 Speech & Language; if broader developmental concern → B1-23 first, then B1-11 cross-care.
- School-age learning concern without developmental delay → B1-12 Learning; if developmental underpinning suspected → B1-23 assessment first.

## §4 Diagnostic workflow

### 4.1 Developmental history

1. **Pregnancy and birth history.** Gestational age; birth weight; NICU stay; complications (HIE, IVH, NEC); maternal infections (TORCH); substance exposure.
2. **Milestone history.** Elicit each milestone with specific age achieved:
   - Gross motor: head control, sitting unsupported, pulling to stand, walking independently, running, climbing stairs.
   - Fine motor: reaching, palmar grasp, pincer grasp, tower building, pencil grasp, drawing.
   - Language: cooing, babbling, first words, two-word phrases, sentences, follows commands.
   - Social: social smile, stranger anxiety, joint attention, pointing, pretend play, peer interaction.
   - Adaptive: self-feeding, dressing, toileting.
3. **Developmental trajectory.** Steady progress (even if delayed) vs. plateau vs. regression. Regression = §7.2.
4. **Family history.** Developmental delay, intellectual disability, autism, genetic syndromes, consanguinity.
5. **Environmental factors.** Stimulation environment; caregiver availability; screen time; language exposure (monolingual/multilingual); socioeconomic stressors.
6. **Prior assessments.** Any previous developmental evaluations, therapy history, early-intervention services.

### 4.2 Developmental examination

- **Corrected age for prematurity:** use corrected age (chronological age minus weeks of prematurity) until 24 months for motor milestones, 36 months for cognitive/language milestones [AAP-DevScreen-2020].
- **Domain-by-domain assessment:**

| Domain | Key observations (age-adapted) |
|---|---|
| Gross motor | Head lag (4m); sitting (6–9m); walking (12–18m); running (24m); ball kick (24m); stairs alternating (36m) |
| Fine motor | Reaching (4m); transfer (6m); pincer (9–12m); tower 2 blocks (15m); tower 6 (24m); copies circle (36m) |
| Language receptive | Turns to voice (4m); responds to name (9m); follows 1-step command (12m); points to body parts (18m); follows 2-step (24m) |
| Language expressive | Coos (3m); babbles (6m); "mama/dada" specific (12m); 5+ words (18m); 2-word phrases (24m); sentences (36m) |
| Social-adaptive | Social smile (2m); stranger anxiety (8m); joint attention (12m); pretend play (18m); parallel play (24m); cooperative play (36m) |

- **Red flags requiring immediate action:**
  - Loss of previously attained milestones at any age → §7.2 regression.
  - No social smile by 3 months.
  - No babbling by 12 months.
  - No words by 18 months.
  - No 2-word phrases by 24 months.
  - Any loss of language or social skills at any age.

### 4.3 Formal screening tools at B1-23

| Tool | Age range | Purpose | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ASQ-3** | 1–66 months | Domain-specific developmental screening | Below cutoff in any domain = delay in that domain |
| **Trivandrum DST** | 0–6 years | Indian-validated developmental screening | ≥ 2 delay items or any warning sign = concern |
| **DASII** | 0–30 months | Indian-adapted developmental assessment (motor + mental DQ) | DQ < 70 = significant delay |
| **VSMS** | 0–15 years | Adaptive-behaviour assessment | Social age / SQ; SQ < 70 = significant adaptive delay |
| **Denver II** | 0–6 years | Developmental screening (supplementary) | Caution / delay items per domain |
| **M-CHAT-R/F** | 16–30 months | Autism screening (coordination with B1-08) | Per B1-08 §4.3 |

### 4.4 Hearing and vision screen

Every child assessed at B1-23 must have:
- Hearing screen (B1-02 cross-care): OAE at minimum; audiometry if ≥ 3 years. Hearing loss is a common treatable cause of language delay.
- Vision screen (B1-01 cross-care): age-appropriate. Vision impairment affects developmental assessment validity.

### 4.5 Laboratory (on indication)

| Test | Indication |
|---|---|
| TSH | Global delay (hypothyroidism screen) |
| Lead level | Developmental delay + environmental risk (older housing, contaminated water, industrial area) |
| Karyotype | Dysmorphic features + developmental delay; suspected Down syndrome, Turner, Klinefelter |
| Fragile X (FMR1) | Intellectual disability without other cause, especially in boys |
| Metabolic screen (TMS, urine organic acids) | Developmental regression, consanguinity, unusual odour, hepatosplenomegaly |
| MRI brain | Abnormal neurological examination, regression, microcephaly, seizures |
| CK (creatine kinase) | Motor delay + hypotonia in boys (Duchenne muscular dystrophy screen) |

### 4.6 Decision tree

```
Child presents with developmental concern
        │
        ▼
§4.1 Developmental history + §4.2 Examination
        │
        ├── Developmental regression → §7.2 neurology + B1-23 bridge
        │
        ▼

§4.4 Hearing + vision screen
        │
        ├── Normal development (age-appropriate) → reassure; A1 surveillance
        ├── Isolated language delay → B1-11 Speech + B1-02 hearing cross

        ├── Global developmental delay → §5 classify + §6 early intervention + §7.3

```

## §5 Severity grading [SACRED SECTION]

### 5.1 Developmental delay severity

| Severity | Criteria | Domains affected | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|---|
| **At risk** | ASQ-3 monitoring zone OR Trivandrum DST 1 delay item; parental concern with normal formal assessment | Any | §6.1 Light — enhanced surveillance + anticipatory guidance |
| **Mild delay** | DASII DQ 70–84 OR 1 domain below ASQ-3 cutoff | Isolated (single domain) | §6.2 Standard — domain-specific early intervention |
| **Moderate delay** | DASII DQ 55–69 OR ≥ 2 domains below ASQ-3 cutoff | Multiple (2+ domains) | §6.2 Standard — multi-domain early intervention + §7.3 for formal assessment |
| **Severe / global delay** | DASII DQ < 55 OR all domains below ASQ-3 cutoff OR VSMS SQ < 55 | Global (all domains) | §6.3 Intensive — comprehensive early intervention + §7.3 for formal diagnosis + genetic/metabolic workup |

### 5.2 Autism-concern severity (coordination with B1-08)

| Level | Criteria | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Low concern** | M-CHAT-R/F 0–2; age-appropriate social development | Routine surveillance |
| **Medium concern** | M-CHAT-R/F 3–7 with negative follow-up interview | Enhanced surveillance; repeat at next A1 |
| **High concern** | M-CHAT-R/F 3–7 with positive follow-up OR ≥ 8; OR clinical autism features | B1-08 cross-care + §7.3 for ADOS/formal diagnosis |

### 5.3 Developmental regression

| Pattern | Action |
|---|---|
| Loss of ≥ 1 previously attained milestone | §7.2 urgent → pediatric neurology / B2-02 within 7 days; B1-23 same-week bridge assessment |
| Language regression in 18–24 months (with preserved motor) | §7.2 + autism evaluation via B1-08 (autistic regression pattern common) |
| Motor + cognitive regression | §7.2 → pediatric neurology immediately; consider neurodegenerative disease |

### 5.4 Boundary rules

- Isolated speech-language delay with normal hearing and normal development in other domains → B1-11 Speech & Language, not B1-23.
- Isolated motor delay (e.g., late walker at 18 months with otherwise normal development) → enhanced surveillance at B1-23 × 3 months; physiotherapy if not walking by 18 months; investigate (CK, neurological exam) if not walking by 24 months.
- Developmental delay + dysmorphic features → always investigate (karyotype, genetics referral, metabolic screen).
- B1-23 does NOT make the formal autism diagnosis — it documents the developmental-delay component and coordinates with B1-08 for the autism pathway.
- Parental concern alone (without formal screening abnormality) is sufficient to justify a B1-23 visit — parental concern is a sensitive predictor of developmental problems.
- Developmental delay in a child with known hearing loss → optimise hearing (B1-02) before attributing delay to cognitive/developmental cause.

## §6 Therapeutic options

### 6.1 Light tier (at-risk — enhanced surveillance)

- Schedule more frequent developmental checks: every 3 months instead of A1 cadence.
- Provide anticipatory developmental guidance: specific activities to stimulate the domain of concern (language stimulation techniques, motor-practice activities, interactive play).
- Parent-app developmental-stimulation content activated.
- Re-administer ASQ-3 at 3-month follow-up; if normalises → return to A1 surveillance; if below cutoff → §6.2.

### 6.2 Standard tier (mild-moderate delay — domain-specific early intervention)

**Language delay:**
- B1-11 Speech & Language cross-referral for speech-language therapy.
- Parent-implemented language-stimulation programme (daily reading, narrating activities, reducing screen time, increasing conversational turns).
- If hearing loss identified → B1-02 hearing management as primary intervention.

**Motor delay:**
- Physiotherapy referral through SKIDS partner physiotherapy centre.
- Home-exercise programme prescribed by physiotherapist.
- If CK elevated in boy → §7.3 for Duchenne workup.
- If abnormal neurological examination → §7.3 pediatric neurology.

**Global delay (≥ 2 domains):**
- Multi-domain early intervention: speech therapy + OT + physiotherapy as indicated.
- SKIDS early-intervention coordinator schedules and tracks therapy appointments.
- Parental counselling: realistic expectations, importance of early intervention, therapy compliance.
- §7.3 referral for formal developmental-diagnostic assessment (DASII or Bayley at partner centre; psychometric testing if ≥ 3 years).

**Social-communication delay with autism concern:**
- B1-08 co-management for behavioural-developmental overlap.
- §7.3 referral for formal autism diagnosis (ADOS-2 at partner DBP centre).
- Begin early-intervention services (speech, OT, behavioural therapy) while awaiting formal diagnosis — do NOT wait for diagnosis to start therapy [AAP-DevScreen-2020].

### 6.3 Intensive tier (severe / global delay — comprehensive programme)

- All §6.2 interventions intensified.
- §7.3 for formal diagnostic workup: genetic testing (karyotype, fragile X, microarray), metabolic screen, MRI brain.
- Special-education assessment and placement support.
- Disability certification support (RPWD Act 2016, UDID card) for eligible children.
- Social-work / family-support referral for families needing financial, logistical, or emotional support.
- Longitudinal monitoring at B1-23 every 3 months.

### 6.4 Established-diagnosis monitoring programme

For children with confirmed diagnoses (Down syndrome, CP, ASD, other):
- B1-23 provides 6-monthly developmental monitoring using VSMS and domain-specific assessment.
- Coordinates with therapy team (speech, OT, PT, special education).
- Screens for comorbidities per syndrome-specific surveillance:
  - **Down syndrome:** thyroid (annual TSH), hearing (annual audiometry per B1-02), vision (annual per B1-01), cardiac (echocardiography per B1-16 cross), atlantoaxial instability screening, coeliac screen.
  - **Cerebral palsy:** hip surveillance, nutritional status (B1-09 cross), drooling management, seizure monitoring (B2-02 cross).
  - **ASD:** B1-08 ongoing behavioural management; B1-23 monitors developmental trajectory and therapy response.

### 6.5 Contraindications and precautions

| Intervention | Contraindication | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Unproven developmental therapies (nootropics, stem-cell therapy, hyperbaric oxygen) | Any | Do NOT prescribe or endorse; counsel family on evidence basis |
| Restrictive diets (gluten-free, casein-free for autism) | No evidence in non-coeliac children | Do NOT recommend; if family already using, ensure nutritional adequacy with B1-09 dietitian |
| Chelation therapy for autism | Any | Dangerous; do NOT prescribe; actively counsel against |

## §7 Hard referral triggers [SACRED SECTION]

### 7.1 Emergency referral (same day)

- [ ] **Developmental regression with acute neurological signs** — seizures, altered consciousness, acute motor deterioration. → ER + pediatric neurology.

### 7.2 Urgent referral (within 7 days)

- [ ] **Developmental regression** — loss of previously attained milestones without acute neurological signs. → Pediatric neurology / B2-02 within 7 days; B1-23 same-week bridge assessment.
- [ ] **Language regression at 18–24 months** — loss of words with or without social withdrawal. → Pediatric neurology / B2-02 + B1-08 for autism evaluation within 7 days.
- [ ] **Motor regression in boy with elevated CK** → Pediatric neurology within 7 days (Duchenne suspicion).
- [ ] **Infantile spasms identified** (subtle head-drop, flexion spasms in clusters — often in infant with developmental plateau) → Pediatric neurology / B2-02 immediately (same-day EEG).

### 7.3 Routine referral (within 4 weeks)

- [ ] **Global developmental delay (all domains delayed)** → DBP or clinical psychologist for formal developmental/psychometric assessment.
- [ ] **Suspected autism (M-CHAT-R/F positive + clinical features)** → DBP or pediatric psychiatrist for ADOS-2 / formal autism diagnosis.
- [ ] **Dysmorphic features + developmental delay** → Clinical genetics for karyotype, microarray, syndromic evaluation.
- [ ] **Developmental delay with abnormal neurological examination** (spasticity, hypotonia, asymmetry) → Pediatric neurology / B2-02.
- [ ] **Developmental delay with microcephaly or macrocephaly** → Pediatric neurology for MRI + workup.
- [ ] **Developmental delay with seizures** → Pediatric neurology / B2-02.
- [ ] **Moderate-severe delay not progressing despite 6 months early intervention** → Re-evaluate diagnosis; consider genetics/metabolic referral; reassess therapy programme.
- [ ] **Intellectual disability assessment** (child ≥ 5 years with persistent delay) → Clinical psychologist for formal IQ testing + adaptive-behaviour assessment.
- [ ] **Suspected metabolic disease** (consanguinity, regression, unusual features) → Pediatric metabolic specialist for TMS, organic acids, enzyme assays.

### 7.4 Handoff communication

Standard packet:
1. Developmental assessment results (ASQ-3, DASII/Bayley, VSMS scores with domain breakdown).
2. Milestone history with ages achieved.
3. Hearing and vision screening results.
4. Growth parameters (including head circumference).
5. Family history (consanguinity, developmental conditions, genetic syndromes).
6. Current early-intervention services with response summary.
7. Companion-generated referral summary.

## §8 Follow-up schedule

| Scenario | First follow-up | Subsequent |
|---|---|---|
| At-risk (enhanced surveillance) | 3 months | 3-monthly until normalises or escalates |
| Mild delay (single domain) | 3 months (after therapy initiation) | 3–6-monthly; annual formal reassessment |
| Moderate delay (multi-domain) | 6 weeks (after therapy initiation) | 3-monthly |
| Severe / global delay | Monthly × 3 months | 3-monthly; 6-monthly formal reassessment |
| Established diagnosis monitoring | 6 months | 6-monthly; annual comprehensive |
| Regression (post-neurology referral) | Per neurology; B1-23 bridge at 2 weeks | Per neurology with B1-23 coordination |
| Prematurity developmental follow-up | Corrected-age milestones at 6, 9, 12, 18, 24, 36 months | Per A1 cadence after 36 months if on track |

§8.2 What to recheck: ASQ-3 or domain-specific tool at every visit; therapy attendance and compliance; parental report of functional progress; hearing/vision status; growth (head circumference for < 36 months); comorbidity screening (for syndrome-specific protocols).

§8.3 Success: developmental trajectory improving (DQ increasing or stabilising); therapy goals being met; functional milestones achieved within therapy targets; family engaged and coping.

§8.7 Loss-to-follow-up: critical — developmental delay without early intervention has worse outcomes. Companion alert to manager + parent at 7 days if B1-23 follow-up missed. Manager phone call at 14 days. NICU-graduate and regression-bridge cases are highest priority.

### 9.4 Parent app rendering

Post-visit: developmental-domain status (visual — on track / needs support / receiving therapy); therapy schedule; milestone tracker (interactive — parent can log achievements); parent-stimulation activities (age-appropriate).

### 9.5 Auto-generated outputs

1. Developmental assessment report (domain breakdown, scores, recommendations).
2. Early-intervention referral packet (therapy prescriptions).
3. School/crèche communication letter (developmental status, accommodations needed).
4. Disability-certification support documentation (where applicable).

### 9.6 Edge cases

- Parent in denial about developmental delay: document concern; use strengths-based language; revisit at follow-up; never force a label.
- Child assessed during illness: note that illness may affect performance; reschedule formal assessment when well.
- Multilingual child with language delay: distinguish developmental language delay from sequential bilingualism effects; B1-11 speech assessment in child's dominant language.
- Therapy services not available locally: Companion generates home-programme activities; tele-therapy referral where available; document service-access barrier for advocacy.
- Offline: paper developmental assessment; upload when connectivity restored.

## §10 Parent-facing communication

### 10.1 Scripted explanations

Developmental concern identified:

> "[Aanya] is taking a bit longer than expected to [walk / talk / use her hands for tasks]. This doesn't mean there's something wrong — many children develop at different speeds. But when we notice a gap, the best thing we can do is give [her] extra support early. I'd like to do a detailed assessment and, if needed, start some therapy sessions that help [her] catch up. The earlier we start, the better [she] responds."

Referral for formal assessment:

> "[Arjun]'s developmental assessment shows delays in several areas. To understand exactly what's going on and plan the best support, I'm referring [him] to a developmental specialist who will do more detailed testing. This is not a label or a diagnosis — it's information that helps us help [him]. In the meantime, we're starting therapy so [he] doesn't lose time."

Developmental regression:

> "I'm concerned because [Aanya] has lost some skills she previously had — [she was saying words and now she's stopped / she was walking and now she's unsteady]. ### 10.5 Cultural sensitivities

- *Developmental delay stigma.* Many Indian families experience shame or denial when a developmental concern is identified. Use strengths-based language: "Here's what [he] does well; here's where [he] needs extra support." Never use "abnormal" or "defective."
- *"He's just a late bloomer."* Acknowledge that some variation is normal; explain that the screening tools help us identify which children genuinely need support versus which will catch up on their own. Frame early intervention as an advantage, not a label.
- *Comparison with siblings / peers.* Indian families commonly compare siblings. Redirect: "Each child has their own developmental path. The goal is to make sure [he] reaches [his] potential, whatever that looks like."
- *Disability stigma.* Families may resist the word "disability" even when applicable. Use functional language: "learning support needs" rather than "intellectual disability" during initial conversations. Formal diagnostic terminology is used when the family is ready and when it serves access to services.
- *"Can therapy cure it?"* Frame realistically: therapy helps the child reach their maximum potential. For some conditions, children catch up completely; for others, therapy helps them function as well as possible. Avoid false promises.
- *Gender-biased intervention access.* In some Indian families, developmental services are prioritised for boys over girls. Counsel that all children deserve equal access to therapy.
- *Traditional remedies.* Some families use religious rituals or traditional remedies for developmental delay. Do not dismiss; ensure these do not replace evidence-based therapy.
- *Cost of therapy.* Early-intervention services (speech, OT, PT) are expensive and often not covered by insurance. SKIDS manager assists with government scheme applications (RPWD Act, ADIP, state disability benefits) and identifies subsidised therapy centres.

## §11 Manager handoff

§11.1 Manager Console: developmental status (on track / at risk / delayed — severity and domains); therapy services in progress; formal-assessment referral status; syndrome-specific monitoring status.

§11.2 Tiering: A1 screening = Basic; structured assessment + early-intervention coordination = Advanced; longitudinal monitoring + multi-protocol cross-care = Premium.

§11.3 Top parent objections:

| Objection | Manager response |
|---|---|
| "He's just a late bloomer — he'll catch up." | "You may be right. The assessment helps us know for sure. If he is going to catch up, the test will show that. If he needs a little extra support, starting early is the best thing we can do." |
| "Therapy is too expensive / too far." | "I can help you apply for government support under the disability act. There are also centres closer to home — let me find one. We can also start with a home programme of activities you do with [him] every day." |
| "We don't want a label for our child." | "This assessment is not about labelling — it's about understanding what [she] needs. The information stays in [her] medical record and helps us plan the right support. You decide what to share with others." |
| "He talks fine at home." | "It's common for children to perform differently in different settings. The formal assessment gives us an objective picture across several areas of development — not just language." |
| "We're already doing everything at home." | "That's great — and we can build on that. The therapist can add specific techniques that target exactly where [he] needs support. What you're doing at home is the foundation." |

§11.4–§11.6: standard manager touchpoints. NICU-graduate and regression-bridge families get highest-priority manager engagement.

## §12 Quality and audit

§12.1 Metrics:
- ≥ 95% of A1 Orange developmental flags result in B1-23 assessment within 2 weeks.
- ≥ 90% of children identified with delay have early-intervention services initiated within 4 weeks of B1-23 assessment.
- ≥ 95% of developmental regression cases have neurology referral initiated same-week.
- ≥ 85% of NICU graduates complete corrected-age developmental follow-up through 36 months.
- Therapy attendance: ≥ 70% of prescribed therapy sessions attended.
- Formal diagnostic assessment completion: ≥ 80% of children referred for formal assessment complete it within 3 months.
- ≥ 95% of §7 triggers result in documented referral with complete handoff packet.
- Loss-to-follow-up: < 10% for at-risk and delayed children.

§12.2 Quarterly: protocol owner reviews all developmental-regression cases, all §7 referrals, early-intervention initiation timeliness, therapy-attendance data, NICU-graduate follow-up compliance, and a random sample of 30 B1-23 visits.

§12.3 Certification: every SKIDS pediatrician delivering B1-23 must complete ASQ-3 administration training (2 hours); developmental-assessment training (8 hours); Trivandrum DST training (2 hours); annual update (2 hours covering new milestones evidence and screening tools). DBP Fellowship-pathway pediatricians receive extended training (see §12.4).

§12.4 Fellowship: SKIDS Developmental-Behavioural Pediatrics Fellowship pathway. Protocol governance for B1-23 + B1-08 across the SKIDS network may be shared by the named DBP — the same governance owner for both developmental and behavioural protocols.
