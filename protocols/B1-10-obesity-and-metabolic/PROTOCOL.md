# SKIDS Obesity & Metabolic Clinic

> The SKIDS Obesity & Metabolic Clinic delivers structured lifestyle-
> intervention programmes for pediatric overweight and obesity,
> metabolic-risk screening (dyslipidaemia, pre-diabetes, NAFLD,
> hypertension), SKIDS dietitian-led dietary management, family-
> based behavioural-change programmes, and co-management with B1-09
> Nutrition & Growth, B1-13 Adolescent, and B1-14 Pulmonology for
> obesity comorbidities. T2DM, severe metabolic syndrome, and
> bariatric-surgery candidacy route per §7.

---

## §1 Service definition

**What this clinic does:**
- Confirms obesity diagnosis using IAP BMI-for-age charts (5–18 years) and WHO BMI-for-age Z-scores (0–5 years); classifies as overweight (BMI 85th–94th centile) or obese (BMI ≥ 95th centile) [IAP-Growth-2015].
- Performs structured metabolic-risk screening: fasting lipid panel, fasting glucose, HbA1c, ALT (NAFLD screen), BP, waist circumference, acanthosis nigricans assessment.
- Delivers the SKIDS Healthy Weight Programme — a 12-week structured family-based lifestyle intervention combining dietitian-led dietary modification, physical-activity prescription, screen-time reduction, sleep optimisation, and motivational interviewing for behaviour change.
- Provides SKIDS dietitian-led individualised dietary plans: calorie-appropriate (not calorie-restrictive in growing children), macronutrient-balanced, culturally adapted for Indian diets, with specific sugar-sweetened-beverage elimination and ultra-processed-food reduction targets.
- Manages pre-diabetes (IFG 100–125 mg/dL or HbA1c 5.7–6.4%) within B1-10 with lifestyle intervention and monitoring; routes confirmed T2DM (FPG ≥ 126 mg/dL or HbA1c ≥ 6.5% or random glucose ≥ 200 mg/dL with symptoms) to B2-01 Endocrine / §7.3 [AAP-T2DM-2013].
- Manages pediatric dyslipidaemia within B1-10 with dietary-modification programme; routes pharmacotherapy candidacy (LDL persistently ≥ 190 mg/dL or ≥ 160 mg/dL with risk factors, after 6-month dietary trial) to §7.3 pediatric cardiology / B1-16 [AAP-Lipid-2011].
- Co-manages NAFLD (elevated ALT without other cause in obese child) with lifestyle intervention; routes to §7.3 pediatric gastroenterology / B1-15 if ALT persistently elevated > 2× ULN after 6-month lifestyle intervention.
- Co-manages obesity-related hypertension: BP monitoring at every visit; BP ≥ 95th centile confirmed → co-refer to B1-16 Cardiac + B1-17 Kidney while continuing lifestyle management.
- Co-manages adolescent PCOS (with B1-13 Adolescent and B2-01 Endocrine): obesity/lifestyle component managed at B1-10; hormonal workup and management at B2-01.
- Co-manages obesity as asthma comorbidity (with B1-14): communicates weight-management progress; obesity reduction improves asthma control.
- Screens for psychosocial complications of obesity: bullying, body-image distress, depression, low self-esteem (cross with B1-08 Behavioural; cross with B1-13 for adolescent psychosocial).

**What this clinic does not do:**
- Prescribe weight-loss pharmacotherapy (orlistat, GLP-1 agonists) in children — §7.3 to pediatric endocrinology / obesity specialist.
- Manage confirmed T2DM (→ B2-01 Endocrine).
- Manage bariatric-surgery candidacy (→ §7.3 to pediatric bariatric centre — rare in Indian pediatrics).
- Manage Prader-Willi syndrome, hypothalamic obesity, or monogenic obesity syndromes (→ §7.3 to pediatric genetics / endocrinology).
- Manage severe NAFLD / NASH with fibrosis (→ §7.3 to pediatric gastroenterology).
- Impose calorie-restrictive diets in children with linear growth potential — calorie-appropriate, not calorie-restrictive, is the principle.

**Age range covered:** 2 – 18 years (obesity diagnosis below age 2 is rare and routes to §7.3 for evaluation of secondary causes).

**SKIDS subscription tier(s):**
- **Basic ₹2,999** — A1 BMI screening and flag routing; first B1-10 assessment on per-visit pricing.
- **Advanced ₹6,999** — SKIDS Healthy Weight Programme (12-week structured intervention) included; 4 dietitian sessions; metabolic-risk screening labs.
- **Premium ₹12,999** — unlimited B1-10 visits; unlimited dietitian sessions; multi-protocol cross-care (B1-10 + B1-09 + B1-13 + B1-14); priority §7 partner referral.

**SKIDS clinical tier:** Tier 1 with allied-staff dependency on the SKIDS dietitian (shared with B1-09). The dietitian is integral to B1-10 operations — without in-clinic dietitian, B1-10 cannot deliver the Healthy Weight Programme. Protocol governance with named pediatric endocrinologist or obesity-medicine specialist. Complex tier per §7 escalates to B2-01 Endocrine (T2DM, syndromic obesity), pediatric cardiology / B1-16 (dyslipidaemia pharmacotherapy, hypertension), pediatric gastroenterology / B1-15 (NAFLD/NASH), or pediatric bariatric centre.

**Departures from cited guidelines:**

1. *Calorie-appropriate not calorie-restrictive.* AAP 2023 permits structured dietary-modification programmes. SKIDS emphasises calorie-appropriate intake (meeting growth needs without excess) rather than calorie restriction, especially in children < 12 years with linear growth potential. Calorie-restrictive approaches are reserved for adolescents ≥ 14 years with severe obesity and completed or near-completed linear growth, and only with dietitian supervision.

2. *Weight-loss pharmacotherapy out of Tier 1 scope.* AAP 2023 includes pharmacotherapy (including GLP-1 agonists) as an option for children ≥ 12 years with obesity. SKIDS routes pharmacotherapy to §7.3 (pediatric endocrinology / obesity specialist). This is conservative but appropriate for junior-pediatrician scope.

3. *Overweight (85th–94th centile) included in B1-10 scope.* A1 Orange flag triggers at BMI ≥ 95th centile. B1-10 also accepts children with BMI 85th–94th centile (overweight) who are referred from A1 with rapid weight gain or concurrent metabolic risk factors. This is a deliberate expansion — early intervention at overweight prevents progression to obesity.

- B1-13 cross: adolescent PCOS + obesity → B1-10 for obesity component; B2-01 for hormonal.
- B1-14 cross: asthma + obesity comorbidity → B1-10 for weight management.

## §4 Diagnostic workflow

### 4.1 Anthropometry and classification

1. **Weight, height, BMI** — calculate BMI (kg/m²); plot on IAP BMI-for-age chart (5–18 years) or WHO BMI-for-age Z-score chart (2–5 years).
2. **Waist circumference** — measure at midpoint between lowest rib and iliac crest; use Indian pediatric waist-circumference references (where available) or ≥ 90th centile as elevated.
3. **Classification:**

| Category | BMI criterion (5–18 years, IAP) | BMI Z-score (2–5 years, WHO) |
|---|---|---|
| Normal weight | 5th–84th centile | −2 to +1 SD |
| Overweight | 85th–94th centile | > +1 to +2 SD |
| Obese | ≥ 95th centile | > +2 to +3 SD |
| Severely obese | ≥ 120% of 95th centile or BMI ≥ 35 (whichever lower) | > +3 SD |

4. **Rapid weight gain definition:** crossing ≥ 2 BMI centile bands upward within 6–12 months (e.g., from 75th to 95th centile).

### 4.2 Structured history

1. **Dietary history** (24-hour recall + food-frequency questionnaire completed with dietitian):
   - Sugar-sweetened beverages (frequency, volume).
   - Ultra-processed food frequency (packaged snacks, fast food, bakery items).
   - Portion sizes (for age).
   - Meal pattern (number of meals, snacking pattern, night eating).
   - Fruit and vegetable servings/day.
   - Family dietary pattern (shared meals, who cooks, food environment).

2. **Physical activity** (7-day recall):
   - Minutes of moderate-vigorous physical activity per day.
   - Organised sport participation.
   - Active commuting (walk/cycle to school).
   - Sedentary time (screen time, homework, sitting).

3. **Screen time** — hours/day; types (passive entertainment, social media, gaming, educational).

4. **Sleep** — hours/night; bedtime consistency; snoring (cross B1-07 for OSA if obese + snoring).

5. **Psychosocial** — bullying, body-image distress, low self-esteem, depression screen (PHQ-A for adolescents, cross B1-08); eating-disorder screen (SCOFF for adolescents, cross B1-13).

6. **Family history** — T2DM, cardiovascular disease, dyslipidaemia, hypertension, obesity, PCOS, NAFLD.

7. **Onset and trajectory** — when weight gain started; any precipitating factors (medications, life events, reduced activity due to injury/illness).

8. **Medications** — any that promote weight gain (corticosteroids, antipsychotics, anticonvulsants, insulin).

9. **Secondary causes screen** — hypothyroidism (fatigue, constipation, cold intolerance), Cushing syndrome (striae, moon face, central obesity + short stature), Prader-Willi (hypotonia, developmental delay, insatiable appetite), hypothalamic obesity (rapid onset after CNS insult).

### 4.3 Examination

- **Anthropometry:** as per §4.1.
- **BP:** per AAP-2017-HBP; use appropriate cuff size; if elevated, repeat × 3 and classify.
- **Acanthosis nigricans:** neck, axillae, groin — marker of insulin resistance; grade 0–4.
- **Skin striae:** purple/violaceous striae → Cushing suspicion → §7.3. White/silvery stretch marks are common in rapid weight gain and are not a red flag.
- **Thyroid examination.**
- **Pubertal staging** (Tanner) — relevant for timing of interventions; early puberty in obese girls is common.
- **Hepatomegaly** — palpable liver → NAFLD suspicion.
- **Orthopaedic:** genu valgum, slipped capital femoral epiphysis risk (hip/knee pain in obese adolescent → §7.2).
- **Respiratory:** signs of OSA (mouth breathing, adenotonsillar hypertrophy → cross B1-03/B1-07).

### 4.4 Metabolic-risk screening (laboratory)

Order for all children with BMI ≥ 95th centile and for overweight (85th–94th) with risk factors:

| Test | Indication | Action threshold |
|---|---|---|
| **Fasting glucose** | All obese | IFG 100–125 mg/dL = pre-diabetes → §6; ≥ 126 mg/dL = T2DM → §7.3 B2-01 |
| **HbA1c** | All obese ≥ 10 years or with risk factors | 5.7–6.4% = pre-diabetes → §6; ≥ 6.5% = T2DM → §7.3 B2-01 |
| **Fasting lipid panel** | All obese; overweight with family hx | LDL ≥ 130 mg/dL = borderline-high; ≥ 160 mg/dL = high; ≥ 190 mg/dL = very high |
| **ALT** | All obese (NAFLD screen) | > 2× the local laboratory's upper limit of normal (ULN) for ALT (typical paediatric ULN: 22–25 U/L for girls, 25–33 U/L for boys; 2× ULN therefore typically 44–50 U/L — use your laboratory's specific reference range) [NASPGHAN-NAFLD-2017] → lifestyle trial 6 months → §7.3 if persistent |
| **TSH** | All obese (secondary cause screen) | Elevated → §7.3 B2-01 Endocrine for hypothyroidism management |
| **Fasting insulin** | Obese with acanthosis or family T2DM | HOMA-IR > 3.16 = insulin resistance |
| **Uric acid** | Severe obesity or metabolic syndrome features | Elevated → gout risk; lifestyle management |

### 4.5 Metabolic syndrome assessment

Use modified ATP-III criteria adapted for pediatrics (IDF pediatric criteria for ≥ 10 years):
- Waist circumference ≥ 90th centile.
- Triglycerides ≥ 150 mg/dL.
- HDL < 40 mg/dL.
- BP ≥ 130/85 mmHg (or ≥ 95th centile for age/height/sex).
- Fasting glucose ≥ 100 mg/dL.

≥ 3 of 5 criteria = metabolic syndrome. Document; intensify lifestyle intervention; route to B2-01 if concurrent T2DM; route to B1-16 if BP ≥ 95th centile confirmed.

### 4.6 Decision tree

```
Child with BMI ≥ 85th centile
        │
        ▼
§4.1 Anthropometry + classification
§4.2 Dietary + activity + psychosocial history
§4.3 Examination (acanthosis, BP, liver, secondary causes)
        │
        ├── Secondary cause suspected (Cushing, Prader-Willi, hypothyroidism)
        │       → §7.3 endocrinology / genetics
        │
        ▼
§4.4 Metabolic-risk screening (labs)
        │
        ├── T2DM confirmed → §7.3 B2-01
        ├── Pre-diabetes → §6 lifestyle + monitoring
        ├── Dyslipidaemia → §6 dietary trial; §7.3 if pharmacotherapy needed
        ├── NAFLD → §6 lifestyle; §7.3 if ALT persistent > 2× ULN
        ├── Hypertension confirmed → co-refer B1-16 + B1-17; continue lifestyle
        │
        ▼
§5 severity grade → §6 Healthy Weight Programme
```

## §5 Severity grading [SACRED SECTION]

### 5.1 Obesity severity

| Severity | BMI criterion | Comorbidities | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Overweight** | 85th–94th centile | None | §6.1 Light — counselling + dietitian |
| **Overweight with risk** | 85th–94th centile | ≥ 1 metabolic risk factor OR family hx T2DM/CVD | §6.2 Standard — Healthy Weight Programme |
| **Obese** | 95th–119% of 95th centile | ± comorbidities | §6.2 Standard — Healthy Weight Programme + metabolic monitoring |
| **Severely obese** | ≥ 120% of 95th centile or BMI ≥ 35 | ± comorbidities | §6.3 Intensive — intensive lifestyle + metabolic monitoring + §7 assessment |

### 5.2 Metabolic-risk severity

| Risk level | Criteria | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **No metabolic risk** | Normal fasting glucose, lipids, ALT, BP | Lifestyle intervention only |
| **Pre-metabolic** | 1 abnormal metabolic marker (borderline lipids, IFG, elevated ALT) | §6.2 + 3-monthly monitoring |
| **Metabolic syndrome** | ≥ 3 of 5 modified ATP-III/IDF criteria | §6.3 + co-referral per component |
| **T2DM confirmed** | FPG ≥ 126, HbA1c ≥ 6.5%, or random glucose ≥ 200 + symptoms | §7.3 B2-01 |

### 5.3 Psychosocial severity

| Level | Features | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Weight-related self-consciousness; no functional impact | §6 lifestyle programme addresses body image |
| **Moderate** | Bullying, school avoidance, social withdrawal | B1-08 cross-care + intensified family support |
| **Severe** | Depression (PHQ-A ≥ 10), eating disorder features (SCOFF ≥ 2), self-harm | B1-08 §7 pathway; B1-13 if adolescent |

### 5.4 Boundary rules

- Overweight without any risk factor stays in A1 surveillance at Yellow level; enters B1-10 only if rapid weight gain or if family/pediatrician requests assessment.
- Pre-diabetes stays in B1-10; confirmed T2DM routes to B2-01. The boundary is FPG ≥ 126 mg/dL or HbA1c ≥ 6.5%.
- Dyslipidaemia stays in B1-10 for 6-month dietary trial; pharmacotherapy decision routes to B1-16 / pediatric cardiology.
- NAFLD stays in B1-10 for 6-month lifestyle trial; persistent elevation routes to B1-15 / pediatric GI.
- Obesity in child < 2 years → §7.3 to evaluate for secondary/syndromic cause before initiating B1-10 programme.
- Severe obesity (≥ 120% of 95th centile) with ≥ 2 comorbidities → consider §7.3 for obesity-specialist assessment even if B1-10 manages the lifestyle component.
- Purple striae → do NOT assume common stretch marks; evaluate for Cushing syndrome (cortisol, 24h urine free cortisol) → §7.3 if abnormal.

## §6 Therapeutic options

### 6.1 Light tier (overweight, no metabolic risk)

**Dietary counselling (1 dietitian session + follow-up):**
- Eliminate sugar-sweetened beverages.
- Reduce ultra-processed food (target: ≤ 1 serving/day).
- Increase fruit/vegetable intake (target: ≥ 5 servings/day).
- Appropriate portion sizes for age.
- Family-based changes: shared meals, no screen-time eating.

**Physical activity prescription:**
- ≥ 60 minutes moderate-vigorous physical activity daily (for ≥ 5 years).
- Reduce sedentary screen time (target: ≤ 2 hours/day for entertainment).
- Encourage active play, organised sport, active commuting.

**Sleep optimisation:** per B1-07 — adequate sleep for age supports healthy weight.

**Goal:** weight maintenance (not weight loss) in growing children; BMI centile will reduce as height increases.

### 6.2 Standard tier (obese, or overweight with risk — SKIDS Healthy Weight Programme)

**12-week structured family-based lifestyle intervention:**

*Week 1–2: Assessment + goal-setting*
- Dietitian: full dietary assessment; individualised dietary plan.
- Physical-activity assessment; individualised activity plan.
- Motivational interviewing: assess readiness to change; identify barriers; set SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound).
- Family engagement: at least one parent/caregiver must participate.

*Week 3–12: Active intervention*
- Dietitian sessions: weekly × 4 weeks, then fortnightly × 4 sessions (8 sessions total).
- Physician visits: week 1, week 6, week 12.
- Weekly parent-app check-ins: food diary, activity log, progress photos (optional).
- Behavioural strategies: stimulus control (remove trigger foods from home); self-monitoring (food/activity diary); positive reinforcement (non-food rewards for goal achievement); problem-solving for barriers.

*Dietary targets (calorie-appropriate, not restrictive):*
- Eliminate SSBs.
- Reduce ultra-processed food to ≤ 1 serving/day.
- Increase whole grains, legumes (dal), vegetables, fruits.
- Protein: adequate for growth (1–1.2 g/kg/day).
- Cultural adaptation: modify Indian dietary patterns (reduce fried foods, sugary sweets, refined-flour snacks) while preserving family food culture.
- No calorie counting for children < 12 years; calorie awareness (not restriction) for ≥ 12 years.

*Activity targets:*
- ≥ 60 minutes MVPA daily.
- Resistance/strength activities ≥ 3 days/week for adolescents.
- Screen time ≤ 2 hours/day entertainment.
- No screens during meals.

### 6.3 Intensive tier (severely obese, or metabolic syndrome)

- Healthy Weight Programme as above with intensified monitoring:
  - Physician visits: every 4 weeks.
  - Dietitian: weekly sessions throughout 12 weeks.
  - Metabolic monitoring: fasting labs at baseline, 3 months, 6 months.
- BP monitoring at every visit; if confirmed ≥ 95th centile → co-refer B1-16.
- Pre-diabetes management: lifestyle intervention (above) is first-line; repeat HbA1c at 3 months. If HbA1c not improving after 6 months of optimised lifestyle → §7.3 B2-01 for metformin consideration.
- Dyslipidaemia management: dietary modification per AAP-Lipid-2011 CHILD-1 and CHILD-2 diets for 6 months. If LDL ≥ 190 mg/dL (or ≥ 160 mg/dL with risk factors) after 6-month dietary trial → §7.3 B1-16 / pediatric cardiology for statin consideration.
- NAFLD: lifestyle intervention; repeat ALT at 3 months, 6 months. If ALT persistently > 2× ULN after 6-month lifestyle → §7.3 B1-15 / pediatric GI for liver ultrasound and further workup.
- Psychosocial support intensified: B1-08 cross-care if PHQ-A positive; family therapy referral if family dysfunction identified.

### 6.4 Adolescent-specific (≥ 12 years)

- Calorie-awareness approach (not strict restriction): dietitian teaches label reading, portion awareness, and energy-balance concepts.
- For adolescents ≥ 14 years with severe obesity (≥ 120% of 95th centile) and completed linear growth: modest calorie deficit (500 kcal/day below maintenance) under dietitian supervision is acceptable per AAP 2023.
- Body-image counselling: frame weight management in terms of health and function, not appearance. Avoid language that promotes shame.
- Adolescent privacy framework (per B1-13): if adolescent requests private dietary counselling without parent, respect per B1-13 confidentiality framework.

### 6.5 Contraindications and precautions

| Intervention | Contraindication | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Calorie restriction | Child < 12 years with active linear growth | Use calorie-appropriate, not restrictive |
| Calorie restriction | Active eating disorder (SCOFF ≥ 2) | Do NOT restrict; route to B1-08/B1-13 §7 |
| Intense exercise programme | Slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE) | Restrict weight-bearing until orthopaedic clearance |
| Intense exercise programme | Severe obesity with uncontrolled asthma | Optimise asthma (B1-14) before intensifying exercise |
| Weight-loss pharmacotherapy | Any (at B1-10 Tier 1) | Route to §7.3 |

### 6.6 Dispensary stock

- BIA device (bioelectrical impedance — shared with B1-09).
- BP cuffs (large child and adult sizes for obese children).
- Stadiometer, calibrated electronic scale.
- Waist-circumference tape.
- Acanthosis nigricans grading chart.
- Dietary assessment tools (food models, portion-size guides).

## §7 Hard referral triggers [SACRED SECTION]

### 7.1 Emergency referral (same day)

- [ ] **DKA presentation** — polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss, Kussmaul breathing, altered consciousness, POC glucose ≥ 250 mg/dL with ketonuria. → ER + B2-01 Endocrine (may be T1DM or T2DM with DKA).
- [ ] **Hypertensive emergency** — BP ≥ 99th centile + 5 mmHg with end-organ symptoms (headache, visual change, seizure). → ER.

### 7.2 Urgent referral (within 7 days)

- [ ] **Slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE)** — obese adolescent with hip/knee pain, limp, limited internal rotation. → Pediatric orthopaedics within 7 days (or ER if acute).
- [ ] **Confirmed T2DM** — FPG ≥ 126 mg/dL or HbA1c ≥ 6.5% (confirmed on repeat). → B2-01 Endocrine within 7 days.
- [ ] **Severe hypertension** — BP ≥ 95th centile + 12 mmHg on 3 readings, asymptomatic. → B1-16 Cardiac within 7 days.

### 7.3 Routine referral (within 4 weeks)

- [ ] **Pre-diabetes not improving after 6 months optimised lifestyle** → B2-01 Endocrine for metformin consideration.
- [ ] **Dyslipidaemia LDL ≥ 190 mg/dL (or ≥ 160 mg/dL with risk factors) after 6-month dietary trial** → B1-16 / pediatric cardiology for statin consideration.
- [ ] **NAFLD ALT persistently > 2× ULN after 6-month lifestyle trial** → B1-15 / pediatric gastroenterology for ultrasound + biopsy consideration.
- [ ] **Confirmed hypertension (BP ≥ 95th centile on 3 occasions)** → B1-16 Cardiac + B1-17 Kidney (co-refer; continue B1-10 lifestyle).
- [ ] **Suspected secondary obesity** — Cushing features (purple striae, moon face, central obesity + short stature), Prader-Willi (hypotonia, developmental delay, insatiable appetite), hypothalamic obesity (rapid onset post-CNS insult), hypothyroidism (elevated TSH). → B2-01 Endocrine / pediatric genetics.
- [ ] **Obesity in child < 2 years** → B2-01 / pediatric genetics for secondary cause evaluation.
- [ ] **Severe obesity with ≥ 2 comorbidities not responding to 6-month intensive lifestyle** → pediatric obesity specialist / endocrinology for pharmacotherapy assessment.
- [ ] **Adolescent PCOS (B1-13 workup positive) + obesity** → B2-01 Endocrine for hormonal management + B1-10 continues lifestyle.
- [ ] **Severe psychosocial complications** — depression with functional impairment, eating disorder confirmed → B1-08 §7.
- [ ] **SCFE non-acute** — hip pain with radiographic findings → pediatric orthopaedics.

### 7.4 Handoff communication

Standard packet:
1. BMI trajectory (longitudinal from A1 + B1-10 visits).
2. Dietary assessment summary (dietitian-generated).
3. Metabolic screening results (glucose, HbA1c, lipids, ALT, TSH).
4. Lifestyle intervention compliance and response (weight/BMI trajectory during programme).
5. BP log.
6. Comorbidity summary (pre-diabetes, dyslipidaemia, NAFLD, hypertension, psychosocial).
7. Companion-generated referral summary.

## §8 Follow-up schedule

| Scenario | First follow-up | Subsequent |
|---|---|---|
| Overweight, no risk (§6.1) | 3 months | 6-monthly; annually if BMI stabilising |
| Obese, Healthy Weight Programme (§6.2) | Week 6 (mid-programme) | Week 12 (end-programme); then 3-monthly × 1 year |
| Severely obese / metabolic syndrome (§6.3) | 4 weeks | Monthly × 3 months; then 3-monthly |
| Pre-diabetes | 3 months (repeat HbA1c) | 3-monthly until resolved or routed to §7.3 |
| Dyslipidaemia (dietary trial) | 3 months | 6 months (decision point for §7.3) |
| NAFLD (lifestyle trial) | 3 months (repeat ALT) | 6 months (decision point for §7.3) |
| Post-programme maintenance | 3-monthly × 1 year | 6-monthly thereafter |

§8.2 What to recheck: BMI (every visit); waist circumference (every visit); BP (every visit); dietary adherence (dietitian assessment); physical-activity log; screen-time log; metabolic labs (per schedule); psychosocial screen (every visit for adolescents).

§8.3 Success: BMI centile reduction (or maintenance with height gain) over 6–12 months; metabolic markers normalising; physical activity target met (≥ 60 min/day); SSB elimination achieved; psychosocial well-being maintained.

§8.7 Loss-to-follow-up: Companion alert to manager + parent if B1-10 follow-up missed during Healthy Weight Programme. Programme dropout is a significant risk — manager re-engagement per §11.

## §10 Parent-facing communication

### 10.1 Scripted explanations

Overweight diagnosis:

> "[Arjun]'s weight is above what we expect for [his] height and age. This puts [him] at the 'overweight' level on the growth chart. This is a signal, not a crisis. The good news is that at [his] age, we don't need to make [him] lose weight — we just need to slow the weight gain down so [his] height catches up. We do that with some changes to what [he] eats and how active [he] is. I'd like [him] to see our dietitian to make a plan that works for your family."

Obese with metabolic risk:

> "[Aanya]'s weight is in the 'obese' range on the growth chart, and the blood tests show [some early changes in her cholesterol / blood sugar / liver markers]. These changes are the body's early warning signs. The good news: they are reversible with lifestyle changes — diet, activity, and less screen time. We have a 12-week programme that works with the whole family. It's not about dieting or restriction — it's about building healthier habits together."

Pre-diabetes:

> "[Arjun]'s blood sugar is in the 'pre-diabetes' range. This does not mean [he] has diabetes — it means [his] body is working harder than it should to control blood sugar because of the extra weight. If we act now with diet and activity changes, we can bring this back to normal. If we don't, it can progress to diabetes. This is reversible."

### 10.5 Cultural sensitivities

- *"Chubby is healthy."* Some Indian families, especially grandparents, equate childhood chubbiness with health and prosperity. Acknowledge the cultural meaning; reframe using the growth chart: "The chart shows [his] weight is above the healthy range for [his] height. A healthy weight protects [him] from problems like diabetes and high blood pressure later."
- *Food as love.* In many Indian families, feeding is an expression of affection. Do not frame dietary changes as "stop feeding your child so much." Frame as: "Feed [him] the same love — just different foods. More dal, vegetables, and roti; less biscuits, chips, and sugary drinks."
- *"But he doesn't eat much."* Portion sizes in Indian households may be larger than perceived. Use food models and portion-size guides during dietitian sessions. 24-hour recall with portion estimation reveals actual intake.
- *Street food / outside food.* Many Indian children eat street food regularly. Do not ban; reduce frequency. Identify healthier street-food alternatives. The dietitian works with the family's actual food environment, not an idealised one.
- *Physical activity barriers.* Safety concerns (traffic, pollution) limit outdoor activity in many Indian cities. Identify indoor alternatives: dance, skipping, stair climbing, home-based exercise videos. Organised sport programmes where available.
- *"It's genetic — nothing we can do."* Acknowledge genetic predisposition; explain that genetics loads the gun but environment pulls the trigger. The programme modifies the environment.
- *Weight stigma language.* Never use "fat," "heavy," or shame-based language. Use clinical terms: "overweight," "obese" (with explanation), or health-focused: "above the healthy weight range."

## §11 Manager handoff

§11.1 Manager Console: BMI classification; metabolic-risk summary; Healthy Weight Programme status (enrolled / active / completed / dropped out); dietitian session count; cross-care status.

§11.2 Tiering: first assessment = Basic; Healthy Weight Programme = Advanced; multi-protocol cross-care + ongoing monitoring = Premium.

§11.3 Top parent objections:

| Objection | Manager response |
|---|---|
| "He's just big-boned / it runs in the family." | "Family history does play a role. That's exactly why the programme is important — children with a family history benefit the most from early lifestyle changes because they have higher metabolic risk." |
| "She'll grow out of it." | "Some children do lose excess weight as they grow taller. But without changes, the trend usually continues — and the blood tests show early changes we want to reverse now while they're still reversible." |
| "We can do this at home — don't need a programme." | "Many families find that helpful. The programme gives you a structured plan, a dietitian who knows children's nutrition, and regular check-ins so you're not guessing. If home changes work, we'll see it on the growth chart at the next visit." |
| "My child will be upset if I change the food." | "The programme works with the whole family — everyone eats the same way. It's not about taking food away from [child]; it's about everyone building healthier habits together." |
| "Can't you just give a medicine for it?" | "For children, lifestyle changes are both safer and more effective than medicines. Medicines are reserved for a very small number of cases where lifestyle alone isn't enough, and those are managed by a specialist." |

§11.4–§11.6: standard manager touchpoints. Healthy Weight Programme dropout is a key re-engagement trigger — manager contacts family within 3 days of a missed session.

## §12 Quality and audit

§12.1 Metrics:
- ≥ 70% of children enrolled in HWP complete the 12-week programme.
- ≥ 50% of obese children show BMI centile reduction (or maintenance with height gain) at 6 months.
- ≥ 80% of children with pre-diabetes revert to normal HbA1c within 12 months of lifestyle intervention.
- Metabolic screening completion: ≥ 95% of obese children have fasting labs at first B1-10 visit.
- ≥ 90% of children with confirmed hypertension are co-referred to B1-16 within 4 weeks.
- ≥ 90% of T2DM cases are routed to B2-01 within 7 days of confirmation.
- SSB elimination: ≥ 60% of HWP participants report zero SSB consumption at programme completion.
- Loss-to-follow-up during HWP: < 30%.

§12.2 Quarterly: protocol owner reviews HWP completion rates, BMI trajectory analysis, metabolic screening completeness, all §7 referrals, dietitian session utilisation, and parent-satisfaction scores.

§12.3 Certification: every SKIDS pediatrician delivering B1-10 must complete motivational-interviewing training (8 hours); pediatric obesity assessment training (4 hours); dietary-assessment interpretation (2 hours — joint with dietitian). Annual update (2 hours covering new guidelines and programme-effectiveness review).

§12.4 Fellowship: SKIDS does not currently have a Pediatric Obesity Medicine Fellowship. Protocol governance sits with the named pediatric endocrinologist or obesity-medicine specialist. Pediatricians with interest may pursue IAP Pediatric Endocrinology certification or AAP Obesity Medicine certification as recognised pathways.
