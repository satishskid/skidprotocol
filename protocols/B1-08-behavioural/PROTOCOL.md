# SKIDS Behavioural Clinic

> The SKIDS Behavioural Clinic manages ADHD with pharmacotherapy and
> behavioural therapy, mild-to-moderate anxiety and depression,
> disruptive-behaviour patterns, and behavioural-feeding / sleep
> overlap. Autism diagnosis, severe psychiatric disease, suicidality,
> psychosis, severe eating disorder, and trauma-specialist therapy
> route per §7. The LISSUN partnership delivers structured therapy
> across cases at all severities.

---

## §1 Service definition

**What this clinic does:**
- Diagnoses and manages attention-deficit / hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) with parent + teacher Vanderbilt scales, behavioural therapy referral, school-liaison support, and pharmacotherapy initiation (methylphenidate first-line; atomoxetine alternative) under the cardiac-screening preconditions documented in §6 [AAP-2019-ADHD].
- Conducts autism screening with M-CHAT-R/F at the recommended age windows and routes M-CHAT-R/F-positive children to formal diagnostic assessment per §7.3 [AAP-BF-4E, CDC-Milestones-2022].
- Manages mild-to-moderate adolescent anxiety (GAD-7 5–14) and depression (PHQ-A 5–14) with structured cognitive-behavioural therapy (CBT) via the LISSUN partner therapy network and SSRI initiation in moderate cases per the AAP guideline [AAP-Anxiety-2020, AAP-Depression-2018].
- Manages disruptive-behaviour patterns and oppositional behaviour with parent-training programmes and family-therapy referral.
- Manages mild OCD and mild tic disorders; routes moderate-severe to §7.3.
- Manages school refusal and adjustment-related stress with structured family + school intervention.
- Co-manages behavioural-feeding (joint with B1-09), behavioural-sleep (joint with B1-07), and adolescent psychosocial concerns (joint with B1-13).
- Co-manages with B2-03 Rheumatology (mood / behavioural changes in juvenile arthritis on steroids), B1-23 Developmental Surveillance (developmental-delay-with-behavioural-features), and B2-02 Neurology (suspected pediatric epilepsy with behavioural presentation, tic disorder evaluation).
- Provides LISSUN-partnered structured therapy across all severities — CBT, parent training, family therapy, social-skills groups, trauma-focused CBT (in scope only for milder presentations; severe trauma routes §7.3).

**What this clinic does not do:**
- Diagnose autism formally — M-CHAT-R/F screening only at B1-08; formal ADOS / clinical diagnosis routes §7.3 to developmental-behavioural pediatrics or pediatric psychiatry.
- Initiate antipsychotic medication, mood stabilisers (lithium, valproate for psychiatric indication), or benzodiazepines for any indication beyond very-short-term acute anxiety; these route §7.3.
- Manage severe depression (PHQ-A ≥15), severe anxiety (GAD-7 ≥15), suicidality, psychosis, mania, severe eating disorder, substance-use disorder requiring detoxification, or trauma requiring specialist therapy. These route per §7.
- Manage suspected pediatric bipolar disorder (§7.2 to pediatric psychiatry).
- Manage tic disorders moderate-severe / Tourette syndrome (§7.3 to pediatric neurology / B2-02).
- Manage OCD moderate-severe (§7.3 to pediatric psychiatry).
- Provide trauma-focused therapy for confirmed trauma (route to LISSUN trauma-specialist therapist or §7.3 to pediatric psychiatry).
- Manage adolescent eating disorders beyond initial screening + routing (§7.3 per A1 / B1-09).
- Manage adolescent substance-use disorders beyond brief intervention + routing (joint with B1-13 + LISSUN; §7.2 / §7.3 for moderate-severe).

**Age range covered:** 18 months – 18 years.
- 18 months – 5 years: M-CHAT-R/F autism screening; behavioural-sleep / behavioural-feeding overlap with B1-07 / B1-09; parent-training programmes; toilet-training difficulties (cross with B1-20).
- 6 – 12 years: ADHD diagnosis and management (Vanderbilt-graded); SDQ broader behavioural-emotional screen; school-refusal, anxiety, disruptive-behaviour patterns; LISSUN parent-training and CBT.
- 13 – 18 years: adolescent depression (PHQ-A) and anxiety (GAD-7); HEEADSSS overlap with A1 / B1-13; substance-use brief intervention (CRAFFT-graded); LISSUN adolescent CBT and family therapy.

**SKIDS subscription tier(s):**
- **Basic ₹2,999** — A1 routing supported. First B1-08 visit on per-visit pricing. LISSUN therapy sessions billed per session. Suitable for time-limited single-issue concerns.
- **Advanced ₹6,999** — up to 6 B1-08 visits per year included; up to 8 LISSUN therapy sessions per year included. Suitable for the typical 6–8-session CBT or parent-training programme.
- **Premium ₹12,999** — unlimited B1-08 visits within the clinically indicated set; up to 24 LISSUN therapy sessions per year included; priority §7 partner referral handoff. Suitable for ADHD on stimulant pharmacotherapy with concurrent CBT and parent training, or for moderate depression / anxiety on SSRI + CBT.

**SKIDS clinical tier:** Tier 1, with two key external dependencies:
- The **SKIDS in-clinic behavioural counsellor** (at-clinic allied staff; parallel to B1-01 optometrist, B1-09 dietitian). The counsellor delivers brief structured interventions, parent-training first sessions, and the link into LISSUN.
- The **LISSUN partnership** — external therapy network delivering structured CBT, parent training, family therapy, social-skills groups, and trauma-focused CBT (within scope) for SKIDS-referred families. LISSUN therapists send back structured progress notes that are integrated into the SKIDS PHR per §9.

Without these two, B1-08 cannot operate at full capability. The protocol degrades to a screening-and-routing role only.

Protocol governance sits with the named developmental-behavioural pediatrician identified in §2 (assignment pending). Complex tier per §7 escalates to pediatric psychiatry (Tier 3 partner), pediatric neurology (B2-02 if available, otherwise Tier 3), or named pediatric ER + LISSUN crisis-arm for §7.1.

**Departures from cited guidelines:**

1. *ADHD pharmacotherapy initiation in primary care.* AAP-2019 supports primary-care ADHD management including pharmacotherapy. NICE-2018 and many Indian centres restrict initiation to specialist input. B1-08 follows AAP — primary-care initiation by the SKIDS pediatrician — under explicit cardiac-screening preconditions documented in §6.3 (cardiovascular history + family-history-of-sudden-cardiac-death screen + resting ECG before any stimulant). The departure is operational rather than clinical and is defended on access grounds (Indian pediatric psychiatry is severely capacity-constrained; primary-care-capable ADHD initiation reduces wait-time harms).

2. *SSRI initiation for moderate adolescent depression in primary care.* GLAD-PC supports primary-care SSRI initiation with structured monitoring. In Indian practice, SSRI initiation is often deferred to psychiatry. B1-08 follows GLAD-PC — fluoxetine first-line — with explicit psychiatric-handoff capacity (LISSUN psychiatrist available for joint-care within the network) and the §6 monitoring framework. SSRI initiation always co-occurs with structured CBT (LISSUN-delivered).

3. *LISSUN as the named therapy partner.* The protocol references LISSUN explicitly as the SKIDS therapy delivery partner for Bangalore (and other SKIDS clinic locations as the partnership scales). This is operationally specific to the SKIDS network and is documented per CLAUDE.md §7.3. [TO BE DECIDED BY SKIDS PEDIATRICIAN]

4. *Privacy and confidentiality framework for adolescents.* B1-08 follows the A1 §10.5 / B1-09 §10.5 framework — adolescent psychosocial information confidential except in cases of self-harm intent, harm-to-others intent, abuse / exploitation. This is more restrictive on disclosure-to-parents than some Indian primary-care practice; it is intentional and is the core enabler of adolescent engagement [WHO-mhGAP-2023, AAP-Suicide-2024].

5. *Mental health stigma framing.* B1-08 communicates clinical findings in functional terms ("attention difficulty," "low mood interfering with school") rather than diagnostic labels at the screening visit. Diagnostic labelling occurs at confirmation visits with explicit family + adolescent consent. This protects family engagement in a stigma-heavy environment without compromising clinical accuracy.

No other material departures.

**Routing decisions:**
- A1 Orange behavioural / mood / autism flag → B1-08 within 2 weeks (per A1 §6.3 routing matrix).
- A1 Red — PHQ-A item 9 positive, suspected psychosis, severe self-harm, abuse disclosure — bypasses B1-08 and routes §7.1 directly.

**Coming from SKIDS Screening (A1):**
- A1 §5.2 Behavioural-emotional Orange (Vanderbilt full criteria, SDQ abnormal, M-CHAT-R/F ≥3 with positive follow-up, CBCL clinical range).

- A1 §5.2 Behavioural-emotional Red (PHQ-A item 9 positive, abuse disclosure, suicidality) — bypasses B1-08, routes §7.1 directly.

## §4 Diagnostic workflow

### 4.1 Structured history

History-taking in B1-08 is longer than most B1 protocols (typically 45–60 minutes for a new presentation). Allow time.

Ask, in this order:

1. *Presenting concern in family's words.* Verbatim. Capture the parent's framing AND the child's framing where developmentally appropriate. The two often differ substantially — that is itself diagnostic.
2. *Specific behaviours of concern.* Frequency, duration, settings (home vs. school vs. social), triggers, escalation patterns, what makes it better, what makes it worse. Examples in the child's life within the last 7 days.
3. *Onset and course.* When did the parent / child first notice? Sudden onset (consider trauma, medical cause) vs. gradual (more typical of developmental conditions). Has it worsened, plateaued, fluctuated?
4. *Functional impact.* School performance trajectory, school attendance pattern, peer relationships, family relationships, self-care, sleep, appetite, hobbies / activities engagement.
5. *Developmental history (for under 12).* Pregnancy / birth complications; developmental milestones (motor, language, social); preschool / early school history; prior assessments.
6. *Medical history.* Any chronic illness, medication use (steroids — mood / behaviour effects; stimulants for asthma; antihistamines), recent infections, head injuries, seizures (witnessed or suspected).
7. *Family history.* Psychiatric diagnoses in first-degree relatives, neurodevelopmental conditions, intellectual disability, substance-use disorders, suicide. Ask explicitly — Indian families often do not volunteer this.
8. *Psychosocial.* Caregiver structure, parental conflict, separation / bereavement, household-member illness, school changes, migration, financial stress, food insecurity (per B1-09), screen-time, exposure to violence (in family or community).
9. *Adolescent-specific (private).* HEEADSSS interview with the adolescent alone, after explicit confidentiality framing per §10.5. Cover Home, Education, Eating, Activities, Drugs, Sexuality, Suicide, Safety.
10. *Sleep, eating, exercise, screen time.* Quantitative. These are often part of the picture.
11. *Prior interventions tried.* Counselling / therapy (with whom, what kind, for how long, response), prior medications, traditional / religious interventions (engage rather than dismiss).
12. *Family expectations of the visit.* What does the family hope for? What outcome would feel like success to them?

**Red flags to elicit at history (any presentation):**
- Suicidality (active intent, plan, prior attempt) — direct disclosure, parent-reported, or PHQ-A item 9.
- Active self-harm without suicidal intent (cutting, burning) — frequency, severity.
- Hallucinations (auditory, visual, command), delusions, disorganised thinking, paranoia.
- Mania / hypomania features (decreased need for sleep, grandiosity, pressured speech, risky behaviour).
- Severe mood changes with seasonal pattern OR psychotic features.
- Disclosure of physical, sexual, or emotional abuse.
- Disclosure of being trafficked or exploited.
- Disclosure of substance use with daily / dependent pattern.
- Severe weight loss with food restriction (eating disorder).
- Acute behavioural change with neurological features (suspect medical cause — autoimmune encephalitis, post-infectious neurological, drug-related).
- Sudden personality change without psychosocial precipitant (possible underlying medical cause).

### 4.2 Examination

Examine for:

- *General observation.* Eye contact, engagement with parent, engagement with examiner, restlessness, organisation of speech, mood / affect appropriate to content.
- *Mental-state-like observation* (informal, throughout the visit). Speech rate / amount / coherence; mood (subjective + observed); thought content (any expressed worries, intrusive thoughts, fears); thought process (linear / tangential / disorganised); perception (any reports of hearing / seeing things others don't); cognition (orientation in age-appropriate terms, attention sufficient to engage, memory).
- *Developmental observation* (under 5y).
  - Joint attention: does the child point at things to share interest? Does the child follow the parent's pointing? (Joint attention deficits raise autism concern.)
  - Imaginative play: does the child use a toy spoon to feed a doll, or use a banana as a phone? (Pretend play developmentally typical from 18 months.)
  - Response to name: does the child turn to their name? (Lack of response by 12 months is a red flag.)
  - Social reciprocity in interaction.
- *Motor observation.* Gait, fine-motor (pencil grasp, tower-stacking), co-ordination. Subtle motor concerns can flag broader developmental issues.
- *Tic observation.* Frequency, type (motor, phonic), suppressibility, premonitory urge (≥7y). Distinguish from stereotypies (typically present in autism, rhythmic, no premonitory urge).
- *Skin and physical exam.* For NF1 stigmata (café-au-lait — cross with B1-05 §5.5), tuberous sclerosis stigmata, signs of self-harm (cutting, burns), signs of physical abuse, drug-use stigmata (track marks rare in adolescents but assess).
- *Vital signs.* Heart rate, blood pressure (with the right cuff size). Important for ADHD pharmacotherapy preconditions and for substance-use / eating-disorder workup.
- *Neurological.* Tone, deep tendon reflexes, cranial nerves on inspection, gait, fine motor — primarily to detect any neurological cause of the behavioural presentation that warrants §7.

### 4.4 In-clinic device tests

Required:
- *Privacy room* — for adolescent HEEADSSS interview.
- *Calibrated BP cuff with paediatric sizes* — for ADHD pharmacotherapy preconditions.
- *Resting ECG capability* — for ADHD pharmacotherapy preconditions before stimulant initiation. SKIDS clinic-grade 12-lead ECG OR partnership with same-day cardiology service. If ECG cannot be obtained in-clinic, the cardiac-screening precondition step routes to B1-16 Cardiac for the ECG before B1-08 initiates stimulant.
- *Calibrated scale + stadiometer* — for stimulant-induced growth-velocity surveillance (cross with B1-09 if growth concern develops).
- *Companion-supported scale-administration tablets* — Vanderbilt parent and teacher forms, PHQ-A, GAD-7, SDQ, CBCL, M-CHAT-R/F, CRAFFT, HEEADSSS structure, all administered electronically with auto-scoring.

Optional / on-indication:
- *Audio-recording capability for HEEADSSS* — only with explicit adolescent consent + clinic-policy permission. Not standard.
- *Pulse oximetry* for any drug-screen-positive adolescent presentation (to rule out concurrent intoxication in the visit).

### 4.5 Laboratory and imaging

Order:
- *Resting 12-lead ECG* — before initiating any stimulant (methylphenidate, atomoxetine has lower cardiac risk but B1-08 follows the conservative AAP / NICE position that pre-treatment ECG is required if any cardiac history or family history of sudden cardiac death; B1-08 universalises pre-treatment ECG given Indian-population cardiac-history under-elicitation and family-history under-disclosure) [AAP-2019-ADHD, NICE-ADHD-2018].
- *Thyroid function (TSH, fT4)* — for any presentation with mood / energy disturbance, weight change, or attention concern. Hypo/hyperthyroidism mimics multiple psychiatric conditions.
- *Full blood count + ferritin + vitamin D + B12* — for any presentation with mood / fatigue / cognitive concern. Often the medical-cause-of-mood-symptoms is iron, vitamin D, or B12 deficiency, which B1-09 manages.
- *Pregnancy test* — for any adolescent female before initiating any psychotropic.
- *Liver function* — baseline before atomoxetine.
- *Random blood glucose* — baseline before any antipsychotic if specialist initiated and B1-08 manages on-going.
- *Toxicology screen* — at adolescent disclosure of substance use, with adolescent consent (privacy framework applies).
- *EEG* — NOT routinely. EEG indication routes via §7 to neurology. Suspected pediatric epilepsy with behavioural presentation is a §7.2 trigger, not a B1-08-managed condition.

Do not order routinely:
- Genetic / metabolic panels for autism screening — these route via §7.3 to clinical genetics during the formal diagnostic assessment.
- Brain imaging for ADHD — there is no MRI / CT indication in routine ADHD diagnosis.
- Routine head MRI for any behavioural presentation in the absence of a focal neurological or cardinal red-flag finding.

## §5 Severity grading [SACRED SECTION]

B1-08 grades severity per condition. Scales drive most grading; clinical judgement supplements where the scale is borderline.

### 5.1 ADHD

| Severity | Threshold | Intervention tier | Subscription tier match |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | DSM-5 criteria met on Vanderbilt parent + teacher (≥ 6 of 9 inattentive or ≥ 6 of 9 hyperactive-impulsive items) AND functional impairment in 2+ settings AND no comorbidity | §6.1 Light: parent training + school accommodations + 12-week behavioural-first observation; pharmacotherapy only if behavioural fails or if family preference + parent-rated severity high [AAP-2019-ADHD] | Advanced ₹6,999 |
| **Moderate** | As Mild plus comorbidity (ODD, anxiety, learning concern) OR significant functional impairment | §6.2 Standard: parent training + school accommodations + LISSUN CBT (if comorbid anxiety / mood) + pharmacotherapy initiation under cardiac-screen preconditions per §6.3 | Premium ₹12,999 (or Advanced if family chooses without bundled CBT) |
| **Severe** | Vanderbilt severe across both informants AND severe impairment AND ≥ 2 comorbidities | §6.3 Intensive: full §6.2 plus close-interval titration; if not responding to first-line stimulant + behavioural at 12 weeks → §7.3 | Premium ₹12,999 |
| **Refractory or with complex comorbidity** | Persistence of severe impairment after 12 weeks of optimised §6.2; OR comorbidity exceeding B1-08 scope (psychosis, mania, autism with severe behaviour, intellectual disability) | §7.3 to pediatric psychiatry / developmental-behavioural pediatrics | — |

### 5.2 Autism screen (M-CHAT-R/F)

These do not have a Mild / Moderate / Severe at B1-08; the screen result triggers a routing decision.

| M-CHAT-R/F result | Action |
|---|---|
| Score 0–2 | Negative screen; no further action; rescreen at next periodic A1 |
| Score 3–7 | Administer the follow-up interview (F). If F positive (≥ 2 items confirmed) → §7.3. If F negative → rescreen at 4–6 weeks; if remains 3–7 with negative F → consider §7.3 routine for clinical evaluation |
| Score ≥ 8 | High risk; §7.3 routine to formal autism diagnostic assessment within 4 weeks |
| Clinical concern despite negative M-CHAT-R/F | §7.3 routine — clinical concern overrides the screen if the clinician's observation supports it |

### 5.3 Adolescent depression (PHQ-A) and anxiety (GAD-7)

| Severity | Score | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Minimal** | PHQ-A 0–4 OR GAD-7 0–4 | §6.1 — counselling, watchful waiting, parent-app activation, 4-week recheck |
| **Mild** | PHQ-A 5–9 OR GAD-7 5–9 | §6.1 / §6.2 — LISSUN CBT 6–8 sessions; recheck at 6 weeks |
| **Moderate** | PHQ-A 10–14 OR GAD-7 10–14 | §6.2 — LISSUN CBT + SSRI initiation (fluoxetine first-line) per §6 [AAP-Depression-2018, AAP-Anxiety-2020] |
| **Moderately severe** | PHQ-A 15–19 OR GAD-7 15–21 | §6.3 / §7.2 — joint-care with LISSUN psychiatrist; weekly visits; psychiatric handoff if not stabilising |
| **Severe** | PHQ-A 20–27 | §7.2 to pediatric psychiatry + LISSUN |
| **PHQ-A item 9 positive at any score** | — | §7.1 emergency [AAP-Suicide-2024] |
| **Active psychotic features OR mania** | — | §7.1 emergency |

**Mismatched-severity rule:** When PHQ-A and GAD-7 indicate different severity tiers (e.g., moderate depression + severe anxiety), manage at the HIGHER of the two severity levels. The more severe condition drives the intervention tier, follow-up cadence, and §7 routing.

### 5.4 Disruptive behaviour / oppositional pattern

| Severity | Findings | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | SDQ conduct subscale borderline; behaviour difficult but family functional; no aggression toward others | §6.1 — parent education + parent-app modules; 8-week recheck |
| **Moderate** | SDQ conduct clinical range; significant family stress; aggression in some contexts; school behaviour concerns | §6.2 — parent-training programme (LISSUN-delivered, 8–10 sessions); school liaison; family-meal / structure interventions |
| **Severe** | Conduct disorder pattern (cruelty to animals, fire-setting, theft); aggression with risk of harm; family unable to manage | §7.2 to pediatric psychiatry + LISSUN |
| **Suspected ODD with comorbid ADHD or mood** | — | §6.2 with concurrent §5.1 / §5.3 management |

### 5.5 OCD and tic disorders

| Condition | Severity | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **OCD mild** | Specific obsessions / compulsions with mild functional impact (Y-BOCS-CY equivalent < 16) | §6.1 — counselling, parent psychoeducation, watchful waiting; LISSUN exposure-and-response-prevention CBT if family prefers |
| **OCD moderate-severe** | Y-BOCS-CY ≥ 16 OR significant functional impact | §7.3 to pediatric psychiatry |
| **Tic disorder mild** | Transient or chronic tics with no functional impact; not Tourette pattern | §6.1 — psychoeducation, observation; most resolve in pediatric population |
| **Tic disorder moderate-severe / Tourette suspicion** | Multiple motor + vocal tics > 1 year OR significant functional / psychosocial impact | §7.3 to pediatric neurology / B2-02 if available |

### 5.6 Behavioural sleep / feeding / school-refusal patterns

These are graded against the dominant overlap protocol's framework:
- *Behavioural sleep:* per B1-07; B1-08 supports the family-system intervention.
- *Behavioural feeding:* per B1-09; B1-08 supports family + child psychological dimension.
- *School refusal:* anxiety subtype most commonly; treated per §5.3 anxiety pathway.

### 5.7 Adjustment / acute stress

| Severity | Findings | Intervention tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Identifiable stressor; symptoms < 4 weeks; functional impact moderate | §6.1 — supportive counselling at SKIDS counsellor; family education |
| **Moderate** | Symptoms 4–12 weeks; functional impact significant | §6.2 — LISSUN brief CBT (4–8 sessions); recheck at 6 weeks |
| **Severe / suspected PTSD with confirmed trauma** | Symptoms > 4 weeks with re-experiencing, avoidance, hyperarousal; suspected trauma | §7.3 to LISSUN trauma-specialist OR pediatric psychiatry |

### 5.8 Adolescent substance use

| CRAFFT score | Action |
|---|---|
| 0 | Reinforce abstinence; brief positive feedback |
| 1 | Brief intervention + 4-week recheck; parent-app substance-use education |
| ≥ 2 | Joint with B1-13 Adolescent + LISSUN; longer brief-intervention programme |
| Severe SUD criteria (daily use, dependence features, withdrawal) | §7.2 to pediatric psychiatry / addiction medicine + LISSUN |
| Acute intoxication / withdrawal | §7.1 to pediatric ER |

### 5.9 Boundary rules

- *Borderline scale scores:* a Vanderbilt score that's just at threshold or a PHQ-A score at the boundary between two tiers is treated at the higher tier for the first treatment cycle. Step down at follow-up if symptoms / scale support it.
- *Re-grading after intervention:* re-administer the same scale at every follow-up. Document delta.
- *Concurrent multi-condition:* ADHD with comorbid anxiety, ADHD with comorbid ODD, adolescent depression with comorbid substance use — all common. Treat each per its severity grading; integrate management to avoid medication interactions and conflicting therapy goals.
- *Family / school context uplift:* a child whose presentation looks Mild on scales but whose family system is clearly destabilising (parental conflict, sibling concerns, financial stress, recent trauma) is treated at one tier higher to reflect the systemic support need.
- *Cultural-context consideration:* some Indian families present with somatic symptoms (headaches, stomach aches, fatigue) that are the cultural-frame expression of mood / anxiety. Take somatic complaints seriously, screen with the appropriate scale, and treat the underlying psychiatric component without dismissing the somatic experience.
- *Stigma-driven under-presentation:* the family who presents with "small" concerns may have larger underlying concerns the family is not yet ready to disclose. The first visit may be the screening visit; the disclosure visit comes later. Pace accordingly.

## §6 Therapeutic options

### 6.1 Light tier (Mild presentations across categories — non-pharmacological)

**For all Light-tier presentations:**
- Single 45-minute SKIDS counsellor session at the first visit.
- Activation of the relevant parent-app modules (per §10.4).
- 4-week recheck visit OR therapy-progress check.
- Counsel: most mild presentations improve with structured family-and-school intervention without medication.

**ADHD Mild — behavioural-first (12 weeks before pharmacotherapy):**

Parent-training programme (LISSUN, 6–8 sessions over 12 weeks):
- Behavioural principles: clear instructions, immediate consequences, structured routines.
- Token economy / reward charts (age-appropriate).
- Time-out (used precisely, not as escalation).
- Parental self-care and stress management.

School-liaison support:
- Structured letter from B1-08 to school: classroom accommodations (front-row seating, frequent breaks, written instructions in addition to verbal, structured assignment chunking).
- Vanderbilt teacher form re-administration at 12 weeks.

Recheck: Vanderbilt parent + teacher at 12 weeks. If improvement → continue behavioural without pharmacotherapy. If no improvement → step up to §6.2 with pharmacotherapy initiation.

**Anxiety / Depression Mild — counselling + LISSUN brief CBT:**

LISSUN CBT 6–8 sessions:
- Cognitive restructuring (age-appropriate).
- Behavioural activation (depression).
- Exposure-based work (anxiety).
- Sleep, exercise, screen-time hygiene as adjuncts.

No pharmacotherapy at Mild tier; CBT first.

Recheck: PHQ-A / GAD-7 at 6 weeks.

**Disruptive behaviour Mild:**
- Parent psychoeducation in single session.
- Parent-app modules: structured routines, tantrum management, no-coercion principles.
- 8-week recheck with SDQ.

**Adjustment / acute stress Mild:**
- Single counselling session at SKIDS counsellor.
- Family education on the time course of normal grief / adjustment reactions.
- 4-week recheck.

**Substance use brief intervention (CRAFFT 1):**
- Adolescent brief intervention (FRAMES-style: Feedback, Responsibility, Advice, Menu of options, Empathy, Self-efficacy) — 20-minute conversation with the SKIDS pediatrician or counsellor.
- 4-week recheck with re-administered CRAFFT.
- Joint with B1-13.

Dispensary stock for §6.1: parent-app QR codes for behavioural and mental-health content; printed parent-training handbooks (English / Hindi / regional language as applicable).

Expected timeline: improvement in 6–12 weeks at Mild tier; if no improvement, step up.

### 6.2 Standard tier (Moderate presentations — therapy + selective pharmacotherapy)

**ADHD Moderate — behavioural + pharmacotherapy:**

Continue parent training + school liaison from §6.1, AND initiate pharmacotherapy under the cardiac-screening preconditions detailed in §6.3.

**Anxiety / Depression Moderate — LISSUN CBT + SSRI:**

LISSUN CBT (8–12 sessions) + SSRI initiation:

| Drug | Indication | Pediatric dose |
|---|---|---|
| Fluoxetine | First-line for depression and anxiety in adolescents ≥ 8 years; the only SSRI with a clear FDA pediatric depression indication and the strongest pediatric evidence base [AAP-Depression-2018, AAP-Anxiety-2020] | Start 10 mg PO once daily × 1–2 weeks; titrate to 20 mg/day; max 40 mg/day; trial duration 6–8 weeks at therapeutic dose before assessing response |
| Sertraline | Alternative for anxiety (often better-tolerated for some adolescents) | Start 25 mg PO once daily × 1 week; titrate by 25–50 mg increments to 50–100 mg/day; max 200 mg/day |
| Escitalopram | Alternative; not first-line in B1-08 | Start 5 mg PO once daily; titrate to 10–20 mg/day |

**SSRI counselling and monitoring (mandatory):**
- Pre-treatment counselling: explicit discussion of the FDA black-box warning on suicidality risk in young people (small absolute risk increase early in treatment — reframe accurately, do not omit, do not catastrophise); expected onset 2–4 weeks for some symptoms, 6–8 weeks for full response; common side effects (GI, headache, sleep disturbance, sexual side effects in adolescents); commitment to weekly check-ins for the first month.
- Weekly check-in (phone or app) for 4 weeks: mood self-report, side-effect screen, suicidality re-screen.
- In-clinic visit at week 4: PHQ-A / GAD-7 re-administer.
- In-clinic visit at week 8: response assessment; if PHQ-A / GAD-7 reduced by ≥ 50% → continue; if not → reassess (LISSUN CBT engagement, dose adequacy, comorbidity, diagnostic accuracy) and consider §7.3 routing.
- Continue for 6–12 months minimum after response; then taper slowly under B1-08 oversight.

**Disruptive behaviour Moderate — parent training programme:**

LISSUN parent-training programme (8–10 sessions over 12 weeks):
- Triple P (Positive Parenting Program) or comparable evidence-based curriculum.
- Family therapy (if family-system involvement is dominant).
- School-liaison support.
- 12-week recheck with SDQ.

If oppositional / conduct features persist OR family unable to engage in parent training → §7.3 routing.

**Adjustment Moderate / mild PTSD with confirmed trauma:**

LISSUN brief CBT (4–8 sessions). If suspected PTSD criteria fully met, route to LISSUN trauma-specialist therapist (some LISSUN therapists are TF-CBT certified) OR §7.3 to pediatric psychiatry.

**Substance use CRAFFT ≥ 2:**

Joint visits B1-08 + B1-13 + LISSUN. Structured 8-session adolescent substance-use brief-intervention programme. 12-week recheck with CRAFFT and clinical assessment.

### 6.3 Intensive tier — ADHD pharmacotherapy in detail

**ADHD pharmacotherapy preconditions (the cardiac screen):**

Before initiating ANY stimulant at B1-08, ALL of the following must be documented:

1. *Cardiovascular history elicited:*
   - Personal history of cardiac symptoms (exertional chest pain, exertional syncope, palpitations).
   - Family history of sudden cardiac death (any first-degree relative dying suddenly, especially under 40).
   - Family history of long-QT syndrome, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, dilated cardiomyopathy, structural heart disease.
2. *Cardiovascular examination documented:* heart rate, blood pressure (with paediatric cuff), murmur evaluation, peripheral pulse evaluation.
3. *Resting 12-lead ECG performed and interpreted:* normal interval, no concerning rhythm features, no Wolff-Parkinson-White, no long-QT pattern, no Brugada pattern.
4. *Family-history risks reviewed:* if any of (1) or (2) raises concern → §7 routine to B1-16 Cardiac for clearance BEFORE stimulant initiation. B1-08 does not initiate stimulants without B1-16 clearance in any concerning case.

**ADHD pharmacotherapy options:**

| Drug | Indication | Pediatric dose | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Methylphenidate immediate-release | First-line; flexible titration in younger children | 0.3 mg/kg/dose (round to nearest 2.5 mg; typical start 5 mg BD for child 15–20 kg; 10 mg BD for child 25–35 kg); titrate by 0.1 mg/kg/dose every 1–2 weeks; max 2 mg/kg/day or 60 mg/day; doses ≥ 6 years per AAP [AAP-2019-ADHD] | Short half-life; useful for confirming response and titrating |
| Methylphenidate extended-release (osmotic OROS or comparable) | First-line for stable maintenance | 18 mg OD for child ≤ 25 kg; 27 mg OD for child 25–40 kg; 36 mg OD for child > 40 kg; titrate by 9–18 mg every 1–2 weeks; max 54 mg/day (or 72 mg/day in adolescents > 50 kg under specialist guidance) | Once-daily dosing improves school adherence |
| Atomoxetine | Alternative if stimulant contraindicated, family refuses stimulant, or significant tic / anxiety comorbidity | 0.5 mg/kg/day × 1 week; titrate to 1.2 mg/kg/day; max 1.4 mg/kg/day or 100 mg/day | Slower onset (4–6 weeks for full response); LFT baseline; suicidality-warning counselling like SSRI |

**Stimulant initiation and monitoring:**
- Start at the lowest dose; titrate upward with weekly Vanderbilt re-administration for the first month.
- Side-effect screen at every visit: appetite reduction (weight monitoring with B1-09 if growth-velocity drops), sleep disturbance, irritability ("rebound" effect), tic exacerbation (re-evaluate diagnosis if tics emerge), mood / suicidality screen.
- BP and HR re-measured at every visit.
- Growth (weight + height) plotted at every visit; if growth velocity drops by ≥ 1 SD over 6 months → drug holiday discussion or atomoxetine switch.
- Annual ECG re-assessment.

**Drug-holiday considerations:**
- Weekend / vacation drug holidays are family-decision; B1-08 supports either approach; document.
- Annual full-medication review every year; consider whether continuation is still needed.

**Stop-medication criteria:**
- Vanderbilt scores normalise sustainably AND family / school report no functional impairment for ≥ 6 months → consider taper.
- Significant side effects without alternative drug option → taper and route §7.3.

**Anxiety / Depression Intensive tier (PHQ-A 15–19 / GAD-7 15–21):**

These may be at the upper border of B1-08 scope. Joint-care with LISSUN psychiatrist arm:
- Weekly visits at SKIDS for the first month.
- LISSUN CBT (12 sessions over 16 weeks).
- SSRI titrated to therapeutic dose.
- LISSUN psychiatrist consultation built into the management plan.
- If not stabilising at 4 weeks → §7.2 routing to pediatric psychiatry.

### 6.4 Contraindications and precautions

| Therapeutic | Contraindication / precaution | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Stimulant (methylphenidate) | Known structural heart disease, cardiac arrhythmia, family history of sudden cardiac death or long-QT, glaucoma, severe hypertension, hyperthyroidism (untreated), pheochromocytoma, MAOI use within 14 days, history of stimulant abuse | Cardiac screen per §6.3; contraindications → atomoxetine alternative or §7.3 |
| Stimulant in young child < 6 years | AAP recommends behavioural-first to age 6; pharmacotherapy in 4–5y range only with specialist input | §7.3 routine to DBP / pediatric psychiatry for any stimulant initiation < 6y |
| Atomoxetine | Liver disease (baseline LFT required); narrow-angle glaucoma; concurrent MAOI; pheochromocytoma | Specialist input |
| SSRI in adolescent | Concurrent MAOI, serotonergic agents (avoid combinations); active suicidality (§7.1); bipolar suspicion (mania risk) | Pre-treatment screen |
| SSRI in adolescent female | Pregnancy potential | Pregnancy test; contraception counselling per B1-13 / privacy framework; document |
| SSRI in any presentation | Black-box warning on emergent suicidality | Mandatory weekly check-in × 4 weeks; pre-treatment counselling documented |
| Antipsychotic | NOT initiated at B1-08 | §7.3 to pediatric psychiatry |
| Mood stabiliser (lithium, valproate) for psychiatric indication | NOT initiated at B1-08 | §7.3 to pediatric psychiatry |
| Benzodiazepine | Avoid in pediatric anxiety; very-short-term in acute panic only with explicit specialist input | §7 routing for any extended use |

### 6.5 Tapering and stopping

- *Stimulants:* generally stopped without taper; document rebound vigilance.
- *Atomoxetine:* taper over 1–2 weeks; abrupt discontinuation usually well-tolerated.
- *SSRI:* slow taper after sustained remission ≥ 6 months; reduce dose by 25% every 2–4 weeks; monitor for relapse and discontinuation symptoms (dizziness, irritability, "brain zaps" — most common with paroxetine, less with fluoxetine which has long half-life).
- *Behavioural therapy / CBT:* completion of structured course (typically 8–12 sessions); booster sessions as needed. Maintenance check-ins via parent app.

## §7 Hard referral triggers [SACRED SECTION]

The case escalates out of B1-08's scope when ANY of the following is true:

### 7.1 Emergency referral (same day, transport directly to ER or LISSUN-pre-notified named pediatric psychiatry crisis arm)

- [ ] **Active suicidality** — direct disclosure during HEEADSSS or open conversation OR PHQ-A item 9 positive at any total PHQ-A score OR caregiver-reported plan / intent / prior attempt within last 4 weeks. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER with mental-health capacity, with LISSUN psychiatrist pre-notified, within the same day. Adolescent does not leave SKIDS clinic unaccompanied; clinic protocol mandates adult caregiver hand-off or direct ambulance transfer. Same retention protocol as A1 §7.1 [AAP-Suicide-2024].
- [ ] **Active psychotic features** — hallucinations (auditory, visual, command), delusions, disorganised thinking, paranoid ideation. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER + pediatric psychiatry, within 1 hour.
- [ ] **Active mania / hypomania** — decreased need for sleep + grandiosity + pressured speech + risky behaviour OR clear bipolar features. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric psychiatry / pediatric ER, within 4 hours.
- [ ] **Severe self-harm** — recent or active cutting / burning with significant tissue injury OR repeated severe self-harm despite prior intervention. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER + LISSUN trauma / safety planning, within 4 hours.
- [ ] **Disclosure of ongoing abuse or exploitation** — sexual, physical, emotional, trafficking. → Named child-protection authority + named pediatric ER + LISSUN within the same day. Same retention protocol as A1 §7.1 — child not discharged to suspected-perpetrator caregiver.
- [ ] **Substance use acute intoxication or withdrawal** in the visit. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER, within 1 hour.
- [ ] **Severe eating disorder with crisis features** — bradycardia, postural hypotension, electrolyte disturbance, syncope, or cardiac compromise from restrictive eating. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER + pediatric psychiatry, within 1 hour. Cross-reference with B1-09 §7.2 + B1-13.
- [ ] **Acute behavioural change with neurological features** — recent-onset confusion, focal weakness, abnormal movements, seizure-like activity, abnormal pupillary response. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER + pediatric neurology, within 1 hour. Possible autoimmune encephalitis or other medical cause of behavioural change.
- [ ] **Acute aggression with risk to self or others** — imminent risk in clinic. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric ER + LISSUN crisis arm + safety personnel, immediately. Clinic-policy de-escalation protocol applies.

### 7.2 Urgent referral (within 7 days)

- [ ] **Severe depression (PHQ-A 20–27)** without active suicidality. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric psychiatry + LISSUN psychiatrist, within 7 days. SSRI may be initiated at B1-08 as a bridge.
- [ ] **Severe anxiety (GAD-7 15–21) not responding to optimised §6.2 at 6 weeks.** → SKIDS-T3 pediatric psychiatry, within 7 days.
- [ ] **Suspected pediatric bipolar disorder** — clear hypomanic / manic episode in history. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric psychiatry, within 7 days. Do not initiate antidepressant in suspected bipolar without psychiatry input (mania-induction risk).
- [ ] **Adolescent eating disorder with rapid weight loss but no crisis features** — joint with B1-09 §7.2 + B1-13. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric psychiatry within 7 days.
- [ ] **Severe substance use disorder** — daily use, dependence features, withdrawal pattern. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric psychiatry / addiction medicine within 7 days. Adolescent privacy framework applies for SUD disclosure handling.
- [ ] **Severe disruptive / conduct disorder** — cruelty to animals, fire-setting, theft, severe aggression, inability of family to manage safely. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric psychiatry + LISSUN family-therapy arm, within 7 days.
- [ ] **Acute behavioural deterioration during established management** — sudden worsening on a stable regimen, especially with new medication or new psychosocial event. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric psychiatry, within 7 days. Reassess for missed precipitant.

### 7.3 Routine referral (next available appointment, ≤ 4 weeks)

- [ ] **M-CHAT-R/F positive (score ≥ 8 OR follow-up F-positive)** for formal autism diagnostic assessment. → SKIDS-T3 developmental-behavioural pediatrics OR pediatric psychiatry with autism expertise, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Refractory ADHD** — not responding to 12 weeks of optimised §6.2 / §6.3 (behavioural + first-line stimulant + alternative); OR significant comorbidity exceeding scope (autism + ADHD + intellectual disability; ADHD + suspected mood / psychosis); OR dose escalation reaching ceiling without response. → SKIDS-T3 DBP / pediatric psychiatry, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **OCD moderate-severe** — Y-BOCS-CY ≥ 16 or significant functional impairment. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric psychiatry, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Tic disorder moderate-severe / Tourette syndrome suspicion** — multiple motor + vocal tics > 1 year OR significant functional / psychosocial impact. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric neurology (or B2-02 if available), within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Persistent functional impairment despite 12 weeks of LISSUN therapy** at the indicated severity tier. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric psychiatry, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Suspected complex trauma / PTSD requiring trauma-specialist therapy** beyond LISSUN brief-CBT scope. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric psychiatry / LISSUN trauma-specialist therapist (TF-CBT-certified), within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Suspected intellectual disability OR significant cognitive impairment** affecting management of behavioural presentation. → SKIDS-T3 DBP / clinical psychology for cognitive assessment (WISC), within 4 weeks. Cross with B1-12 Learning.
- [ ] **Suspected pediatric epilepsy** with behavioural presentation (e.g., absence seizures presenting as inattention; possible benign rolandic epilepsy). → SKIDS-T3 pediatric neurology (or B2-02), within 4 weeks. Do not start ADHD medication until epilepsy ruled out.
- [ ] **Family / caregiver mental health emergency** — primary caregiver with active psychiatric crisis. → Named adult psychiatric care + child-safeguarding assessment + family-support planning, within 4 weeks.
- [ ] **Family request for second opinion** — appropriately granted and supported. → SKIDS-T3 pediatric psychiatry / DBP, within 4 weeks.

### 7.4 Handoff communication

When B1-08 fires a §7 referral, the SKIDS pediatrician + LISSUN-side clinical lead (where LISSUN involved) sends the receiving clinician (auto-generated by Companion):

1. **Child identification:** name, DOB, child_id, parent contact, parent consent confirmation, current medications, known allergies, atopic / chronic-condition comorbidities.
2. **The trigger:** which §7 sub-trigger fired with the underlying clinical / scale finding.
3. **Diagnosis (provisional or working):** ICD-10 / DSM-5 framing where determined; explicit "pending formal diagnosis" where appropriate (e.g., autism screen positive but not formally diagnosed).
4. **Clinical context:** structured history summary; relevant family history; psychosocial summary.
5. **Scale trajectory:** all available Vanderbilt / PHQ-A / GAD-7 / SDQ / CRAFFT scores in chronological order; quantified delta.
6. **Therapies tried at B1-08 / LISSUN and outcomes:** pharmacotherapy (drug, dose, duration, response), CBT or parent training (sessions completed, response), school accommodations, behavioural plans.
7. **Investigations done:** ECG, TSH, FBC + ferritin + vitamin D + B12, pregnancy test if relevant.
8. **Bridge interventions started** (e.g., empirical SSRI initiation pending psychiatry review for severe depression).
9. **Safety plan documented** (for any §7.1 referral): collaborative safety plan (Stanley-Brown model) content, lethal-means counselling done, parent agreed to means restriction (medications locked away, etc.).
10. **Reason for referral:** one to two sentences.
11. **Urgency tier and target window:** §7.1 / §7.2 / §7.3 with the time bound.
12. **SKIDS-T3 / clinic** + appointment booking status.
13. **LISSUN therapist note** where therapy already engaged — sessions completed, treatment focus, formulation summary.

The packet is auto-generated by Companion. SKIDS clinician reviews and signs off. For §7.1 emergencies, phone-call confirmation in addition to the packet, and for suicidality §7.1 specifically, LISSUN psychiatrist is pre-notified to receive the case.

## §8 Follow-up schedule

### 8.1 Follow-up frequency

| Severity tier / condition | First follow-up | Subsequent | Annual review |
|---|---|---|---|
| ADHD Mild on behavioural-first | 12 weeks | every 12 weeks | Yes |
| ADHD Moderate / Severe on stimulant | weekly × 4 (titration), then 4 weeks, then 8–12 weeks | annual ECG | Yes |
| ADHD Moderate / Severe on atomoxetine | weekly check-in × 4, in-clinic at 4 weeks, then 8 weeks | annual ECG | Yes |
| Anxiety / Depression Mild on CBT | 6 weeks | 12 weeks | Yes |
| Anxiety / Depression Moderate on SSRI + CBT | weekly × 4 (post-initiation), then 4 weeks, then 8 weeks, then 12 weeks; SSRI at 6 months and 12 months for taper discussion | annual | Yes |
| Disruptive behaviour Standard on parent-training | 4 weeks (mid-programme), 12 weeks (end-programme) | 6 months | Yes |
| OCD / tic Mild observation | 12 weeks | 6 months | Yes |
| Adjustment Standard on LISSUN brief CBT | 6 weeks (mid-programme) | 12 weeks (end-programme) | — |
| Substance use brief intervention | 4 weeks | 12 weeks | — |
| §7-routed | per receiving clinician + B1-08 surveillance role | — | — |

### 8.2 What to recheck at each follow-up

- *Scale re-administration:* same scale family; document delta.
- *Adherence:* medication, therapy attendance.
- *Side-effect screen:* per drug.
- *Functional impact:* school performance, family, peer, self-care.
- *Safety screen:* suicidality re-screen at every visit on adolescent depression / anxiety, and at any visit where any concerning marker emerges.
- *Growth (for stimulants):* weight + height at every visit; cross with B1-09 if velocity drops.
- *Cardiovascular (for stimulants):* HR + BP at every visit; annual ECG.
- *LISSUN session integration:* progress note review.

### 8.3 Treatment success criteria

- *ADHD:* Vanderbilt scores reduced to sub-clinical OR functional impairment substantially improved per family + teacher report; sustained ≥ 6 months.
- *Anxiety / Depression:* PHQ-A / GAD-7 reduction ≥ 50% by 6–8 weeks AND continued improvement to remission (≤ 4); sustained ≥ 6 months on SSRI for taper consideration.
- *Disruptive behaviour:* SDQ conduct subscale moved from clinical to borderline / normal; family reports manageable behaviour.
- *Adjustment:* return to baseline functioning.
- *Substance use brief intervention:* CRAFFT score reduced; sustained behaviour change.

### 8.4 Step-down criteria

- ADHD on stimulant: stable Vanderbilt scores for ≥ 6 months → consider drug holiday or taper (annual review).
- SSRI: sustained remission ≥ 6 months → slow taper with monitoring.
- LISSUN therapy: structured course completion → maintenance / booster sessions PRN.

### 8.5 Step-up criteria (within Tier 1)

- ADHD Mild on behavioural-first not improving at 12 weeks → step up to §6.2 with pharmacotherapy initiation.
- Anxiety / Depression Mild not improving on CBT alone at 6 weeks → step up to SSRI initiation.
- Disruptive Mild not improving on parent psychoeducation → step up to LISSUN parent-training programme.

### 8.6 Step-out criteria

Per §7. Specifically: refractory severity at any pathway; emergent suicidality / psychosis / mania; eating-disorder crisis features; severe substance use; suspected medical cause of behavioural change.

### 8.7 Disenrolment / loss-to-follow-up

A child active on B1-08 management on stimulant or SSRI who misses two consecutive follow-ups is contacted by Manager + named ped + LISSUN therapist. Loss-to-follow-up on stimulant is high-priority because:
- Diversion risk (medication may be misused outside the family).
- Discontinuation without taper is generally tolerated for stimulants but may cause functional deterioration.

Loss-to-follow-up on SSRI is high-priority because of relapse risk and discontinuation-syndrome concerns. Both are quarterly-review triggers under §12.

Scale administration and scoring locally cached. Prescription, LISSUN booking, alerts queue for sync. §7.1 emergency in offline mode: hard-stop with phone-call guidance.

## §10 Parent-facing communication

### 10.1 Scripted explanations

When grading is Mild ADHD on behavioural-first:

> "[Rohit]'s scores from his teacher and what you describe at home are consistent with what we'd call attention difficulty — at a level that's affecting his school performance and family time but not severely. The first thing we want to try is a structured parent-training programme through our partner LISSUN — you'll meet with the therapist for six to eight sessions over twelve weeks, learning specific techniques for routines, instructions, and consequences. We'll also write to his school with some classroom-based suggestions. We come back in twelve weeks and re-do the questionnaires. If things are improving, we continue without medication. If they're not, we'd discuss medication then. The reason we're not jumping to medication: at this severity, the behavioural approach often gets us where we need to be."

When grading is Moderate ADHD with stimulant initiation:

> "[Rohit]'s attention difficulty is at a level where the behavioural work alone is unlikely to be enough — and we've got [some / no] evidence that the behavioural approach has plateaued. We're going to add a medication called methylphenidate, which is the most-studied medication for this in children. Before we start, I need a clear cardiac history, his examination today is fine, and we need a heart tracing — an ECG — which we can do here today. Why the heart check: stimulants can rarely cause heart problems in children with underlying heart conditions, so we screen everyone before starting. Once we've cleared that, we start at a low dose and adjust upward over a few weeks. Common side effects: appetite reduction, sleep difficulty, and sometimes irritability when the medication wears off. We monitor his weight, height, blood pressure, and we re-do the questionnaires every four weeks. The medication isn't a cure; it makes the work of attention easier. The behavioural work continues alongside."

When grading is Moderate adolescent depression with SSRI + CBT initiation:

> "[Aanya], we've talked together about how you've been feeling, and the questionnaire scores match what you've described — moderate depression. There are two things that work: a structured talking therapy called CBT, which we set up through our partner LISSUN, and a medication called fluoxetine. For moderate depression, the evidence is best when we use both together. The medication takes about six weeks to fully work; you may feel some improvement before that. There's a thing the medication packets warn about — that in some young people there can be a small increase in suicidal thoughts when they first start. That risk is small but we take it seriously, so we'll be in touch every week for the first month — phone or app — and back in clinic at four weeks. If at any point your thoughts go to harming yourself, you call this number [emergency number] right away. The medication isn't permanent — most adolescents stay on it for six to twelve months once they're feeling better, and then we taper it off."

When PHQ-A item 9 is positive (§7.1):

> "Thank you for telling me about those thoughts. What you're feeling is something I want a colleague at [partner ER / pediatric psychiatry] to help with today. This isn't because you're being sent away — it's because the kind of help you need right now is best given there. I'm going to call them now and we'll go together. While we're waiting, your parent / caregiver and I are going to put together a safety plan with you — what helps you feel safer right now, who you can call. Are you okay if I bring [parent] in for the safety-planning conversation, with you in the room?"

Subsequent: lethal-means counselling with parent ("we want to make sure that any medications, sharp objects, etc., are not easily accessible at home over the next few weeks; we'll give you a checklist").

When M-CHAT-R/F screen positive (§7.3):

> "[Aanya]'s screening today suggests we should look more carefully at how she communicates and interacts. The screening doesn't give us the answer — it tells us a fuller assessment is the right next step. I'm referring [her] to [named DBP / pediatric psychiatry] for that fuller assessment. The appointment is [date]. Please bring any prior reports, videos of [her] interacting at home, and a list of your specific concerns. Whatever the assessment shows, we'll continue to support [her] from this clinic. Many children who screen positive do not have autism; some do; either way, getting the right answer early is helpful."

### 10.5 Cultural sensitivities

- *Mental-health stigma.* Indian families often experience significant stigma around psychiatric diagnoses — diagnostic labels can affect marriage prospects, school admissions, family identity. Communicate clinical findings in *functional terms* at the screening visit ("attention difficulty," "low mood interfering with school," "anxiety affecting daily life") rather than in formal diagnostic labels. Move to formal labels at confirmation visits with explicit family + adolescent consent, framing the labelling as enabling care access (school accommodations, insurance, longitudinal record-keeping) rather than as identity-pinning.
- *Adolescent privacy and confidentiality.* The privacy framework is non-negotiable: adolescent psychosocial information is confidential except in three circumstances — (1) active intent to harm self, (2) active intent to harm others, (3) being abused / exploited. Frame this at the start of the private interview, the same script every time. The Companion logs the discussion. This is what enables adolescent engagement; without it, B1-08 is not effective.
- *Family-system therapy.* Indian families are typically multi-generational; behavioural concerns are family-system concerns. Where appropriate, involve grandparents in parent-training sessions; framing it as "family-system change" rather than "fixing your child" reduces child-blaming and engages the broader system.
- *School-liaison constraints.* Some Indian schools resist accommodation requests; some are very supportive. Frame the school-liaison letter not as a demand but as a clinical recommendation; offer to discuss with the school directly if the family wishes. Document school responsiveness.
- *Religious and traditional interventions.* Pujas, prayers, traditional-medicine practitioners are commonly consulted in parallel. Engage rather than dismiss; maintain the clinical pathway alongside the family's chosen spiritual practice. Discuss explicitly any specific traditional remedies the family uses, as some may contain substances with neuropsychiatric effects (lead in sindur / kohl, neuroactive herbs).
- *ADHD pharmacotherapy hesitancy.* Stimulants meet substantial Indian-family hesitancy ("we don't want to give psychiatric medication to a child"). Frame the medication accurately: methylphenidate is one of the most-studied medications in pediatrics; the risk-benefit data supports use at the indicated severity; the alternative is cumulative academic, social, and self-image cost from untreated significant impairment. Document family hesitancy and behavioural-tier-only management if family declines pharmacotherapy.
- *Suicidality stigma.* Disclosure of suicidality is loaded with shame and family-honour concerns in Indian context. Receive disclosures matter-of-factly without affect; frame the §7.1 routing not as scandal but as serious clinical concern requiring specialist support; engage parents in the safety plan with explicit "this is not a verdict on parenting" framing.
- *Substance-use disclosure.* Adolescent substance-use disclosure under privacy framework risks family-honour fallout if disclosed. Engage adolescent in the SUD pathway under privacy; involve family only with adolescent consent unless §7.1 / §7.2 criteria fire.
- *Eating-disorder framing.* Restrictive eating in Indian adolescents (especially girls) may be culturally praised as discipline / beauty. Frame the eating concern in health-and-functional terms with family; engage adolescent privately in the §4 / §5 / §6 work.
- *Caste, class, and language* in the therapy relationship. LISSUN therapists ideally match the family's primary language; cultural-match is documented as a quality factor in the LISSUN partnership.

## §12 Quality and audit

### 12.1 Outcome metrics

B1-08 is working well when:

- ≥ 70% of ADHD Mild on behavioural-first achieve sustained Vanderbilt improvement at 12 weeks.
- ≥ 70% of ADHD Moderate / Severe on stimulant achieve sustained Vanderbilt improvement at 12 weeks.
- 100% of stimulant initiations have completed cardiac-screen documentation (history + exam + ECG + family history) — Companion's hard block enforces this; metric is audit confirmation.
- ≥ 60% of moderate adolescent depression on SSRI + CBT achieve PHQ-A response (reduction ≥ 50%) at 8 weeks.
- ≥ 70% of moderate adolescent anxiety on CBT (± SSRI) achieve GAD-7 response at 6–8 weeks.
- 100% of PHQ-A item 9 positive cases (§7.1) result in same-day handoff to LISSUN-pre-notified ER.
- 100% of suicidality §7.1 cases have a documented safety plan + lethal-means counselling.
- ≥ 95% of M-CHAT-R/F-positive cases (§7.3) attend the formal autism diagnostic assessment within 4 weeks.
- ≥ 80% of LISSUN-referred families attend at least 80% of scheduled sessions.
- < 5% of stimulant prescriptions exceed the maximum-dose ceiling for age — antimicrobial-stewardship-equivalent metric for stimulant practice.
- Adolescent privacy-disclosure mismatches (information disclosed without adolescent consent) = 0 — any breach is a hard quality fail.
- Loss-to-follow-up on stimulant or SSRI < 8% per quarter.

### 12.2 Quarterly case review

The protocol owner reviews every quarter, with the named LISSUN clinical lead countersigning:
- A random sample of 30 B1-08 visits balanced across pathways.
- All §7.1 referrals (suicidality, psychosis, abuse, eating-disorder crisis) — timing, handoff packet quality, safety plan quality, family communication.
- All stimulant initiations (cardiac-screen documentation completeness, dose appropriateness, response trajectory, side-effect handling).
- All SSRI initiations (counselling documentation, weekly check-in completion, response trajectory).
- All severity-tier downgrades on Screen 5.
- All M-CHAT-R/F-positive cases routed to formal diagnosis (timing, family communication).
- All adolescent privacy events (confidentiality discussions held; any disclosure breach).
- All loss-to-follow-up cases on active medication.
